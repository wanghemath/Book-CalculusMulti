9  Chapter 9. Linear Approximation, Tangent Planes, and Differentials

The local linear model behind tangent planes, sensitivity, and uncertainty

9.1 Chapter overview

The central message of this chapter is simple and powerful:

ImportantCore principle

A differentiable multivariable function is approximately linear near a point.

In single-variable calculus, the tangent line is the best first-order approximation to a curve near a point. In multivariable calculus, the same idea becomes the tangent plane for a graph \(z=f(x,y)\) and the tangent hyperplane for a function of many variables. Algebraically, the approximation is built from partial derivatives. Geometrically, it is the flat object that best matches the surface near the point. Computationally, it is the first local model used in numerical methods, optimization, uncertainty propagation, and statistical approximation.

This chapter is one of the main bridges from Calculus III to graduate-level applied mathematics, statistics, data science, AI, and machine learning. Linear approximation is the mathematical idea behind sensitivity analysis, local regression, the delta method, Newton-type algorithms, first-order optimization, and the local behavior of loss functions.

NoteTeaching-material connection

This chapter develops the material on tangent planes, linearization, differential approximation, differentiability, total differentials, and tangent planes to level surfaces. It uses the examples \(z=3x^2+y^2\), gradient-based approximation, and the level-surface problem \(F(x,y,z)=x^3+y^2e^z+xyz\) as core models.

9.2 Learning goals

After studying this chapter, you should be able to:

  1. explain why tangent planes are multivariable tangent lines;
  2. find the tangent plane to a surface \(z=f(x,y)\) at a point;
  3. compute the linearization \(L(x,y)\) of a function \(f(x,y)\) at \((a,b)\);
  4. use linearization to estimate nearby function values;
  5. write linear approximation in gradient-dot-product form;
  6. explain the difference between partial derivatives existing and differentiability;
  7. compute total differentials and use them for error propagation;
  8. find tangent planes to level surfaces \(F(x,y,z)=c\);
  9. interpret local linear models in statistics, data science, and machine learning;
  10. use Python to visualize tangent planes and approximation errors.

9.3 9.1 Review: tangent lines and local linearity

For a single-variable function \(y=f(x)\), the tangent line at \(x=a\) is

\[ L(x)=f(a)+f'(a)(x-a). \]

The tangent line is useful because when \(x\) is close to \(a\),

\[ f(x) \approx L(x). \]

The number \(f'(a)\) tells how the output changes per unit change in the input. If \(\Delta x=x-a\), then

\[ \Delta y=f(x)-f(a) \approx f'(a)\Delta x. \]

This is the one-variable version of the multivariable formula

\[ \Delta z \approx f_x(a,b)\Delta x+f_y(a,b)\Delta y. \]

The right side is linear in the input changes \(\Delta x\) and \(\Delta y\). That is why we call it a linear approximation.

TipLocal does not mean global

A tangent line or tangent plane usually gives a good approximation only near the point of tangency. Far away from the point, the nonlinear curvature of the graph becomes important.

9.4 9.2 Tangent planes to graphs \(z=f(x,y)\)

Let \(z=f(x,y)\) be differentiable near \((a,b)\), and let

\[ P=(a,b,f(a,b)) \]

be the point on the graph. The tangent plane at \(P\) is

\[ z-f(a,b)=f_x(a,b)(x-a)+f_y(a,b)(y-b). \]

Equivalently,

\[ z=f(a,b)+f_x(a,b)(x-a)+f_y(a,b)(y-b). \]

The function on the right is the linearization of \(f\) at \((a,b)\):

\[ L(x,y)=f(a,b)+f_x(a,b)(x-a)+f_y(a,b)(y-b). \]

The approximation

\[ f(x,y)\approx L(x,y) \]

is called the linear approximation or tangent plane approximation.

9.4.1 Why this is the right plane

At the point \((a,b)\), the tangent plane must match three pieces of first-order information:

  1. the height \(f(a,b)\);
  2. the slope in the \(x\)-direction, \(f_x(a,b)\);
  3. the slope in the \(y\)-direction, \(f_y(a,b)\).

The plane

\[ z=L(x,y) \]

has exactly these three properties.

A useful normal vector to the tangent plane is

\[ \langle f_x(a,b), f_y(a,b), -1\rangle. \]

This comes from rewriting the tangent plane as

\[ f_x(a,b)(x-a)+f_y(a,b)(y-b)-(z-f(a,b))=0. \]

9.5 9.3 Worked example: tangent plane to an elliptic paraboloid

9.5.1 Example 9.1

Find the tangent plane to

\[ z=f(x,y)=3x^2+y^2 \]

at the point \((1,2,7)\).

9.5.2 Solution

First check the height:

\[ f(1,2)=3(1)^2+(2)^2=7. \]

Compute the partial derivatives:

\[ f_x(x,y)=6x, \qquad f_y(x,y)=2y. \]

At \((1,2)\),

\[ f_x(1,2)=6, \qquad f_y(1,2)=4. \]

Therefore the tangent plane is

\[ z-7=6(x-1)+4(y-2). \]

Solving for \(z\) gives

\[ z=6x+4y-7. \]

Thus the linearization is

\[ L(x,y)=6x+4y-7. \]

To estimate \(f(1.1,1.9)\), compute

\[ L(1.1,1.9)=6(1.1)+4(1.9)-7=7.2. \]

The exact value is

\[ f(1.1,1.9)=3(1.1)^2+(1.9)^2=7.24. \]

The tangent plane gives a close approximation because \((1.1,1.9)\) is near \((1,2)\).

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x = np.linspace(0.2, 1.8, 80)
y = np.linspace(1.2, 2.8, 80)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)
Z = 3*X**2 + Y**2
L = 7 + 6*(X - 1) + 4*(Y - 2)

fig = plt.figure()
ax = fig.add_subplot(111, projection='3d')
ax.plot_surface(X, Y, Z, alpha=0.65, linewidth=0)
ax.plot_surface(X, Y, L, alpha=0.35, linewidth=0)
ax.scatter([1], [2], [7], s=60)
ax.set_xlabel('$x$')
ax.set_ylabel('$y$')
ax.set_zlabel('$z$')
ax.set_title('Surface and tangent plane')
plt.show()

9.6 9.4 Linear approximation in gradient form

The linear approximation formula can be written more compactly using the gradient. Let

\[ \Delta x=x-a, \qquad \Delta y=y-b, \qquad \Delta \mathbf{x}=\langle \Delta x,\Delta y\rangle. \]

Then

\[ f(x,y)-f(a,b)\approx \nabla f(a,b)\cdot \Delta \mathbf{x}. \]

Equivalently,

\[ \Delta z\approx \nabla f(a,b)\cdot \Delta \mathbf{x}. \]

This formula is one of the most important formulas in multivariable calculus. It says:

Near a point, the change in the output is approximately the dot product of the gradient and the input displacement.

For a function \(f:\mathbb R^n\to \mathbb R\), the same formula is

\[ f(\mathbf{x})\approx f(\mathbf{a})+\nabla f(\mathbf{a})\cdot (\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{a}). \]

9.6.1 Example 9.2

Suppose

\[ f(2,1)=13, \qquad \nabla f(2,1)=\langle 12,2\rangle. \]

Estimate \(f(2.1,0.9)\).

9.6.2 Solution

The input displacement is

\[ \Delta \mathbf{x}=\langle 2.1-2,0.9-1\rangle =\langle 0.1,-0.1\rangle. \]

Therefore

\[ \Delta z\approx \langle 12,2\rangle\cdot \langle 0.1,-0.1\rangle =12(0.1)+2(-0.1)=1. \]

So

\[ f(2.1,0.9)\approx 13+1=14. \]

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

p = np.array([2.0, 1.0])
q = np.array([2.1, 0.9])
grad = np.array([12.0, 2.0])
delta = q - p

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.scatter([p[0], q[0]], [p[1], q[1]], s=70)
ax.annotate('P=(2,1)', p + np.array([-0.08, 0.03]))
ax.annotate('Q=(2.1,0.9)', q + np.array([0.02, -0.05]))
ax.quiver(p[0], p[1], delta[0], delta[1], angles='xy', scale_units='xy', scale=1)
ax.quiver(p[0], p[1], 0.08*grad[0], 0.08*grad[1], angles='xy', scale_units='xy', scale=1)
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
ax.set_xlim(1.85, 3.1)
ax.set_ylim(0.65, 1.35)
ax.set_xlabel('$x$')
ax.set_ylabel('$y$')
ax.set_title('Displacement and gradient direction')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
plt.show()

9.7 9.5 Approximation error and curvature

The linearization keeps only first-order information. The error comes from second-order and higher-order behavior. For the paraboloid

\[ f(x,y)=3x^2+y^2, \]

the tangent plane at \((1,2)\) is

\[ L(x,y)=6x+4y-7. \]

The error is

\[ E(x,y)=f(x,y)-L(x,y). \]

For this example,

\[ E(x,y)=3(x-1)^2+(y-2)^2. \]

This is always nonnegative because the surface is convex and lies above its tangent plane. The error is small near \((1,2)\) and grows quadratically as we move away.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x = np.linspace(0.4, 1.6, 200)
y = np.linspace(1.4, 2.6, 200)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)
f = 3*X**2 + Y**2
L = 7 + 6*(X - 1) + 4*(Y - 2)
E = f - L

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
cs = ax.contourf(X, Y, E, levels=20)
fig.colorbar(cs, ax=ax, label='error f - L')
ax.scatter([1], [2], s=60)
ax.set_xlabel('$x$')
ax.set_ylabel('$y$')
ax.set_title('Error of tangent plane approximation')
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
plt.show()
NoteConnection to Chapter 18

Later, Taylor approximation will make this precise. The second-order term involves the Hessian matrix. Linear approximation is first-order Taylor approximation; quadratic approximation is second-order Taylor approximation.

9.8 9.6 Differentiability

A function \(f(x,y)\) is differentiable at \((a,b)\) if its change can be written as a linear part plus an error that becomes negligible compared with the size of the input change.

One way to state this is:

\[ \Delta z=f_x(a,b)\Delta x+f_y(a,b)\Delta y+\varepsilon_1\Delta x+\varepsilon_2\Delta y, \]

where

\[ \varepsilon_1\to 0, \qquad \varepsilon_2\to 0 \]

as \((\Delta x,\Delta y)\to (0,0)\).

Equivalently,

\[ f(a+\Delta x,b+\Delta y)=f(a,b)+f_x(a,b)\Delta x+f_y(a,b)\Delta y+\text{small error}. \]

The important idea is not just that the partial derivatives exist. The important idea is that together they produce a good linear model near the point.

WarningPartial derivatives alone are not enough

A function may have partial derivatives at a point but fail to be differentiable there. Differentiability is stronger than the existence of coordinate-direction slopes.

A practical sufficient condition is the following.

TipSufficient condition for differentiability

If \(f_x\) and \(f_y\) exist near \((a,b)\) and are continuous at \((a,b)\), then \(f\) is differentiable at \((a,b)\).

This theorem is one reason continuous partial derivatives are so important. In most computational and applied examples, we verify differentiability by verifying that the partial derivatives are continuous.

9.9 9.7 A cautionary example

Consider

\[ f(x,y)= \begin{cases} \dfrac{xy}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}, & (x,y)\neq (0,0),\\ 0, & (x,y)=(0,0). \end{cases} \]

At the origin, the coordinate partial derivatives exist:

\[ f_x(0,0)=0, \qquad f_y(0,0)=0. \]

So the candidate linearization is \(L(x,y)=0\). However, along the line \(y=x\),

\[ f(x,x)=\frac{x^2}{\sqrt{2x^2}}=\frac{|x|}{\sqrt 2}. \]

This is not negligible compared with the distance \(\sqrt{x^2+x^2}=\sqrt 2|x|\). Therefore the function is not differentiable at the origin.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x = np.linspace(-1, 1, 250)
y = np.linspace(-1, 1, 250)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)
R = np.sqrt(X**2 + Y**2)
Z = np.zeros_like(R)
mask = R > 1e-12
Z[mask] = X[mask]*Y[mask]/R[mask]

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
cs = ax.contourf(X, Y, Z, levels=30)
fig.colorbar(cs, ax=ax, label='$f(x,y)$')
ax.plot(x, x, linewidth=2)
ax.axhline(0, linewidth=1)
ax.axvline(0, linewidth=1)
ax.set_xlabel('$x$')
ax.set_ylabel('$y$')
ax.set_title('Behavior near the origin')
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
plt.show()

9.10 9.8 Tangent planes to level surfaces

Many important surfaces are not given as graphs \(z=f(x,y)\). Instead, they are given implicitly as level surfaces

\[ F(x,y,z)=c. \]

Suppose

\[ P=(a,b,c_0) \]

is a point on the level surface. If

\[ \nabla F(P)\neq \mathbf{0}, \]

then the gradient vector is normal to the level surface at \(P\). Therefore the tangent plane is

\[ \nabla F(P)\cdot \langle x-a,y-b,z-c_0\rangle=0. \]

In coordinates, if

\[ \nabla F(P)=\langle F_x(P),F_y(P),F_z(P)\rangle, \]

then the tangent plane is

\[ F_x(P)(x-a)+F_y(P)(y-b)+F_z(P)(z-c_0)=0. \]

9.10.1 Why the gradient is normal

If a curve \(\mathbf r(t)\) lies on the level surface, then

\[ F(\mathbf r(t))=c. \]

Differentiating with respect to \(t\) gives

\[ \nabla F(\mathbf r(t))\cdot \mathbf r'(t)=0. \]

Thus the gradient is perpendicular to every tangent direction \(\mathbf r'(t)\) on the surface.

9.11 9.9 Worked example: linearization and tangent plane to a level surface

9.11.1 Example 9.3

Let

\[ F(x,y,z)=x^3+y^2e^z+xyz \]

and let

\[ P=(1,2,0). \]

  1. Find the linearization of \(F\) at \(P\).
  2. Estimate \(F(1.1,1.9,0)\) using the linearization.
  3. Find the tangent plane to the level surface through \(P\).

9.11.2 Solution

First evaluate the function:

\[ F(1,2,0)=1^3+2^2e^0+1\cdot 2\cdot 0=5. \]

Compute the partial derivatives:

\[ F_x=3x^2+yz, \]

\[ F_y=2ye^z+xz, \]

\[ F_z=y^2e^z+xy. \]

At \(P=(1,2,0)\),

\[ F_x(P)=3, \qquad F_y(P)=4, \qquad F_z(P)=6. \]

So the linearization is

\[ L(x,y,z)=F(P)+F_x(P)(x-1)+F_y(P)(y-2)+F_z(P)(z-0). \]

Therefore

\[ L(x,y,z)=5+3(x-1)+4(y-2)+6z. \]

After simplifying,

\[ L(x,y,z)=3x+4y+6z-6. \]

Now estimate:

\[ F(1.1,1.9,0)\approx L(1.1,1.9,0)=3(1.1)+4(1.9)-6=4.9. \]

The level surface through \(P\) is

\[ F(x,y,z)=5. \]

Its tangent plane is

\[ 3(x-1)+4(y-2)+6z=0. \]

Equivalently,

\[ 3x+4y+6z-11=0. \]

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Solve the tangent plane for z: 3(x-1)+4(y-2)+6z=0
x = np.linspace(0.6, 1.4, 80)
y = np.linspace(1.5, 2.5, 80)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)
Zplane = -(3*(X - 1) + 4*(Y - 2))/6

# Approximate the level surface locally by solving F(x,y,z)=5 with Newton iterations in z.
def F(x, y, z):
    return x**3 + y**2*np.exp(z) + x*y*z

Z = Zplane.copy()
for _ in range(6):
    dFdz = Y**2*np.exp(Z) + X*Y
    Z = Z - (F(X, Y, Z) - 5)/dFdz

fig = plt.figure()
ax = fig.add_subplot(111, projection='3d')
ax.plot_surface(X, Y, Z, alpha=0.65, linewidth=0)
ax.plot_surface(X, Y, Zplane, alpha=0.35, linewidth=0)
ax.scatter([1], [2], [0], s=60)
ax.set_xlabel('$x$')
ax.set_ylabel('$y$')
ax.set_zlabel('$z$')
ax.set_title('Level surface and tangent plane')
plt.show()

9.12 9.10 Total differentials

For a differentiable function \(z=f(x,y)\), define \(dx\) and \(dy\) as independent small changes in the input variables. The total differential is

\[ dz=f_x(x,y)\,dx+f_y(x,y)\,dy. \]

At a specific point \((a,b)\), this becomes

\[ dz=f_x(a,b)\,dx+f_y(a,b)\,dy. \]

The differential is the linear approximation to the actual change:

\[ \Delta z\approx dz. \]

For a function of three variables,

\[ w=f(x,y,z), \]

the total differential is

\[ dw=f_x\,dx+f_y\,dy+f_z\,dz. \]

More generally, for \(f:\mathbb R^n\to \mathbb R\),

\[ df=\nabla f(\mathbf{x})\cdot d\mathbf{x}. \]

NoteWhy differentials matter

Deltas such as \(\Delta x\) and \(\Delta y\) represent actual changes. Differentials such as \(dx\) and \(dy\) are independent input changes used in the linear model. The differential \(dz\) is the predicted output change from the tangent plane.

9.13 9.11 Error propagation and sensitivity analysis

In applied problems, inputs are often measured with error. Linear approximation allows us to estimate how input error affects output error.

Suppose

\[ z=f(x,y) \]

and the possible measurement errors are

\[ |dx|\leq \epsilon_x, \qquad |dy|\leq \epsilon_y. \]

Then a first-order estimate for the output error is

\[ |dz|\leq |f_x(x,y)|\epsilon_x+|f_y(x,y)|\epsilon_y. \]

This is a deterministic worst-case estimate.

If instead \(dx\) and \(dy\) are random errors with covariance matrix \(\Sigma\), then the approximate variance of the output is

\[ \operatorname{Var}(df)\approx \nabla f(\mathbf{x})^T\Sigma\nabla f(\mathbf{x}). \]

This is the core idea behind the delta method in statistics.

9.13.1 Example 9.4: volume of a cylinder

The volume of a cylinder is

\[ V(r,h)=\pi r^2h. \]

The differential is

\[ dV=2\pi rh\,dr+\pi r^2\,dh. \]

At \(r=3\) and \(h=10\),

\[ dV=60\pi\,dr+9\pi\,dh. \]

If \(dr=0.02\) and \(dh=-0.05\), then

\[ dV\approx 60\pi(0.02)+9\pi(-0.05)=0.75\pi. \]

So the predicted volume change is approximately

\[ 0.75\pi\approx 2.36. \]

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

r0, h0 = 3.0, 10.0
r = np.linspace(2.9, 3.1, 160)
h = np.linspace(9.8, 10.2, 160)
R, H = np.meshgrid(r, h)
V = np.pi*R**2*H
V0 = np.pi*r0**2*h0
L = V0 + 2*np.pi*r0*h0*(R - r0) + np.pi*r0**2*(H - h0)
E = V - L

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
cs = ax.contourf(R, H, E, levels=20)
fig.colorbar(cs, ax=ax, label='exact minus linear')
ax.scatter([r0], [h0], s=60)
ax.set_xlabel('radius $r$')
ax.set_ylabel('height $h$')
ax.set_title('Linear approximation error for cylinder volume')
plt.show()

9.14 9.12 Linearization as a local model in statistics and machine learning

Many statistical and machine-learning models depend on parameters. A simple example is a two-parameter linear prediction model

\[ \hat y=\beta_0+\beta_1x. \]

A more complicated model may have many parameters:

\[ \hat y=f(x;\theta_1,\theta_2,\ldots,\theta_n). \]

Near a parameter vector \(\boldsymbol\theta_0\), linear approximation gives

\[ f(x;\boldsymbol\theta)\approx f(x;\boldsymbol\theta_0)+\nabla_{\boldsymbol\theta}f(x;\boldsymbol\theta_0)\cdot (\boldsymbol\theta-\boldsymbol\theta_0). \]

This is the local linear model used in:

  • sensitivity analysis of model outputs;
  • uncertainty propagation for estimated parameters;
  • first-order optimization methods;
  • local approximation of nonlinear regression models;
  • gradient-based learning in neural networks.

9.14.1 Local linearization of a sigmoid

The logistic sigmoid is

\[ \sigma(t)=\frac{1}{1+e^{-t}}. \]

If

\[ t=\beta_0+\beta_1x, \]

then the model output is

\[ p(x;\beta_0,\beta_1)=\sigma(\beta_0+\beta_1x). \]

For fixed \(x\), this is a function of the parameters \(\beta_0\) and \(\beta_1\). Its gradient tells how the predicted probability changes when the parameters change.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x0 = 1.5
b0_star, b1_star = -0.4, 1.2

def sigmoid(t):
    return 1/(1 + np.exp(-t))

b0 = np.linspace(-1.2, 0.4, 140)
b1 = np.linspace(0.4, 2.0, 140)
B0, B1 = np.meshgrid(b0, b1)

9.15 9.13 Numerical linearization from finite differences

In real applications, we may not have a symbolic formula for the gradient. We may only have a black-box function that can be evaluated. Then we can approximate the gradient numerically.

For a function \(f(x,y)\), central finite differences give

\[ f_x(a,b)\approx \frac{f(a+h,b)-f(a-h,b)}{2h}, \]

\[ f_y(a,b)\approx \frac{f(a,b+h)-f(a,b-h)}{2h}. \]

Then the numerical linearization is

\[ L(x,y)=f(a,b)+\widehat f_x(a,b)(x-a)+\widehat f_y(a,b)(y-b). \]

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt


def f(x, y):
    return np.sin(x*y) + 0.2*x**2 - 0.1*y**3


a, b = 1.0, 0.8
h = 1e-4
fx_hat = (f(a + h, b) - f(a - h, b))/(2*h)
fy_hat = (f(a, b + h) - f(a, b - h))/(2*h)

x = np.linspace(0.5, 1.5, 120)
y = np.linspace(0.3, 1.3, 120)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)
Z = f(X, Y)
L = f(a, b) + fx_hat*(X - a) + fy_hat*(Y - b)

fig = plt.figure()
ax = fig.add_subplot(111, projection='3d')
ax.plot_surface(X, Y, Z, alpha=0.65, linewidth=0)
ax.plot_surface(X, Y, L, alpha=0.35, linewidth=0)
ax.scatter([a], [b], [f(a,b)], s=60)
ax.set_xlabel('$x$')
ax.set_ylabel('$y$')
ax.set_zlabel('$z$')
ax.set_title('Numerical tangent plane')
plt.show()

print('finite-difference gradient:', (fx_hat, fy_hat))
finite-difference gradient: (0.9573653668826232, 0.5047067071861644)

9.16 9.14 Vector-valued and matrix viewpoints

The scalar-valued formula

\[ f(\mathbf{x})\approx f(\mathbf{a})+\nabla f(\mathbf{a})\cdot(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{a}) \]

has a natural extension to vector-valued functions. If

\[ \mathbf{F}:\mathbb R^n\to \mathbb R^m, \]

then the best first-order approximation is

\[ \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x})\approx \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{a})+J_{\mathbf{F}}(\mathbf{a})(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{a}), \]

where \(J_{\mathbf{F}}(\mathbf{a})\) is the Jacobian matrix. This is the subject of the next chapter.

For now, the key idea is that derivatives in many variables are linear maps. The gradient-dot-product formula is the scalar-output version of this principle.

9.17 9.15 Common mistakes

WarningMistake 1: using the wrong base point

The tangent plane formula must use the point of tangency \((a,b)\) and the height \(f(a,b)\). Do not plug in the nearby point where you want the estimate.

WarningMistake 2: mixing up graph surfaces and level surfaces

For a graph \(z=f(x,y)\), use \[ z-f(a,b)=f_x(a,b)(x-a)+f_y(a,b)(y-b). \] For a level surface \(F(x,y,z)=c\), use \[ \nabla F(P)\cdot \langle x-a,y-b,z-c_0\rangle=0. \]

WarningMistake 3: thinking partial derivatives guarantee differentiability

Partial derivatives measure coordinate-direction slopes. Differentiability means there is a good linear approximation in all directions at once.

For \(z=f(x,y)\):

Tangent plane to \(z=f(x,y)\):

## Chapter overview

The central message of this chapter is simple and powerful:

::: {.callout-important title='Core principle'}
A differentiable multivariable function is approximately linear near a point.
:::

In single-variable calculus, the tangent line is the best first-order approximation to a curve near a point. In multivariable calculus, the same idea becomes the **tangent plane** for a graph $z=f(x,y)$ and the **tangent hyperplane** for a function of many variables. Algebraically, the approximation is built from partial derivatives. Geometrically, it is the flat object that best matches the surface near the point. Computationally, it is the first local model used in numerical methods, optimization, uncertainty propagation, and statistical approximation.

This chapter is one of the main bridges from Calculus III to graduate-level applied mathematics, statistics, data science, AI, and machine learning. Linear approximation is the mathematical idea behind sensitivity analysis, local regression, the delta method, Newton-type algorithms, first-order optimization, and the local behavior of loss functions.

## 9.1 Review: tangent lines and local linearity

For a single-variable function $y=f(x)$, the tangent line at $x=a$ is

$$
L(x)=f(a)+f'(a)(x-a).
$$

The tangent line is useful because when $x$ is close to $a$,

$$
f(x) \approx L(x).
$$

The number $f'(a)$ tells how the output changes per unit change in the input. If $\Delta x=x-a$, then

$$
\Delta y=f(x)-f(a) \approx f'(a)\Delta x.
$$

This is the one-variable version of the multivariable formula

$$
\Delta z \approx f_x(a,b)\Delta x+f_y(a,b)\Delta y.
$$

The right side is linear in the input changes $\Delta x$ and $\Delta y$. That is why we call it a **linear approximation**.

::: {.callout-tip title='Local does not mean global'}
A tangent line or tangent plane usually gives a good approximation only near the point of tangency. Far away from the point, the nonlinear curvature of the graph becomes important.
:::

## 9.2 Tangent planes to graphs $z=f(x,y)$

Let $z=f(x,y)$ be differentiable near $(a,b)$, and let

$$
P=(a,b,f(a,b))
$$

be the point on the graph. The tangent plane at $P$ is

$$
z-f(a,b)=f_x(a,b)(x-a)+f_y(a,b)(y-b).
$$

Equivalently,

$$
z=f(a,b)+f_x(a,b)(x-a)+f_y(a,b)(y-b).
$$

The function on the right is the **linearization** of $f$ at $(a,b)$:

$$
L(x,y)=f(a,b)+f_x(a,b)(x-a)+f_y(a,b)(y-b).
$$

The approximation

$$
f(x,y)\approx L(x,y)
$$

is called the **linear approximation** or **tangent plane approximation**.

### Why this is the right plane

At the point $(a,b)$, the tangent plane must match three pieces of first-order information:

1. the height $f(a,b)$;
2. the slope in the $x$-direction, $f_x(a,b)$;
3. the slope in the $y$-direction, $f_y(a,b)$.

The plane

$$
z=L(x,y)
$$

has exactly these three properties.

A useful normal vector to the tangent plane is

$$
\langle f_x(a,b), f_y(a,b), -1\rangle.
$$

This comes from rewriting the tangent plane as

$$
f_x(a,b)(x-a)+f_y(a,b)(y-b)-(z-f(a,b))=0.
$$

## 9.3 Worked example: tangent plane to an elliptic paraboloid

### Example 9.1

Find the tangent plane to

$$
z=f(x,y)=3x^2+y^2
$$

at the point $(1,2,7)$.

### Solution

First check the height:

$$
f(1,2)=3(1)^2+(2)^2=7.
$$

Compute the partial derivatives:

$$
f_x(x,y)=6x,
\qquad
f_y(x,y)=2y.
$$

At $(1,2)$,

$$
f_x(1,2)=6,
\qquad
f_y(1,2)=4.
$$

Therefore the tangent plane is

$$
z-7=6(x-1)+4(y-2).
$$

Solving for $z$ gives

$$
z=6x+4y-7.
$$

Thus the linearization is

$$
L(x,y)=6x+4y-7.
$$

To estimate $f(1.1,1.9)$, compute

$$
L(1.1,1.9)=6(1.1)+4(1.9)-7=7.2.
$$

The exact value is

$$
f(1.1,1.9)=3(1.1)^2+(1.9)^2=7.24.
$$

The tangent plane gives a close approximation because $(1.1,1.9)$ is near $(1,2)$.

## 9.4 Linear approximation in gradient form

The linear approximation formula can be written more compactly using the gradient. Let

$$
\Delta x=x-a,
\qquad
\Delta y=y-b,
\qquad
\Delta \mathbf{x}=\langle \Delta x,\Delta y\rangle.
$$

Then

$$
f(x,y)-f(a,b)\approx \nabla f(a,b)\cdot \Delta \mathbf{x}.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
\Delta z\approx \nabla f(a,b)\cdot \Delta \mathbf{x}.
$$

This formula is one of the most important formulas in multivariable calculus. It says:

> Near a point, the change in the output is approximately the dot product of the gradient and the input displacement.

For a function $f:\mathbb R^n\to \mathbb R$, the same formula is

$$
f(\mathbf{x})\approx f(\mathbf{a})+\nabla f(\mathbf{a})\cdot (\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{a}).
$$

### Example 9.2

Suppose

$$
f(2,1)=13,
\qquad
\nabla f(2,1)=\langle 12,2\rangle.
$$

Estimate $f(2.1,0.9)$.

### Solution

The input displacement is

$$
\Delta \mathbf{x}=\langle 2.1-2,0.9-1\rangle
=\langle 0.1,-0.1\rangle.
$$

Therefore

$$
\Delta z\approx \langle 12,2\rangle\cdot \langle 0.1,-0.1\rangle
=12(0.1)+2(-0.1)=1.
$$

So

$$
f(2.1,0.9)\approx 13+1=14.
$$

## 9.5 Approximation error and curvature

The linearization keeps only first-order information. The error comes from second-order and higher-order behavior. For the paraboloid

$$
f(x,y)=3x^2+y^2,
$$

the tangent plane at $(1,2)$ is

$$
L(x,y)=6x+4y-7.
$$

The error is

$$
E(x,y)=f(x,y)-L(x,y).
$$

For this example,

$$
E(x,y)=3(x-1)^2+(y-2)^2.
$$

This is always nonnegative because the surface is convex and lies above its tangent plane. The error is small near $(1,2)$ and grows quadratically as we move away.

## 9.6 Differentiability

A function $f(x,y)$ is **differentiable** at $(a,b)$ if its change can be written as a linear part plus an error that becomes negligible compared with the size of the input change.

One way to state this is:

$$
\Delta z=f_x(a,b)\Delta x+f_y(a,b)\Delta y+\varepsilon_1\Delta x+\varepsilon_2\Delta y,
$$

where

$$
\varepsilon_1\to 0,
\qquad
\varepsilon_2\to 0
$$

as $(\Delta x,\Delta y)\to (0,0)$.

Equivalently,

$$
f(a+\Delta x,b+\Delta y)=f(a,b)+f_x(a,b)\Delta x+f_y(a,b)\Delta y+\text{small error}.
$$

The important idea is not just that the partial derivatives exist. The important idea is that together they produce a good linear model near the point.

::: {.callout-warning title='Partial derivatives alone are not enough'}
A function may have partial derivatives at a point but fail to be differentiable there. Differentiability is stronger than the existence of coordinate-direction slopes.
:::

A practical sufficient condition is the following.

::: {.callout-tip title='Sufficient condition for differentiability'}
If $f_x$ and $f_y$ exist near $(a,b)$ and are continuous at $(a,b)$, then $f$ is differentiable at $(a,b)$.
:::

This theorem is one reason continuous partial derivatives are so important. In most computational and applied examples, we verify differentiability by verifying that the partial derivatives are continuous.

## 9.7 A cautionary example

Consider

$$
f(x,y)=
\begin{cases}
\dfrac{xy}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}, & (x,y)\neq (0,0),\\
0, & (x,y)=(0,0).
\end{cases}
$$

At the origin, the coordinate partial derivatives exist:

$$
f_x(0,0)=0,
\qquad
f_y(0,0)=0.
$$

So the candidate linearization is $L(x,y)=0$. However, along the line $y=x$,

$$
f(x,x)=\frac{x^2}{\sqrt{2x^2}}=\frac{|x|}{\sqrt 2}.
$$

This is not negligible compared with the distance $\sqrt{x^2+x^2}=\sqrt 2|x|$. Therefore the function is not differentiable at the origin.


## 9.8 Tangent planes to level surfaces

Many important surfaces are not given as graphs $z=f(x,y)$. Instead, they are given implicitly as level surfaces

$$
F(x,y,z)=c.
$$

Suppose

$$
P=(a,b,c_0)
$$

is a point on the level surface. If

$$
\nabla F(P)\neq \mathbf{0},
$$

then the gradient vector is normal to the level surface at $P$. Therefore the tangent plane is

$$
\nabla F(P)\cdot \langle x-a,y-b,z-c_0\rangle=0.
$$

In coordinates, if

$$
\nabla F(P)=\langle F_x(P),F_y(P),F_z(P)\rangle,
$$

then the tangent plane is

$$
F_x(P)(x-a)+F_y(P)(y-b)+F_z(P)(z-c_0)=0.
$$

### Why the gradient is normal

If a curve $\mathbf r(t)$ lies on the level surface, then

$$
F(\mathbf r(t))=c.
$$

Differentiating with respect to $t$ gives

$$
\nabla F(\mathbf r(t))\cdot \mathbf r'(t)=0.
$$

Thus the gradient is perpendicular to every tangent direction $\mathbf r'(t)$ on the surface.

## 9.9 Worked example: linearization and tangent plane to a level surface

### Example 9.3

$$

1. Find the linearization of $F$ at $P$.
2. Estimate $F(1.1,1.9,0)$ using the linearization.
3. Find the tangent plane to the level surface through $P$.

### Solution

First evaluate the function:

$$
F(1,2,0)=1^3+2^2e^0+1\cdot 2\cdot 0=5.
$$

Compute the partial derivatives:

$$
F_x=3x^2+yz,
$$

$$
F_y=2ye^z+xz,
$$

$$
F_z=y^2e^z+xy.
$$

At $P=(1,2,0)$,

$$
F_x(P)=3,
\qquad
F_y(P)=4,
\qquad
F_z(P)=6.
$$

So the linearization is

$$
L(x,y,z)=F(P)+F_x(P)(x-1)+F_y(P)(y-2)+F_z(P)(z-0).
$$

Therefore

$$
L(x,y,z)=5+3(x-1)+4(y-2)+6z.
$$

After simplifying,

$$
L(x,y,z)=3x+4y+6z-6.
$$

Now estimate:

$$
F(1.1,1.9,0)\approx L(1.1,1.9,0)=3(1.1)+4(1.9)-6=4.9.
$$

The level surface through $P$ is

$$
F(x,y,z)=5.
$$

Its tangent plane is

$$
3(x-1)+4(y-2)+6z=0.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
3x+4y+6z-11=0.
$$

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch09-level-surface-tangent-plane
#| fig-cap: 'A tangent plane to the level surface F(x,y,z)=5 near P=(1,2,0).'
#| fig-width: 7
#| fig-height: 5
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Solve the tangent plane for z: 3(x-1)+4(y-2)+6z=0
x = np.linspace(0.6, 1.4, 80)
y = np.linspace(1.5, 2.5, 80)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)
Zplane = -(3*(X - 1) + 4*(Y - 2))/6

# Approximate the level surface locally by solving F(x,y,z)=5 with Newton iterations in z.
def F(x, y, z):
    return x**3 + y**2*np.exp(z) + x*y*z

Z = Zplane.copy()
for _ in range(6):
    dFdz = Y**2*np.exp(Z) + X*Y
    Z = Z - (F(X, Y, Z) - 5)/dFdz

fig = plt.figure()
ax = fig.add_subplot(111, projection='3d')
ax.plot_surface(X, Y, Z, alpha=0.65, linewidth=0)
ax.plot_surface(X, Y, Zplane, alpha=0.35, linewidth=0)
ax.scatter([1], [2], [0], s=60)
ax.set_xlabel('$x$')
ax.set_ylabel('$y$')
ax.set_zlabel('$z$')
ax.set_title('Level surface and tangent plane')
plt.show()
```

## 9.10 Total differentials

For a differentiable function $z=f(x,y)$, define $dx$ and $dy$ as independent small changes in the input variables. The **total differential** is

$$
dz=f_x(x,y)\,dx+f_y(x,y)\,dy.
$$

At a specific point $(a,b)$, this becomes

$$
dz=f_x(a,b)\,dx+f_y(a,b)\,dy.
$$

The differential is the linear approximation to the actual change:

$$
\Delta z\approx dz.
$$

For a function of three variables,

$$
w=f(x,y,z),
$$

the total differential is

$$
dw=f_x\,dx+f_y\,dy+f_z\,dz.
$$

More generally, for $f:\mathbb R^n\to \mathbb R$,

$$
df=\nabla f(\mathbf{x})\cdot d\mathbf{x}.
$$

::: {.callout-note title='Why differentials matter'}
Deltas such as $\Delta x$ and $\Delta y$ represent actual changes. Differentials such as $dx$ and $dy$ are independent input changes used in the linear model. The differential $dz$ is the predicted output change from the tangent plane.
:::

## 9.11 Error propagation and sensitivity analysis

In applied problems, inputs are often measured with error. Linear approximation allows us to estimate how input error affects output error.

Suppose

$$
z=f(x,y)
$$

and the possible measurement errors are

$$
|dx|\leq \epsilon_x,
\qquad
|dy|\leq \epsilon_y.
$$

Then a first-order estimate for the output error is

$$
|dz|\leq |f_x(x,y)|\epsilon_x+|f_y(x,y)|\epsilon_y.
$$

This is a deterministic worst-case estimate.

If instead $dx$ and $dy$ are random errors with covariance matrix $\Sigma$, then the approximate variance of the output is

$$
\operatorname{Var}(df)\approx \nabla f(\mathbf{x})^T\Sigma\nabla f(\mathbf{x}).
$$

This is the core idea behind the **delta method** in statistics.

### Example 9.4: volume of a cylinder

The volume of a cylinder is

$$
V(r,h)=\pi r^2h.
$$

The differential is

$$
dV=2\pi rh\,dr+\pi r^2\,dh.
$$

At $r=3$ and $h=10$,

$$
dV=60\pi\,dr+9\pi\,dh.
$$

If $dr=0.02$ and $dh=-0.05$, then

$$
dV\approx 60\pi(0.02)+9\pi(-0.05)=0.75\pi.
$$

So the predicted volume change is approximately

$$
0.75\pi\approx 2.36.
$$

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch09-error-propagation-cylinder
#| fig-cap: 'Linear error propagation predicts how measurement errors in radius and height affect cylinder volume.'
#| fig-width: 7
#| fig-height: 5
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

r0, h0 = 3.0, 10.0
r = np.linspace(2.9, 3.1, 160)
h = np.linspace(9.8, 10.2, 160)
R, H = np.meshgrid(r, h)
V = np.pi*R**2*H
V0 = np.pi*r0**2*h0
L = V0 + 2*np.pi*r0*h0*(R - r0) + np.pi*r0**2*(H - h0)
E = V - L

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
cs = ax.contourf(R, H, E, levels=20)
fig.colorbar(cs, ax=ax, label='exact minus linear')
ax.scatter([r0], [h0], s=60)
ax.set_xlabel('radius $r$')
ax.set_ylabel('height $h$')
ax.set_title('Linear approximation error for cylinder volume')
plt.show()
```

## 9.12 Linearization as a local model in statistics and machine learning

Many statistical and machine-learning models depend on parameters. A simple example is a two-parameter linear prediction model

$$
\hat y=\beta_0+\beta_1x.
$$

A more complicated model may have many parameters:

$$
\hat y=f(x;\theta_1,\theta_2,\ldots,\theta_n).
$$

Near a parameter vector $\boldsymbol\theta_0$, linear approximation gives

$$
f(x;\boldsymbol\theta)\approx f(x;\boldsymbol\theta_0)+\nabla_{\boldsymbol\theta}f(x;\boldsymbol\theta_0)\cdot (\boldsymbol\theta-\boldsymbol\theta_0).
$$

This is the local linear model used in:

- sensitivity analysis of model outputs;
- uncertainty propagation for estimated parameters;
- first-order optimization methods;
- local approximation of nonlinear regression models;
- gradient-based learning in neural networks.

### Local linearization of a sigmoid

The logistic sigmoid is

$$
\sigma(t)=\frac{1}{1+e^{-t}}.
$$

If

$$
t=\beta_0+\beta_1x,
$$

then the model output is

$$
p(x;\beta_0,\beta_1)=\sigma(\beta_0+\beta_1x).
$$

For fixed $x$, this is a function of the parameters $\beta_0$ and $\beta_1$. Its gradient tells how the predicted probability changes when the parameters change.

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch09-logistic-local-linearization
#| fig-cap: 'A nonlinear probability model is locally approximated by its tangent plane in parameter space.'
#| fig-width: 7
#| fig-height: 5
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x0 = 1.5
b0_star, b1_star = -0.4, 1.2

def sigmoid(t):
    return 1/(1 + np.exp(-t))

b0 = np.linspace(-1.2, 0.4, 140)
b1 = np.linspace(0.4, 2.0, 140)
B0, B1 = np.meshgrid(b0, b1)
P = sigmoid(B0 + B1*x0)

p0 = sigmoid(b0_star + b1_star*x0)
deriv = p0*(1 - p0)
dp_db0 = deriv
dp_db1 = deriv*x0
L = p0 + dp_db0*(B0 - b0_star) + dp_db1*(B1 - b1_star)

fig = plt.figure()
ax = fig.add_subplot(111, projection='3d')
ax.plot_surface(B0, B1, P, alpha=0.65, linewidth=0)
ax.plot_surface(B0, B1, L, alpha=0.35, linewidth=0)
ax.scatter([b0_star], [b1_star], [p0], s=60)
ax.set_xlabel(r'$\beta_0$')
ax.set_ylabel(r'$\beta_1$')
ax.set_zlabel('probability')
ax.set_title('Local linearization of logistic probability')
plt.show()
```

## 9.13 Numerical linearization from finite differences

In real applications, we may not have a symbolic formula for the gradient. We may only have a black-box function that can be evaluated. Then we can approximate the gradient numerically.

For a function $f(x,y)$, central finite differences give

$$
f_x(a,b)\approx \frac{f(a+h,b)-f(a-h,b)}{2h},
$$

$$
f_y(a,b)\approx \frac{f(a,b+h)-f(a,b-h)}{2h}.
$$

Then the numerical linearization is

$$
L(x,y)=f(a,b)+\widehat f_x(a,b)(x-a)+\widehat f_y(a,b)(y-b).
$$

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch09-finite-difference-linearization
#| fig-cap: 'Finite differences can approximate the gradient used in a local linear model.'
#| fig-width: 7
#| fig-height: 5
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt


def f(x, y):
    return np.sin(x*y) + 0.2*x**2 - 0.1*y**3


a, b = 1.0, 0.8
h = 1e-4
fx_hat = (f(a + h, b) - f(a - h, b))/(2*h)
fy_hat = (f(a, b + h) - f(a, b - h))/(2*h)

x = np.linspace(0.5, 1.5, 120)
y = np.linspace(0.3, 1.3, 120)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)
Z = f(X, Y)
L = f(a, b) + fx_hat*(X - a) + fy_hat*(Y - b)

fig = plt.figure()
ax = fig.add_subplot(111, projection='3d')
ax.plot_surface(X, Y, Z, alpha=0.65, linewidth=0)
ax.plot_surface(X, Y, L, alpha=0.35, linewidth=0)
ax.scatter([a], [b], [f(a,b)], s=60)
ax.set_xlabel('$x$')
ax.set_ylabel('$y$')
ax.set_zlabel('$z$')
ax.set_title('Numerical tangent plane')
plt.show()

print('finite-difference gradient:', (fx_hat, fy_hat))
```

## 9.14 Vector-valued and matrix viewpoints

The scalar-valued formula

$$
f(\mathbf{x})\approx f(\mathbf{a})+\nabla f(\mathbf{a})\cdot(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{a})
$$

has a natural extension to vector-valued functions. If

$$
\mathbf{F}:\mathbb R^n\to \mathbb R^m,
$$

then the best first-order approximation is

$$
\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x})\approx \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{a})+J_{\mathbf{F}}(\mathbf{a})(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{a}),
$$

where $J_{\mathbf{F}}(\mathbf{a})$ is the **Jacobian matrix**. This is the subject of the next chapter.

For now, the key idea is that derivatives in many variables are linear maps. The gradient-dot-product formula is the scalar-output version of this principle.

## 9.15 Common mistakes

::: {.callout-warning title='Mistake 1: using the wrong base point'}
The tangent plane formula must use the point of tangency $(a,b)$ and the height $f(a,b)$. Do not plug in the nearby point where you want the estimate.
:::

::: {.callout-warning title='Mistake 2: mixing up graph surfaces and level surfaces'}
For a graph $z=f(x,y)$, use
$$
z-f(a,b)=f_x(a,b)(x-a)+f_y(a,b)(y-b).
$$
For a level surface $F(x,y,z)=c$, use
$$
\nabla F(P)\cdot \langle x-a,y-b,z-c_0\rangle=0.
$$
:::

::: {.callout-warning title='Mistake 3: thinking partial derivatives guarantee differentiability'}
Partial derivatives measure coordinate-direction slopes. Differentiability means there is a good linear approximation in all directions at once.
:::

## 9.16 Summary of key formulas

For $z=f(x,y)$:

$$
L(x,y)=f(a,b)+f_x(a,b)(x-a)+f_y(a,b)(y-b).
$$

Tangent plane to $z=f(x,y)$:

$$
z-f(a,b)=f_x(a,b)(x-a)+f_y(a,b)(y-b).
$$

Gradient form:

$$
f(x,y)-f(a,b)\approx \nabla f(a,b)\cdot \langle x-a,y-b\rangle.
$$

Total differential:

$$
dz=f_x\,dx+f_y\,dy.
$$

For $f:\mathbb R^n\to \mathbb R$:

$$
f(\mathbf{x})\approx f(\mathbf{a})+\nabla f(\mathbf{a})\cdot(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{a}).
$$

Tangent plane to $F(x,y,z)=c$ at $P=(a,b,c_0)$:

$$
F_x(P)(x-a)+F_y(P)(y-b)+F_z(P)(z-c_0)=0.
$$

## Chapter summary

Linear approximation is the first major synthesis point of multivariable calculus. Partial derivatives become a gradient. The gradient becomes a local linear model. The local linear model becomes a tangent plane, a differential, a sensitivity estimate, and a computational approximation.

For graphs $z=f(x,y)$, the tangent plane is

$$
z=f(a,b)+f_x(a,b)(x-a)+f_y(a,b)(y-b).
$$

For level surfaces $F(x,y,z)=c$, the tangent plane is determined by the normal vector $\nabla F(P)$:

$$
\nabla F(P)\cdot(\mathbf{x}-P)=0.
$$

In applied mathematics and statistics, this chapter is the foundation for error propagation, the delta method, local optimization, and first-order approximation of nonlinear models. In machine learning and AI, it is the idea behind using gradients to approximate and improve complicated models one small step at a time.
