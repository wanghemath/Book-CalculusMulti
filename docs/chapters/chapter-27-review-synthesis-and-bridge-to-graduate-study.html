27  Review, Synthesis, and Bridge to Graduate Study

Multivariable calculus is not a list of formulas. It is a language for describing how quantities change, accumulate, interact, flow, and optimize in many dimensions. The goal of this final chapter is to organize the whole book into a small number of durable ideas that students can carry into applied mathematics, statistics, data science, machine learning, physics, engineering, and quantitative modeling.

The chapters of the book have developed four connected viewpoints:

  1. Geometry: points, vectors, curves, surfaces, regions, and fields.
  2. Local change: derivatives, gradients, Jacobians, Hessians, and approximations.
  3. Accumulation: double integrals, triple integrals, surface integrals, line integrals, and change of variables.
  4. Global structure: conservative fields, Green’s theorem, Stokes theorem, the Divergence theorem, and conservation laws.

Modern computational work uses all four viewpoints at once. A statistical model has parameters in a high-dimensional space. A machine learning algorithm follows gradients of a loss function. A probability distribution is a density whose mass is computed by integration. A physical system is described by fields, fluxes, and conservation laws. A numerical algorithm replaces an exact formula by an approximation whose geometry must still be understood.

This chapter is a synthesis chapter. It reviews the main objects, explains how to choose the correct tool, and gives a computational capstone: a small calculus toolkit that approximates gradients, Jacobians, Hessians, multiple integrals, and vector-field quantities.

27.1 Learning goals

After studying this chapter, you should be able to:

  • organize multivariable calculus by object type: scalar functions, vector functions, regions, curves, surfaces, and vector fields;
  • decide whether a problem is asking for local change, accumulation, optimization, or global flow;
  • use gradients, Jacobians, and Hessians as the main derivative objects in modern computation;
  • connect double and triple integrals to mass, probability, expectation, and volume;
  • connect line and surface integrals to work, circulation, flux, and conservation;
  • explain how the major integral theorems generalize the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus;
  • implement a small numerical toolkit for gradients, Jacobians, Hessians, and integrals;
  • prepare for graduate courses in applied mathematics, statistics, data science, AI, and machine learning.
TipThe central habit

Before computing, identify the object. Is it a scalar field, vector field, curve, surface, region, density, loss function, or constraint? The correct formula usually becomes clear once the mathematical object is clear.

27.2 27.1 The big map of the subject

A compact way to remember the book is by the following table.

Object Typical notation Question Main tools
Point or vector \(x \in \mathbb R^n\) Where are we? What direction? norm, dot product, projection
Scalar field \(f: \mathbb R^n \to \mathbb R\) How does a quantity change? gradient, Hessian, Taylor expansion
Vector function \(r(t)\) How does a curve move? velocity, speed, acceleration, arc length
Region \(R \subset \mathbb R^2\), \(E \subset \mathbb R^3\) How much accumulates? double and triple integrals
Coordinate map \(T(u,v)\) or \(T(u,v,w)\) How does area or volume transform? Jacobian determinant
Vector field \(F: \mathbb R^n \to \mathbb R^n\) How does a field push or flow? line integrals, flux, curl, divergence
Constraint \(g(x)=c\) What is optimal under restriction? Lagrange multipliers
Model or loss \(L(\theta)\) How do we fit or optimize? gradients, Hessians, descent methods

The most important distinction is between local and global information. A derivative tells us what happens near a point. An integral tells us what accumulates over an interval, region, curve, or surface. A theorem such as Green’s theorem or the Divergence theorem connects local information to global information.

27.3 27.2 Geometry: the coordinate language

The first part of the book developed the coordinate language of space. The essential objects are points, vectors, distances, angles, projections, lines, planes, and surfaces.

For \(x=(x_1,\ldots,x_n)\) and \(y=(y_1,\ldots,y_n)\),

\[ \|x-y\| = \sqrt{(x_1-y_1)^2+\cdots+(x_n-y_n)^2}. \]

The dot product is

\[ x\cdot y = x_1y_1+\cdots+x_ny_n, \]

and it measures alignment:

\[ x\cdot y = \|x\|\,\|y\|\cos\theta. \]

Projection is the geometry behind least squares and many data methods. The projection of \(x\) onto the direction of a nonzero vector \(v\) is

\[ \operatorname{proj}_v x = \frac{x\cdot v}{v\cdot v}v. \]

A hyperplane in \(\mathbb R^n\) has equation

\[ w\cdot x = b. \]

This simple equation appears in tangent planes, constraints, regression, support vector machines, and linear classifiers.

27.3.1 Example 27.1: Projection as a least-squares approximation

Let \(x=(3,4)\) and \(v=(1,2)\). The best approximation to \(x\) among all multiples of \(v\) is the projection of \(x\) onto \(v\):

\[ \operatorname{proj}_v x = \frac{x\cdot v}{v\cdot v}v = \frac{3+8}{1+4}(1,2) = \frac{11}{5}(1,2). \]

The residual

\[ r = x - \operatorname{proj}_v x \]

is orthogonal to \(v\). This is the same geometry that appears when fitting a linear model by least squares: the residual is orthogonal to the space of fitted values.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x = np.array([3.0, 4.0])
v = np.array([1.0, 2.0])
proj = (x @ v) / (v @ v) * v
resid = x - proj

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(5.5, 5.5))
ax.quiver(0, 0, x[0], x[1], angles='xy', scale_units='xy', scale=1, width=0.01)
ax.quiver(0, 0, proj[0], proj[1], angles='xy', scale_units='xy', scale=1, width=0.01)
ax.quiver(proj[0], proj[1], resid[0], resid[1], angles='xy', scale_units='xy', scale=1, width=0.01)
ax.text(x[0] + 0.1, x[1], '$x$')
ax.text(proj[0] + 0.1, proj[1] - 0.2, r'$\operatorname{proj}_v x$')
ax.text(proj[0] + resid[0]/2 + 0.1, proj[1] + resid[1]/2, 'residual')
ax.set_xlim(-0.5, 4.0)

ax.set_aspect('equal')

27.4 27.3 Derivatives: local change and local models

For a scalar function \(f:\mathbb R^n\to \mathbb R\), the first derivative is the gradient

\[ \nabla f(x) = \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1},\ldots,\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}\right). \]

The gradient does three things at once:

  • it gives the best linear approximation;
  • it gives directional derivatives by dot product;
  • it points in the direction of fastest increase.

The first-order Taylor approximation near \(a\) is

\[ f(a+h) \approx f(a)+\nabla f(a)\cdot h. \]

The directional derivative in a unit direction \(u\) is

\[ D_u f(a)=\nabla f(a)\cdot u. \]

The second derivative is organized by the Hessian matrix

\[ H_f(a)= \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1^2} & \cdots & \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1\partial x_n} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_n\partial x_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_n^2} \end{bmatrix}. \]

The second-order Taylor approximation is

\[ f(a+h) \approx f(a)+\nabla f(a)\cdot h+\frac12 h^T H_f(a) h. \]

This formula is the local geometry behind optimization, uncertainty approximation, and many algorithms in statistics and machine learning.

27.4.1 Example 27.2: Local quadratic geometry

Let

\[ f(x,y)=x^2+4y^2. \]

At the origin, \(\nabla f(0,0)=0\) and

\[ H_f(0,0)= \begin{bmatrix} 2&0\\ 0&8 \end{bmatrix}. \]

The eigenvalues are positive, so the origin is a local minimum. The curvature is larger in the \(y\)-direction than in the \(x\)-direction. Gradient descent therefore moves differently depending on the direction and step size.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x = np.linspace(-2, 2, 250)
y = np.linspace(-1.5, 1.5, 250)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)
Z = X**2 + 4*Y**2

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(6, 5))
cs = ax.contour(X, Y, Z, levels=18)
ax.clabel(cs, inline=True, fontsize=8)
ax.set_xlabel('$x$')
ax.set_ylabel('$y$')
ax.set_title('Contours of $f(x,y)=x^2+4y^2$')

ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)
plt.show()

27.5 27.4 Jacobians: derivatives of vector-valued maps

For a map \(F:\mathbb R^n\to \mathbb R^m\), the derivative is the Jacobian matrix

\[ J_F(x)= \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial F_1}{\partial x_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial F_1}{\partial x_n} \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\ \frac{\partial F_m}{\partial x_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial F_m}{\partial x_n} \end{bmatrix}. \]

Near a point \(a\),

\[ F(a+h) \approx F(a)+J_F(a)h. \]

The Jacobian is also the linear map used in the multivariable chain rule. If \(F=G\circ H\), then

\[ J_F(x)=J_G(H(x))J_H(x). \]

In computation, this is the matrix form of backpropagation. In integration, the determinant of the Jacobian measures how area or volume changes under a coordinate transformation.

27.5.1 Example 27.3: The polar-coordinate Jacobian

The polar-coordinate map is

\[ T(r,\theta)=(r\cos\theta,r\sin\theta). \]

Its Jacobian matrix is

\[ J_T(r,\theta)= \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -r\sin\theta\\ \sin\theta & r\cos\theta \end{bmatrix}, \]

so

\[ |\det J_T(r,\theta)|=r. \]

That is why \(dA=r\,dr\,d\theta\).

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(6, 6))
for r in np.linspace(0.4, 2.0, 5):
    theta = np.linspace(0, 2*np.pi, 400)
    ax.plot(r*np.cos(theta), r*np.sin(theta), lw=1)
for theta in np.linspace(0, 2*np.pi, 16, endpoint=False):
    r = np.linspace(0, 2.0, 200)
    ax.plot(r*np.cos(theta), r*np.sin(theta), lw=1)
ax.set_aspect('equal')
ax.set_xlabel('$x$')

ax.set_title('Polar grid in the plane')

plt.show()

27.6 27.5 Integrals: accumulation over geometric objects

A definite integral accumulates over an interval. Multivariable calculus extends this idea to regions, solids, curves, and surfaces.

Integral Object Common meaning
\(\int_a^b f(x)\,dx\) interval signed area, total change
\(\iint_R f(x,y)\,dA\) plane region volume, mass, probability
\(\iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV\) solid region mass, volume, expectation
\(\int_C f\,ds\) curve mass of a wire
\(\int_C F\cdot dr\) oriented curve work, circulation
\(\iint_S f\,dS\) surface mass of a shell
\(\iint_S F\cdot n\,dS\) oriented surface flux

In statistics, integrals compute probability, expectation, variance, and normalization constants. In applied mathematics, integrals compute mass, energy, flow, and conservation quantities. In machine learning, integrals appear in expected risk, Bayesian inference, generative models, and continuous approximations.

27.6.1 Example 27.4: Probability as a double integral

Suppose a joint density is

\[ f(x,y)=4xy, \qquad 0\le x\le 1,\quad 0\le y\le 1. \]

Then

\[ \iint_{[0,1]^2} 4xy\,dA = 1, \]

so it is a valid density. The probability of the event \(x+y\le 1\) is

\[ \int_0^1 \int_0^{1-x} 4xy\,dy\,dx = \int_0^1 2x(1-x)^2\,dx = \frac16. \]

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x = np.linspace(0, 1, 300)
y = np.linspace(0, 1, 300)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)
Z = 4*X*Y
mask = X + Y <= 1

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(6, 5))
cs = ax.contourf(X, Y, np.where(mask, Z, np.nan), levels=16)
ax.plot([0, 1], [1, 0], lw=2)
ax.set_xlabel('$x$')
ax.set_ylabel('$y$')
ax.set_title(r'Density over the event $x+y\leq 1$')
fig.colorbar(cs, ax=ax, label='density')
ax.set_aspect('equal')
plt.show()

27.7 27.6 Optimization: from critical points to algorithms

Optimization is one of the main bridges from calculus to data science and machine learning. The central objects are:

  • the gradient \(\nabla f\), which gives the local direction of steepest increase;
  • the Hessian \(H_f\), which describes curvature;
  • the constraint gradients \(\nabla g_i\), which describe normal directions to constraint surfaces.

For unconstrained optimization, critical points satisfy

\[ \nabla f(x)=0. \]

For a one-constraint problem, the Lagrange multiplier condition is

\[ \nabla f(x)=\lambda \nabla g(x), \qquad g(x)=c. \]

For a two-constraint problem,

\[ \nabla f(x)=\lambda \nabla g(x)+\mu \nabla h(x), \qquad g(x)=c, \qquad h(x)=d. \]

Gradient descent uses the update

\[ x_{k+1}=x_k-\alpha \nabla f(x_k). \]

Newton’s method uses curvature:

\[ x_{k+1}=x_k-H_f(x_k)^{-1}\nabla f(x_k). \]

The main graduate-level habit is to ask not only where the optimum is, but also whether the problem is well-conditioned, whether the Hessian is positive definite, and whether constraints or regularization change the geometry.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

A = np.array([[3.0, 0.8], [0.8, 1.0]])
b = np.array([-1.0, 0.5])

def f_xy(z):
    return 0.5*z @ A @ z + b @ z

def grad_xy(z):
    return A @ z + b

z = np.array([2.4, 1.8])
alpha = 0.22
path = [z.copy()]
for _ in range(18):
    z = z - alpha*grad_xy(z)
    path.append(z.copy())
path = np.array(path)

x = np.linspace(-2.5, 2.8, 240)
y = np.linspace(-2.2, 2.2, 240)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)
Z = np.zeros_like(X)
for i in range(X.shape[0]):
    for j in range(X.shape[1]):
        Z[i, j] = f_xy(np.array([X[i, j], Y[i, j]]))

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(6, 5))
ax.contour(X, Y, Z, levels=24)
ax.plot(path[:, 0], path[:, 1], marker='o')
ax.set_xlabel('$x_1$')
ax.set_ylabel('$x_2$')
ax.set_title('Gradient descent path')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)
plt.show()

27.8 27.7 Vector fields and the global theorems

Vector calculus studies fields that assign a vector to each point. The main differential operators are:

\[ \operatorname{div}F = \nabla\cdot F, \qquad \operatorname{curl}F = \nabla\times F. \]

The divergence measures local source or sink behavior. The curl measures local rotation. The global theorems convert integrals over boundaries into integrals over interiors.

27.8.1 The four Fundamental-Theorem patterns

Theorem Boundary integral Interior integral Meaning
Fundamental Theorem of Calculus \(f(b)-f(a)\) \(\int_a^b f'(x)\,dx\) endpoint change equals accumulated derivative
Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals \(f(B)-f(A)\) \(\int_C \nabla f\cdot dr\) potential difference equals work
Green’s theorem \(\oint_C P\,dx+Q\,dy\) \(\iint_R (Q_x-P_y)\,dA\) boundary circulation equals interior curl
Stokes theorem \(\oint_{\partial S} F\cdot dr\) \(\iint_S (\nabla\times F)\cdot n\,dS\) boundary circulation equals surface curl flux
Divergence theorem \(\iint_{\partial E} F\cdot n\,dS\) \(\iiint_E \nabla\cdot F\,dV\) boundary flux equals interior source

The pattern is powerful: a global boundary measurement is controlled by a local derivative inside.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x = np.linspace(-2, 2, 17)

U = -Y
V = X

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(6, 6))

ax.set_aspect('equal')

27.9 27.8 A problem-solving decision tree

A good problem-solving strategy is often more useful than a memorized formula. Use the following decision tree.

Step 1: Identify the object.

  • Scalar function \(f(x,y)\) or \(f(x,y,z)\)?
  • Vector-valued function \(r(t)\)?
  • Region \(R\) or solid \(E\)?
  • Curve \(C\) or surface \(S\)?
  • Vector field \(F\)?
  • Constraint \(g(x)=c\)?
  • Density or loss function?

Step 2: Identify the action.

  • Find a rate of change: use derivative, gradient, Jacobian, or Hessian.
  • Approximate locally: use linearization or Taylor expansion.
  • Find maximum or minimum: use critical points, Hessian test, boundary check, or Lagrange multipliers.
  • Accumulate over a region: use double or triple integrals.
  • Change coordinates: compute a Jacobian determinant.
  • Accumulate along a curve: use line integrals.
  • Compute flux through a surface: use surface integrals or the Divergence theorem.
  • Compute circulation: use line integrals, Green’s theorem, or Stokes theorem.

Step 3: Interpret the result.

A numerical answer is rarely the final mathematical answer. Ask what the number means: volume, mass, probability, expected value, rate of increase, work, flux, circulation, curvature, uncertainty, or model error.

27.11 27.10 Computational capstone: a mini calculus toolkit

This section builds a small numerical toolkit. The goal is not to replace exact mathematics, but to make the formulas executable. A graduate student should be able to move between exact formulas, numerical approximations, and visual checks.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt


def finite_gradient(f, x, h=1e-6):
    x = np.asarray(x, dtype=float)
    g = np.zeros_like(x)
    for j in range(len(x)):
        e = np.zeros_like(x)
        e[j] = 1.0
        g[j] = (f(x + h*e) - f(x - h*e))/(2*h)
    return g


def finite_hessian(f, x, h=1e-4):
    x = np.asarray(x, dtype=float)
    n = len(x)
    H = np.zeros((n, n))
    for i in range(n):
        for j in range(n):
            ei = np.zeros(n)
            ej = np.zeros(n)
            ei[i] = 1.0
            ej[j] = 1.0
            H[i, j] = (
                f(x + h*ei + h*ej)
                - f(x + h*ei - h*ej)
                - f(x - h*ei + h*ej)
                + f(x - h*ei - h*ej)
            )/(4*h*h)
    return H

f = lambda z: z[0]**2 + z[0]*z[1] + 3*z[1]**2
point = np.array([1.0, -2.0])
g = finite_gradient(f, point)
H = finite_hessian(f, point)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(5.5, 3.5))
ax.axis('off')

27.14 27.13 Common mistakes to avoid

  1. Confusing points and vectors. A point gives location; a vector gives displacement. Coordinates can represent either, but the meaning differs.
  2. Forgetting that multivariable limits require all paths. Checking one path is not enough to prove a limit exists.
  3. Using the gradient without normalizing the direction. Directional derivatives use unit vectors.
  4. Forgetting boundary cases in optimization. Critical points inside a region are not enough for absolute extrema.
  5. Missing the Jacobian factor. Coordinate changes require area or volume scaling.
  6. Using the wrong orientation. Line and surface integrals over oriented objects depend on direction.
  7. Applying Green, Stokes, or Divergence theorem without checking hypotheses. Holes, singularities, and missing boundaries matter.
  8. Treating numerical output as exact. Computation supports reasoning but does not replace it.

27.15 27.14 Cumulative worked example

Consider the scalar field

\[ f(x,y)=x^2+xy+2y^2. \]

Gradient.

\[ \nabla f(x,y)=(2x+y, x+4y). \]

At \((1,-1)\),

\[ \nabla f(1,-1)=(1,-3). \]

Directional derivative. In the unit direction

\[ u=\frac{1}{\sqrt 5}(1,2), \]

we have

\[ D_u f(1,-1)=(1,-3)\cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt 5}(1,2)=\frac{-5}{\sqrt 5}=-\sqrt 5. \]

Hessian.

\[ H_f= \begin{bmatrix} 2&1\\ 1&4 \end{bmatrix}. \]

The Hessian is positive definite because its leading principal minors are positive. Therefore \(f\) is strictly convex and has a unique global minimum where \(\nabla f=0\), namely \((0,0)\).

Integral. The average value over the rectangle \([-1,1]\times[-1,1]\) is

\[ \frac{1}{4}\int_{-1}^1\int_{-1}^1 (x^2+xy+2y^2)\,dy\,dx. \]

By symmetry, the \(xy\) term integrates to zero. The average is

\[ \frac14\left(\frac{4}{3}+\frac{8}{3}\right)=1. \]

This one example uses gradients, directional derivatives, Hessians, optimization, symmetry, and double integrals.

## 27.2 Geometry: the coordinate language

The first part of the book developed the coordinate language of space. The essential objects are points, vectors, distances, angles, projections, lines, planes, and surfaces.

For $x=(x_1,\ldots,x_n)$ and $y=(y_1,\ldots,y_n)$,

$$
\|x-y\| = \sqrt{(x_1-y_1)^2+\cdots+(x_n-y_n)^2}.
$$

The dot product is

$$
x\cdot y = x_1y_1+\cdots+x_ny_n,
$$

and it measures alignment:

$$
x\cdot y = \|x\|\,\|y\|\cos\theta.
$$

Projection is the geometry behind least squares and many data methods. The projection of $x$ onto the direction of a nonzero vector $v$ is

$$
\operatorname{proj}_v x = \frac{x\cdot v}{v\cdot v}v.
$$

A hyperplane in $\mathbb R^n$ has equation

$$
w\cdot x = b.
$$

This simple equation appears in tangent planes, constraints, regression, support vector machines, and linear classifiers.

### Example 27.1: Projection as a least-squares approximation

Let $x=(3,4)$ and $v=(1,2)$. The best approximation to $x$ among all multiples of $v$ is the projection of $x$ onto $v$:

$$
\operatorname{proj}_v x = \frac{x\cdot v}{v\cdot v}v
= \frac{3+8}{1+4}(1,2)
= \frac{11}{5}(1,2).
$$

The residual

$$
r = x - \operatorname{proj}_v x
$$

is orthogonal to $v$. This is the same geometry that appears when fitting a linear model by least squares: the residual is orthogonal to the space of fitted values.

## 27.3 Derivatives: local change and local models

For a scalar function $f:\mathbb R^n\to \mathbb R$, the first derivative is the gradient

$$
\nabla f(x) = \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1},\ldots,\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}\right).
$$

The gradient does three things at once:

- it gives the best linear approximation;
- it gives directional derivatives by dot product;
- it points in the direction of fastest increase.

The first-order Taylor approximation near $a$ is

$$
f(a+h) \approx f(a)+\nabla f(a)\cdot h.
$$

The directional derivative in a unit direction $u$ is

$$
D_u f(a)=\nabla f(a)\cdot u.
$$

The second derivative is organized by the Hessian matrix

$$
H_f(a)=
\begin{bmatrix}
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1^2} & \cdots & \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1\partial x_n} \\
\vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_n\partial x_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_n^2}
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

The second-order Taylor approximation is

$$
f(a+h) \approx f(a)+\nabla f(a)\cdot h+\frac12 h^T H_f(a) h.
$$

This formula is the local geometry behind optimization, uncertainty approximation, and many algorithms in statistics and machine learning.

### Example 27.2: Local quadratic geometry

Let

$$
f(x,y)=x^2+4y^2.
$$

At the origin, $\nabla f(0,0)=0$ and

$$
H_f(0,0)=
\begin{bmatrix}
2&0\\
0&8
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

The eigenvalues are positive, so the origin is a local minimum. The curvature is larger in the $y$-direction than in the $x$-direction. Gradient descent therefore moves differently depending on the direction and step size.

## 27.4 Jacobians: derivatives of vector-valued maps

For a map $F:\mathbb R^n\to \mathbb R^m$, the derivative is the Jacobian matrix

$$
J_F(x)=
\begin{bmatrix}
\frac{\partial F_1}{\partial x_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial F_1}{\partial x_n} \\
\vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
\frac{\partial F_m}{\partial x_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial F_m}{\partial x_n}
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Near a point $a$,

$$
F(a+h) \approx F(a)+J_F(a)h.
$$

The Jacobian is also the linear map used in the multivariable chain rule. If $F=G\circ H$, then

$$
J_F(x)=J_G(H(x))J_H(x).
$$

In computation, this is the matrix form of backpropagation. In integration, the determinant of the Jacobian measures how area or volume changes under a coordinate transformation.

### Example 27.3: The polar-coordinate Jacobian

The polar-coordinate map is

$$
T(r,\theta)=(r\cos\theta,r\sin\theta).
$$

Its Jacobian matrix is

$$
J_T(r,\theta)=
\begin{bmatrix}
\cos\theta & -r\sin\theta\\
\sin\theta & r\cos\theta
\end{bmatrix},
$$

so

$$
|\det J_T(r,\theta)|=r.
$$

That is why $dA=r\,dr\,d\theta$.

## 27.5 Integrals: accumulation over geometric objects

A definite integral accumulates over an interval. Multivariable calculus extends this idea to regions, solids, curves, and surfaces.

| Integral | Object | Common meaning |
|---|---|---|
| $\int_a^b f(x)\,dx$ | interval | signed area, total change |
| $\iint_R f(x,y)\,dA$ | plane region | volume, mass, probability |
| $\iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV$ | solid region | mass, volume, expectation |
| $\int_C f\,ds$ | curve | mass of a wire |
| $\int_C F\cdot dr$ | oriented curve | work, circulation |
| $\iint_S f\,dS$ | surface | mass of a shell |
| $\iint_S F\cdot n\,dS$ | oriented surface | flux |

In statistics, integrals compute probability, expectation, variance, and normalization constants. In applied mathematics, integrals compute mass, energy, flow, and conservation quantities. In machine learning, integrals appear in expected risk, Bayesian inference, generative models, and continuous approximations.

### Example 27.4: Probability as a double integral

Suppose a joint density is

$$
f(x,y)=4xy, \qquad 0\le x\le 1,\quad 0\le y\le 1.
$$

Then

$$
\iint_{[0,1]^2} 4xy\,dA = 1,
$$

so it is a valid density. The probability of the event $x+y\le 1$ is

$$
\int_0^1 \int_0^{1-x} 4xy\,dy\,dx
= \int_0^1 2x(1-x)^2\,dx
= \frac16.
$$

## 27.6 Optimization: from critical points to algorithms

Optimization is one of the main bridges from calculus to data science and machine learning. The central objects are:

- the **gradient** $\nabla f$, which gives the local direction of steepest increase;
- the **Hessian** $H_f$, which describes curvature;
- the **constraint gradients** $\nabla g_i$, which describe normal directions to constraint surfaces.

For unconstrained optimization, critical points satisfy

$$
\nabla f(x)=0.
$$

For a one-constraint problem, the Lagrange multiplier condition is

$$
\nabla f(x)=\lambda \nabla g(x),
\qquad g(x)=c.
$$

For a two-constraint problem,

$$
\nabla f(x)=\lambda \nabla g(x)+\mu \nabla h(x),
\qquad g(x)=c,
\qquad h(x)=d.
$$

Gradient descent uses the update

$$
x_{k+1}=x_k-\alpha \nabla f(x_k).
$$

Newton's method uses curvature:

$$
x_{k+1}=x_k-H_f(x_k)^{-1}\nabla f(x_k).
$$

The main graduate-level habit is to ask not only where the optimum is, but also whether the problem is well-conditioned, whether the Hessian is positive definite, and whether constraints or regularization change the geometry.

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch27-gradient-descent-review
#| fig-cap: 'Gradient descent follows local negative-gradient directions on a loss landscape.'
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

A = np.array([[3.0, 0.8], [0.8, 1.0]])
b = np.array([-1.0, 0.5])

def f_xy(z):
    return 0.5*z @ A @ z + b @ z

def grad_xy(z):
    return A @ z + b

z = np.array([2.4, 1.8])
alpha = 0.22
path = [z.copy()]
for _ in range(18):
    z = z - alpha*grad_xy(z)
    path.append(z.copy())
path = np.array(path)

x = np.linspace(-2.5, 2.8, 240)
y = np.linspace(-2.2, 2.2, 240)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)

for i in range(X.shape[0]):
    for j in range(X.shape[1]):
        Z[i, j] = f_xy(np.array([X[i, j], Y[i, j]]))

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(6, 5))

ax.plot(path[:, 0], path[:, 1], marker='o')
```

## 27.7 Vector fields and the global theorems

Vector calculus studies fields that assign a vector to each point. The main differential operators are:

$$
\operatorname{div}F = \nabla\cdot F,
\qquad
\operatorname{curl}F = \nabla\times F.
$$

The divergence measures local source or sink behavior. The curl measures local rotation. The global theorems convert integrals over boundaries into integrals over interiors.

### The four Fundamental-Theorem patterns

| Theorem | Boundary integral | Interior integral | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fundamental Theorem of Calculus | $f(b)-f(a)$ | $\int_a^b f'(x)\,dx$ | endpoint change equals accumulated derivative |
| Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals | $f(B)-f(A)$ | $\int_C \nabla f\cdot dr$ | potential difference equals work |
| Green's theorem | $\oint_C P\,dx+Q\,dy$ | $\iint_R (Q_x-P_y)\,dA$ | boundary circulation equals interior curl |
| Stokes theorem | $\oint_{\partial S} F\cdot dr$ | $\iint_S (\nabla\times F)\cdot n\,dS$ | boundary circulation equals surface curl flux |
| Divergence theorem | $\iint_{\partial E} F\cdot n\,dS$ | $\iiint_E \nabla\cdot F\,dV$ | boundary flux equals interior source |

The pattern is powerful: a global boundary measurement is controlled by a local derivative inside.

## 27.8 A problem-solving decision tree

A good problem-solving strategy is often more useful than a memorized formula. Use the following decision tree.

**Step 1: Identify the object.**

- Scalar function $f(x,y)$ or $f(x,y,z)$?
- Vector-valued function $r(t)$?
- Region $R$ or solid $E$?
- Curve $C$ or surface $S$?
- Vector field $F$?
- Constraint $g(x)=c$?
- Density or loss function?

**Step 2: Identify the action.**

- Find a rate of change: use derivative, gradient, Jacobian, or Hessian.
- Approximate locally: use linearization or Taylor expansion.
- Find maximum or minimum: use critical points, Hessian test, boundary check, or Lagrange multipliers.
- Accumulate over a region: use double or triple integrals.
- Change coordinates: compute a Jacobian determinant.
- Accumulate along a curve: use line integrals.
- Compute flux through a surface: use surface integrals or the Divergence theorem.
- Compute circulation: use line integrals, Green's theorem, or Stokes theorem.

**Step 3: Interpret the result.**

A numerical answer is rarely the final mathematical answer. Ask what the number means: volume, mass, probability, expected value, rate of increase, work, flux, circulation, curvature, uncertainty, or model error.

## 27.10 Computational capstone: a mini calculus toolkit

This section builds a small numerical toolkit. The goal is not to replace exact mathematics, but to make the formulas executable. A graduate student should be able to move between exact formulas, numerical approximations, and visual checks.

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch27-gradient-check-toolkit
#| fig-cap: 'A finite-difference gradient approximates the first derivative of a multivariable function.'
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt


def finite_gradient(f, x, h=1e-6):
    x = np.asarray(x, dtype=float)
    g = np.zeros_like(x)
    for j in range(len(x)):
        e = np.zeros_like(x)
        e[j] = 1.0
        g[j] = (f(x + h*e) - f(x - h*e))/(2*h)
    return g


def finite_hessian(f, x, h=1e-4):
    x = np.asarray(x, dtype=float)
    n = len(x)
    H = np.zeros((n, n))
    for i in range(n):
        for j in range(n):
            ei = np.zeros(n)
            ej = np.zeros(n)
            ei[i] = 1.0
            ej[j] = 1.0
            H[i, j] = (
                f(x + h*ei + h*ej)
                - f(x + h*ei - h*ej)
                - f(x - h*ei + h*ej)
                + f(x - h*ei - h*ej)
            )/(4*h*h)
    return H

f = lambda z: z[0]**2 + z[0]*z[1] + 3*z[1]**2
point = np.array([1.0, -2.0])
g = finite_gradient(f, point)
H = finite_hessian(f, point)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(5.5, 3.5))
ax.axis('off')
text = (
    'At x = (1, -2)\n'
    f'finite gradient = {np.round(g, 6)}\n'
    f'finite Hessian =\n{np.round(H, 6)}'
)
ax.text(0.05, 0.7, text, fontsize=12, family='monospace', va='top')
plt.show()
```

## 27.13 Common mistakes to avoid

1. **Confusing points and vectors.** A point gives location; a vector gives displacement. Coordinates can represent either, but the meaning differs.
2. **Forgetting that multivariable limits require all paths.** Checking one path is not enough to prove a limit exists.
3. **Using the gradient without normalizing the direction.** Directional derivatives use unit vectors.
4. **Forgetting boundary cases in optimization.** Critical points inside a region are not enough for absolute extrema.
5. **Missing the Jacobian factor.** Coordinate changes require area or volume scaling.
6. **Using the wrong orientation.** Line and surface integrals over oriented objects depend on direction.
7. **Applying Green, Stokes, or Divergence theorem without checking hypotheses.** Holes, singularities, and missing boundaries matter.
8. **Treating numerical output as exact.** Computation supports reasoning but does not replace it.

## 27.14 Cumulative worked example

Consider the scalar field

$$
f(x,y)=x^2+xy+2y^2.
$$

**Gradient.**

$$
\nabla f(x,y)=(2x+y, x+4y).
$$

At $(1,-1)$,

$$
\nabla f(1,-1)=(1,-3).
$$

**Directional derivative.** In the unit direction

$$
u=\frac{1}{\sqrt 5}(1,2),
$$

we have

$$
D_u f(1,-1)=(1,-3)\cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt 5}(1,2)=\frac{-5}{\sqrt 5}=-\sqrt 5.
$$

**Hessian.**

$$
H_f=
\begin{bmatrix}
2&1\\
1&4
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

The Hessian is positive definite because its leading principal minors are positive. Therefore $f$ is strictly convex and has a unique global minimum where $\nabla f=0$, namely $(0,0)$.

**Integral.** The average value over the rectangle $[-1,1]\times[-1,1]$ is

$$
\frac{1}{4}\int_{-1}^1\int_{-1}^1 (x^2+xy+2y^2)\,dy\,dx.
$$

By symmetry, the $xy$ term integrates to zero. The average is

$$
\frac14\left(\frac{4}{3}+\frac{8}{3}\right)=1.
$$

This one example uses gradients, directional derivatives, Hessians, optimization, symmetry, and double integrals.
