15  Polar Coordinates and Change of Variables in the Plane

Circular geometry, Jacobians, transformed regions, probability densities, and coordinate systems

Chapter goal. Many two-dimensional integrals become simple only after we choose coordinates that match the geometry. Rectangular coordinates are natural for boxes and vertical slices, but circular and radial regions call for polar coordinates. More generally, a change of variables replaces a complicated region in the \(xy\)-plane by a simpler region in a new \(uv\)-plane, at the cost of multiplying by a Jacobian factor.

The central idea of this chapter is:

\[ \text{new area element} = \text{local area scaling factor}\times \text{new coordinate area element}. \]

For polar coordinates this becomes

\[ dA = r\,dr\,d\theta. \]

For a general transformation \((u,v)\mapsto (x,y)\), this becomes

\[ dA = \left|\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right|\,du\,dv. \]

This idea is important in multivariable calculus, probability, statistics, numerical integration, geometric modeling, and machine learning. It explains why densities change under transformations, why radial symmetry simplifies Gaussian integrals, and why determinants measure local volume distortion.

15.1 Learning objectives

After studying this chapter, you should be able to:

  1. convert between rectangular coordinates \((x,y)\) and polar coordinates \((r,\theta)\);
  2. describe disks, annuli, sectors, and shifted circles using polar inequalities;
  3. explain geometrically why \(dA=r\,dr\,d\theta\);
  4. set up and evaluate double integrals in polar coordinates;
  5. decide when polar coordinates are preferable to rectangular coordinates;
  6. compute the Jacobian determinant of a transformation;
  7. apply the change-of-variables formula for double integrals;
  8. interpret Jacobians as local area-scaling factors;
  9. connect coordinate transformations to probability densities and modern data modeling.

15.2 15.1 Why change coordinates?

A coordinate system is not the object itself. It is a language for describing the object.

The disk

\[ x^2+y^2\le 1 \]

is a simple geometric object, but in rectangular coordinates it requires square roots if we describe it by vertical slices:

\[ -1\le x\le 1,\qquad -\sqrt{1-x^2}\le y\le \sqrt{1-x^2}. \]

In polar coordinates, the same disk becomes

\[ 0\le r\le 1, \qquad 0\le \theta\le 2\pi. \]

The geometry did not change; only the description changed. The new description matches the radial symmetry of the region.

This principle appears throughout applied mathematics:

  • circular regions are easier in polar coordinates;
  • ellipses often become circles after a scaling transformation;
  • correlated Gaussian densities become independent Gaussian densities after a linear transformation;
  • complicated constraints may become rectangles in new variables;
  • latent-variable models often use a transformation from simple variables to observed variables.

A good coordinate system reduces complexity.

15.3 15.2 Polar coordinates

In polar coordinates, a point in the plane is described by two numbers:

  • \(r\): the distance from the origin;
  • \(\theta\): the angle from the positive \(x\)-axis.

The conversion formulas are

\[ x = r\cos\theta, \qquad y = r\sin\theta, \]

and

\[ r^2 = x^2+y^2, \qquad \tan\theta = \frac{y}{x} \]

when \(x\ne 0\), with the quadrant determining the correct angle.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

r = 3.0
theta = np.pi/5
x = r*np.cos(theta)
y = r*np.sin(theta)

ax.text(x+0.12, y+0.12, r'$P=(r\cos\theta,r\sin\theta)$')
ax.plot([x, x], [0, y], linestyle='--', linewidth=1)
ax.plot([0, x], [y, y], linestyle='--', linewidth=1)

ax.text(0.85*np.cos(theta/2), 0.85*np.sin(theta/2), r'$\theta$')
ax.text(x/2, y/2+0.15, r'$r$')

15.3.1 Nonuniqueness of polar coordinates

A rectangular coordinate pair \((x,y)\) is unique. Polar coordinates are not unique.

For example, the point \((1,1)\) has polar coordinates

\[ \left(\sqrt{2},\frac{\pi}{4}\right), \qquad \left(\sqrt{2},\frac{\pi}{4}+2\pi\right), \qquad \left(\sqrt{2},\frac{\pi}{4}+4\pi\right), \]

and many more. The origin has \(r=0\) and any value of \(\theta\).

For double integrals, we usually take \(r\ge 0\) and choose an interval of angles that covers the desired region exactly once.

NotePractical convention

When setting up polar integrals, choose bounds so that almost every point in the region is counted exactly once. Boundaries may overlap, but overlapping boundaries have area zero and do not change the value of the double integral.

15.4 15.3 Common polar regions

Polar coordinates are most useful when the region is described naturally by distance from the origin and angle.

15.4.1 Disk centered at the origin

The disk \(x^2+y^2\le a^2\) becomes

\[ 0\le r\le a, \qquad 0\le \theta\le 2\pi. \]

15.4.2 Annulus centered at the origin

The annulus \(a^2\le x^2+y^2\le b^2\) becomes

\[ a\le r\le b, \qquad 0\le \theta\le 2\pi. \]

15.4.3 Sector

A circular sector is described by

\[ 0\le r\le a, \qquad \alpha\le \theta\le \beta. \]

15.4.4 Upper half-disk

The upper half of the disk \(x^2+y^2\le a^2\) is

\[ 0\le r\le a, \qquad 0\le \theta\le \pi. \]

15.4.5 Shifted circle

The circle

\[ (x-a)^2+y^2=a^2 \]

passes through the origin and has center \((a,0)\). Expanding gives

\[ x^2+y^2 = 2ax. \]

In polar coordinates this becomes

\[ r^2 = 2ar\cos\theta, \]

so for \(r\ge 0\) the boundary is

\[ r=2a\cos\theta. \]

The interior is usually described by

\[ -\frac{\pi}{2}\le \theta\le \frac{\pi}{2}, \qquad 0\le r\le 2a\cos\theta. \]

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

fig, axes = plt.subplots(2, 2, figsize=(7, 7))

# Disk
ax = axes[0, 0]

ax.set_title('Annulus: 1 <= r <= 2')

# Sector

15.5 15.4 The polar area element

The most important formula in polar integration is

\[ dA = r\,dr\,d\theta. \]

This factor \(r\) is not optional. It records the fact that a small angular change \(d\theta\) sweeps out a longer arc when the point is farther from the origin.

Consider a small polar rectangle:

\[ r\le \rho\le r+dr, \qquad \theta\le \phi\le \theta+d\theta. \]

Its radial thickness is approximately \(dr\). Its angular width is approximately arc length \(r\,d\theta\). Therefore its area is approximately

\[ (r\,d\theta)(dr)=r\,dr\,d\theta. \]

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

r0 = 1.5
dr = 0.35
th0 = np.pi/5
dth = np.pi/9
th = np.linspace(th0, th0+dth, 120)

x_outer = (r0+dr)*np.cos(th)
y_outer = (r0+dr)*np.sin(th)
x_inner = r0*np.cos(th[::-1])
y_inner = r0*np.sin(th[::-1])
poly_x = np.r_[x_outer, x_inner]
poly_y = np.r_[y_outer, y_inner]

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(6.5, 5.5))
for rad in [0.75, 1.15, 1.5, 1.85, 2.25]:
    th_full = np.linspace(0, np.pi/2, 200)
    ax.plot(rad*np.cos(th_full), rad*np.sin(th_full), color='gray', alpha=0.35, linewidth=0.8)
for angle in np.linspace(0, np.pi/2, 7):
    ax.plot([0, 2.4*np.cos(angle)], [0, 2.4*np.sin(angle)], color='gray', alpha=0.35, linewidth=0.8)

ax.fill(poly_x, poly_y, alpha=0.55)
ax.plot(poly_x, poly_y, linewidth=2)
ax.text(1.85*np.cos(th0+dth/2), 1.85*np.sin(th0+dth/2)+0.15, r'$r\,d\theta$')
ax.text((r0+0.2)*np.cos(th0+dth)+0.08, (r0+0.2)*np.sin(th0+dth), r'$dr$')
ax.text(1.0*np.cos(th0/2), 1.0*np.sin(th0/2), r'$\theta$')
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
ax.set_xlim(0, 2.5)
ax.set_ylim(0, 2.5)
ax.set_xlabel('$x$')
ax.set_ylabel('$y$')
ax.set_title('Polar area element')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.2)
plt.show()
WarningCommon mistake

Do not write \(dA=dr\,d\theta\). The correct formula is \(dA=r\,dr\,d\theta\). The factor \(r\) is the local stretching factor from angular movement.

15.6 15.5 Double integrals in polar coordinates

Suppose a region \(D\) is described in polar form as

\[ \alpha\le \theta\le \beta, \qquad h_1(\theta)\le r\le h_2(\theta). \]

Then

\[ \iint_D f(x,y)\,dA = \int_\alpha^\beta\int_{h_1(\theta)}^{h_2(\theta)} f(r\cos\theta,r\sin\theta)\,r\,dr\,d\theta. \]

The process has three steps:

  1. rewrite the region in polar bounds;
  2. substitute \(x=r\cos\theta\) and \(y=r\sin\theta\) into the integrand;
  3. replace \(dA\) by \(r\,dr\,d\theta\).

15.6.1 Worked example 1: Area of a disk

Find the area of the disk \(D:x^2+y^2\le a^2\).

Since \(D\) is \(0\le r\le a\), \(0\le \theta\le 2\pi\),

\[ \operatorname{Area}(D) = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^a r\,dr\,d\theta = \int_0^{2\pi}\frac{a^2}{2}\,d\theta = \pi a^2. \]

This is the usual area formula, now derived as a double integral.

15.6.2 Worked example 2: Converting a rectangular-coordinate integral

Convert

\[ \int_{-1}^{1}\int_0^{\sqrt{1-x^2}} 4\,dy\,dx \]

into polar coordinates and evaluate it.

The region is the upper half of the unit disk:

\[ x^2+y^2\le 1, \qquad y\ge 0. \]

In polar coordinates,

\[ 0\le r\le 1, \qquad 0\le \theta\le \pi. \]

Therefore

\[ \int_{-1}^{1}\int_0^{\sqrt{1-x^2}} 4\,dy\,dx = \int_0^\pi\int_0^1 4r\,dr\,d\theta =2\pi. \]

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x = np.linspace(-1, 1, 400)
y = np.sqrt(1 - x**2)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(6, 4.8))
ax.fill_between(x, 0, y, alpha=0.4)
ax.plot(x, y)
ax.plot(x, -y, color='gray', alpha=0.4, linestyle='--')
ax.axhline(0, linewidth=1)
ax.axvline(0, linewidth=1)
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')

ax.set_ylabel('$y$')
ax.set_title('Upper half of the unit disk')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)
plt.show()

15.6.3 Worked example 3: An annular integral

Evaluate

\[ \iint_D \cos(x^2+y^2)\,dA, \]

where \(D\) is the region between \(r=3\) and \(r=4\) in the upper half-plane.

The bounds are

\[ 3\le r\le 4, \qquad 0\le \theta\le \pi. \]

Since \(x^2+y^2=r^2\) and \(dA=r\,dr\,d\theta\),

\[ \iint_D \cos(x^2+y^2)\,dA = \int_0^\pi\int_3^4 \cos(r^2)r\,dr\,d\theta. \]

Let \(u=r^2\), so \(du=2r\,dr\). Then

\[ \int_3^4 \cos(r^2)r\,dr = \frac12\int_9^{16}\cos u\,du = \frac12(\sin 16-\sin 9). \]

Thus

\[ \iint_D \cos(x^2+y^2)\,dA = \frac{\pi}{2}(\sin 16-\sin 9). \]

This example shows a typical reason for using polar coordinates: the expression \(x^2+y^2\) becomes the simpler expression \(r^2\).

15.7 15.6 Volume using polar coordinates

If a solid lies under \(z=f(x,y)\) and above a region \(D\), then its volume is

\[ V=\iint_D f(x,y)\,dA. \]

When \(D\) is circular or the function depends on \(x^2+y^2\), polar coordinates often simplify the integral.

15.7.1 Worked example 4: Paraboloid over a quadrant

Find the volume under

\[ z=6-2x^2-2y^2 \]

above the first-quadrant part of the region where \(z\ge 0\).

The condition \(z\ge 0\) gives

\[ 6-2x^2-2y^2\ge 0, \]

so

\[ x^2+y^2\le 3. \]

In the first quadrant,

\[ 0\le \theta\le \frac{\pi}{2}, \qquad 0\le r\le \sqrt{3}. \]

Therefore

\[ V= \int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^{\sqrt{3}} (6-2r^2)r\,dr\,d\theta. \]

Compute the inner integral:

\[ \int_0^{\sqrt{3}} (6r-2r^3)\,dr = \left[3r^2-\frac12r^4\right]_0^{\sqrt{3}} =9-\frac92 =\frac92. \]

Hence

\[ V=\frac{\pi}{2}\cdot \frac92 =\frac{9\pi}{4}. \]

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from mpl_toolkits.mplot3d import Axes3D  # noqa: F401

r = np.linspace(0, np.sqrt(3), 90)
th = np.linspace(0, np.pi/2, 90)
R, TH = np.meshgrid(r, th)
X = R*np.cos(TH)
Y = R*np.sin(TH)
Z = 6 - 2*R**2

fig = plt.figure(figsize=(7, 5.5))
ax = fig.add_subplot(111, projection='3d')
ax.plot_surface(X, Y, Z, alpha=0.85, linewidth=0, antialiased=True)
ax.set_xlabel('$x$')

ax.set_zlabel('$z$')
ax.set_title('Paraboloid above a quarter disk')
plt.show()

15.8 15.7 Shifted circular regions

Polar coordinates are not only for circles centered at the origin. They also work well for some shifted circles, especially when the circle passes through the origin.

Consider

\[ D=\{(x,y):(x-2)^2+y^2\le 4\}. \]

Expanding gives

\[ x^2-4x+4+y^2\le 4, \]

so

\[ x^2+y^2\le 4x. \]

In polar coordinates,

\[ r^2\le 4r\cos\theta. \]

For \(r\ge 0\), this becomes

\[ 0\le r\le 4\cos\theta. \]

We need \(4\cos\theta\ge 0\), so one convenient angular interval is

\[ -\frac{\pi}{2}\le \theta\le \frac{\pi}{2}. \]

Thus

\[ D=\left\{(r,\theta):-\frac{\pi}{2}\le \theta\le \frac{\pi}{2},\ 0\le r\le 4\cos\theta\right\}. \]

The area is

\[ \int_{-\pi/2}^{\pi/2}\int_0^{4\cos\theta} r\,dr\,d\theta = \int_{-\pi/2}^{\pi/2} 8\cos^2\theta\,d\theta =4\pi. \]

This agrees with the area of a circle of radius \(2\).

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

th = np.linspace(-np.pi/2, np.pi/2, 500)
r = 4*np.cos(th)
x = r*np.cos(th)
y = r*np.sin(th)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(6.5, 5.5))
ax.fill(x, y, alpha=0.35)
ax.plot(x, y, linewidth=2, label=r'$r=4\cos\theta$')
for angle in np.linspace(-np.pi/2, np.pi/2, 9):
    rr = 4*np.cos(angle)
    ax.plot([0, rr*np.cos(angle)], [0, rr*np.sin(angle)], color='gray', alpha=0.35, linewidth=0.8)
ax.scatter([2], [0], s=45, label='center')
ax.axhline(0, linewidth=1)
ax.axvline(0, linewidth=1)
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
ax.set_xlabel('$x$')
ax.set_ylabel('$y$')
ax.set_title('Shifted disk in polar coordinates')
ax.legend()
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)
plt.show()

15.9 15.8 Strategy for choosing polar coordinates

Polar coordinates are often a good choice when at least one of the following appears:

  1. The region involves \(x^2+y^2\).
  2. The boundary is a circle, disk, annulus, sector, or ray from the origin.
  3. The integrand contains \(x^2+y^2\), \(\sqrt{x^2+y^2}\), or radial symmetry.
  4. A rectangular-coordinate integral contains square roots such as \(\sqrt{a^2-x^2}\).
  5. The geometry is best described by distance and angle.

Polar coordinates may not help if the region is rectangular and the integrand has no radial structure.

15.9.1 A comparison example

The integral

\[ \int_{-1}^{1}\int_{-\sqrt{1-x^2}}^{\sqrt{1-x^2}} e^{-(x^2+y^2)}\,dy\,dx \]

is hard in rectangular coordinates because the inner integral has no elementary antiderivative. In polar coordinates it becomes

\[ \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 e^{-r^2}r\,dr\,d\theta, \]

which is elementary:

\[ 2\pi\left[-\frac12e^{-r^2}\right]_0^1 =\pi(1-e^{-1}). \]

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x = np.linspace(-1.2, 1.2, 350)
y = np.linspace(-1.2, 1.2, 350)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)
Z = np.exp(-(X**2 + Y**2))
mask = X**2 + Y**2 <= 1
Z_masked = np.where(mask, Z, np.nan)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(6.5, 5.5))
cont = ax.contourf(X, Y, Z_masked, levels=20)
ax.contour(X, Y, X**2 + Y**2, levels=[1], linewidths=2)
fig.colorbar(cont, ax=ax, label=r'$e^{-(x^2+y^2)}$')
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
ax.set_xlabel('$x$')
ax.set_ylabel('$y$')
ax.set_title('Radial integrand over a disk')
plt.show()

15.10 15.9 General change of variables

Polar coordinates are one example of a broader idea.

Let a transformation \(T\) map a point \((u,v)\) in a new coordinate plane to a point \((x,y)\) in the original plane:

\[ T(u,v)=(x(u,v),y(u,v)). \]

The Jacobian determinant of the transformation is

\[ \frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)} = \begin{vmatrix} \frac{\partial x}{\partial u} & \frac{\partial x}{\partial v} \\ \frac{\partial y}{\partial u} & \frac{\partial y}{\partial v} \end{vmatrix} = \frac{\partial x}{\partial u}\frac{\partial y}{\partial v} - \frac{\partial x}{\partial v}\frac{\partial y}{\partial u}. \]

The absolute value of this determinant measures local area scaling.

15.10.1 Change-of-variables formula

If \(T\) maps a region \(S\) in the \(uv\)-plane to a region \(D\) in the \(xy\)-plane, and if the transformation behaves well on the region, then

\[ \iint_D f(x,y)\,dA = \iint_S f(x(u,v),y(u,v)) \left|\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right|\,du\,dv. \]

This formula has the same structure as polar integration:

\[ \text{integrand after substitution}\times \text{area-scaling factor}. \]

15.10.2 Polar coordinates as a Jacobian example

For polar coordinates,

\[ x=r\cos\theta, \qquad y=r\sin\theta. \]

The Jacobian is

\[ \frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(r,\theta)} = \begin{vmatrix} \cos\theta & -r\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta & r\cos\theta \end{vmatrix} =r\cos^2\theta+r\sin^2\theta =r. \]

Therefore

\[ dA=r\,dr\,d\theta. \]

The familiar polar formula is just the general change-of-variables formula applied to the polar map.

15.11 15.10 Linear transformations and area scaling

A linear transformation

\[ \begin{pmatrix}x\\y\end{pmatrix} = A\begin{pmatrix}u\\v\end{pmatrix} \]

scales every area by the constant factor \(|\det A|\).

For example, let

\[ x=2u+v, \qquad y=u+3v. \]

Then

\[ A=\begin{pmatrix}2&1\\1&3\end{pmatrix}, \qquad \det A=5. \]

So any region in the \(uv\)-plane has image area five times as large in the \(xy\)-plane.

15.11.1 Worked example 5: Area of a parallelogram

Let \(S=[0,1]\times[0,1]\) and define

\[ x=2u+v, \qquad y=u+3v. \]

The image \(D=T(S)\) is a parallelogram. Its area is

\[ \operatorname{Area}(D) = \iint_D 1\,dA = \iint_S 1\cdot |5|\,du\,dv =5. \]

This agrees with the determinant interpretation: the unit square is stretched into a parallelogram with area \(5\).

15.12 15.11 Nonlinear transformations

For nonlinear transformations, the Jacobian usually varies from point to point. The transformation may stretch one part of the region more than another.

For example,

\[ x=u, \qquad y=v+0.4u^2. \]

The Jacobian is

\[ \frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)} = \begin{vmatrix} 1&0\\0.8u&1 \end{vmatrix} =1. \]

Although this transformation bends horizontal grid lines into parabolas, it preserves area locally because the Jacobian determinant is \(1\).

import numpy as np

15.12.1 Worked example 6: A triangular transformation

Let

\[ x=u, \qquad y=uv, \]

where

\[ 1\le u\le 2, \qquad 0\le v\le 1. \]

The Jacobian is

\[ \frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)} = \begin{vmatrix} 1&0\\v&u \end{vmatrix} =u. \]

The image region satisfies

\[ 1\le x\le 2, \qquad 0\le y\le x. \]

If we want to evaluate

\[ \iint_D (x+y)\,dA, \]

then

\[ \iint_D (x+y)\,dA = \int_1^2\int_0^1 (u+uv)u\,dv\,du. \]

Simplifying,

\[ \int_1^2\int_0^1 u^2(1+v)\,dv\,du = \int_1^2 \frac32u^2\,du =\frac32\cdot\frac{7}{3} =\frac72. \]

15.13 15.12 Change of variables in probability

Change of variables is essential in probability and statistics.

If \((X,Y)\) has joint density \(f_{X,Y}(x,y)\) and

\[ (X,Y)=T(U,V), \]

then the density of \((U,V)\) involves the Jacobian factor. Informally,

\[ \text{probability mass} = \text{density}\times \text{area}. \]

When area changes, density must change in the opposite way so that probability is preserved.

15.13.1 Radial probability

Suppose a density depends only on radius:

\[ f(x,y)=c e^{-(x^2+y^2)}. \]

To find the normalizing constant \(c\), require

\[ 1=\iint_{\mathbb R^2} c e^{-(x^2+y^2)}\,dA. \]

Using polar coordinates,

\[ 1=c\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^\infty e^{-r^2}r\,dr\,d\theta =c(2\pi)\left(\frac12\right) =c\pi. \]

So

\[ c=\frac1\pi. \]

This is a two-dimensional radial density. Similar ideas lead to the normalization constants for multivariate normal distributions.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(15)

# For density (1/pi) exp(-r^2), the variable R^2 is exponential with mean 1.

th = rng.uniform(0, 2*np.pi, size=n)
x = r*np.cos(th)

15.14 15.13 Computational viewpoint

In computation, a change of variables is more than a symbolic trick. It is a method for transforming a difficult numerical problem into an easier one.

15.14.1 Monte Carlo in a disk

To approximate

\[ \iint_{x^2+y^2\le 1} e^{-(x^2+y^2)}\,dA, \]

we can sample uniformly from the disk. A correct polar simulation must not sample \(r\) uniformly on \([0,1]\). Since area in polar coordinates is \(r\,dr\,d\theta\), the radius distribution must put more points near the outer part of the disk.

A uniform point in the unit disk can be generated by

\[ \Theta\sim \operatorname{Uniform}(0,2\pi), \qquad R=\sqrt{U}, \]

where \(U\sim \operatorname{Uniform}(0,1)\).

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(151)
n = 3000
th = rng.uniform(0, 2*np.pi, size=n)

r_wrong = rng.uniform(0, 1, size=n)
x_wrong = r_wrong*np.cos(th)
y_wrong = r_wrong*np.sin(th)

r_right = np.sqrt(rng.uniform(0, 1, size=n))
x_right = r_right*np.cos(th)
y_right = r_right*np.sin(th)

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(7, 3.8))
axes[0].scatter(x_wrong, y_wrong, s=4, alpha=0.25)
axes[0].set_title('Incorrect: $R=U$')
axes[1].scatter(x_right, y_right, s=4, alpha=0.25)
axes[1].set_title(r'Correct: $R=\sqrt{U}$')

for ax in axes:
    ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
    ax.set_xlim(-1.05, 1.05)
    ax.set_ylim(-1.05, 1.05)
    ax.grid(True, alpha=0.2)

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

The exact value is

\[ \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 e^{-r^2}r\,dr\,d\theta =\pi(1-e^{-1}). \]

The simulation estimate is close to that value.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(152)
exact = np.pi*(1 - np.exp(-1))
sizes = np.array([100, 300, 1000, 3000, 10000, 30000])
estimates = []

for n in sizes:
    th = rng.uniform(0, 2*np.pi, size=n)
    r = np.sqrt(rng.uniform(0, 1, size=n))
    values = np.exp(-r**2)
    estimates.append(np.pi*values.mean())  # area of unit disk times average value

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(6.5, 4.8))
ax.plot(sizes, estimates, marker='o', label='Monte Carlo estimate')
ax.axhline(exact, linestyle='--', label='exact value')
ax.set_xscale('log')
ax.set_xlabel('sample size')
ax.set_ylabel('integral estimate')
ax.set_title('Monte Carlo integration over the unit disk')
ax.legend()
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)

15.15 15.14 Applications in data, statistics, and AI

15.15.1 Radial basis functions

Many machine-learning models use radial basis functions such as

\[ \phi(x,y)=e^{-\gamma(x^2+y^2)}. \]

These functions are constant on circles centered at the origin. Polar coordinates reveal their radial structure immediately.

15.15.2 Gaussian models

The bivariate normal density has elliptical level curves. After shifting, scaling, and rotating coordinates, those ellipses become circles. This is a change-of-variables idea. The determinant of the covariance matrix controls how volume changes.

15.15.3 Normalizing flows

In modern generative modeling, a normalizing flow transforms a simple latent variable \(z\) into a more complicated observed variable \(x=T(z)\). The probability density changes by a Jacobian determinant. In two dimensions the formula is the same idea as this chapter; in high dimensions it becomes

\[ \text{density correction}=\left|\det J_T\right|^{-1}. \]

15.15.4 Numerical integration

Monte Carlo methods, importance sampling, and coordinate transformations all rely on understanding how area or volume changes under a transformation. A poor coordinate system can make an integral numerically unstable; a good one can make the same problem nearly one-dimensional.

15.16 15.15 Chapter summary

The key formulas are:

\[ x=r\cos\theta, \qquad y=r\sin\theta, \qquad r^2=x^2+y^2. \]

The polar area element is

\[ dA=r\,dr\,d\theta. \]

Thus, for a polar region

\[ D=\{(r,\theta):\alpha\le \theta\le \beta,\\ h_1(\theta)\le r\le h_2(\theta)\}, \]

we have

\[ \iint_D f(x,y)\,dA = \int_\alpha^\beta\int_{h_1(\theta)}^{h_2(\theta)} f(r\cos\theta,r\sin\theta)r\,dr\,d\theta. \]

For a general transformation

\[ T(u,v)=(x(u,v),y(u,v)), \]

the Jacobian determinant is

\[ \frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)} = \begin{vmatrix} x_u & x_v \\ y_u & y_v \end{vmatrix}, \]

and the change-of-variables formula is

\[ \iint_D f(x,y)\,dA = \iint_S f(x(u,v),y(u,v)) \left|\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right|\,du\,dv. \]

The absolute value of the Jacobian determinant is the local area-scaling factor.

> **Chapter goal.** Many two-dimensional integrals become simple only after we choose coordinates that match the geometry. Rectangular coordinates are natural for boxes and vertical slices, but circular and radial regions call for polar coordinates. More generally, a change of variables replaces a complicated region in the $xy$-plane by a simpler region in a new $uv$-plane, at the cost of multiplying by a Jacobian factor.

The central idea of this chapter is:

$$
\text{new area element} = \text{local area scaling factor}\times \text{new coordinate area element}.
$$

For polar coordinates this becomes

$$
dA = r\,dr\,d\theta.
$$

For a general transformation $(u,v)\mapsto (x,y)$, this becomes

$$
dA = \left|\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right|\,du\,dv.
$$

This idea is important in multivariable calculus, probability, statistics, numerical integration, geometric modeling, and machine learning. It explains why densities change under transformations, why radial symmetry simplifies Gaussian integrals, and why determinants measure local volume distortion.

## 15.1 Why change coordinates?

A coordinate system is not the object itself. It is a language for describing the object.

The disk

$$
x^2+y^2\le 1
$$

is a simple geometric object, but in rectangular coordinates it requires square roots if we describe it by vertical slices:

$$
-1\le x\le 1,\qquad -\sqrt{1-x^2}\le y\le \sqrt{1-x^2}.
$$

In polar coordinates, the same disk becomes

$$
0\le r\le 1,
\qquad 0\le \theta\le 2\pi.
$$

The geometry did not change; only the description changed. The new description matches the radial symmetry of the region.

This principle appears throughout applied mathematics:

- circular regions are easier in polar coordinates;
- ellipses often become circles after a scaling transformation;
- correlated Gaussian densities become independent Gaussian densities after a linear transformation;
- complicated constraints may become rectangles in new variables;
- latent-variable models often use a transformation from simple variables to observed variables.

A good coordinate system reduces complexity.

## 15.2 Polar coordinates

In polar coordinates, a point in the plane is described by two numbers:

- $r$: the distance from the origin;
- $\theta$: the angle from the positive $x$-axis.

The conversion formulas are

$$
x = r\cos\theta,
\qquad
 y = r\sin\theta,
$$

and

$$
r^2 = x^2+y^2,
\qquad
\tan\theta = \frac{y}{x}
$$

when $x\ne 0$, with the quadrant determining the correct angle.

### Nonuniqueness of polar coordinates

A rectangular coordinate pair $(x,y)$ is unique. Polar coordinates are not unique.

For example, the point $(1,1)$ has polar coordinates

$$
\left(\sqrt{2},\frac{\pi}{4}\right),
\qquad
\left(\sqrt{2},\frac{\pi}{4}+2\pi\right),
\qquad
\left(\sqrt{2},\frac{\pi}{4}+4\pi\right),
$$

and many more. The origin has $r=0$ and any value of $\theta$.

For double integrals, we usually take $r\ge 0$ and choose an interval of angles that covers the desired region exactly once.

::: {.callout-note}
## Practical convention

When setting up polar integrals, choose bounds so that almost every point in the region is counted exactly once. Boundaries may overlap, but overlapping boundaries have area zero and do not change the value of the double integral.
:::

## 15.3 Common polar regions

Polar coordinates are most useful when the region is described naturally by distance from the origin and angle.

### Disk centered at the origin

The disk $x^2+y^2\le a^2$ becomes

$$
0\le r\le a,
\qquad 0\le \theta\le 2\pi.
$$

### Annulus centered at the origin

The annulus $a^2\le x^2+y^2\le b^2$ becomes

$$
a\le r\le b,
\qquad 0\le \theta\le 2\pi.
$$

### Sector

A circular sector is described by

$$
0\le r\le a,
\qquad \alpha\le \theta\le \beta.
$$

### Upper half-disk

The upper half of the disk $x^2+y^2\le a^2$ is

$$
0\le r\le a,
\qquad 0\le \theta\le \pi.
$$

### Shifted circle

The circle

$$
(x-a)^2+y^2=a^2
$$

passes through the origin and has center $(a,0)$. Expanding gives

$$
x^2+y^2 = 2ax.
$$

In polar coordinates this becomes

$$
r^2 = 2ar\cos\theta,
$$

so for $r\ge 0$ the boundary is

$$
r=2a\cos\theta.
$$

The interior is usually described by

$$
-\frac{\pi}{2}\le \theta\le \frac{\pi}{2},
\qquad
0\le r\le 2a\cos\theta.
$$

## 15.4 The polar area element

The most important formula in polar integration is

$$
dA = r\,dr\,d\theta.
$$

This factor $r$ is not optional. It records the fact that a small angular change $d\theta$ sweeps out a longer arc when the point is farther from the origin.

Consider a small polar rectangle:

$$
r\le \rho\le r+dr,
\qquad
\theta\le \phi\le \theta+d\theta.
$$

Its radial thickness is approximately $dr$. Its angular width is approximately arc length $r\,d\theta$. Therefore its area is approximately

$$
(r\,d\theta)(dr)=r\,dr\,d\theta.
$$

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch15-polar-area-element

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

r0 = 1.5

th0 = np.pi/5
dth = np.pi/9

x_outer = (r0+dr)*np.cos(th)
y_outer = (r0+dr)*np.sin(th)
x_inner = r0*np.cos(th[::-1])
y_inner = r0*np.sin(th[::-1])
poly_x = np.r_[x_outer, x_inner]
poly_y = np.r_[y_outer, y_inner]

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(6.5, 5.5))
for rad in [0.75, 1.15, 1.5, 1.85, 2.25]:
```

## 15.5 Double integrals in polar coordinates

Suppose a region $D$ is described in polar form as

$$
\alpha\le \theta\le \beta,
\qquad
h_1(\theta)\le r\le h_2(\theta).
$$

Then

$$
\iint_D f(x,y)\,dA
=
\int_\alpha^\beta\int_{h_1(\theta)}^{h_2(\theta)}
f(r\cos\theta,r\sin\theta)\,r\,dr\,d\theta.
$$

The process has three steps:

1. rewrite the region in polar bounds;
2. substitute $x=r\cos\theta$ and $y=r\sin\theta$ into the integrand;
3. replace $dA$ by $r\,dr\,d\theta$.

### Worked example 1: Area of a disk

Find the area of the disk $D:x^2+y^2\le a^2$.

Since $D$ is $0\le r\le a$, $0\le \theta\le 2\pi$,

$$
\operatorname{Area}(D)
=
\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^a r\,dr\,d\theta
=
\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{a^2}{2}\,d\theta
=
\pi a^2.
$$

This is the usual area formula, now derived as a double integral.

### Worked example 2: Converting a rectangular-coordinate integral

Convert

$$
\int_{-1}^{1}\int_0^{\sqrt{1-x^2}} 4\,dy\,dx
$$

into polar coordinates and evaluate it.

The region is the upper half of the unit disk:

$$
x^2+y^2\le 1,
\qquad y\ge 0.
$$

In polar coordinates,

$$
0\le r\le 1,
\qquad 0\le \theta\le \pi.
$$

Therefore

$$
\int_{-1}^{1}\int_0^{\sqrt{1-x^2}} 4\,dy\,dx
=
\int_0^\pi\int_0^1 4r\,dr\,d\theta
=2\pi.
$$

### Worked example 3: An annular integral

Evaluate

$$
\iint_D \cos(x^2+y^2)\,dA,
$$

where $D$ is the region between $r=3$ and $r=4$ in the upper half-plane.

The bounds are

$$
3\le r\le 4,
\qquad
0\le \theta\le \pi.
$$

Since $x^2+y^2=r^2$ and $dA=r\,dr\,d\theta$,

$$
\iint_D \cos(x^2+y^2)\,dA
=
\int_0^\pi\int_3^4 \cos(r^2)r\,dr\,d\theta.
$$

Let $u=r^2$, so $du=2r\,dr$. Then

$$
\int_3^4 \cos(r^2)r\,dr
=
\frac12\int_9^{16}\cos u\,du
=
\frac12(\sin 16-\sin 9).
$$

Thus

$$
\iint_D \cos(x^2+y^2)\,dA
=
\frac{\pi}{2}(\sin 16-\sin 9).
$$

This example shows a typical reason for using polar coordinates: the expression $x^2+y^2$ becomes the simpler expression $r^2$.

## 15.6 Volume using polar coordinates

If a solid lies under $z=f(x,y)$ and above a region $D$, then its volume is

$$
V=\iint_D f(x,y)\,dA.
$$

When $D$ is circular or the function depends on $x^2+y^2$, polar coordinates often simplify the integral.

### Worked example 4: Paraboloid over a quadrant

Find the volume under

$$
z=6-2x^2-2y^2
$$

above the first-quadrant part of the region where $z\ge 0$.

The condition $z\ge 0$ gives

$$
6-2x^2-2y^2\ge 0,
$$

so

$$
x^2+y^2\le 3.
$$

In the first quadrant,

$$
0\le \theta\le \frac{\pi}{2},
\qquad
0\le r\le \sqrt{3}.
$$

Therefore

$$
V=
\int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^{\sqrt{3}} (6-2r^2)r\,dr\,d\theta.
$$

Compute the inner integral:

$$
\int_0^{\sqrt{3}} (6r-2r^3)\,dr
=
\left[3r^2-\frac12r^4\right]_0^{\sqrt{3}}
=9-\frac92
=\frac92.
$$

Hence

$$
V=\frac{\pi}{2}\cdot \frac92
=\frac{9\pi}{4}.
$$

## 15.7 Shifted circular regions

Polar coordinates are not only for circles centered at the origin. They also work well for some shifted circles, especially when the circle passes through the origin.

Consider

$$
D=\{(x,y):(x-2)^2+y^2\le 4\}.
$$

Expanding gives

$$
x^2-4x+4+y^2\le 4,
$$

so

$$
x^2+y^2\le 4x.
$$

In polar coordinates,

$$
r^2\le 4r\cos\theta.
$$

For $r\ge 0$, this becomes

$$
0\le r\le 4\cos\theta.
$$

We need $4\cos\theta\ge 0$, so one convenient angular interval is

$$
-\frac{\pi}{2}\le \theta\le \frac{\pi}{2}.
$$

Thus

$$
D=\left\{(r,\theta):-\frac{\pi}{2}\le \theta\le \frac{\pi}{2},\ 0\le r\le 4\cos\theta\right\}.
$$

The area is

$$
\int_{-\pi/2}^{\pi/2}\int_0^{4\cos\theta} r\,dr\,d\theta
=
\int_{-\pi/2}^{\pi/2} 8\cos^2\theta\,d\theta
=4\pi.
$$

This agrees with the area of a circle of radius $2$.

## 15.8 Strategy for choosing polar coordinates

Polar coordinates are often a good choice when at least one of the following appears:

1. The region involves $x^2+y^2$.
2. The boundary is a circle, disk, annulus, sector, or ray from the origin.
3. The integrand contains $x^2+y^2$, $\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$, or radial symmetry.
4. A rectangular-coordinate integral contains square roots such as $\sqrt{a^2-x^2}$.
5. The geometry is best described by distance and angle.

Polar coordinates may not help if the region is rectangular and the integrand has no radial structure.

### A comparison example

The integral

$$
\int_{-1}^{1}\int_{-\sqrt{1-x^2}}^{\sqrt{1-x^2}} e^{-(x^2+y^2)}\,dy\,dx
$$

is hard in rectangular coordinates because the inner integral has no elementary antiderivative. In polar coordinates it becomes

$$
\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 e^{-r^2}r\,dr\,d\theta,
$$

which is elementary:

$$
2\pi\left[-\frac12e^{-r^2}\right]_0^1
=\pi(1-e^{-1}).
$$

## 15.9 General change of variables

Polar coordinates are one example of a broader idea.

Let a transformation $T$ map a point $(u,v)$ in a new coordinate plane to a point $(x,y)$ in the original plane:

$$
T(u,v)=(x(u,v),y(u,v)).
$$

The Jacobian determinant of the transformation is

$$
\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}
=
\begin{vmatrix}
\frac{\partial x}{\partial u} & \frac{\partial x}{\partial v} \\
\frac{\partial y}{\partial u} & \frac{\partial y}{\partial v}
\end{vmatrix}
=
\frac{\partial x}{\partial u}\frac{\partial y}{\partial v}
-
\frac{\partial x}{\partial v}\frac{\partial y}{\partial u}.
$$

The absolute value of this determinant measures local area scaling.

### Change-of-variables formula

If $T$ maps a region $S$ in the $uv$-plane to a region $D$ in the $xy$-plane, and if the transformation behaves well on the region, then

$$
\iint_D f(x,y)\,dA
=
\iint_S f(x(u,v),y(u,v))
\left|\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right|\,du\,dv.
$$

This formula has the same structure as polar integration:

$$
\text{integrand after substitution}\times \text{area-scaling factor}.
$$

### Polar coordinates as a Jacobian example

For polar coordinates,

$$
x=r\cos\theta,
\qquad
 y=r\sin\theta.
$$

The Jacobian is

$$
\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(r,\theta)}
=
\begin{vmatrix}
\cos\theta & -r\sin\theta \\
\sin\theta & r\cos\theta
\end{vmatrix}
=r\cos^2\theta+r\sin^2\theta
=r.
$$

Therefore

$$
dA=r\,dr\,d\theta.
$$

The familiar polar formula is just the general change-of-variables formula applied to the polar map.

## 15.10 Linear transformations and area scaling

A linear transformation

$$
\begin{pmatrix}x\\y\end{pmatrix}
=
A\begin{pmatrix}u\\v\end{pmatrix}
$$

scales every area by the constant factor $|\det A|$.

For example, let

$$
x=2u+v,
\qquad
 y=u+3v.
$$

Then

$$
A=\begin{pmatrix}2&1\\1&3\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
\det A=5.
$$

So any region in the $uv$-plane has image area five times as large in the $xy$-plane.

### Worked example 5: Area of a parallelogram

Let $S=[0,1]\times[0,1]$ and define

$$
x=2u+v,
\qquad
 y=u+3v.
$$

The image $D=T(S)$ is a parallelogram. Its area is

$$
\operatorname{Area}(D)
=
\iint_D 1\,dA
=
\iint_S 1\cdot |5|\,du\,dv
=5.
$$

This agrees with the determinant interpretation: the unit square is stretched into a parallelogram with area $5$.

## 15.11 Nonlinear transformations

For nonlinear transformations, the Jacobian usually varies from point to point. The transformation may stretch one part of the region more than another.

For example,

$$
x=u,
\qquad
 y=v+0.4u^2.
$$

The Jacobian is

$$
\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}
=
\begin{vmatrix}
1&0\\0.8u&1
\end{vmatrix}
=1.
$$

Although this transformation bends horizontal grid lines into parabolas, it preserves area locally because the Jacobian determinant is $1$.

### Worked example 6: A triangular transformation

Let

$$
x=u,
\qquad
 y=uv,
$$

where

$$
1\le u\le 2,
\qquad 0\le v\le 1.
$$

The Jacobian is

$$
\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}
=
\begin{vmatrix}
1&0\\v&u
\end{vmatrix}
=u.
$$

The image region satisfies

$$
1\le x\le 2,
\qquad
0\le y\le x.
$$

If we want to evaluate

$$
\iint_D (x+y)\,dA,
$$

then

$$
\iint_D (x+y)\,dA
=
\int_1^2\int_0^1 (u+uv)u\,dv\,du.
$$

Simplifying,

$$
\int_1^2\int_0^1 u^2(1+v)\,dv\,du
=
\int_1^2 \frac32u^2\,du
=\frac32\cdot\frac{7}{3}
=\frac72.
$$

## 15.12 Change of variables in probability

Change of variables is essential in probability and statistics.

If $(X,Y)$ has joint density $f_{X,Y}(x,y)$ and

$$
(X,Y)=T(U,V),
$$

then the density of $(U,V)$ involves the Jacobian factor. Informally,

$$
\text{probability mass} = \text{density}\times \text{area}.
$$

When area changes, density must change in the opposite way so that probability is preserved.

### Radial probability

Suppose a density depends only on radius:

$$
f(x,y)=c e^{-(x^2+y^2)}.
$$

To find the normalizing constant $c$, require

$$
1=\iint_{\mathbb R^2} c e^{-(x^2+y^2)}\,dA.
$$

Using polar coordinates,

$$
1=c\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^\infty e^{-r^2}r\,dr\,d\theta
=c(2\pi)\left(\frac12\right)
=c\pi.
$$

So

$$
c=\frac1\pi.
$$

This is a two-dimensional radial density. Similar ideas lead to the normalization constants for multivariate normal distributions.

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch15-radial-density-simulation
#| fig-cap: 'Samples from a radial distribution. Polar coordinates separate radius and angle, which is useful for simulation and probability calculations.'
#| fig-width: 6.5
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(15)
n = 4000
# For density (1/pi) exp(-r^2), the variable R^2 is exponential with mean 1.
r = np.sqrt(rng.exponential(scale=1.0, size=n))
th = rng.uniform(0, 2*np.pi, size=n)
x = r*np.cos(th)
y = r*np.sin(th)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(6.5, 5.5))
ax.scatter(x, y, s=4, alpha=0.25)
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
ax.set_xlabel('$x$')
ax.set_ylabel('$y$')
ax.set_title('Random points from a radial density')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)
plt.show()
```

## 15.13 Computational viewpoint

In computation, a change of variables is more than a symbolic trick. It is a method for transforming a difficult numerical problem into an easier one.

### Monte Carlo in a disk

To approximate

$$
\iint_{x^2+y^2\le 1} e^{-(x^2+y^2)}\,dA,
$$

we can sample uniformly from the disk. A correct polar simulation must not sample $r$ uniformly on $[0,1]$. Since area in polar coordinates is $r\,dr\,d\theta$, the radius distribution must put more points near the outer part of the disk.

A uniform point in the unit disk can be generated by

$$
\Theta\sim \operatorname{Uniform}(0,2\pi),
\qquad
R=\sqrt{U},
$$

where $U\sim \operatorname{Uniform}(0,1)$.

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch15-uniform-disk-sampling
#| fig-cap: 'Uniform sampling in a disk requires R = sqrt(U), not R = U. The Jacobian factor r explains the difference.'
#| fig-width: 7
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(151)
n = 3000
th = rng.uniform(0, 2*np.pi, size=n)

r_wrong = rng.uniform(0, 1, size=n)
x_wrong = r_wrong*np.cos(th)
y_wrong = r_wrong*np.sin(th)

r_right = np.sqrt(rng.uniform(0, 1, size=n))
x_right = r_right*np.cos(th)
y_right = r_right*np.sin(th)

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(7, 3.8))
axes[0].scatter(x_wrong, y_wrong, s=4, alpha=0.25)
axes[0].set_title('Incorrect: $R=U$')
axes[1].scatter(x_right, y_right, s=4, alpha=0.25)
axes[1].set_title(r'Correct: $R=\sqrt{U}$')

for ax in axes:
    ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
    ax.set_xlim(-1.05, 1.05)
    ax.set_ylim(-1.05, 1.05)
    ax.grid(True, alpha=0.2)

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()
```

The exact value is

$$
\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 e^{-r^2}r\,dr\,d\theta
=\pi(1-e^{-1}).
$$

The simulation estimate is close to that value.

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch15-monte-carlo-disk-integral
#| fig-cap: 'Monte Carlo estimates of a disk integral approach the exact polar-coordinate value as the sample size increases.'
#| fig-width: 6.5
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(152)
exact = np.pi*(1 - np.exp(-1))
sizes = np.array([100, 300, 1000, 3000, 10000, 30000])
estimates = []

for n in sizes:
    th = rng.uniform(0, 2*np.pi, size=n)
    r = np.sqrt(rng.uniform(0, 1, size=n))
    values = np.exp(-r**2)
    estimates.append(np.pi*values.mean())  # area of unit disk times average value

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(6.5, 4.8))
ax.plot(sizes, estimates, marker='o', label='Monte Carlo estimate')
ax.axhline(exact, linestyle='--', label='exact value')
ax.set_xscale('log')
ax.set_xlabel('sample size')
ax.set_ylabel('integral estimate')
ax.set_title('Monte Carlo integration over the unit disk')
ax.legend()
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)
plt.show()
```

## 15.14 Applications in data, statistics, and AI

### Radial basis functions

Many machine-learning models use radial basis functions such as

$$
\phi(x,y)=e^{-\gamma(x^2+y^2)}.
$$

These functions are constant on circles centered at the origin. Polar coordinates reveal their radial structure immediately.

### Gaussian models

The bivariate normal density has elliptical level curves. After shifting, scaling, and rotating coordinates, those ellipses become circles. This is a change-of-variables idea. The determinant of the covariance matrix controls how volume changes.

### Normalizing flows

In modern generative modeling, a normalizing flow transforms a simple latent variable $z$ into a more complicated observed variable $x=T(z)$. The probability density changes by a Jacobian determinant. In two dimensions the formula is the same idea as this chapter; in high dimensions it becomes

$$
\text{density correction}=\left|\det J_T\right|^{-1}.
$$

### Numerical integration

Monte Carlo methods, importance sampling, and coordinate transformations all rely on understanding how area or volume changes under a transformation. A poor coordinate system can make an integral numerically unstable; a good one can make the same problem nearly one-dimensional.

## 15.15 Chapter summary

The key formulas are:

$$
x=r\cos\theta,
\qquad
 y=r\sin\theta,
\qquad
r^2=x^2+y^2.
$$

The polar area element is

$$
dA=r\,dr\,d\theta.
$$

Thus, for a polar region

$$
D=\{(r,\theta):\alpha\le \theta\le \beta,\\ h_1(\theta)\le r\le h_2(\theta)\},
$$

we have

$$
\iint_D f(x,y)\,dA
=
\int_\alpha^\beta\int_{h_1(\theta)}^{h_2(\theta)} f(r\cos\theta,r\sin\theta)r\,dr\,d\theta.
$$

For a general transformation

$$
T(u,v)=(x(u,v),y(u,v)),
$$

the Jacobian determinant is

$$
\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}
=
\begin{vmatrix}
x_u & x_v \\
y_u & y_v
\end{vmatrix},
$$

and the change-of-variables formula is

$$
\iint_D f(x,y)\,dA
=
\iint_S f(x(u,v),y(u,v))
\left|\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right|\,du\,dv.
$$

The absolute value of the Jacobian determinant is the local area-scaling factor.
