16  Chapter 16. Triple Integrals and Geometry in \(\mathbb R^3\)

Volume, mass, probability, and continuous sums over solid regions

A double integral sums over a two-dimensional region. A triple integral sums over a three-dimensional solid. The change sounds small: replace tiny rectangles by tiny boxes. Conceptually, however, this is a major step. Triple integrals allow us to compute volume, mass, charge, probability, average value, and spatial accumulation over objects in space.

The central geometric skill in this chapter is describing a solid region by inequalities. Once the region is described correctly, the integral is often straightforward. Most errors in triple integrals are not integration errors; they are errors in reading the geometry.

This chapter develops triple integrals in rectangular coordinates. Cylindrical and spherical coordinates are treated in the next chapter. For now, we focus on the most important idea:

A triple integral is a continuous sum over a solid region.

16.1 Learning goals

After studying this chapter, you should be able to:

  1. Interpret \(\iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV\) as a continuous sum over a solid region \(E\).
  2. Compute volume as \(\iiint_E 1\,dV\).
  3. Set up triple integrals using projections onto coordinate planes.
  4. Describe solids with bounds such as \(g_1(x,y) \le z \le g_2(x,y)\).
  5. Change the order of integration in simple three-dimensional regions.
  6. Compute mass, average value, and probability using triple integrals.
  7. Use Python to visualize regions and approximate triple integrals numerically.

16.2 16.1 From double integrals to triple integrals

Recall the structure of a double integral over a region \(R \subset \mathbb R^2\):

\[ \iint_R f(x,y)\,dA. \]

It is the limit of sums of the form

\[ \sum f(x_{ij}^*,y_{ij}^*)\Delta A. \]

For a triple integral over a solid region \(E \subset \mathbb R^3\), we replace area pieces by volume pieces:

\[ \iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV = \lim \sum f(x_{ijk}^*,y_{ijk}^*,z_{ijk}^*)\Delta V. \]

In rectangular coordinates,

\[ \Delta V = \Delta x\,\Delta y\,\Delta z, \qquad dV = dx\,dy\,dz. \]

The order of the differentials depends on the order in which we integrate. For example,

\[ \int_a^b\int_c^d\int_p^q f(x,y,z)\,dz\,dy\,dx \]

means that we integrate first with respect to \(z\), then \(y\), then \(x\).

NoteThe guiding picture

A double integral sums tiny columns above an area region. A triple integral sums tiny boxes inside a solid region.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

16.3 16.2 Volume as a triple integral

The simplest triple integral occurs when the integrand is \(1\).

ImportantVolume of a solid

If \(E \subset \mathbb R^3\) is a solid region, then

\[ \operatorname{Vol}(E)=\iiint_E 1\,dV. \]

This formula says that volume is the sum of all infinitesimal volume elements \(dV\) inside the region. It is the three-dimensional analog of

\[ \operatorname{Area}(R)=\iint_R 1\,dA. \]

16.3.1 Example 16.1: A rectangular box

Let

\[ E=[0,2]\times[1,4]\times[-1,3]. \]

Then

\[ \operatorname{Vol}(E) =\int_0^2\int_1^4\int_{-1}^3 1\,dz\,dy\,dx. \]

Evaluate from inside to outside:

\[ \int_{-1}^3 1\,dz=4, \]

so

\[ \operatorname{Vol}(E) =\int_0^2\int_1^4 4\,dy\,dx =\int_0^2 12\,dx =24. \]

Of course, the box has side lengths \(2\), \(3\), and \(4\), so the volume is \(2\cdot 3\cdot 4=24\).

16.4 16.3 Solids between two surfaces

A very common solid has the form

\[ E=\{(x,y,z):(x,y)\in D,\ g_1(x,y)\le z\le g_2(x,y)\}. \]

Here \(D\) is the projection of \(E\) onto the \(xy\)-plane. The solid lies above the lower surface

\[ z=g_1(x,y) \]

and below the upper surface

\[ z=g_2(x,y). \]

Then

\[ \iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV =\iint_D\left[\int_{g_1(x,y)}^{g_2(x,y)} f(x,y,z)\,dz\right]dA. \]

If \(D\) is a Type I region,

\[ D=\{(x,y):a\le x\le b,\ h_1(x)\le y\le h_2(x)\}, \]

then

\[ \iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV =\int_a^b\int_{h_1(x)}^{h_2(x)}\int_{g_1(x,y)}^{g_2(x,y)} f(x,y,z)\,dz\,dy\,dx. \]

TipProjection-first method

To set up a triple integral, first choose a projection plane. Then write the outer two bounds for the projection and the inner bounds for the variable perpendicular to that projection.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x = np.linspace(-1.2, 1.2, 80)

16.4.1 Example 16.2: Volume under a plane over a triangle

Let \(E\) be the tetrahedron in the first octant bounded by the coordinate planes and

\[ x+y+z=1. \]

Because the coordinate planes are \(x=0\), \(y=0\), and \(z=0\), the region is

\[ E=\{(x,y,z):x\ge 0,\ y\ge 0,\ z\ge 0,\ x+y+z\le 1\}. \]

Project onto the \(xy\)-plane. Since \(z\) ranges from \(0\) to \(1-x-y\), we need

\[ 1-x-y\ge 0, \]

so the projection is the triangle

\[ D=\{(x,y):0\le x\le 1,\ 0\le y\le 1-x\}. \]

Thus

\[ \operatorname{Vol}(E) =\int_0^1\int_0^{1-x}\int_0^{1-x-y}1\,dz\,dy\,dx. \]

Evaluate:

\[ \operatorname{Vol}(E) =\int_0^1\int_0^{1-x}(1-x-y)\,dy\,dx. \]

The inner integral is

\[ \int_0^{1-x}(1-x-y)\,dy =\left[(1-x)y-\frac{y^2}{2}\right]_0^{1-x} =\frac{(1-x)^2}{2}. \]

Therefore

\[ \operatorname{Vol}(E) =\int_0^1\frac{(1-x)^2}{2}\,dx =\frac16. \]

This matches the formula for a tetrahedron with intercepts \(1\), \(1\), and \(1\).

16.5 16.4 Integrating functions over solids

Volume is only one example. If \(f(x,y,z)\) measures a density, intensity, probability density, energy density, or cost density, then

\[ \iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV \]

sums that quantity over the whole solid.

16.5.1 Example 16.3: A triple integral over the standard tetrahedron

Evaluate

\[ \iiint_E y\,dV, \]

where

\[ E=\{(x,y,z):x\ge 0,\ y\ge 0,\ z\ge 0,\ x+y+z\le 1\}. \]

Using the same bounds as before,

\[ \iiint_E y\,dV =\int_0^1\int_0^{1-x}\int_0^{1-x-y} y\,dz\,dy\,dx. \]

Since \(y\) does not depend on \(z\),

\[ \int_0^{1-x-y}y\,dz=y(1-x-y). \]

Thus

\[ \iiint_E y\,dV =\int_0^1\int_0^{1-x} y(1-x-y)\,dy\,dx. \]

For fixed \(x\),

\[ \int_0^{1-x} y(1-x-y)\,dy =\int_0^{1-x}((1-x)y-y^2)\,dy =\frac{(1-x)^3}{6}. \]

Therefore

\[ \iiint_E y\,dV =\int_0^1\frac{(1-x)^3}{6}\,dx =\frac1{24}. \]

Because of symmetry,

\[ \iiint_E x\,dV=\iiint_E y\,dV=\iiint_E z\,dV=\frac1{24}. \]

16.6 16.5 Mass and density in three dimensions

Suppose an object occupies a solid region \(E\) and has density

\[ \rho=\rho(x,y,z). \]

The mass of a small box is approximately

\[ \Delta m \approx \rho(x_{ijk}^*,y_{ijk}^*,z_{ijk}^*)\Delta V. \]

Summing and passing to a limit gives the mass formula.

ImportantMass from density

If an object occupies \(E\subset\mathbb R^3\) and has density \(\rho(x,y,z)\), then

\[ M=\iiint_E \rho(x,y,z)\,dV. \]

16.6.1 Example 16.4: Density increasing with height

Let \(E\) be the box

\[ 0\le x\le 2, \qquad 0\le y\le 3, \qquad 0\le z\le 1, \]

and suppose the density is

\[ \rho(x,y,z)=1+z. \]

Then

\[ M=\int_0^2\int_0^3\int_0^1(1+z)\,dz\,dy\,dx. \]

First,

\[ \int_0^1(1+z)\,dz=\frac32. \]

Thus

\[ M=\int_0^2\int_0^3\frac32\,dy\,dx =\int_0^2\frac92\,dx =9. \]

If the density were constant \(1\), the mass would be the volume \(6\). Since the density increases with height, the mass is larger.

16.7 16.6 Average value over a solid

For a function \(f(x,y,z)\) on a solid \(E\), its average value over \(E\) is

\[ f_{\operatorname{avg}} =\frac{1}{\operatorname{Vol}(E)}\iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV. \]

This is the three-dimensional version of the average value of a function over an interval or a plane region.

16.7.1 Example 16.5: Average height in a tetrahedron

For the standard tetrahedron

\[ E=\{(x,y,z):x,y,z\ge 0,\ x+y+z\le 1\}, \]

the average value of \(z\) is

\[ z_{\operatorname{avg}} =\frac{1}{\operatorname{Vol}(E)}\iiint_E z\,dV. \]

By symmetry, \(\iiint_E z\,dV=1/24\) and \(\operatorname{Vol}(E)=1/6\). Therefore

\[ z_{\operatorname{avg}}=\frac{1/24}{1/6}=\frac14. \]

The centroid of the tetrahedron is

\[ \left(\frac14,\frac14,\frac14\right). \]

This example previews the role of triple integrals in computing centers of mass.

16.8 16.7 Other projection directions

Projection onto the \(xy\)-plane is common, but not always best. A solid may also be described by projecting onto the \(yz\)-plane or the \(xz\)-plane.

16.8.1 Projection onto the \(yz\)-plane

If

\[ E=\{(x,y,z):(y,z)\in D,\ g_1(y,z)\le x\le g_2(y,z)\}, \]

then

\[ \iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV =\iint_D\left[\int_{g_1(y,z)}^{g_2(y,z)} f(x,y,z)\,dx\right]dA. \]

16.8.2 Projection onto the \(xz\)-plane

If

\[ E=\{(x,y,z):(x,z)\in D,\ g_1(x,z)\le y\le g_2(x,z)\}, \]

then

\[ \iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV =\iint_D\left[\int_{g_1(x,z)}^{g_2(x,z)} f(x,y,z)\,dy\right]dA. \]

WarningDo not choose the projection too early

A difficult integral often becomes easier if you project in a different direction. Before writing bounds, sketch the solid and ask: which variable naturally runs between two simple surfaces?

16.9 16.8 Changing the order of integration

In double integrals, changing order means rewriting a region in the plane. In triple integrals, changing order means rewriting a solid in space. There are six possible orders:

\[ dz\,dy\,dx, \qquad dz\,dx\,dy, \qquad dy\,dz\,dx, \qquad dy\,dx\,dz, \qquad dx\,dz\,dy, \qquad dx\,dy\,dz. \]

The main principle is simple:

The innermost variable should have bounds that describe a line segment through the solid parallel to that coordinate axis.

16.9.1 Example 16.6: The tetrahedron in another order

For the standard tetrahedron

\[ E=\{(x,y,z):x,y,z\ge 0,\ x+y+z\le 1\}, \]

we wrote

\[ \iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV =\int_0^1\int_0^{1-x}\int_0^{1-x-y} f(x,y,z)\,dz\,dy\,dx. \]

Now write the order \(dx\,dz\,dy\). For fixed \(y\) and \(z\),

\[ 0\le x\le 1-y-z. \]

The projection onto the \(yz\)-plane is

\[ 0\le y\le 1, \qquad 0\le z\le 1-y. \]

Therefore

\[ \iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV =\int_0^1\int_0^{1-y}\int_0^{1-y-z} f(x,y,z)\,dx\,dz\,dy. \]

Because the tetrahedron is symmetric, all six orders have similar bounds.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

fig = plt.figure(figsize=(7, 5))
ax = fig.add_subplot(111, projection='3d')

for z0 in [0.0, 0.2, 0.4, 0.6, 0.8]:
    a = 1 - z0
    triangle = np.array([[0, 0, z0], [a, 0, z0], [0, a, z0], [0, 0, z0]])
    ax.plot(triangle[:,0], triangle[:,1], triangle[:,2], linewidth=2)

    xs = [p[0], 0]
    ys = [p[1], 0]
    zs = [p[2], 1]
    ax.plot(xs, ys, zs, linestyle='--')

ax.set_xlim(0, 1)
ax.set_ylim(0, 1)
ax.set_zlim(0, 1)
ax.set_xlabel('x')
ax.set_ylabel('y')
ax.set_zlabel('z')
ax.set_title('Slicing by planes z = constant')
ax.view_init(elev=24, azim=-55)
plt.show()

16.10 16.9 Triple integrals and probability

A joint probability density for three continuous random variables \(X,Y,Z\) is a nonnegative function \(p(x,y,z)\) such that

\[ \iiint_{\mathbb R^3} p(x,y,z)\,dV=1. \]

For a solid event \(E\subset \mathbb R^3\),

\[ P((X,Y,Z)\in E)=\iiint_E p(x,y,z)\,dV. \]

This interpretation is essential in statistics and machine learning. Many models use high-dimensional integrals, but the geometric meaning begins here: probability is accumulated density over a region.

16.10.1 Example 16.7: Uniform distribution in a box

Suppose \((X,Y,Z)\) is uniformly distributed in the box

\[ 0\le x\le 2, \qquad 0\le y\le 1, \qquad 0\le z\le 3. \]

The volume is \(6\), so the density is

\[ p(x,y,z)=\frac16 \]

inside the box and \(0\) outside. The probability of the sub-box

\[ 0\le x\le 1, \qquad 0\le y\le 1, \qquad 1\le z\le 3 \]

is

\[ \int_0^1\int_0^1\int_1^3\frac16\,dz\,dy\,dx =\frac{2}{6} =\frac13. \]

16.11 16.10 Numerical triple integration

In practice, many triple integrals cannot be evaluated exactly. Numerical integration is then necessary.

For a rectangular box

\[ [a,b]\times[c,d]\times[p,q], \]

a midpoint rule approximation is

\[ \iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV \approx \sum_{i=1}^m\sum_{j=1}^n\sum_{k=1}^\ell f(\bar x_i,\bar y_j,\bar z_k)\Delta x\Delta y\Delta z. \]

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def midpoint_triple(N):
    # Integral of f=x+y+z over [0,1]^3 equals 3/2.
    xs = (np.arange(N) + 0.5) / N
    ys = (np.arange(N) + 0.5) / N
    zs = (np.arange(N) + 0.5) / N
    X, Y, Z = np.meshgrid(xs, ys, zs, indexing='ij')
    values = X + Y + Z
    return values.mean()  # volume is 1

Ns = np.arange(2, 31)
approx = np.array([midpoint_triple(N) for N in Ns])
exact = 1.5

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 4))
ax.plot(Ns, approx, marker='o', label='midpoint approximation')
ax.axhline(exact, linestyle='--', label='exact value')
ax.set_xlabel('N subdivisions in each direction')
ax.set_ylabel('integral estimate')
ax.set_title('Triple midpoint rule on the unit cube')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
ax.legend()
plt.show()

Because \(f(x,y,z)=x+y+z\) is linear, the midpoint rule is exact for every \(N\) in this example. For nonlinear functions, the approximations generally improve as the grid is refined.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def midpoint_nonlinear(N):
    xs = (np.arange(N) + 0.5) / N
    ys = (np.arange(N) + 0.5) / N
    zs = (np.arange(N) + 0.5) / N
    X, Y, Z = np.meshgrid(xs, ys, zs, indexing='ij')
    values = np.exp(-(X**2 + Y**2 + Z**2))
    return values.mean()

Ns = np.arange(3, 51)
approx = np.array([midpoint_nonlinear(N) for N in Ns])
reference = midpoint_nonlinear(180)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 4))
ax.plot(Ns, approx, marker='o', markersize=3, label='N-grid midpoint')
ax.axhline(reference, linestyle='--', label='fine-grid reference')
ax.set_xlabel('N subdivisions in each direction')
ax.set_ylabel('estimate')
ax.set_title(r'Estimate of $\int_0^1\int_0^1\int_0^1 e^{-(x^2+y^2+z^2)}\,dV$')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
ax.legend()
plt.show()

16.12 16.11 Monte Carlo integration in three dimensions

Monte Carlo integration estimates an integral by random sampling. Suppose \(E\) is a box of volume \(V\) and we want

\[ I=\iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV. \]

If \(P_1,\dots,P_N\) are uniformly sampled from \(E\), then

\[ I\approx V\cdot \frac1N\sum_{i=1}^N f(P_i). \]

Monte Carlo methods become especially important in high-dimensional statistics, Bayesian inference, uncertainty quantification, and machine learning.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(16)
Ns = np.unique(np.logspace(2, 5, 45).astype(int))
estimates = []
for N in Ns:
    pts = rng.uniform(-1, 1, size=(N, 3))
    inside = np.sum(np.sum(pts**2, axis=1) <= 1)
    estimates.append(8 * inside / N)

exact = 4*np.pi/3
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 4))
ax.plot(Ns, estimates, marker='o', markersize=3, label='Monte Carlo estimate')
ax.axhline(exact, linestyle='--', label=r'exact $4\pi/3$')
ax.set_xscale('log')
ax.set_xlabel('number of random points')
ax.set_ylabel('estimated volume')
ax.set_title('Volume of the unit ball by random sampling')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
ax.legend()
plt.show()

16.13 16.12 Moments and centers of mass

Triple integrals also compute balance points. Suppose an object occupies \(E\) with density \(\rho(x,y,z)\) and mass

\[ M=\iiint_E \rho(x,y,z)\,dV. \]

The moments about the coordinate planes are

\[ M_{yz}=\iiint_E x\rho(x,y,z)\,dV, \]

\[ M_{xz}=\iiint_E y\rho(x,y,z)\,dV, \]

\[ M_{xy}=\iiint_E z\rho(x,y,z)\,dV. \]

The center of mass is

\[ (\bar x,\bar y,\bar z) = \left( \frac{M_{yz}}{M}, \frac{M_{xz}}{M}, \frac{M_{xy}}{M} \right). \]

If \(\rho\) is constant, the center of mass is the centroid of the region.

16.13.1 Example 16.8: Centroid of the standard tetrahedron

For the standard tetrahedron, by symmetry and the computation above,

\[ \bar x=\bar y=\bar z=\frac14. \]

Thus the centroid is

\[ \left(\frac14,\frac14,\frac14\right). \]

This point is closer to the origin than to the opposite face because the tetrahedron is widest near the coordinate planes.

16.14 16.13 Triple integrals in the age of data and AI

Triple integrals appear naturally in modern applied mathematics, statistics, and machine learning.

16.14.1 Spatial data and voxel models

Medical imaging, climate models, fluid simulations, and 3D computer vision often represent space using voxels. A voxel is a small three-dimensional box. A triple integral is the mathematical limit of summing quantities over voxels.

Examples:

  • total mass in a scanned object,
  • total heat in a region,
  • average intensity in a 3D image,
  • probability that a random spatial point lies in a target region.

16.14.2 Probability and Bayesian inference

For three parameters \(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3\), a posterior density \(p(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3\mid \text{data})\) satisfies

\[ \iiint p(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3\mid \text{data})\,d\theta_1\,d\theta_2\,d\theta_3=1. \]

A probability statement about the parameters is a triple integral over a region in parameter space.

16.14.3 Continuous loss and risk

In statistical learning, expected loss may be written as an integral over feature space. In three variables, a risk functional may look like

\[ R(w)=\iiint L(w;x,y,z)p(x,y,z)\,dV. \]

This is a triple integral of loss weighted by a probability density.

16.15 16.14 Common mistakes

  1. Forgetting the projection. Bounds such as \(0\le z\le g(x,y)\) are incomplete unless the allowed \((x,y)\) values are described.
  2. Using the wrong upper surface. Always check which surface is above on the projection region.
  3. Mixing the order of bounds and differentials. In \(dz\,dy\,dx\), the \(z\)-bounds come first, then \(y\)-bounds, then \(x\)-bounds.
  4. Treating dependent variables as constants incorrectly. When integrating with respect to \(z\), expressions involving \(x\) and \(y\) are constant, but the \(z\)-bounds may depend on \(x\) and \(y\).
  5. Assuming every solid is a box. Most real regions require variable bounds.
  6. Not checking units. If \(f\) has units of density, then \(f\,dV\) has units of mass.

16.16 16.15 Summary

A triple integral is a continuous sum over a solid region:

\[ \iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV. \]

The most important special cases are:

\[ \operatorname{Vol}(E)=\iiint_E 1\,dV, \]

\[ M=\iiint_E \rho(x,y,z)\,dV, \]

\[ f_{\operatorname{avg}}= rac{1}{\operatorname{Vol}(E)}\iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV. \]

The most common rectangular-coordinate setup is

\[ E=\{(x,y,z):(x,y)\in D,\ g_1(x,y)\le z\le g_2(x,y)\}, \]

which gives

\[ \iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV =\iint_D\left[\int_{g_1(x,y)}^{g_2(x,y)} f(x,y,z)\,dz\right]dA. \]

The key skill is not merely integration. The key skill is turning geometry into correct bounds.

A double integral sums over a two-dimensional region. A triple integral sums over a three-dimensional solid. The change sounds small: replace tiny rectangles by tiny boxes. Conceptually, however, this is a major step. Triple integrals allow us to compute volume, mass, charge, probability, average value, and spatial accumulation over objects in space.

The central geometric skill in this chapter is **describing a solid region by inequalities**. Once the region is described correctly, the integral is often straightforward. Most errors in triple integrals are not integration errors; they are errors in reading the geometry.

This chapter develops triple integrals in rectangular coordinates. Cylindrical and spherical coordinates are treated in the next chapter. For now, we focus on the most important idea:

> A triple integral is a continuous sum over a solid region.

## 16.1 From double integrals to triple integrals

Recall the structure of a double integral over a region $R \subset \mathbb R^2$:

$$
\iint_R f(x,y)\,dA.
$$

It is the limit of sums of the form

$$
\sum f(x_{ij}^*,y_{ij}^*)\Delta A.
$$

For a triple integral over a solid region $E \subset \mathbb R^3$, we replace area pieces by volume pieces:

$$
\iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV
  = \lim \sum f(x_{ijk}^*,y_{ijk}^*,z_{ijk}^*)\Delta V.
$$

In rectangular coordinates,

$$
\Delta V = \Delta x\,\Delta y\,\Delta z,
\qquad
 dV = dx\,dy\,dz.
$$

The order of the differentials depends on the order in which we integrate. For example,

$$
\int_a^b\int_c^d\int_p^q f(x,y,z)\,dz\,dy\,dx
$$

means that we integrate first with respect to $z$, then $y$, then $x$.

::: {.callout-note}
## The guiding picture
A double integral sums tiny columns above an area region. A triple integral sums tiny boxes inside a solid region.
:::

## 16.2 Volume as a triple integral

The simplest triple integral occurs when the integrand is $1$.

::: {.callout-important}
## Volume of a solid
If $E \subset \mathbb R^3$ is a solid region, then

$$
\operatorname{Vol}(E)=\iiint_E 1\,dV.
$$
:::

This formula says that volume is the sum of all infinitesimal volume elements $dV$ inside the region. It is the three-dimensional analog of

$$
\operatorname{Area}(R)=\iint_R 1\,dA.
$$

### Example 16.1: A rectangular box

Let

$$
E=[0,2]\times[1,4]\times[-1,3].
$$

Then

$$
\operatorname{Vol}(E)
=\int_0^2\int_1^4\int_{-1}^3 1\,dz\,dy\,dx.
$$

Evaluate from inside to outside:

$$
\int_{-1}^3 1\,dz=4,
$$

so

$$
\operatorname{Vol}(E)
=\int_0^2\int_1^4 4\,dy\,dx
=\int_0^2 12\,dx
=24.
$$

Of course, the box has side lengths $2$, $3$, and $4$, so the volume is $2\cdot 3\cdot 4=24$.

## 16.3 Solids between two surfaces

A very common solid has the form

$$
E=\{(x,y,z):(x,y)\in D,\ g_1(x,y)\le z\le g_2(x,y)\}.
$$

Here $D$ is the projection of $E$ onto the $xy$-plane. The solid lies above the lower surface

$$
z=g_1(x,y)
$$

and below the upper surface

$$
z=g_2(x,y).
$$

Then

$$
\iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV
=\iint_D\left[\int_{g_1(x,y)}^{g_2(x,y)} f(x,y,z)\,dz\right]dA.
$$

If $D$ is a Type I region,

$$
D=\{(x,y):a\le x\le b,\ h_1(x)\le y\le h_2(x)\},
$$

then

$$
\iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV
=\int_a^b\int_{h_1(x)}^{h_2(x)}\int_{g_1(x,y)}^{g_2(x,y)}
f(x,y,z)\,dz\,dy\,dx.
$$

::: {.callout-tip}
## Projection-first method
To set up a triple integral, first choose a projection plane. Then write the outer two bounds for the projection and the inner bounds for the variable perpendicular to that projection.
:::

### Example 16.2: Volume under a plane over a triangle

Let $E$ be the tetrahedron in the first octant bounded by the coordinate planes and

$$
x+y+z=1.
$$

Because the coordinate planes are $x=0$, $y=0$, and $z=0$, the region is

$$
E=\{(x,y,z):x\ge 0,\ y\ge 0,\ z\ge 0,\ x+y+z\le 1\}.
$$

Project onto the $xy$-plane. Since $z$ ranges from $0$ to $1-x-y$, we need

$$
1-x-y\ge 0,
$$

so the projection is the triangle

$$
D=\{(x,y):0\le x\le 1,\ 0\le y\le 1-x\}.
$$

Thus

$$
\operatorname{Vol}(E)
=\int_0^1\int_0^{1-x}\int_0^{1-x-y}1\,dz\,dy\,dx.
$$

Evaluate:

$$
\operatorname{Vol}(E)
=\int_0^1\int_0^{1-x}(1-x-y)\,dy\,dx.
$$

The inner integral is

$$
\int_0^{1-x}(1-x-y)\,dy
=\left[(1-x)y-\frac{y^2}{2}\right]_0^{1-x}
=\frac{(1-x)^2}{2}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\operatorname{Vol}(E)
=\int_0^1\frac{(1-x)^2}{2}\,dx
=\frac16.
$$

This matches the formula for a tetrahedron with intercepts $1$, $1$, and $1$.

## 16.4 Integrating functions over solids

Volume is only one example. If $f(x,y,z)$ measures a density, intensity, probability density, energy density, or cost density, then

$$
\iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV
$$

sums that quantity over the whole solid.

### Example 16.3: A triple integral over the standard tetrahedron

Evaluate

$$
\iiint_E y\,dV,
$$

where

$$
E=\{(x,y,z):x\ge 0,\ y\ge 0,\ z\ge 0,\ x+y+z\le 1\}.
$$

Using the same bounds as before,

$$
\iiint_E y\,dV
=\int_0^1\int_0^{1-x}\int_0^{1-x-y} y\,dz\,dy\,dx.
$$

Since $y$ does not depend on $z$,

$$
\int_0^{1-x-y}y\,dz=y(1-x-y).
$$

Thus

$$
\iiint_E y\,dV
=\int_0^1\int_0^{1-x} y(1-x-y)\,dy\,dx.
$$

For fixed $x$,

$$
\int_0^{1-x} y(1-x-y)\,dy
=\int_0^{1-x}((1-x)y-y^2)\,dy
=\frac{(1-x)^3}{6}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\iiint_E y\,dV
=\int_0^1\frac{(1-x)^3}{6}\,dx
=\frac1{24}.
$$

Because of symmetry,

$$
\iiint_E x\,dV=\iiint_E y\,dV=\iiint_E z\,dV=\frac1{24}.
$$

## 16.5 Mass and density in three dimensions

Suppose an object occupies a solid region $E$ and has density

$$
\rho=\rho(x,y,z).
$$

The mass of a small box is approximately

$$
\Delta m \approx \rho(x_{ijk}^*,y_{ijk}^*,z_{ijk}^*)\Delta V.
$$

Summing and passing to a limit gives the mass formula.

::: {.callout-important}
## Mass from density
If an object occupies $E\subset\mathbb R^3$ and has density $\rho(x,y,z)$, then

$$
M=\iiint_E \rho(x,y,z)\,dV.
$$
:::

### Example 16.4: Density increasing with height

Let $E$ be the box

$$
0\le x\le 2,
\qquad
0\le y\le 3,
\qquad
0\le z\le 1,
$$

and suppose the density is

$$
\rho(x,y,z)=1+z.
$$

Then

$$
M=\int_0^2\int_0^3\int_0^1(1+z)\,dz\,dy\,dx.
$$

First,

$$
\int_0^1(1+z)\,dz=\frac32.
$$

Thus

$$
M=\int_0^2\int_0^3\frac32\,dy\,dx
=\int_0^2\frac92\,dx
=9.
$$

If the density were constant $1$, the mass would be the volume $6$. Since the density increases with height, the mass is larger.

## 16.6 Average value over a solid

For a function $f(x,y,z)$ on a solid $E$, its average value over $E$ is

$$
f_{\operatorname{avg}}
=\frac{1}{\operatorname{Vol}(E)}\iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV.
$$

This is the three-dimensional version of the average value of a function over an interval or a plane region.

### Example 16.5: Average height in a tetrahedron

For the standard tetrahedron

$$
E=\{(x,y,z):x,y,z\ge 0,\ x+y+z\le 1\},
$$

the average value of $z$ is

$$
z_{\operatorname{avg}}
=\frac{1}{\operatorname{Vol}(E)}\iiint_E z\,dV.
$$

By symmetry, $\iiint_E z\,dV=1/24$ and $\operatorname{Vol}(E)=1/6$. Therefore

$$
z_{\operatorname{avg}}=\frac{1/24}{1/6}=\frac14.
$$

The centroid of the tetrahedron is

$$
\left(\frac14,\frac14,\frac14\right).
$$

This example previews the role of triple integrals in computing centers of mass.

## 16.7 Other projection directions

Projection onto the $xy$-plane is common, but not always best. A solid may also be described by projecting onto the $yz$-plane or the $xz$-plane.

### Projection onto the $yz$-plane

If

$$
E=\{(x,y,z):(y,z)\in D,\ g_1(y,z)\le x\le g_2(y,z)\},
$$

then

$$
\iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV
=\iint_D\left[\int_{g_1(y,z)}^{g_2(y,z)} f(x,y,z)\,dx\right]dA.
$$

### Projection onto the $xz$-plane

If

$$
E=\{(x,y,z):(x,z)\in D,\ g_1(x,z)\le y\le g_2(x,z)\},
$$

then

$$
\iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV
=\iint_D\left[\int_{g_1(x,z)}^{g_2(x,z)} f(x,y,z)\,dy\right]dA.
$$

::: {.callout-warning}
## Do not choose the projection too early
A difficult integral often becomes easier if you project in a different direction. Before writing bounds, sketch the solid and ask: which variable naturally runs between two simple surfaces?
:::

## 16.8 Changing the order of integration

In double integrals, changing order means rewriting a region in the plane. In triple integrals, changing order means rewriting a solid in space. There are six possible orders:

$$
dz\,dy\,dx,
\qquad
 dz\,dx\,dy,
\qquad
 dy\,dz\,dx,
\qquad
 dy\,dx\,dz,
\qquad
 dx\,dz\,dy,
\qquad
 dx\,dy\,dz.
$$

The main principle is simple:

> The innermost variable should have bounds that describe a line segment through the solid parallel to that coordinate axis.

### Example 16.6: The tetrahedron in another order

For the standard tetrahedron

$$
E=\{(x,y,z):x,y,z\ge 0,\ x+y+z\le 1\},
$$

we wrote

$$
\iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV
=\int_0^1\int_0^{1-x}\int_0^{1-x-y} f(x,y,z)\,dz\,dy\,dx.
$$

Now write the order $dx\,dz\,dy$. For fixed $y$ and $z$,

$$
0\le x\le 1-y-z.
$$

The projection onto the $yz$-plane is

$$
0\le y\le 1,
\qquad
0\le z\le 1-y.
$$

Therefore

$$
\iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV
=\int_0^1\int_0^{1-y}\int_0^{1-y-z} f(x,y,z)\,dx\,dz\,dy.
$$

Because the tetrahedron is symmetric, all six orders have similar bounds.

## 16.9 Triple integrals and probability

A joint probability density for three continuous random variables $X,Y,Z$ is a nonnegative function $p(x,y,z)$ such that

$$
\iiint_{\mathbb R^3} p(x,y,z)\,dV=1.
$$

For a solid event $E\subset \mathbb R^3$,

$$
P((X,Y,Z)\in E)=\iiint_E p(x,y,z)\,dV.
$$

This interpretation is essential in statistics and machine learning. Many models use high-dimensional integrals, but the geometric meaning begins here: probability is accumulated density over a region.

### Example 16.7: Uniform distribution in a box

Suppose $(X,Y,Z)$ is uniformly distributed in the box

$$
0\le x\le 2,
\qquad
0\le y\le 1,
\qquad
0\le z\le 3.
$$

The volume is $6$, so the density is

$$
p(x,y,z)=\frac16
$$

inside the box and $0$ outside. The probability of the sub-box

$$
0\le x\le 1,
\qquad
0\le y\le 1,
\qquad
1\le z\le 3
$$

is

$$
\int_0^1\int_0^1\int_1^3\frac16\,dz\,dy\,dx
=\frac{2}{6}
=\frac13.
$$

## 16.10 Numerical triple integration

In practice, many triple integrals cannot be evaluated exactly. Numerical integration is then necessary.

For a rectangular box

$$
[a,b]\times[c,d]\times[p,q],
$$

a midpoint rule approximation is

$$
\iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV
\approx
\sum_{i=1}^m\sum_{j=1}^n\sum_{k=1}^\ell
f(\bar x_i,\bar y_j,\bar z_k)\Delta x\Delta y\Delta z.
$$

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch16-midpoint-convergence
#| fig-cap: 'Midpoint approximations for a triple integral converge as the grid is refined.'
#| fig-width: 7
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def midpoint_triple(N):
    # Integral of f=x+y+z over [0,1]^3 equals 3/2.
    xs = (np.arange(N) + 0.5) / N
    ys = (np.arange(N) + 0.5) / N
    zs = (np.arange(N) + 0.5) / N
    X, Y, Z = np.meshgrid(xs, ys, zs, indexing='ij')
    values = X + Y + Z
    return values.mean()  # volume is 1

Ns = np.arange(2, 31)
approx = np.array([midpoint_triple(N) for N in Ns])
exact = 1.5

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 4))
ax.plot(Ns, approx, marker='o', label='midpoint approximation')
ax.axhline(exact, linestyle='--', label='exact value')
ax.set_xlabel('N subdivisions in each direction')
ax.set_ylabel('integral estimate')
ax.set_title('Triple midpoint rule on the unit cube')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
ax.legend()
plt.show()
```

Because $f(x,y,z)=x+y+z$ is linear, the midpoint rule is exact for every $N$ in this example. For nonlinear functions, the approximations generally improve as the grid is refined.

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch16-nonlinear-midpoint
#| fig-cap: 'Midpoint approximations for a nonlinear function over the unit cube.'
#| fig-width: 7
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def midpoint_nonlinear(N):
    xs = (np.arange(N) + 0.5) / N
    ys = (np.arange(N) + 0.5) / N

ax.set_ylabel('estimate')

ax.legend()
plt.show()
```

## 16.11 Monte Carlo integration in three dimensions

Monte Carlo integration estimates an integral by random sampling. Suppose $E$ is a box of volume $V$ and we want

$$
I=\iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV.
$$

If $P_1,\dots,P_N$ are uniformly sampled from $E$, then

$$
I\approx V\cdot \frac1N\sum_{i=1}^N f(P_i).
$$

Monte Carlo methods become especially important in high-dimensional statistics, Bayesian inference, uncertainty quantification, and machine learning.

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch16-monte-carlo-ball
#| fig-cap: 'Monte Carlo estimation of the volume of the unit ball.'
#| fig-width: 7
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(16)
Ns = np.unique(np.logspace(2, 5, 45).astype(int))
estimates = []
for N in Ns:
    pts = rng.uniform(-1, 1, size=(N, 3))
    inside = np.sum(np.sum(pts**2, axis=1) <= 1)
    estimates.append(8 * inside / N)

exact = 4*np.pi/3
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 4))
ax.plot(Ns, estimates, marker='o', markersize=3, label='Monte Carlo estimate')
ax.axhline(exact, linestyle='--', label=r'exact $4\pi/3$')
ax.set_xscale('log')
ax.set_xlabel('number of random points')
ax.set_ylabel('estimated volume')
ax.set_title('Volume of the unit ball by random sampling')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
ax.legend()
plt.show()
```

## 16.12 Moments and centers of mass

Triple integrals also compute balance points. Suppose an object occupies $E$ with density $\rho(x,y,z)$ and mass

$$
M=\iiint_E \rho(x,y,z)\,dV.
$$

The moments about the coordinate planes are

$$
M_{yz}=\iiint_E x\rho(x,y,z)\,dV,
$$

$$
M_{xz}=\iiint_E y\rho(x,y,z)\,dV,
$$

$$
M_{xy}=\iiint_E z\rho(x,y,z)\,dV.
$$

The center of mass is

$$
(\bar x,\bar y,\bar z)
=
\left(
\frac{M_{yz}}{M},
\frac{M_{xz}}{M},
\frac{M_{xy}}{M}
\right).
$$

If $\rho$ is constant, the center of mass is the centroid of the region.

### Example 16.8: Centroid of the standard tetrahedron

For the standard tetrahedron, by symmetry and the computation above,

$$
\bar x=\bar y=\bar z=\frac14.
$$

Thus the centroid is

$$
\left(\frac14,\frac14,\frac14\right).
$$

This point is closer to the origin than to the opposite face because the tetrahedron is widest near the coordinate planes.

## 16.13 Triple integrals in the age of data and AI

Triple integrals appear naturally in modern applied mathematics, statistics, and machine learning.

### Spatial data and voxel models

Medical imaging, climate models, fluid simulations, and 3D computer vision often represent space using voxels. A voxel is a small three-dimensional box. A triple integral is the mathematical limit of summing quantities over voxels.

Examples:

- total mass in a scanned object,
- total heat in a region,
- average intensity in a 3D image,
- probability that a random spatial point lies in a target region.

### Probability and Bayesian inference

For three parameters $\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3$, a posterior density $p(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3\mid \text{data})$ satisfies

$$
\iiint p(\theta_1,\theta_2,\theta_3\mid \text{data})\,d\theta_1\,d\theta_2\,d\theta_3=1.
$$

A probability statement about the parameters is a triple integral over a region in parameter space.

### Continuous loss and risk

In statistical learning, expected loss may be written as an integral over feature space. In three variables, a risk functional may look like

$$
R(w)=\iiint L(w;x,y,z)p(x,y,z)\,dV.
$$

This is a triple integral of loss weighted by a probability density.

## 16.14 Common mistakes

1. **Forgetting the projection.** Bounds such as $0\le z\le g(x,y)$ are incomplete unless the allowed $(x,y)$ values are described.
2. **Using the wrong upper surface.** Always check which surface is above on the projection region.
3. **Mixing the order of bounds and differentials.** In $dz\,dy\,dx$, the $z$-bounds come first, then $y$-bounds, then $x$-bounds.
4. **Treating dependent variables as constants incorrectly.** When integrating with respect to $z$, expressions involving $x$ and $y$ are constant, but the $z$-bounds may depend on $x$ and $y$.
5. **Assuming every solid is a box.** Most real regions require variable bounds.
6. **Not checking units.** If $f$ has units of density, then $f\,dV$ has units of mass.

## 16.15 Summary

A triple integral is a continuous sum over a solid region:

$$
\iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV.
$$

The most important special cases are:

$$
\operatorname{Vol}(E)=\iiint_E 1\,dV,
$$

$$
M=\iiint_E \rho(x,y,z)\,dV,
$$

$$
f_{\operatorname{avg}}=rac{1}{\operatorname{Vol}(E)}\iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV.
$$

The most common rectangular-coordinate setup is

$$
E=\{(x,y,z):(x,y)\in D,\ g_1(x,y)\le z\le g_2(x,y)\},
$$

which gives

$$
\iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV
=\iint_D\left[\int_{g_1(x,y)}^{g_2(x,y)} f(x,y,z)\,dz\right]dA.
$$

The key skill is not merely integration. The key skill is **turning geometry into correct bounds**.
