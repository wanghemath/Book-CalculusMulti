14  Chapter 14. Double Integrals over General Regions

Type I and Type II regions, reversing order, area, mass, and probability

Chapter goal. Rectangles are the training ground for double integrals, but most meaningful regions are not rectangles. This chapter develops double integrals over curved and irregular plane regions. The main skill is translating a geometric region into correct limits of integration, choosing an efficient order, and interpreting the result as area, volume, mass, average value, or probability.

A double integral over a general region has the same conceptual meaning as before:

\[ \iint_D f(x,y)\,dA \]

adds the values of \(f\) over a two-dimensional region \(D\). The new difficulty is that \(D\) may be bounded by curves. Instead of constant limits such as \(a\le x\le b\) and \(c\le y\le d\), the inner limits often depend on the outer variable.

This chapter is a bridge from rectangular integration to the geometric and computational thinking needed in probability, statistics, machine learning, numerical methods, and vector calculus.

14.1 Learning objectives

After studying this chapter, you should be able to:

  1. describe Type I regions using inequalities of the form \(a\le x\le b\), \(p(x)\le y\le q(x)\);
  2. describe Type II regions using inequalities of the form \(c\le y\le d\), \(g(y)\le x\le h(y)\);
  3. set up and evaluate double integrals over nonrectangular regions;
  4. sketch a region from integral limits and write integral limits from a sketch;
  5. reverse the order of integration when it simplifies the computation;
  6. compute area using \(\iint_D 1\,dA\);
  7. split a complicated region into simpler pieces;
  8. interpret double integrals over general regions as volume, mass, average value, and probability.

14.2 14.1 Why rectangles are not enough

In the previous chapter, the base region was usually a rectangle

\[ R=[a,b]\times[c,d]. \]

For such a region, Fubini’s theorem gives

\[ \iint_R f(x,y)\,dA = \int_a^b\int_c^d f(x,y)\,dy\,dx = \int_c^d\int_a^b f(x,y)\,dx\,dy. \]

But many natural regions are bounded by curves:

  • a disk or annulus in geometry;
  • a triangular feasible set in optimization;
  • a region under a probability-density curve;
  • the set of parameters satisfying an inequality;
  • an irregular geographic region in spatial modeling;
  • a decision region in classification.

For example, the region below the parabola \(y=4-x^2\) and above the line \(y=3x\) is not a rectangle. Its vertical height changes as \(x\) changes. A double integral over this region must reflect that changing geometry.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x = np.linspace(0, 1, 300)

ax.plot(x, y_bottom, label='$y=3x$')

for x0 in [0.2, 0.5, 0.8]:
    ax.plot([x0, x0], [3*x0, 4-x0**2], linewidth=2)

The key skill is to decide how to slice the region.

  • A vertical slice leads to limits \(p(x)\le y\le q(x)\).
  • A horizontal slice leads to limits \(g(y)\le x\le h(y)\).

The same region may be easy in one direction and difficult in the other.

14.3 14.2 Type I regions: vertical slicing

A plane region \(D\) is called a Type I region if it can be described as

\[ D= \{(x,y):a\le x\le b,\ p(x)\le y\le q(x)\}, \]

where \(p\) and \(q\) are continuous functions and \(p(x)\le q(x)\) for \(a\le x\le b\).

Geometrically, we move from left to right. For each fixed \(x\), the variable \(y\) begins at the lower curve \(y=p(x)\) and ends at the upper curve \(y=q(x)\).

ImportantDouble integral over a Type I region

If \(D=\{(x,y):a\le x\le b,\ p(x)\le y\le q(x)\}\) and \(f\) is continuous on \(D\), then

\[ \iint_D f(x,y)\,dA = \int_a^b\int_{p(x)}^{q(x)} f(x,y)\,dy\,dx. \]

The inner integral adds along a vertical slice. The outer integral adds all slices from \(x=a\) to \(x=b\).

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x = np.linspace(0, 3, 400)
p = 0.4 + 0.2*np.sin(2*x)
q = 2.2 + 0.25*np.cos(1.5*x)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(6.5, 5))
ax.plot(x, p, label='$y=p(x)$')
ax.plot(x, q, label='$y=q(x)$')
ax.fill_between(x, p, q, alpha=0.25)
for x0 in np.linspace(0.5, 2.5, 5):
    p0 = 0.4 + 0.2*np.sin(2*x0)
    q0 = 2.2 + 0.25*np.cos(1.5*x0)
    ax.plot([x0, x0], [p0, q0], linewidth=2)
ax.text(0, p[0]-0.25, '$a$', ha='center')
ax.text(3, p[-1]-0.25, '$b$', ha='center')
ax.set_xlabel('$x$')
ax.set_ylabel('$y$')
ax.set_title('Type I region')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)
ax.legend()
plt.show()

14.3.1 Example 14.1: A region between a parabola and a line

Let \(D\) be the region in the first quadrant bounded by

\[ y=4-x^2 \qquad \text{and} \qquad y=3x. \]

Compute

\[ \iint_D 2xy\,dA. \]

Step 1: Find the intersection points.

Set the curves equal:

\[ 4-x^2=3x. \]

Then

\[ x^2+3x-4=0, \qquad (x+4)(x-1)=0. \]

The first-quadrant intersection has \(x=1\), and then \(y=3\). The region begins at \(x=0\) and ends at \(x=1\).

Step 2: Identify lower and upper curves.

For \(0\le x\le 1\),

\[ 3x\le y\le 4-x^2. \]

Therefore

\[ D= \{(x,y):0\le x\le 1,\ 3x\le y\le 4-x^2\}. \]

Step 3: Set up and evaluate.

\[ \begin{aligned} \iint_D 2xy\,dA &= \int_0^1\int_{3x}^{4-x^2} 2xy\,dy\,dx \\ &= \int_0^1 x\left[y^2\right]_{3x}^{4-x^2}\,dx \\ &= \int_0^1 x\left((4-x^2)^2-9x^2\right)\,dx \\ &= \int_0^1 \left(16x-17x^3+x^5\right)\,dx \\ &= \left[8x^2-\frac{17}{4}x^4+\frac{1}{6}x^6\right]_0^1 \\ &= 8-\frac{17}{4}+\frac{1}{6} = \frac{47}{12}. \end{aligned} \]

The result is positive because \(2xy\ge 0\) on the first-quadrant region.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x = np.linspace(0, 1, 400)
y_low = 3*x
y_high = 4 - x**2

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(6.5, 5))
ax.plot(x, y_low, label='$y=3x$')
ax.plot(x, y_high, label='$y=4-x^2$')
ax.fill_between(x, y_low, y_high, alpha=0.3)
ax.scatter([1], [3], zorder=5)
ax.text(1.03, 3, '$(1,3)$')
ax.set_xlabel('$x$')
ax.set_ylabel('$y$')
ax.set_title('A Type I region')
ax.set_xlim(-0.1, 1.25)
ax.set_ylim(-0.2, 4.3)
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)
ax.legend()
plt.show()

14.4 14.3 Type II regions: horizontal slicing

A plane region \(D\) is called a Type II region if it can be described as

\[ D= \{(x,y):c\le y\le d,\ g(y)\le x\le h(y)\}, \]

where \(g\) and \(h\) are continuous functions and \(g(y)\le h(y)\) for \(c\le y\le d\).

Geometrically, we move from bottom to top. For each fixed \(y\), the variable \(x\) begins at the left curve \(x=g(y)\) and ends at the right curve \(x=h(y)\).

ImportantDouble integral over a Type II region

If \(D=\{(x,y):c\le y\le d,\ g(y)\le x\le h(y)\}\) and \(f\) is continuous on \(D\), then

\[ \iint_D f(x,y)\,dA = \int_c^d\int_{g(y)}^{h(y)} f(x,y)\,dx\,dy. \]

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

y = np.linspace(0, 3, 400)
g = 0.4 + 0.2*np.cos(2*y)
h = 2.5 + 0.25*np.sin(1.5*y)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(6.5, 5))
ax.plot(g, y, label='$x=g(y)$')
ax.plot(h, y, label='$x=h(y)$')
ax.fill_betweenx(y, g, h, alpha=0.25)
for y0 in np.linspace(0.5, 2.5, 5):
    g0 = 0.4 + 0.2*np.cos(2*y0)
    h0 = 2.5 + 0.25*np.sin(1.5*y0)
    ax.plot([g0, h0], [y0, y0], linewidth=2)
ax.set_xlabel('$x$')
ax.set_ylabel('$y$')
ax.set_title('Type II region')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)
ax.legend()
plt.show()

14.4.1 Example 14.2: A region between \(x=y\) and \(x=y^2\)

Let \(D\) be the region bounded by

\[ x=y \qquad \text{and} \qquad x=y^2. \]

Compute

\[ \iint_D (2xy+6y^2)\,dA. \]

Step 1: Find the intersections.

Set \(y=y^2\):

\[ y^2-y=0, \qquad y(y-1)=0. \]

Thus \(y=0\) and \(y=1\). For \(0\le y\le 1\), we have

\[ y^2\le x\le y. \]

Step 2: Write the Type II integral.

\[ \iint_D (2xy+6y^2)\,dA = \int_0^1\int_{y^2}^{y} (2xy+6y^2)\,dx\,dy. \]

Step 3: Evaluate.

\[ \begin{aligned} \int_0^1\int_{y^2}^{y} (2xy+6y^2)\,dx\,dy &= \int_0^1 \left[x^2y+6xy^2\right]_{x=y^2}^{x=y}\,dy \\ &= \int_0^1 \left(y^3+6y^3-y^5-6y^4\right)\,dy \\ &= \int_0^1 \left(7y^3-6y^4-y^5\right)\,dy \\ &= \left[\frac{7}{4}y^4-\frac{6}{5}y^5-\frac{1}{6}y^6\right]_0^1 \\ &= \frac{7}{4}-\frac{6}{5}-\frac{1}{6} = \frac{23}{60}. \end{aligned} \]

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

y = np.linspace(0, 1, 400)
x_left = y**2
x_right = y

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(6.5, 5))
ax.plot(x_left, y, label='$x=y^2$')
ax.plot(x_right, y, label='$x=y$')
ax.fill_betweenx(y, x_left, x_right, alpha=0.3)
for y0 in [0.2, 0.5, 0.8]:
    ax.plot([y0**2, y0], [y0, y0], linewidth=2)
ax.set_xlabel('$x$')
ax.set_ylabel('$y$')
ax.set_title('A Type II region')
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)
ax.legend()
plt.show()

14.5 14.4 How to choose the order of integration

When setting up a double integral, ask two questions.

Question 1. What does a typical slice look like?

A vertical slice is useful if a line \(x=\text{constant}\) enters and exits the region in one piece. A horizontal slice is useful if a line \(y=\text{constant}\) enters and exits the region in one piece.

Question 2. Which order makes the integrand easier?

Sometimes both descriptions are possible, but only one gives an elementary integral.

A practical workflow is:

  1. sketch the curves;
  2. find intersection points;
  3. try vertical slices and horizontal slices;
  4. choose the description with simpler bounds or simpler integration;
  5. compute only after the geometry is correct.
TipGeometry first, algebra second

Many mistakes in double integrals are not integration mistakes. They are region mistakes. Before computing, check:

  • Does the lower curve really lie below the upper curve?
  • Does the left curve really lie left of the right curve?
  • Are the intersection values correct?
  • Does each slice stay inside the region for the full range of the outer variable?

14.6 14.5 Reversing the order of integration

Some iterated integrals are difficult in the given order but easy after reversing the order.

Consider

\[ \int_0^1\int_y^1 e^{-x^2}\,dx\,dy. \]

The inner integral

\[ \int e^{-x^2}\,dx \]

has no elementary antiderivative. But the integral is not hopeless. The region is

\[ D= \{(x,y):0\le y\le 1,\ y\le x\le 1\}. \]

This describes the triangle below the line \(y=x\) and inside the unit square. Rewriting in Type I form gives

\[ D= \{(x,y):0\le x\le 1,\ 0\le y\le x\}. \]

Therefore

\[ \int_0^1\int_y^1 e^{-x^2}\,dx\,dy = \int_0^1\int_0^x e^{-x^2}\,dy\,dx. \]

Now \(e^{-x^2}\) is constant with respect to \(y\):

\[ \begin{aligned} \int_0^1\int_0^x e^{-x^2}\,dy\,dx &= \int_0^1 xe^{-x^2}\,dx \\ &= \left[-\frac{1}{2}e^{-x^2}\right]_0^1 \\ &= \frac{1}{2}(1-e^{-1}). \end{aligned} \]

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x = np.linspace(0, 1, 300)
fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(6, 5))
ax.plot(x, x, label='$y=x$')
ax.plot([0, 1], [0, 0], color='black')
ax.plot([1, 1], [0, 1], color='black')
ax.fill_between(x, 0, x, alpha=0.3)
for x0 in [0.25, 0.5, 0.75]:
    ax.plot([x0, x0], [0, x0], linewidth=2)
ax.set_xlabel('$x$')
ax.set_ylabel('$y$')
ax.set_title('Region for reversing order')
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)
ax.legend()
plt.show()

14.6.1 Example 14.3: From region to reversed integral

Rewrite the integral

\[ \int_0^4\int_{y/2}^{\sqrt{y+1}} f(x,y)\,dx\,dy \]

with the order \(dy\,dx\).

The given limits describe

\[ 0\le y\le 4, \qquad \frac{y}{2}\le x\le \sqrt{y+1}. \]

Convert each boundary to \(y\) as a function of \(x\):

\[ x=\frac{y}{2}\quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad y=2x, \]

and

\[ x=\sqrt{y+1}\quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad y=x^2-1. \]

The region is between \(y=x^2-1\) and \(y=2x\), with \(0\le y\le 4\). The horizontal band restriction causes the region to split when vertical slices change their lower boundary. We find the relevant \(x\)-range from the endpoints:

  • at \(y=0\), \(x\) ranges from \(0\) to \(1\);
  • at \(y=4\), \(x\) ranges from \(2\) to \(\sqrt{5}\).

A careful sketch shows

\[ \int_0^4\int_{y/2}^{\sqrt{y+1}} f(x,y)\,dx\,dy = \int_0^1\int_0^{2x} f(x,y)\,dy\,dx + \int_1^2\int_{x^2-1}^{2x} f(x,y)\,dy\,dx + \int_2^{\sqrt{5}}\int_{x^2-1}^{4} f(x,y)\,dy\,dx. \]

This example illustrates why reversing order can require splitting. The geometry decides whether one integral is enough.

14.7 14.6 Area of a plane region

The area of a plane region \(D\) can be computed by integrating the constant function \(1\):

ImportantArea by double integral

\[ \operatorname{Area}(D)=\iint_D 1\,dA. \]

For a Type I region,

\[ \operatorname{Area}(D) = \int_a^b\int_{p(x)}^{q(x)}1\,dy\,dx = \int_a^b(q(x)-p(x))\,dx. \]

For a Type II region,

\[ \operatorname{Area}(D) = \int_c^d\int_{g(y)}^{h(y)}1\,dx\,dy = \int_c^d(h(y)-g(y))\,dy. \]

14.7.1 Example 14.4: Area between a line and a parabola

Find the area of the region bounded by

\[ y=x-1 \qquad \text{and} \qquad y^2=2x+6. \]

Write both curves as \(x\) in terms of \(y\):

\[ y=x-1 \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad x=y+1, \]

and

\[ y^2=2x+6 \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad x=\frac{y^2-6}{2}. \]

Find intersections by substituting \(x=y+1\) into \(y^2=2x+6\):

\[ y^2=2(y+1)+6, \qquad y^2-2y-8=0, \qquad (y-4)(y+2)=0. \]

Thus \(-2\le y\le 4\). The parabola lies on the left and the line lies on the right, so

\[ \begin{aligned} \operatorname{Area}(D) &= \int_{-2}^{4}\int_{(y^2-6)/2}^{y+1} 1\,dx\,dy \\ &= \int_{-2}^{4}\left(y+1-\frac{y^2-6}{2}\right)\,dy \\ &= \int_{-2}^{4}\left(y+4-\frac{y^2}{2}\right)\,dy \\ &=18. \end{aligned} \]

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

y = np.linspace(-2, 4, 400)
x_left = (y**2 - 6)/2
x_right = y + 1

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(6.5, 5))
ax.plot(x_left, y, label='$x=(y^2-6)/2$')
ax.plot(x_right, y, label='$x=y+1$')
ax.fill_betweenx(y, x_left, x_right, alpha=0.3)
ax.scatter([(-2**2 - 6)/2, 5], [-2, 4], zorder=5)
ax.set_xlabel('$x$')
ax.set_ylabel('$y$')
ax.set_title('Area by horizontal slices')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)
ax.legend()
plt.show()

14.8 14.7 Splitting regions

Not every region is Type I or Type II in a single piece. Some regions must be split into simpler pieces.

Suppose

\[ D=D_1\cup D_2 \]

where the interiors of \(D_1\) and \(D_2\) do not overlap. Then

\[ \iint_D f(x,y)\,dA = \iint_{D_1} f(x,y)\,dA + \iint_{D_2} f(x,y)\,dA. \]

This is the two-dimensional version of splitting a one-variable integral at a point.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x1 = np.linspace(-1, 0, 200)
x2 = np.linspace(0, 1, 200)
upper1 = 1 - x1**2
upper2 = 1 - x2**2
lower1 = -0.2 + 0*x1
lower2 = x2 - 0.2

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(6.5, 5))
ax.fill_between(x1, lower1, upper1, alpha=0.3, label='$D_1$')
ax.fill_between(x2, lower2, upper2, alpha=0.3, label='$D_2$')
ax.plot(np.r_[x1, x2], 1 - np.r_[x1, x2]**2, color='black', label='upper boundary')
ax.plot(x1, lower1, color='black')
ax.plot(x2, lower2, color='black')
ax.axvline(0, linestyle='--', linewidth=1)
ax.set_xlabel('$x$')
ax.set_ylabel('$y$')
ax.set_title('A region split into two pieces')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)
ax.legend()
plt.show()
NoteWhen splitting is necessary

Splitting is needed when a single vertical or horizontal slice does not enter and exit the region through the same pair of boundary curves for the whole range. Splitting is not a failure; it is part of correct geometric modeling.

14.9 14.8 Volume under a surface over a curved base

If \(f(x,y)\ge 0\) on a region \(D\), then

\[ V=\iint_D f(x,y)\,dA \]

is the volume of the solid above \(D\) and below the surface \(z=f(x,y)\).

14.9.1 Example 14.5: Volume over a triangular region

Let \(D\) be the triangular region with vertices \((0,0)\), \((1,0)\), and \((1,2)\). Find the volume under

\[ z=3+x+y \]

over \(D\).

The region is

\[ D=\{(x,y):0\le x\le 1,\ 0\le y\le 2x\}. \]

Therefore

\[ \begin{aligned} V &=\int_0^1\int_0^{2x}(3+x+y)\,dy\,dx \\ &=\int_0^1\left[(3+x)y+\frac{y^2}{2}\right]_0^{2x}\,dx \\ &=\int_0^1\left(6x+2x^2+2x^2\right)\,dx \\ &=\int_0^1(6x+4x^2)\,dx \\ &=3+\frac{4}{3} = \frac{13}{3}. \end{aligned} \]

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from matplotlib.tri import Triangulation

# Triangular grid: 0 <= x <= 1, 0 <= y <= 2x
xs = []
ys = []
for x0 in np.linspace(0, 1, 35):
    for y0 in np.linspace(0, 2*x0, max(2, int(35*x0)+2)):
        xs.append(x0)
        ys.append(y0)
xs = np.array(xs)
ys = np.array(ys)
zs = 3 + xs + ys
tri = Triangulation(xs, ys)

14.10 14.9 Density, mass, and average value over general regions

If a thin plate occupies a region \(D\) and has density \(\rho(x,y)\), then the mass is

\[ m=\iint_D \rho(x,y)\,dA. \]

The average value of a function \(f\) over \(D\) is

\[ f_{\operatorname{avg}} = \frac{1}{\operatorname{Area}(D)}\iint_D f(x,y)\,dA. \]

These formulas are the same as for rectangles, but now the geometry of \(D\) must be handled carefully.

14.10.1 Example 14.6: Average value on a triangle

Find the average value of

\[ f(x,y)=x+2y \]

over

\[ D=\{(x,y):0\le x\le 1,\ 0\le y\le 1-x\}. \]

The area is

\[ \operatorname{Area}(D)=\frac{1}{2}. \]

Compute the integral:

\[ \begin{aligned} \iint_D (x+2y)\,dA &= \int_0^1\int_0^{1-x}(x+2y)\,dy\,dx \\ &= \int_0^1\left[xy+y^2\right]_0^{1-x}\,dx \\ &= \int_0^1\left(x(1-x)+(1-x)^2\right)\,dx \\ &= \int_0^1(1-x)\,dx = \frac{1}{2}. \end{aligned} \]

Thus

\[ f_{\operatorname{avg}} = \frac{1/2}{1/2}=1. \]

14.11 14.10 Probability over a region

In statistics, a joint density \(p(x,y)\) assigns probability density to points in the plane. If \((X,Y)\) has joint density \(p\), then

\[ P((X,Y)\in D)=\iint_D p(x,y)\,dA. \]

This is one of the most important applications of double integrals over general regions.

14.11.1 Example 14.7: Probability over a triangular event

Suppose \((X,Y)\) is uniformly distributed over the unit square \([0,1]\times[0,1]\). Since the total area is \(1\), the density is \(p(x,y)=1\) on the square.

Find

\[ P(Y\le X^2). \]

The event is

\[ D=\{(x,y):0\le x\le 1,\ 0\le y\le x^2\}. \]

Thus

\[ P(Y\le X^2) = \int_0^1\int_0^{x^2}1\,dy\,dx = \int_0^1 x^2\,dx = \frac{1}{3}. \]

14.11.2 A nonuniform density example

Let

\[ p(x,y)=2 \]

on the triangular region

\[ T=\{(x,y):0\le x\le 1,\ 0\le y\le 1-x\}, \]

and \(p(x,y)=0\) outside \(T\). Since \(\operatorname{Area}(T)=1/2\), the total probability is \(2\cdot 1/2=1\).

For the event \(Y\le X\), the relevant region is

\[ 0\le x\le 1, \qquad 0\le y\le \min(x,1-x). \]

The minimum changes at \(x=1/2\), so split the integral:

\[ \begin{aligned} P(Y\le X) &= \int_0^{1/2}\int_0^x 2\,dy\,dx + \int_{1/2}^{1}\int_0^{1-x}2\,dy\,dx \\ &= \int_0^{1/2}2x\,dx + \int_{1/2}^{1}2(1-x)\,dx \\ &= \frac{1}{4}+\frac{1}{4} = \frac{1}{2}. \end{aligned} \]

14.12 14.11 Numerical integration over irregular regions

For complicated regions or complicated functions, exact integration may be difficult. Two common numerical strategies are:

  1. grid-based approximation;
  2. Monte Carlo integration.

Suppose \(D\) lies inside a rectangle \(R\) of area \(A(R)\). The integral can be written as

\[ \iint_D f(x,y)\,dA = \iint_R f(x,y)\mathbf 1_D(x,y)\,dA, \]

where \(\mathbf 1_D(x,y)\) is \(1\) inside \(D\) and \(0\) outside \(D\).

A Monte Carlo approximation samples random points in \(R\):

\[ \iint_D f(x,y)\,dA \approx A(R)\cdot \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N f(X_i,Y_i)\mathbf 1_D(X_i,Y_i). \]

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(14)
N = 6000
x = rng.uniform(0, 1, N)
y = rng.uniform(0, 4, N)
inside = (y >= 3*x) & (y <= 4 - x**2)

# Estimate integral of f(x,y)=2xy over D from Example 14.1
values = 2*x*y*inside
estimate = 4*np.mean(values)  # enclosing rectangle area = 4
exact = 47/12

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 5))
ax.scatter(x[~inside], y[~inside], s=4, alpha=0.15, label='outside')
ax.scatter(x[inside], y[inside], s=4, alpha=0.25, label='inside')
xx = np.linspace(0, 1, 300)
ax.plot(xx, 3*xx, color='black')
ax.plot(xx, 4-xx**2, color='black')
ax.set_xlabel('$x$')
ax.set_ylabel('$y$')
ax.set_title(f'Monte Carlo estimate: {estimate:.4f}; exact: {exact:.4f}')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)
ax.legend()
plt.show()

Monte Carlo integration is not usually the most accurate method for low-dimensional smooth problems. However, it scales naturally to high-dimensional problems, which is why it is central in statistics, Bayesian computation, and machine learning.

14.14 14.13 Checklist for setting up a double integral

When solving a double-integral problem over a general region, use this checklist.

  1. Sketch the region. Mark all boundary curves.
  2. Find intersection points. These determine the outer limits.
  3. Choose vertical or horizontal slices. Decide whether the region is Type I, Type II, or must be split.
  4. Write the inequalities. Do not compute until the region is correct.
  5. Check lower/upper or left/right boundaries. Test a sample point if needed.
  6. Set up the integral. Put the inner variable in the inner differential.
  7. Simplify before integrating. Sometimes reversing order is better.
  8. Interpret the result. Is it area, volume, mass, average value, probability, or signed accumulation?

14.15 14.14 Modern applications

14.15.1 Statistics and probability

A joint density over a nonrectangular region appears when variables are constrained. Examples include:

  • \(0\le y\le x\le 1\) for ordered random variables;
  • \(x+y\le 1\) for compositional data;
  • \(y\le x^2\) for nonlinear events;
  • feasible regions in Bayesian priors.

The probability of an event is a double integral over the event region.

14.15.2 Machine learning

Many models depend on two or more parameters. A double integral over a parameter region can represent average loss, expected regularized risk, or uncertainty over a feasible parameter set.

For example, if \(L(\theta_1,\theta_2)\) is a loss function and \(D\) is a feasible set, then

\[ \frac{1}{\operatorname{Area}(D)}\iint_D L(\theta_1,\theta_2)\,dA \]

is the average loss over \(D\).

14.15.3 Applied mathematics

In applied mathematics, regions are often determined by physical constraints: a membrane shape, a flow region, an obstacle boundary, a material interface, or a domain for a partial differential equation. Double integrals over such regions compute total mass, energy, heat, charge, and probability.

14.16 Chapter summary

A double integral over a general region adds a function over a curved or constrained two-dimensional domain. The central task is translating geometry into limits.

  • A Type I region has the form

\[ D=\{(x,y):a\le x\le b,\ p(x)\le y\le q(x)\}. \]

Then

\[ \iint_D f(x,y)\,dA = \int_a^b\int_{p(x)}^{q(x)} f(x,y)\,dy\,dx. \]

  • A Type II region has the form

\[ D=\{(x,y):c\le y\le d,\ g(y)\le x\le h(y)\}. \]

Then

\[ \iint_D f(x,y)\,dA = \int_c^d\int_{g(y)}^{h(y)} f(x,y)\,dx\,dy. \]

  • Area is computed by

\[ \operatorname{Area}(D)=\iint_D 1\,dA. \]

  • Regions can be split:

\[ \iint_D f\,dA = \iint_{D_1} f\,dA+ \iint_{D_2} f\,dA. \]

  • Reversing order can turn a difficult integral into an elementary one.

The strongest students do not memorize limits. They learn to see slices.

## 14.1 Why rectangles are not enough

In the previous chapter, the base region was usually a rectangle

$$
R=[a,b]\times[c,d].
$$

For such a region, Fubini's theorem gives

$$
\iint_R f(x,y)\,dA
=
\int_a^b\int_c^d f(x,y)\,dy\,dx
=
\int_c^d\int_a^b f(x,y)\,dx\,dy.
$$

But many natural regions are bounded by curves:

- a disk or annulus in geometry;
- a triangular feasible set in optimization;
- a region under a probability-density curve;
- the set of parameters satisfying an inequality;
- an irregular geographic region in spatial modeling;
- a decision region in classification.

For example, the region below the parabola $y=4-x^2$ and above the line $y=3x$ is not a rectangle. Its vertical height changes as $x$ changes. A double integral over this region must reflect that changing geometry.

The key skill is to decide how to slice the region.

- A **vertical slice** leads to limits $p(x)\le y\le q(x)$.
- A **horizontal slice** leads to limits $g(y)\le x\le h(y)$.

The same region may be easy in one direction and difficult in the other.

## 14.2 Type I regions: vertical slicing

A plane region $D$ is called a **Type I region** if it can be described as

$$
D=
\{(x,y):a\le x\le b,\ p(x)\le y\le q(x)\},
$$

where $p$ and $q$ are continuous functions and $p(x)\le q(x)$ for $a\le x\le b$.

Geometrically, we move from left to right. For each fixed $x$, the variable $y$ begins at the lower curve $y=p(x)$ and ends at the upper curve $y=q(x)$.

::: {.callout-important}
## Double integral over a Type I region

If $D=\{(x,y):a\le x\le b,\ p(x)\le y\le q(x)\}$ and $f$ is continuous on $D$, then

$$
\iint_D f(x,y)\,dA
=
\int_a^b\int_{p(x)}^{q(x)} f(x,y)\,dy\,dx.
$$
:::

The inner integral adds along a vertical slice. The outer integral adds all slices from $x=a$ to $x=b$.

### Example 14.1: A region between a parabola and a line

Let $D$ be the region in the first quadrant bounded by

$$
y=4-x^2
\qquad \text{and} \qquad
 y=3x.
$$

Compute

$$
\iint_D 2xy\,dA.
$$

**Step 1: Find the intersection points.**

Set the curves equal:

$$
4-x^2=3x.
$$

Then

$$
x^2+3x-4=0,
\qquad
(x+4)(x-1)=0.
$$

The first-quadrant intersection has $x=1$, and then $y=3$. The region begins at $x=0$ and ends at $x=1$.

**Step 2: Identify lower and upper curves.**

For $0\le x\le 1$,

$$
3x\le y\le 4-x^2.
$$

Therefore

$$
D=
\{(x,y):0\le x\le 1,\ 3x\le y\le 4-x^2\}.
$$

**Step 3: Set up and evaluate.**

$$
\begin{aligned}
\iint_D 2xy\,dA
&=
\int_0^1\int_{3x}^{4-x^2} 2xy\,dy\,dx \\
&=
\int_0^1 x\left[y^2\right]_{3x}^{4-x^2}\,dx \\
&=
\int_0^1 x\left((4-x^2)^2-9x^2\right)\,dx \\
&=
\int_0^1 \left(16x-17x^3+x^5\right)\,dx \\
&=
\left[8x^2-\frac{17}{4}x^4+\frac{1}{6}x^6\right]_0^1 \\
&=
8-\frac{17}{4}+\frac{1}{6}
=
\frac{47}{12}.
\end{aligned}
$$

The result is positive because $2xy\ge 0$ on the first-quadrant region.

## 14.3 Type II regions: horizontal slicing

A plane region $D$ is called a **Type II region** if it can be described as

$$
D=
\{(x,y):c\le y\le d,\ g(y)\le x\le h(y)\},
$$

where $g$ and $h$ are continuous functions and $g(y)\le h(y)$ for $c\le y\le d$.

Geometrically, we move from bottom to top. For each fixed $y$, the variable $x$ begins at the left curve $x=g(y)$ and ends at the right curve $x=h(y)$.

::: {.callout-important}
## Double integral over a Type II region

If $D=\{(x,y):c\le y\le d,\ g(y)\le x\le h(y)\}$ and $f$ is continuous on $D$, then

$$
\iint_D f(x,y)\,dA
=
\int_c^d\int_{g(y)}^{h(y)} f(x,y)\,dx\,dy.
$$
:::

### Example 14.2: A region between $x=y$ and $x=y^2$

Let $D$ be the region bounded by

$$
x=y
\qquad \text{and} \qquad
x=y^2.
$$

Compute

$$
\iint_D (2xy+6y^2)\,dA.
$$

**Step 1: Find the intersections.**

Set $y=y^2$:

$$
y^2-y=0,
\qquad
 y(y-1)=0.
$$

Thus $y=0$ and $y=1$. For $0\le y\le 1$, we have

$$
y^2\le x\le y.
$$

**Step 2: Write the Type II integral.**

$$
\iint_D (2xy+6y^2)\,dA
=
\int_0^1\int_{y^2}^{y} (2xy+6y^2)\,dx\,dy.
$$

**Step 3: Evaluate.**

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_0^1\int_{y^2}^{y} (2xy+6y^2)\,dx\,dy
&=
\int_0^1 \left[x^2y+6xy^2\right]_{x=y^2}^{x=y}\,dy \\
&=
\int_0^1 \left(y^3+6y^3-y^5-6y^4\right)\,dy \\
&=
\int_0^1 \left(7y^3-6y^4-y^5\right)\,dy \\
&=
\left[\frac{7}{4}y^4-\frac{6}{5}y^5-\frac{1}{6}y^6\right]_0^1 \\
&=
\frac{7}{4}-\frac{6}{5}-\frac{1}{6}
=
\frac{23}{60}.
\end{aligned}
$$

## 14.4 How to choose the order of integration

When setting up a double integral, ask two questions.

**Question 1. What does a typical slice look like?**

A vertical slice is useful if a line $x=\text{constant}$ enters and exits the region in one piece. A horizontal slice is useful if a line $y=\text{constant}$ enters and exits the region in one piece.

**Question 2. Which order makes the integrand easier?**

Sometimes both descriptions are possible, but only one gives an elementary integral.

A practical workflow is:

1. sketch the curves;
2. find intersection points;
3. try vertical slices and horizontal slices;
4. choose the description with simpler bounds or simpler integration;
5. compute only after the geometry is correct.

::: {.callout-tip}
## Geometry first, algebra second

Many mistakes in double integrals are not integration mistakes. They are region mistakes. Before computing, check:

- Does the lower curve really lie below the upper curve?
- Does the left curve really lie left of the right curve?
- Are the intersection values correct?
- Does each slice stay inside the region for the full range of the outer variable?
:::

## 14.5 Reversing the order of integration

Some iterated integrals are difficult in the given order but easy after reversing the order.

Consider

$$
\int_0^1\int_y^1 e^{-x^2}\,dx\,dy.
$$

The inner integral

$$
\int e^{-x^2}\,dx
$$

has no elementary antiderivative. But the integral is not hopeless. The region is

$$
D=
\{(x,y):0\le y\le 1,\ y\le x\le 1\}.
$$

This describes the triangle below the line $y=x$ and inside the unit square. Rewriting in Type I form gives

$$
D=
\{(x,y):0\le x\le 1,\ 0\le y\le x\}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\int_0^1\int_y^1 e^{-x^2}\,dx\,dy
=
\int_0^1\int_0^x e^{-x^2}\,dy\,dx.
$$

Now $e^{-x^2}$ is constant with respect to $y$:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\int_0^1\int_0^x e^{-x^2}\,dy\,dx
&=
\int_0^1 xe^{-x^2}\,dx \\
&=
\left[-\frac{1}{2}e^{-x^2}\right]_0^1 \\
&=
\frac{1}{2}(1-e^{-1}).
\end{aligned}
$$

### Example 14.3: From region to reversed integral

Rewrite the integral

$$
\int_0^4\int_{y/2}^{\sqrt{y+1}} f(x,y)\,dx\,dy
$$

with the order $dy\,dx$.

The given limits describe

$$
0\le y\le 4,
\qquad
\frac{y}{2}\le x\le \sqrt{y+1}.
$$

Convert each boundary to $y$ as a function of $x$:

$$
x=\frac{y}{2}\quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad y=2x,
$$

and

$$
x=\sqrt{y+1}\quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad y=x^2-1.
$$

The region is between $y=x^2-1$ and $y=2x$, with $0\le y\le 4$. The horizontal band restriction causes the region to split when vertical slices change their lower boundary. We find the relevant $x$-range from the endpoints:

- at $y=0$, $x$ ranges from $0$ to $1$;
- at $y=4$, $x$ ranges from $2$ to $\sqrt{5}$.

A careful sketch shows

$$
\int_0^4\int_{y/2}^{\sqrt{y+1}} f(x,y)\,dx\,dy
=
\int_0^1\int_0^{2x} f(x,y)\,dy\,dx
+
\int_1^2\int_{x^2-1}^{2x} f(x,y)\,dy\,dx
+
\int_2^{\sqrt{5}}\int_{x^2-1}^{4} f(x,y)\,dy\,dx.
$$

This example illustrates why reversing order can require splitting. The geometry decides whether one integral is enough.

## 14.6 Area of a plane region

The area of a plane region $D$ can be computed by integrating the constant function $1$:

::: {.callout-important}
## Area by double integral

$$
\operatorname{Area}(D)=\iint_D 1\,dA.
$$
:::

For a Type I region,

$$
\operatorname{Area}(D)
=
\int_a^b\int_{p(x)}^{q(x)}1\,dy\,dx
=
\int_a^b(q(x)-p(x))\,dx.
$$

For a Type II region,

$$
\operatorname{Area}(D)
=
\int_c^d\int_{g(y)}^{h(y)}1\,dx\,dy
=
\int_c^d(h(y)-g(y))\,dy.
$$

### Example 14.4: Area between a line and a parabola

Find the area of the region bounded by

$$
y=x-1
\qquad \text{and} \qquad
 y^2=2x+6.
$$

Write both curves as $x$ in terms of $y$:

$$
y=x-1 \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad x=y+1,
$$

and

$$
y^2=2x+6 \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad x=\frac{y^2-6}{2}.
$$

Find intersections by substituting $x=y+1$ into $y^2=2x+6$:

$$
y^2=2(y+1)+6,
\qquad
 y^2-2y-8=0,
\qquad
(y-4)(y+2)=0.
$$

Thus $-2\le y\le 4$. The parabola lies on the left and the line lies on the right, so

$$
\begin{aligned}
\operatorname{Area}(D)
&=
\int_{-2}^{4}\int_{(y^2-6)/2}^{y+1} 1\,dx\,dy \\
&=
\int_{-2}^{4}\left(y+1-\frac{y^2-6}{2}\right)\,dy \\
&=
\int_{-2}^{4}\left(y+4-\frac{y^2}{2}\right)\,dy \\
&=18.
\end{aligned}
$$

## 14.7 Splitting regions

Not every region is Type I or Type II in a single piece. Some regions must be split into simpler pieces.

Suppose

$$
D=D_1\cup D_2
$$

where the interiors of $D_1$ and $D_2$ do not overlap. Then

$$
\iint_D f(x,y)\,dA
=
\iint_{D_1} f(x,y)\,dA
+
\iint_{D_2} f(x,y)\,dA.
$$

This is the two-dimensional version of splitting a one-variable integral at a point.


::: {.callout-note}
## When splitting is necessary

Splitting is needed when a single vertical or horizontal slice does not enter and exit the region through the same pair of boundary curves for the whole range. Splitting is not a failure; it is part of correct geometric modeling.
:::

## 14.8 Volume under a surface over a curved base

If $f(x,y)\ge 0$ on a region $D$, then

$$
V=\iint_D f(x,y)\,dA
$$

is the volume of the solid above $D$ and below the surface $z=f(x,y)$.

### Example 14.5: Volume over a triangular region

Let $D$ be the triangular region with vertices $(0,0)$, $(1,0)$, and $(1,2)$. Find the volume under

$$
z=3+x+y
$$

over $D$.

The region is

$$
D=\{(x,y):0\le x\le 1,\ 0\le y\le 2x\}.
$$

Therefore

$$
\begin{aligned}
V
&=\int_0^1\int_0^{2x}(3+x+y)\,dy\,dx \\
&=\int_0^1\left[(3+x)y+\frac{y^2}{2}\right]_0^{2x}\,dx \\
&=\int_0^1\left(6x+2x^2+2x^2\right)\,dx \\
&=\int_0^1(6x+4x^2)\,dx \\
&=3+\frac{4}{3}
=
\frac{13}{3}.
\end{aligned}
$$

## 14.9 Density, mass, and average value over general regions

If a thin plate occupies a region $D$ and has density $\rho(x,y)$, then the mass is

$$
m=\iint_D \rho(x,y)\,dA.
$$

The average value of a function $f$ over $D$ is

$$
f_{\operatorname{avg}}
=
\frac{1}{\operatorname{Area}(D)}\iint_D f(x,y)\,dA.
$$

These formulas are the same as for rectangles, but now the geometry of $D$ must be handled carefully.

### Example 14.6: Average value on a triangle

Find the average value of

$$
f(x,y)=x+2y
$$

over

$$
D=\{(x,y):0\le x\le 1,\ 0\le y\le 1-x\}.
$$

The area is

$$
\operatorname{Area}(D)=\frac{1}{2}.
$$

Compute the integral:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\iint_D (x+2y)\,dA
&=
\int_0^1\int_0^{1-x}(x+2y)\,dy\,dx \\
&=
\int_0^1\left[xy+y^2\right]_0^{1-x}\,dx \\
&=
\int_0^1\left(x(1-x)+(1-x)^2\right)\,dx \\
&=
\int_0^1(1-x)\,dx
=
\frac{1}{2}.
\end{aligned}
$$

Thus

$$
f_{\operatorname{avg}}
=
\frac{1/2}{1/2}=1.
$$

## 14.10 Probability over a region

In statistics, a joint density $p(x,y)$ assigns probability density to points in the plane. If $(X,Y)$ has joint density $p$, then

$$
P((X,Y)\in D)=\iint_D p(x,y)\,dA.
$$

This is one of the most important applications of double integrals over general regions.

### Example 14.7: Probability over a triangular event

Suppose $(X,Y)$ is uniformly distributed over the unit square $[0,1]\times[0,1]$. Since the total area is $1$, the density is $p(x,y)=1$ on the square.

Find

$$
P(Y\le X^2).
$$

The event is

$$
D=\{(x,y):0\le x\le 1,\ 0\le y\le x^2\}.
$$

Thus

$$
P(Y\le X^2)
=
\int_0^1\int_0^{x^2}1\,dy\,dx
=
\int_0^1 x^2\,dx
=
\frac{1}{3}.
$$

### A nonuniform density example

Let

$$
p(x,y)=2
$$

on the triangular region

$$
T=\{(x,y):0\le x\le 1,\ 0\le y\le 1-x\},
$$

and $p(x,y)=0$ outside $T$. Since $\operatorname{Area}(T)=1/2$, the total probability is $2\cdot 1/2=1$.

For the event $Y\le X$, the relevant region is

$$
0\le x\le 1,
\qquad
0\le y\le \min(x,1-x).
$$

The minimum changes at $x=1/2$, so split the integral:

$$
\begin{aligned}
P(Y\le X)
&=
\int_0^{1/2}\int_0^x 2\,dy\,dx
+
\int_{1/2}^{1}\int_0^{1-x}2\,dy\,dx \\
&=
\int_0^{1/2}2x\,dx
+
\int_{1/2}^{1}2(1-x)\,dx \\
&=
\frac{1}{4}+\frac{1}{4}
=
\frac{1}{2}.
\end{aligned}
$$

## 14.11 Numerical integration over irregular regions

For complicated regions or complicated functions, exact integration may be difficult. Two common numerical strategies are:

1. grid-based approximation;
2. Monte Carlo integration.

Suppose $D$ lies inside a rectangle $R$ of area $A(R)$. The integral can be written as

$$
\iint_D f(x,y)\,dA
=
\iint_R f(x,y)\mathbf 1_D(x,y)\,dA,
$$

where $\mathbf 1_D(x,y)$ is $1$ inside $D$ and $0$ outside $D$.

A Monte Carlo approximation samples random points in $R$:

$$
\iint_D f(x,y)\,dA
\approx
A(R)\cdot \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N f(X_i,Y_i)\mathbf 1_D(X_i,Y_i).
$$

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch14-monte-carlo-irregular
#| fig-cap: 'Monte Carlo integration over a curved region by sampling points in an enclosing rectangle.'
#| fig-width: 7
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(14)
N = 6000
x = rng.uniform(0, 1, N)
y = rng.uniform(0, 4, N)
inside = (y >= 3*x) & (y <= 4 - x**2)

# Estimate integral of f(x,y)=2xy over D from Example 14.1
values = 2*x*y*inside
estimate = 4*np.mean(values)  # enclosing rectangle area = 4
exact = 47/12

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 5))
ax.scatter(x[~inside], y[~inside], s=4, alpha=0.15, label='outside')
ax.scatter(x[inside], y[inside], s=4, alpha=0.25, label='inside')
xx = np.linspace(0, 1, 300)
ax.plot(xx, 3*xx, color='black')
ax.plot(xx, 4-xx**2, color='black')
ax.set_xlabel('$x$')
ax.set_ylabel('$y$')
ax.set_title(f'Monte Carlo estimate: {estimate:.4f}; exact: {exact:.4f}')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)
ax.legend()
plt.show()
```

Monte Carlo integration is not usually the most accurate method for low-dimensional smooth problems. However, it scales naturally to high-dimensional problems, which is why it is central in statistics, Bayesian computation, and machine learning.

## 14.12 Computational workflow in Python

For symbolic or numerical work, the region should be encoded explicitly. The following code computes the integral from Example 14.1 using nested numerical quadrature from `scipy` if available, and falls back to a grid approximation if not.

```{python}
#| label: ch14-computational-check
#| fig-cap: 'Numerical verification of the integral over a Type I region.'
#| fig-width: 6.5
import numpy as np

try:
    from scipy.integrate import dblquad
    val, err = dblquad(
        lambda y, x: 2*x*y,
        0, 1,
        lambda x: 3*x,
        lambda x: 4 - x**2
    )
    print('scipy dblquad value:', val)
    print('estimated numerical error:', err)
except Exception as exc:
    print('scipy unavailable; using midpoint grid approximation')
    m = 800
    xs = (np.arange(m) + 0.5)/m
    total = 0.0
    for x0 in xs:
        y0, y1 = 3*x0, 4 - x0**2
        height = y1 - y0
        # midpoint in y direction
        ym = 0.5*(y0 + y1)
        total += 2*x0*ym*height*(1/m)
    print('grid approximation:', total)

print('exact value:', 47/12)
```

::: {.callout-warning}
## A common implementation mistake

When using numerical integration libraries, check the order of arguments. Some libraries call the integrand as `f(y, x)` for an integral written in the order $dy\,dx$. The mathematical order and the software argument order do not always look the same.
:::

## 14.13 Checklist for setting up a double integral

When solving a double-integral problem over a general region, use this checklist.

1. **Sketch the region.** Mark all boundary curves.
2. **Find intersection points.** These determine the outer limits.
3. **Choose vertical or horizontal slices.** Decide whether the region is Type I, Type II, or must be split.
4. **Write the inequalities.** Do not compute until the region is correct.
5. **Check lower/upper or left/right boundaries.** Test a sample point if needed.
6. **Set up the integral.** Put the inner variable in the inner differential.
7. **Simplify before integrating.** Sometimes reversing order is better.
8. **Interpret the result.** Is it area, volume, mass, average value, probability, or signed accumulation?

## 14.14 Modern applications

### Statistics and probability

A joint density over a nonrectangular region appears when variables are constrained. Examples include:

- $0\le y\le x\le 1$ for ordered random variables;
- $x+y\le 1$ for compositional data;
- $y\le x^2$ for nonlinear events;
- feasible regions in Bayesian priors.

The probability of an event is a double integral over the event region.

### Machine learning

Many models depend on two or more parameters. A double integral over a parameter region can represent average loss, expected regularized risk, or uncertainty over a feasible parameter set.

For example, if $L(\theta_1,\theta_2)$ is a loss function and $D$ is a feasible set, then

$$
\frac{1}{\operatorname{Area}(D)}\iint_D L(\theta_1,\theta_2)\,dA
$$

is the average loss over $D$.

### Applied mathematics

In applied mathematics, regions are often determined by physical constraints: a membrane shape, a flow region, an obstacle boundary, a material interface, or a domain for a partial differential equation. Double integrals over such regions compute total mass, energy, heat, charge, and probability.

## Chapter summary

A double integral over a general region adds a function over a curved or constrained two-dimensional domain. The central task is translating geometry into limits.

- A Type I region has the form

$$
D=\{(x,y):a\le x\le b,\ p(x)\le y\le q(x)\}.
$$

Then

$$
\iint_D f(x,y)\,dA
=
\int_a^b\int_{p(x)}^{q(x)} f(x,y)\,dy\,dx.
$$

- A Type II region has the form

$$
D=\{(x,y):c\le y\le d,\ g(y)\le x\le h(y)\}.
$$

Then

$$
\iint_D f(x,y)\,dA
=
\int_c^d\int_{g(y)}^{h(y)} f(x,y)\,dx\,dy.
$$

- Area is computed by

$$
\operatorname{Area}(D)=\iint_D 1\,dA.
$$

- Regions can be split:

$$
\iint_D f\,dA
=
\iint_{D_1} f\,dA+
\iint_{D_2} f\,dA.
$$

- Reversing order can turn a difficult integral into an elementary one.

The strongest students do not memorize limits. They learn to see slices.
