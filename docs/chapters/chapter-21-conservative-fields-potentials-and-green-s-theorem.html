21  Chapter 21. Conservative Fields, Potentials, and Green’s Theorem

Path independence, circulation, flux, and the bridge from local derivatives to global integrals

The previous chapter introduced line integrals. We learned how to integrate a scalar field along a curve and how to integrate a vector field along an oriented path. The main question now becomes more structural:

When does a line integral depend only on the endpoints, and when does it depend on the whole path?

This question is not merely a computational shortcut. It is one of the conceptual centers of vector calculus. In physics, it separates conservative forces from forces with circulation. In geometry, it connects local derivatives to global behavior around closed curves. In optimization, it explains why gradients of loss functions behave differently from rotational vector fields. In data science and machine learning, it gives a language for understanding energy functions, potential landscapes, and dynamics that are or are not driven by a scalar objective.

This chapter develops two major results:

  1. the Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals, which says that line integrals of gradient fields depend only on endpoints;
  2. Green’s Theorem, which converts a line integral around a closed curve into a double integral over the enclosed region.

Together, they form the two-dimensional foundation for Stokes’ theorem and the divergence theorem.

21.1 Learning goals

After studying this chapter, you should be able to:

  1. recognize gradient fields and conservative vector fields;
  2. find potential functions for conservative fields;
  3. use the Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals;
  4. understand path independence and closed-loop integrals;
  5. test whether a plane vector field is conservative on a suitable domain;
  6. state and apply Green’s theorem in circulation form;
  7. use Green’s theorem to compute areas and circulations;
  8. understand the role of orientation, holes, and singularities;
  9. connect conservative fields and circulation to optimization, probability flow, and learning dynamics.
NoteThe main theme

A derivative is local: it describes behavior near one point. An integral is global: it accumulates behavior over a curve or a region. This chapter explains when local derivative information completely controls global line integrals.

21.2 Gradient fields and conservative vector fields

A scalar function

\[ f(x,y) \]

has gradient field

\[ \nabla f(x,y)=\langle f_x(x,y),f_y(x,y)\rangle. \]

A scalar function

\[ f(x,y,z) \]

has gradient field

\[ \nabla f(x,y,z)=\langle f_x(x,y,z),f_y(x,y,z),f_z(x,y,z)\rangle. \]

A vector field \(F\) is called conservative if it is the gradient of some scalar function \(f\):

\[ F=\nabla f. \]

The function \(f\) is called a potential function for \(F\).

This terminology is natural in physical examples. A gravitational or electrostatic field often points in the direction in which potential energy changes most rapidly. In optimization, the vector field \(-\nabla L\) points in the direction of steepest decrease of the loss function \(L\).

ImportantPotential function

If

\[ F=\langle P,Q\rangle \]

is conservative in the plane, then there is a scalar function \(f\) such that

\[ P=f_x, \qquad Q=f_y. \]

The scalar function \(f\) is the potential. The vector field \(F\) is the gradient field generated by \(f\).

import numpy as np

21.3 The Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals

The one-variable Fundamental Theorem of Calculus says that if \(F'(x)=f(x)\), then

\[ \int_a^b f(x)\,dx=F(b)-F(a). \]

The multivariable version has the same spirit. If a vector field is a gradient field, then its line integral is the change in the potential.

TipFundamental Theorem for Line Integrals

Let \(C\) be a smooth curve from point \(A\) to point \(B\), and let \(f\) be differentiable on a region containing \(C\). Then

\[ \int_C \nabla f\cdot dr=f(B)-f(A). \]

Therefore, the integral depends only on the endpoints, not on the path.

21.3.1 Why the theorem is true

Suppose \(C\) is parameterized by

\[ r(t)=\langle x(t),y(t)\rangle, \qquad a\le t\le b. \]

Then

\[ \int_C \nabla f\cdot dr =\int_a^b \nabla f(r(t))\cdot r'(t)\,dt. \]

By the chain rule,

\[ \frac{d}{dt}f(r(t))=\nabla f(r(t))\cdot r'(t). \]

Therefore,

\[ \int_C \nabla f\cdot dr =\int_a^b \frac{d}{dt}f(r(t))\,dt =f(r(b))-f(r(a)). \]

The theorem is just the chain rule plus the one-variable Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.

21.3.2 Worked example: use a potential instead of parameterizing the path

Let

\[ F(x,y)=\langle 2xy, x^2+3y^2\rangle. \]

Compute

\[ \int_C F\cdot dr \]

for any smooth curve \(C\) from \(A=(1,0)\) to \(B=(2,1)\).

We look for a potential \(f\) such that

\[ f_x=2xy, \qquad f_y=x^2+3y^2. \]

Integrating \(f_x=2xy\) with respect to \(x\) gives

\[ f(x,y)=x^2y+g(y). \]

Then

\[ f_y=x^2+g'(y). \]

Matching \(f_y=x^2+3y^2\) gives

\[ g'(y)=3y^2, \qquad g(y)=y^3+C. \]

Thus one potential is

\[ f(x,y)=x^2y+y^3. \]

By the Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals,

\[ \int_C F\cdot dr=f(2,1)-f(1,0)=(4+1)-0=5. \]

No parameterization of \(C\) is needed.

21.4 Path independence

A vector line integral is path independent if any two curves with the same starting point and ending point give the same value.

For a conservative field,

\[ \int_C F\cdot dr=f(B)-f(A), \]

so the integral is path independent.

The converse is also true under standard connectedness assumptions: if line integrals of \(F\) are path independent on a region, then \(F\) is conservative on that region.

ImportantEquivalent ideas

For a well-behaved vector field on a suitable region, the following ideas are essentially equivalent:

  1. \(F\) is conservative.
  2. \(F=\nabla f\) for some potential function \(f\).
  3. \(\int_C F\cdot dr\) depends only on endpoints.
  4. \(\int_C F\cdot dr=0\) for every closed curve \(C\).
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

A = np.array([0.0, 0.0])
B = np.array([2.0, 1.2])
t = np.linspace(0, 1, 300)
path1 = A[:, None]*(1-t) + B[:, None]*t
path2 = np.vstack([2*t, 1.2*t + 0.55*np.sin(np.pi*t)])

x = np.linspace(-0.3, 2.3, 23)
y = np.linspace(-0.3, 1.7, 23)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)
U = 2*X*Y

ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')

21.4.1 Numerical check: two paths, one answer

For

\[ F(x,y)=\langle 2xy,x^2+3y^2\rangle=\nabla(x^2y+y^3), \]

the line integral from \((0,0)\) to \((2,1.2)\) should equal

\[ f(2,1.2)-f(0,0). \]

The code below approximates the integral along two different paths.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

N = 3000
t = np.linspace(0, 1, N)

def F(x, y):
    return 2*x*y, x**2 + 3*y**2

def line_integral(x, y):
    dxdt = np.gradient(x, t)
    dydt = np.gradient(y, t)
    P, Q = F(x, y)
    return np.trapz(P*dxdt + Q*dydt, t)

x1 = 2*t
y1 = 1.2*t

x2 = 2*t
y2 = 1.2*t + 0.55*np.sin(np.pi*t)

I1 = line_integral(x1, y1)
I2 = line_integral(x2, y2)
exact = (2**2)*(1.2) + 1.2**3

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
labels = ['straight path', 'curved path', 'endpoint formula']
values = [I1, I2, exact]
ax.bar(labels, values)
ax.set_ylabel('line integral value')
ax.set_title('The two path integrals agree')
ax.tick_params(axis='x', rotation=15)
plt.show()

print(f'Straight path approximation: {I1:.8f}')
print(f'Curved path approximation:   {I2:.8f}')
print(f'Endpoint formula:            {exact:.8f}')
Straight path approximation: 6.52800036
Curved path approximation:   6.52800066
Endpoint formula:            6.52800000

21.5 How to find a potential function

Suppose

\[ F(x,y)=\langle P(x,y),Q(x,y)\rangle. \]

To find a potential function \(f\):

  1. integrate \(P=f_x\) with respect to \(x\);
  2. include an unknown function of \(y\);
  3. differentiate the result with respect to \(y\);
  4. compare with \(Q=f_y\);
  5. solve for the unknown function.

21.5.1 Worked example: a conservative field

Let

\[ F(x,y)=\langle y\cos(xy)+2x, x\cos(xy)+4y^3\rangle. \]

We want \(f_x=y\cos(xy)+2x\). Integrating with respect to \(x\) gives

\[ f(x,y)=\sin(xy)+x^2+g(y). \]

Then

\[ f_y=x\cos(xy)+g'(y). \]

Matching the second component gives

\[ g'(y)=4y^3, \qquad g(y)=y^4+C. \]

So one potential is

\[ f(x,y)=\sin(xy)+x^2+y^4. \]

21.6 The component test in the plane

For a plane vector field

\[ F=\langle P,Q\rangle, \]

if \(F=\nabla f\), then

\[ P=f_x, \qquad Q=f_y. \]

If the second partial derivatives are continuous, then

\[ P_y=f_{xy}=f_{yx}=Q_x. \]

Therefore, every conservative field satisfies

\[ P_y=Q_x. \]

On a simply connected region, this condition is also sufficient.

WarningA necessary condition is not always sufficient

The equation \(P_y=Q_x\) is a local test. It guarantees conservativeness only when the domain has no holes and the field is defined throughout the region. Singularities and holes can create nonzero circulation even when \(P_y=Q_x\) away from the singularity.

21.6.1 Worked example: not conservative

Let

\[ F(x,y)=\langle -y,x\rangle. \]

Here

\[ P=-y, \qquad Q=x. \]

Then

\[ P_y=-1, \qquad Q_x=1. \]

Since \(P_y\ne Q_x\), the field is not conservative.

This field rotates counterclockwise around the origin. Closed-loop integrals are generally nonzero.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x = np.linspace(-2, 2, 21)
y = np.linspace(-2, 2, 21)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)

U1, V1 = X, Y
U2, V2 = -Y, X

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(10, 4.4))
for ax, U, V, title in [
    (axes[0], U1, V1, 'Conservative radial field'),
    (axes[1], U2, V2, 'Rotational field')
]:
    speed = np.hypot(U, V)
    ax.quiver(X, Y, U/(1+speed), V/(1+speed), angles='xy')
    theta = np.linspace(0, 2*np.pi, 300)
    ax.plot(np.cos(theta), np.sin(theta), linewidth=2)
    ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
    ax.set_title(title)
    ax.set_xlabel(r'$x$')
    ax.set_ylabel(r'$y$')
    ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)
plt.show()

21.7 Closed curves and circulation

A curve \(C\) is closed if its endpoint is the same as its starting point. For a plane field

\[ F=\langle P,Q\rangle, \]

the line integral around a closed curve is often written

\[ \oint_C F\cdot dr = \oint_C P\,dx+Q\,dy. \]

This is called the circulation of the vector field around \(C\).

If \(F\) is conservative, then every closed-loop integral is zero:

\[ \oint_C \nabla f\cdot dr=0. \]

If a closed-loop integral is not zero, then the field cannot be conservative.

21.7.1 Worked example: circulation around a circle

Let

\[ F(x,y)=\langle -y,x\rangle, \]

and let \(C\) be the circle

\[ r(t)=\langle R\cos t,R\sin t\rangle, \qquad 0\le t\le 2\pi. \]

Then

\[ dr=\langle -R\sin t,R\cos t\rangle\,dt, \]

and

\[ F(r(t))=\langle -R\sin t,R\cos t\rangle. \]

Thus

\[ F(r(t))\cdot r'(t)=R^2\sin^2 t+R^2\cos^2 t=R^2. \]

Therefore,

\[ \oint_C F\cdot dr=\int_0^{2\pi}R^2\,dt=2\pi R^2. \]

The circulation is twice the area of the disk.

21.8 Green’s theorem: circulation form

Green’s theorem is a two-dimensional result that converts circulation around a boundary curve into a double integral over the enclosed region.

Let \(D\) be a plane region with positively oriented boundary curve \(C\). Positive orientation means that the region stays on the left as the curve is traversed; for a simple outer boundary, this is counterclockwise orientation.

TipGreen’s Theorem: circulation form

Let

\[ F=\langle P,Q\rangle \]

where \(P\) and \(Q\) have continuous first partial derivatives on a region containing \(D\). If \(C\) is the positively oriented boundary of \(D\), then

\[ \oint_C P\,dx+Q\,dy = \iint_D \left(Q_x-P_y\right)\,dA. \]

The quantity

\[ Q_x-P_y \]

is the two-dimensional scalar curl of the field. Green’s theorem says:

Circulation around the boundary equals total curl inside the region.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

t = np.linspace(0, 2*np.pi, 500)
r = 1.0 + 0.18*np.cos(3*t) + 0.12*np.sin(5*t)
x = r*np.cos(t)
y = r*np.sin(t)

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.fill(x, y, alpha=0.22)
ax.plot(x, y, linewidth=2)
for s in np.linspace(0.2, 5.8, 12):
    rs = 1.0 + 0.18*np.cos(3*s) + 0.12*np.sin(5*s)
    xs, ys = rs*np.cos(s), rs*np.sin(s)
    dxds = (-0.54*np.sin(3*s) + 0.60*np.cos(5*s))*np.cos(s) - rs*np.sin(s)
    dyds = (-0.54*np.sin(3*s) + 0.60*np.cos(5*s))*np.sin(s) + rs*np.cos(s)
    norm = np.hypot(dxds, dyds)
    ax.arrow(xs, ys, 0.12*dxds/norm, 0.12*dyds/norm,
             head_width=0.04, length_includes_head=True)
ax.text(0, 0, r'$D$', ha='center', va='center', fontsize=14)
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
ax.set_xlabel(r'$x$')
ax.set_ylabel(r'$y$')
ax.set_title('Positive orientation: region on the left')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)
plt.show()

21.8.1 Worked example: use Green’s theorem on a rectangle

Let

\[ F(x,y)=\langle -y,x\rangle. \]

Compute

\[ \oint_C F\cdot dr \]

where \(C\) is the positively oriented boundary of the rectangle

\[ 0\le x\le a, \qquad 0\le y\le b. \]

Here

\[ P=-y, \qquad Q=x. \]

Then

\[ Q_x-P_y=1-(-1)=2. \]

Therefore,

\[ \oint_C -y\,dx+x\,dy = \iint_D 2\,dA =2ab. \]

The answer is twice the area of the rectangle.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

a, b = 2.4, 1.3
N = 500

# Counterclockwise rectangle parameterized piecewise by sampled points.
x_bottom = np.linspace(0, a, N)
y_bottom = np.zeros(N)
x_right = a*np.ones(N)
y_right = np.linspace(0, b, N)
x_top = np.linspace(a, 0, N)
y_top = b*np.ones(N)
x_left = np.zeros(N)
y_left = np.linspace(b, 0, N)

x = np.concatenate([x_bottom, x_right, x_top, x_left])
y = np.concatenate([y_bottom, y_right, y_top, y_left])

dx = np.diff(x)
dy = np.diff(y)
x_mid = 0.5*(x[:-1] + x[1:])
y_mid = 0.5*(y[:-1] + y[1:])
P_mid = -y_mid
Q_mid = x_mid
line_approx = np.sum(P_mid*dx + Q_mid*dy)
green_value = 2*a*b

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.plot(x, y, linewidth=2)
ax.fill(x, y, alpha=0.18)
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
ax.set_xlabel(r'$x$')
ax.set_ylabel(r'$y$')
ax.set_title('Rectangle boundary')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)
plt.show()

print(f'Boundary line integral approximation: {line_approx:.8f}')
print(f'Green theorem value:                 {green_value:.8f}')
Boundary line integral approximation: 6.24000000
Green theorem value:                 6.24000000

21.9 Area from a line integral

Green’s theorem can be used to compute area. If we choose \(P\) and \(Q\) so that

\[ Q_x-P_y=1, \]

then

\[ \text{Area}(D)=\iint_D 1\,dA = \oint_C P\,dx+Q\,dy. \]

Three common choices are:

\[ P=0, \quad Q=x, \qquad \text{so} \qquad \text{Area}(D)=\oint_C x\,dy. \]

\[ P=-y, \quad Q=0, \qquad \text{so} \qquad \text{Area}(D)=\oint_C -y\,dx. \]

\[ P=-\frac{y}{2}, \quad Q=\frac{x}{2}, \qquad \text{so} \qquad \text{Area}(D)=\frac12\oint_C x\,dy-y\,dx. \]

The last formula is especially symmetric.

21.9.1 Worked example: area of an ellipse

Let \(C\) be the ellipse

\[ r(t)=\langle a\cos t,b\sin t\rangle, \qquad 0\le t\le 2\pi. \]

Using

\[ \text{Area}(D)=\frac12\oint_C x\,dy-y\,dx, \]

we have

\[ x=a\cos t, \qquad y=b\sin t, \]

\[ dx=-a\sin t\,dt, \qquad dy=b\cos t\,dt. \]

Thus

\[ x\,dy-y\,dx =ab\cos^2t\,dt+ab\sin^2t\,dt =ab\,dt. \]

Therefore,

\[ \text{Area}(D)=\frac12\int_0^{2\pi}ab\,dt=\pi ab. \]

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

t = np.linspace(0, 2*np.pi, 1000)
a, b = 2.2, 1.1
x = a*np.cos(t)
y = b*np.sin(t)

dxdt = np.gradient(x, t)
dydt = np.gradient(y, t)
area_approx = 0.5*np.trapz(x*dydt - y*dxdt, t)
area_exact = np.pi*a*b

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.plot(x, y, linewidth=2)
ax.fill(x, y, alpha=0.2)
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
ax.set_xlabel(r'$x$')
ax.set_ylabel(r'$y$')
ax.set_title('Area of an ellipse from a boundary integral')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)
plt.show()

print(f'Boundary-area approximation: {area_approx:.8f}')
print(f'Exact area:                  {area_exact:.8f}')
Boundary-area approximation: 7.60260410
Exact area:                  7.60265422

21.10 Green’s theorem: flux form

Green’s theorem also has a flux form. Let

\[ F=\langle P,Q\rangle. \]

The outward flux across \(C\) is

\[ \oint_C F\cdot n\,ds, \]

where \(n\) is the outward unit normal. For a positively oriented plane curve, the outward normal satisfies

\[ n\,ds=\langle dy,-dx\rangle. \]

Therefore,

\[ F\cdot n\,ds=P\,dy-Q\,dx. \]

Green’s theorem in flux form is

\[ \oint_C F\cdot n\,ds = \iint_D (P_x+Q_y)\,dA. \]

The quantity

\[ P_x+Q_y \]

is the two-dimensional divergence of \(F\). Thus:

Outward flux through the boundary equals total divergence inside the region.

This result foreshadows the divergence theorem in three dimensions.

21.10.1 Worked example: outward flux of a radial field

Let

\[ F(x,y)=\langle x,y\rangle. \]

Then

\[ \operatorname{div}F=P_x+Q_y=1+1=2. \]

If \(C\) bounds a region \(D\), then

\[ \oint_C F\cdot n\,ds=\iint_D 2\,dA=2\operatorname{Area}(D). \]

For a disk of radius \(R\), this gives

\[ \oint_C F\cdot n\,ds=2\pi R^2. \]

21.11 Holes, singularities, and why hypotheses matter

The field

\[ F(x,y)=\left\langle \frac{-y}{x^2+y^2},\frac{x}{x^2+y^2}\right\rangle \]

is defined everywhere except the origin. Away from the origin, one can check that

\[ P_y=Q_x. \]

However, the field is not conservative on any region that loops around the origin. On the unit circle

\[ r(t)=\langle \cos t,\sin t\rangle, \]

we have

\[ F(r(t))=\langle -\sin t,\cos t\rangle=r'(t), \]

so

\[ \oint_C F\cdot dr=\int_0^{2\pi}1\,dt=2\pi. \]

The origin is a singularity. The domain has a hole. This example is a warning that local derivative tests do not always determine global behavior.

import numpy as np

21.12 Numerical curl and discrete Green’s theorem intuition

Green’s theorem says that circulation around a boundary measures accumulated curl inside. In computational settings, one often estimates derivatives on a grid. The scalar curl of a plane vector field

\[ F=\langle P,Q\rangle \]

is

\[ \operatorname{curl}_{2D}F=Q_x-P_y. \]

A gradient field has zero scalar curl when second partial derivatives commute. A rotational field has visible nonzero scalar curl.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x = np.linspace(-2, 2, 90)

# Gradient field of phi = x^2 y + y^3.

Q_grad = X**2 + 3*Y**2

# Rotational field.
P_rot = -Y
Q_rot = X

def scalar_curl(P, Q):
    Q_y_index, Q_x_index = np.gradient(Q, dy, dx)
    P_y_index, P_x_index = np.gradient(P, dy, dx)

21.13 Modern interpretation: potentials, losses, and dynamics

In applied mathematics, statistics, data science, and machine learning, a scalar objective function is often the central object:

\[ L(\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_n). \]

The gradient field

\[ \nabla L \]

points in the direction of steepest increase, while

\[ -\nabla L \]

points in the direction of steepest decrease. Gradient descent follows a trajectory determined by a conservative field:

\[ \frac{d\theta}{dt}=-\nabla L(\theta). \]

Along such a path,

\[ \frac{d}{dt}L(\theta(t))=\nabla L(\theta(t))\cdot \theta'(t) =-|\nabla L(\theta(t))|^2\le 0. \]

So a pure gradient flow cannot cycle around a closed loop while strictly decreasing the loss. In contrast, a rotational component can create circulation and oscillation. This distinction matters in dynamical systems, adversarial optimization, non-equilibrium probability flows, and training dynamics for models with coupled parameters.

NoteConservative versus rotational learning dynamics

A pure gradient field comes from a scalar objective. A rotational field does not. Many modern algorithms can be viewed as combining gradient-like motion with rotational or momentum-like effects. Green’s theorem gives a mathematical language for detecting and measuring such circulation in two-dimensional slices of a high-dimensional system.

21.14 Problem-solving guide

When faced with a line integral of a plane vector field:

  1. Check whether the field is conservative. Try to find a potential function.
  2. If the curve is open and the field is conservative, use endpoints.
  3. If the curve is closed, check Green’s theorem. The boundary integral may become an easier double integral.
  4. Check orientation. Counterclockwise outer boundaries are positive. Reversing orientation changes the sign.
  5. Check the domain. Holes or singularities can invalidate a simple conservative-field conclusion.
  6. Choose the easier representation. Some problems are easier as line integrals; others are easier as double integrals.

21.15 Common mistakes

WarningWatch for these errors
  • Treating \(P_y=Q_x\) as sufficient without checking the domain.
  • Forgetting that Green’s theorem requires a closed boundary curve.
  • Using the wrong orientation and missing a minus sign.
  • Confusing circulation form with flux form.
  • Forgetting that a potential function is determined only up to an additive constant.
  • Applying Green’s theorem across a singularity where the field is not defined.

The previous chapter introduced line integrals.  We learned how to integrate a scalar field along a curve and how to integrate a vector field along an oriented path.  The main question now becomes more structural:

> When does a line integral depend only on the endpoints, and when does it depend on the whole path?

This question is not merely a computational shortcut.  It is one of the conceptual centers of vector calculus.  In physics, it separates conservative forces from forces with circulation. ## Gradient fields and conservative vector fields

A scalar function

$$
f(x,y)
$$

has gradient field

$$
\nabla f(x,y)=\langle f_x(x,y),f_y(x,y)\rangle.
$$

A scalar function

$$
f(x,y,z)
$$

has gradient field

$$
\nabla f(x,y,z)=\langle f_x(x,y,z),f_y(x,y,z),f_z(x,y,z)\rangle.
$$

A vector field $F$ is called **conservative** if it is the gradient of some scalar function $f$:

$$
F=\nabla f.
$$

The function $f$ is called a **potential function** for $F$.

This terminology is natural in physical examples.  A gravitational or electrostatic field often points in the direction in which potential energy changes most rapidly.  In optimization, the vector field $-\nabla L$ points in the direction of steepest decrease of the loss function $L$.

::: {.callout-important}
## Potential function

If

$$
F=\langle P,Q\rangle
$$

is conservative in the plane, then there is a scalar function $f$ such that

$$
P=f_x,
\qquad
Q=f_y.
$$

The scalar function $f$ is the potential.  The vector field $F$ is the gradient field generated by $f$.
:::

## The Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals

The one-variable Fundamental Theorem of Calculus says that if $F'(x)=f(x)$, then

$$
\int_a^b f(x)\,dx=F(b)-F(a).
$$

The multivariable version has the same spirit.  If a vector field is a gradient field, then its line integral is the change in the potential.

::: {.callout-tip}
## Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals

Let $C$ be a smooth curve from point $A$ to point $B$, and let $f$ be differentiable on a region containing $C$.  Then

$$
\int_C \nabla f\cdot dr=f(B)-f(A).
$$

Therefore, the integral depends only on the endpoints, not on the path.
:::

### Why the theorem is true

Suppose $C$ is parameterized by

$$
r(t)=\langle x(t),y(t)\rangle,
\qquad a\le t\le b.
$$

Then

$$
\int_C \nabla f\cdot dr
=\int_a^b \nabla f(r(t))\cdot r'(t)\,dt.
$$

By the chain rule,

$$
\frac{d}{dt}f(r(t))=\nabla f(r(t))\cdot r'(t).
$$

Therefore,

$$
\int_C \nabla f\cdot dr
=\int_a^b \frac{d}{dt}f(r(t))\,dt
=f(r(b))-f(r(a)).
$$

The theorem is just the chain rule plus the one-variable Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.

### Worked example: use a potential instead of parameterizing the path

Let

$$
F(x,y)=\langle 2xy, x^2+3y^2\rangle.
$$

Compute

$$
\int_C F\cdot dr
$$

for any smooth curve $C$ from $A=(1,0)$ to $B=(2,1)$.

We look for a potential $f$ such that

$$
f_x=2xy,
\qquad
f_y=x^2+3y^2.
$$

Integrating $f_x=2xy$ with respect to $x$ gives

$$
f(x,y)=x^2y+g(y).
$$

Then

$$
f_y=x^2+g'(y).
$$

Matching $f_y=x^2+3y^2$ gives

$$
g'(y)=3y^2,
\qquad
 g(y)=y^3+C.
$$

Thus one potential is

$$
f(x,y)=x^2y+y^3.
$$

By the Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals,

$$
\int_C F\cdot dr=f(2,1)-f(1,0)=(4+1)-0=5.
$$

No parameterization of $C$ is needed.

## Path independence

A vector line integral is **path independent** if any two curves with the same starting point and ending point give the same value.

For a conservative field,

$$
\int_C F\cdot dr=f(B)-f(A),
$$

so the integral is path independent.

The converse is also true under standard connectedness assumptions: if line integrals of $F$ are path independent on a region, then $F$ is conservative on that region.

::: {.callout-important}
## Equivalent ideas

For a well-behaved vector field on a suitable region, the following ideas are essentially equivalent:

1. $F$ is conservative.
2. $F=\nabla f$ for some potential function $f$.
3. $\int_C F\cdot dr$ depends only on endpoints.
4. $\int_C F\cdot dr=0$ for every closed curve $C$.
:::

## How to find a potential function

Suppose

$$
F(x,y)=\langle P(x,y),Q(x,y)\rangle.
$$

To find a potential function $f$:

1. integrate $P=f_x$ with respect to $x$;
2. include an unknown function of $y$;
3. differentiate the result with respect to $y$;
4. compare with $Q=f_y$;
5. solve for the unknown function.

### Worked example: a conservative field

Let

$$
F(x,y)=\langle y\cos(xy)+2x, x\cos(xy)+4y^3\rangle.
$$

We want $f_x=y\cos(xy)+2x$.  Integrating with respect to $x$ gives

$$
f(x,y)=\sin(xy)+x^2+g(y).
$$

Then

$$
f_y=x\cos(xy)+g'(y).
$$

Matching the second component gives

$$
g'(y)=4y^3,
\qquad
 g(y)=y^4+C.
$$

So one potential is

$$
f(x,y)=\sin(xy)+x^2+y^4.
$$

## The component test in the plane

For a plane vector field

$$
F=\langle P,Q\rangle,
$$

if $F=\nabla f$, then

$$
P=f_x,
\qquad
Q=f_y.
$$

If the second partial derivatives are continuous, then

$$
P_y=f_{xy}=f_{yx}=Q_x.
$$

Therefore, every conservative field satisfies

$$
P_y=Q_x.
$$

On a simply connected region, this condition is also sufficient.

::: {.callout-warning}
## A necessary condition is not always sufficient

The equation $P_y=Q_x$ is a local test.  It guarantees conservativeness only when the domain has no holes and the field is defined throughout the region.  Singularities and holes can create nonzero circulation even when $P_y=Q_x$ away from the singularity.
:::

### Worked example: not conservative

Let

$$
F(x,y)=\langle -y,x\rangle.
$$

Here

$$
P=-y,
\qquad
Q=x.
$$

Then

$$
P_y=-1,
\qquad
Q_x=1.
$$

Since $P_y\ne Q_x$, the field is not conservative.

This field rotates counterclockwise around the origin.  Closed-loop integrals are generally nonzero.

## Closed curves and circulation

A curve $C$ is **closed** if its endpoint is the same as its starting point.  For a plane field

$$
F=\langle P,Q\rangle,
$$

the line integral around a closed curve is often written

$$
\oint_C F\cdot dr
=
\oint_C P\,dx+Q\,dy.
$$

This is called the **circulation** of the vector field around $C$.

If $F$ is conservative, then every closed-loop integral is zero:

$$
\oint_C \nabla f\cdot dr=0.
$$

If a closed-loop integral is not zero, then the field cannot be conservative.

### Worked example: circulation around a circle

Let

$$
F(x,y)=\langle -y,x\rangle,
$$

and let $C$ be the circle

$$
r(t)=\langle R\cos t,R\sin t\rangle,
\qquad
0\le t\le 2\pi.
$$

Then

$$
dr=\langle -R\sin t,R\cos t\rangle\,dt,
$$

and

$$
F(r(t))=\langle -R\sin t,R\cos t\rangle.
$$

Thus

$$
F(r(t))\cdot r'(t)=R^2\sin^2 t+R^2\cos^2 t=R^2.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\oint_C F\cdot dr=\int_0^{2\pi}R^2\,dt=2\pi R^2.
$$

The circulation is twice the area of the disk.

## Green's theorem: circulation form

Green's theorem is a two-dimensional result that converts circulation around a boundary curve into a double integral over the enclosed region.

Let $D$ be a plane region with positively oriented boundary curve $C$.  Positive orientation means that the region stays on the left as the curve is traversed; for a simple outer boundary, this is counterclockwise orientation.

::: {.callout-tip}
## Green's Theorem: circulation form

Let

$$
F=\langle P,Q\rangle
$$

where $P$ and $Q$ have continuous first partial derivatives on a region containing $D$.  If $C$ is the positively oriented boundary of $D$, then

$$
\oint_C P\,dx+Q\,dy
=
\iint_D \left(Q_x-P_y\right)\,dA.
$$
:::

The quantity

$$
Q_x-P_y
$$

is the two-dimensional scalar curl of the field.  Green's theorem says:

> Circulation around the boundary equals total curl inside the region.

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch21-green-theorem-region
#| fig-cap: 'Green’s theorem relates circulation around a positively oriented boundary to total curl over the enclosed region.'
#| fig-width: 7
#| fig-height: 5
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

t = np.linspace(0, 2*np.pi, 500)
r = 1.0 + 0.18*np.cos(3*t) + 0.12*np.sin(5*t)
x = r*np.cos(t)
y = r*np.sin(t)

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.fill(x, y, alpha=0.22)
ax.plot(x, y, linewidth=2)
for s in np.linspace(0.2, 5.8, 12):
    rs = 1.0 + 0.18*np.cos(3*s) + 0.12*np.sin(5*s)
    xs, ys = rs*np.cos(s), rs*np.sin(s)
    dxds = (-0.54*np.sin(3*s) + 0.60*np.cos(5*s))*np.cos(s) - rs*np.sin(s)
    dyds = (-0.54*np.sin(3*s) + 0.60*np.cos(5*s))*np.sin(s) + rs*np.cos(s)
    norm = np.hypot(dxds, dyds)
    ax.arrow(xs, ys, 0.12*dxds/norm, 0.12*dyds/norm,
             head_width=0.04, length_includes_head=True)
ax.text(0, 0, r'$D$', ha='center', va='center', fontsize=14)
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
ax.set_xlabel(r'$x$')
ax.set_ylabel(r'$y$')
ax.set_title('Positive orientation: region on the left')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)
plt.show()
```

### Worked example: use Green's theorem on a rectangle

Let

$$
F(x,y)=\langle -y,x\rangle.
$$

Compute

$$
\oint_C F\cdot dr
$$

where $C$ is the positively oriented boundary of the rectangle

$$
0\le x\le a,
\qquad
0\le y\le b.
$$

Here

$$
P=-y,
\qquad
Q=x.
$$

Then

$$
Q_x-P_y=1-(-1)=2.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\oint_C -y\,dx+x\,dy
=
\iint_D 2\,dA
=2ab.
$$

The answer is twice the area of the rectangle.

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch21-green-rectangle-numerical
#| fig-cap: 'Numerical verification of Green’s theorem for the field $F=\langle -y,x\rangle$ on a rectangle.'
#| fig-width: 6.5
#| fig-height: 4.5
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

a, b = 2.4, 1.3
N = 500

# Counterclockwise rectangle parameterized piecewise by sampled points.
x_bottom = np.linspace(0, a, N)
y_bottom = np.zeros(N)
x_right = a*np.ones(N)

dx = np.diff(x)
dy = np.diff(y)
x_mid = 0.5*(x[:-1] + x[1:])
y_mid = 0.5*(y[:-1] + y[1:])
P_mid = -y_mid
Q_mid = x_mid
line_approx = np.sum(P_mid*dx + Q_mid*dy)
green_value = 2*a*b

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
```

## Area from a line integral

Green's theorem can be used to compute area.  If we choose $P$ and $Q$ so that

$$
Q_x-P_y=1,
$$

then

$$
\text{Area}(D)=\iint_D 1\,dA
=
\oint_C P\,dx+Q\,dy.
$$

Three common choices are:

$$
P=0,
\quad
Q=x,
\qquad
\text{so}
\qquad
\text{Area}(D)=\oint_C x\,dy.
$$

$$
P=-y,
\quad
Q=0,
\qquad
\text{so}
\qquad
\text{Area}(D)=\oint_C -y\,dx.
$$

$$
P=-\frac{y}{2},
\quad
Q=\frac{x}{2},
\qquad
\text{so}
\qquad
\text{Area}(D)=\frac12\oint_C x\,dy-y\,dx.
$$

The last formula is especially symmetric.

### Worked example: area of an ellipse

Let $C$ be the ellipse

$$
r(t)=\langle a\cos t,b\sin t\rangle,
\qquad
0\le t\le 2\pi.
$$

Using

$$
\text{Area}(D)=\frac12\oint_C x\,dy-y\,dx,
$$

we have

$$
x=a\cos t,
\qquad
 y=b\sin t,
$$

$$
dx=-a\sin t\,dt,
\qquad
 dy=b\cos t\,dt.
$$

Thus

$$
x\,dy-y\,dx
=ab\cos^2t\,dt+ab\sin^2t\,dt
=ab\,dt.
$$

Therefore,

$$
\text{Area}(D)=\frac12\int_0^{2\pi}ab\,dt=\pi ab.
$$

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch21-area-by-boundary
#| fig-cap: 'Area can be computed from boundary data alone using Green’s theorem.'
#| fig-width: 6.5
#| fig-height: 5
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

t = np.linspace(0, 2*np.pi, 1000)
a, b = 2.2, 1.1
x = a*np.cos(t)
y = b*np.sin(t)

dxdt = np.gradient(x, t)
dydt = np.gradient(y, t)
area_approx = 0.5*np.trapz(x*dydt - y*dxdt, t)
area_exact = np.pi*a*b

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.plot(x, y, linewidth=2)
ax.fill(x, y, alpha=0.2)
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
ax.set_xlabel(r'$x$')
ax.set_ylabel(r'$y$')
ax.set_title('Area of an ellipse from a boundary integral')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)
plt.show()

print(f'Boundary-area approximation: {area_approx:.8f}')
print(f'Exact area:                  {area_exact:.8f}')
```

## Green's theorem: flux form

Green's theorem also has a flux form.  Let

$$
F=\langle P,Q\rangle.
$$

The outward flux across $C$ is

$$
\oint_C F\cdot n\,ds,
$$

where $n$ is the outward unit normal.  For a positively oriented plane curve, the outward normal satisfies

$$
n\,ds=\langle dy,-dx\rangle.
$$

Therefore,

$$
F\cdot n\,ds=P\,dy-Q\,dx.
$$

Green's theorem in flux form is

$$
\oint_C F\cdot n\,ds
=
\iint_D (P_x+Q_y)\,dA.
$$

The quantity

$$
P_x+Q_y
$$

is the two-dimensional divergence of $F$.  Thus:

> Outward flux through the boundary equals total divergence inside the region.

This result foreshadows the divergence theorem in three dimensions.

### Worked example: outward flux of a radial field

Let

$$
F(x,y)=\langle x,y\rangle.
$$

Then

$$
\operatorname{div}F=P_x+Q_y=1+1=2.
$$

If $C$ bounds a region $D$, then

$$
\oint_C F\cdot n\,ds=\iint_D 2\,dA=2\operatorname{Area}(D).
$$

For a disk of radius $R$, this gives

$$
\oint_C F\cdot n\,ds=2\pi R^2.
$$

## Holes, singularities, and why hypotheses matter

The field

$$
F(x,y)=\left\langle \frac{-y}{x^2+y^2},\frac{x}{x^2+y^2}\right\rangle
$$

is defined everywhere except the origin.  Away from the origin, one can check that

$$
P_y=Q_x.
$$

However, the field is not conservative on any region that loops around the origin.  On the unit circle

$$
r(t)=\langle \cos t,\sin t\rangle,
$$

we have

$$
F(r(t))=\langle -\sin t,\cos t\rangle=r'(t),
$$

so

$$
\oint_C F\cdot dr=\int_0^{2\pi}1\,dt=2\pi.
$$

The origin is a singularity.  The domain has a hole.  This example is a warning that local derivative tests do not always determine global behavior.

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch21-singular-circulation
#| fig-cap: 'A field with a singularity at the origin. The local curl is zero away from the origin, but the circulation around the origin is nonzero.'
```

```{python}
def singular_circulation(R, N=5000):
```

## Numerical curl and discrete Green's theorem intuition

Green's theorem says that circulation around a boundary measures accumulated curl inside.  In computational settings, one often estimates derivatives on a grid.  The scalar curl of a plane vector field

$$
F=\langle P,Q\rangle
$$

is

$$
\operatorname{curl}_{2D}F=Q_x-P_y.
$$

A gradient field has zero scalar curl when second partial derivatives commute.  A rotational field has visible nonzero scalar curl.

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch21-discrete-curl
#| fig-cap: 'Numerical scalar curl for a gradient field and a rotational field. Gradient fields have near-zero curl; rotational fields do not.'
#| fig-width: 8
#| fig-height: 4.5
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x = np.linspace(-2, 2, 90)
y = np.linspace(-2, 2, 90)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)

dx = x[1] - x[0]
dy = y[1] - y[0]

# Gradient field of phi = x^2 y + y^3.
P_grad = 2*X*Y
Q_grad = X**2 + 3*Y**2

# Rotational field.
P_rot = -Y
Q_rot = X

def scalar_curl(P, Q):
    Q_y_index, Q_x_index = np.gradient(Q, dy, dx)
    P_y_index, P_x_index = np.gradient(P, dy, dx)
    return Q_x_index - P_y_index

curl_grad = scalar_curl(P_grad, Q_grad)
curl_rot = scalar_curl(P_rot, Q_rot)

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(10, 4.2))
for ax, C, title in [
    (axes[0], curl_grad, 'Numerical curl of a gradient field'),
    (axes[1], curl_rot, 'Numerical curl of a rotational field')
]:
    im = ax.imshow(C, extent=[x.min(), x.max(), y.min(), y.max()], origin='lower', aspect='equal')
    ax.set_title(title)
    ax.set_xlabel(r'$x$')
    ax.set_ylabel(r'$y$')
    fig.colorbar(im, ax=ax, shrink=0.82)
plt.show()
```

## Modern interpretation: potentials, losses, and dynamics

In applied mathematics, statistics, data science, and machine learning, a scalar objective function is often the central object:

$$
L(\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_n).
$$

The gradient field

$$
\nabla L
$$

points in the direction of steepest increase, while

$$
-\nabla L
$$

points in the direction of steepest decrease.  Gradient descent follows a trajectory determined by a conservative field:

$$
\frac{d\theta}{dt}=-\nabla L(\theta).
$$

Along such a path,

$$
\frac{d}{dt}L(\theta(t))=\nabla L(\theta(t))\cdot \theta'(t)
=-|\nabla L(\theta(t))|^2\le 0.
$$

So a pure gradient flow cannot cycle around a closed loop while strictly decreasing the loss.  In contrast, a rotational component can create circulation and oscillation.  This distinction matters in dynamical systems, adversarial optimization, non-equilibrium probability flows, and training dynamics for models with coupled parameters.

::: {.callout-note}
## Conservative versus rotational learning dynamics

A pure gradient field comes from a scalar objective.  A rotational field does not.  Many modern algorithms can be viewed as combining gradient-like motion with rotational or momentum-like effects.  Green's theorem gives a mathematical language for detecting and measuring such circulation in two-dimensional slices of a high-dimensional system.
:::

## Problem-solving guide

When faced with a line integral of a plane vector field:

1. **Check whether the field is conservative.**  Try to find a potential function.
2. **If the curve is open and the field is conservative, use endpoints.**
3. **If the curve is closed, check Green's theorem.**  The boundary integral may become an easier double integral.
4. **Check orientation.**  Counterclockwise outer boundaries are positive.  Reversing orientation changes the sign.
5. **Check the domain.**  Holes or singularities can invalidate a simple conservative-field conclusion.
6. **Choose the easier representation.**  Some problems are easier as line integrals; others are easier as double integrals.

## Common mistakes

::: {.callout-warning}
## Watch for these errors

- Treating $P_y=Q_x$ as sufficient without checking the domain.
- Forgetting that Green's theorem requires a closed boundary curve.
- Using the wrong orientation and missing a minus sign.
- Confusing circulation form with flux form.
- Forgetting that a potential function is determined only up to an additive constant.
- Applying Green's theorem across a singularity where the field is not defined.
:::

## Chapter summary

This chapter developed the bridge from line integrals to global vector-calculus theorems.

- A vector field is conservative if it is the gradient of a potential function.
- Line integrals of conservative fields depend only on endpoints.
- The Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals is the chain rule plus the one-variable Fundamental Theorem of Calculus.
- Closed-loop integrals of conservative fields are zero.
- In the plane, the condition $P_y=Q_x$ is the local conservative-field test, but the domain matters.
- Green's theorem converts circulation around a closed curve into a double integral of scalar curl over the enclosed region.
- Green's theorem also has a flux form involving divergence.
- Area can be computed from a boundary integral.
- Singularities and holes explain why local derivative information may not determine global circulation.

In the next chapter, these ideas move from plane regions to surfaces in three-dimensional space.  Curl, divergence, surface integrals, and flux are the ingredients needed for Stokes' theorem and the divergence theorem.
