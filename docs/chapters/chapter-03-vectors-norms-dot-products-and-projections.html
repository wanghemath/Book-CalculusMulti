3  Chapter 3. Vectors, Norms, Dot Products, and Projections

Direction, magnitude, similarity, work, and the geometry of data

3.1 Chapter overview

Chapter 2 introduced Euclidean space as the natural home for points such as \((x,y)\), \((x,y,z)\), and \((x_1,\dots,x_n)\). This chapter develops the algebra and geometry of vectors in that space.

A vector can represent a displacement, a velocity, a force, a row of data, a direction of change, a model parameter, or a feature embedding. In multivariable calculus, vectors are the language used to describe motion, tangent lines, tangent planes, gradients, flux, optimization, and linear approximation. In statistics and machine learning, vectors are also used to store observations, coefficients, residuals, gradients, and learned representations.

The main ideas are:

  • vector addition and scalar multiplication;
  • norm, distance, and unit direction;
  • dot product, angle, and orthogonality;
  • projection and decomposition;
  • cosine similarity and work;
  • the connection between geometric formulas and numerical computation.
NoteTeaching-material connection

This chapter covers displacement vectors, vector addition, scalar multiplication, norms, unit vectors, dot products, angles, projections, perpendicular vectors, and work.

3.2 Learning goals

After studying this chapter, you should be able to:

  1. perform vector addition, subtraction, and scalar multiplication in \(\mathbb R^n\);
  2. compute the Euclidean norm of a vector and construct unit vectors;
  3. interpret distance as the norm of a displacement vector;
  4. compute dot products and use them to find angles;
  5. recognize orthogonal vectors and understand why orthogonality means zero dot product;
  6. project one vector onto another and decompose a vector into parallel and perpendicular parts;
  7. use the dot product to compute work and cosine similarity;
  8. connect vector geometry to data science, statistics, optimization, and AI.

3.3 3.1 Vectors as displacement, direction, and data

A vector in \(\mathbb R^n\) is an ordered list

\[ v=(v_1,v_2,\ldots,v_n). \]

Geometrically, we often draw a vector as an arrow. Algebraically, we compute with its components. The same component list can represent different kinds of objects:

Context Vector meaning
Geometry displacement, direction, normal vector
Physics force, velocity, acceleration
Statistics data point, residual vector, coefficient vector
Machine learning feature vector, weight vector, gradient vector
Optimization search direction, update direction

If \(A=(a_1,\ldots,a_n)\) and \(B=(b_1,\ldots,b_n)\) are points, the displacement from \(A\) to \(B\) is

\[ \overrightarrow{AB}=B-A=(b_1-a_1,\ldots,b_n-a_n). \]

3.3.1 Example 3.1: A displacement vector in space

Let

\[ A=(1,-2,3), \qquad B=(2,1,5). \]

Then

\[ \overrightarrow{AB}=(2-1,1-(-2),5-3)=(1,3,2). \]

This vector tells us how to move from \(A\) to \(B\): increase \(x\) by \(1\), increase \(y\) by \(3\), and increase \(z\) by \(2\).

TipPoint versus vector

The ordered triple \((1,3,2)\) can be interpreted as a point or as a vector. As a point, it is a location. As a vector, it is a displacement. In calculus and applied mathematics, the interpretation matters as much as the arithmetic.

3.4 3.2 Vector addition and scalar multiplication

If

\[ u=(u_1,\ldots,u_n), \qquad v=(v_1,\ldots,v_n), \]

then vector addition is defined componentwise:

\[ u+v=(u_1+v_1,\ldots,u_n+v_n). \]

If \(c\) is a real number, scalar multiplication is

\[ cv=(cv_1,\ldots,cv_n). \]

Geometrically:

  • \(u+v\) means follow displacement \(u\) and then displacement \(v\);
  • \(v-u\) means the displacement from the endpoint of \(u\) to the endpoint of \(v\);
  • \(cv\) stretches \(v\) by the factor \(|c|\);
  • if \(c<0\), then \(cv\) also reverses direction.

3.4.1 Example 3.2: Net displacement

A particle is displaced first by \((3,2)\) and then by \((2,-1)\). Its net displacement is

\[ (3,2)+(2,-1)=(5,1). \]

The final location depends on the initial location, but the net displacement does not.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

u = np.array([3.0, 2.0])
v = np.array([2.0, -1.0])
w = u + v

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
origin = np.array([0.0, 0.0])

ax.quiver(*origin, *u, angles='xy', scale_units='xy', scale=1, width=0.008)
ax.quiver(*u, *v, angles='xy', scale_units='xy', scale=1, width=0.008)
ax.quiver(*origin, *w, angles='xy', scale_units='xy', scale=1, width=0.006, alpha=0.7)

ax.text(u[0]/2, u[1]/2 + 0.15, r'$u$', fontsize=12)
ax.text(u[0] + v[0]/2, u[1] + v[1]/2 - 0.35, r'$v$', fontsize=12)
ax.text(w[0]/2, w[1]/2 - 0.45, r'$u+v$', fontsize=12)

ax.set_xlim(-0.5, 6)
ax.set_ylim(-0.5, 3)
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
ax.set_xlabel('x')
ax.set_ylabel('y')
plt.show()

3.5 3.3 Norm, length, and unit vectors

The Euclidean norm of a vector \(v=(v_1,\ldots,v_n)\) is

\[ \|v\|=\sqrt{v_1^2+v_2^2+\cdots+v_n^2}. \]

This generalizes the Pythagorean theorem. In \(\mathbb R^2\), it gives the length of an arrow in the plane. In \(\mathbb R^3\), it gives the length of an arrow in space. In \(\mathbb R^n\), it gives the standard size of a data vector or parameter vector.

A vector \(u\) is a unit vector if

\[ \|u\|=1. \]

If \(v\ne 0\), then the unit vector in the direction of \(v\) is

\[ \widehat v=\frac{v}{\|v\|}. \]

The vector \(v\) can be decomposed as

\[ v=\|v\|\widehat v. \]

This formula says that a nonzero vector is determined by two pieces of information: its magnitude and its direction.

3.5.1 Example 3.3: Unit vector parallel to \((3,-4)\)

Let

\[ v=(3,-4). \]

Then

\[ \|v\|=\sqrt{3^2+(-4)^2}=5. \]

Therefore a unit vector parallel to \(v\) and pointing in the same direction is

\[ \widehat v=\frac{1}{5}(3,-4)=\left(\frac35,-\frac45\right). \]

A unit vector parallel to \(v\) but pointing in the opposite direction is

\[ -\widehat v=\left(-\frac35,\frac45\right). \]

3.5.2 Example 3.4: Unit vectors perpendicular to \((3,-4)\)

In the plane, if \(v=(a,b)\), then \(( -b,a)\) and \((b,-a)\) are perpendicular to \(v\). For \(v=(3,-4)\), two perpendicular vectors are

\[ (4,3), \qquad (-4,-3). \]

Both have length \(5\), so the corresponding unit vectors are

\[ \left(\frac45,\frac35\right), \qquad \left(-\frac45,-\frac35\right). \]

WarningCommon mistake

Do not confuse a vector with its norm. The vector \((3,-4)\) has direction and magnitude. The norm \(\|(3,-4)\|=5\) is only a number.

3.6 3.4 Distance as norm of displacement

The distance between two points is the length of the displacement vector connecting them. If

\[ A=(a_1,\ldots,a_n), \qquad B=(b_1,\ldots,b_n), \]

then

\[ \operatorname{dist}(A,B)=\|B-A\|=\sqrt{(b_1-a_1)^2+\cdots+(b_n-a_n)^2}. \]

This is one of the most important bridges between geometry and computation. In data science, distance is used in nearest-neighbor methods, clustering, anomaly detection, and similarity search.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

v = np.array([3.0, -4.0])
vhat = v / np.linalg.norm(v)
origin = np.array([0.0, 0.0])

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.quiver(*origin, *v, angles='xy', scale_units='xy', scale=1, width=0.008)
ax.quiver(*origin, *vhat, angles='xy', scale_units='xy', scale=1, width=0.010, alpha=0.8)
ax.text(1.5, -2.2, r'$v=(3,-4)$', fontsize=12)
ax.text(0.25, -0.9, r'$\widehat v$', fontsize=12)
ax.set_xlim(-1, 4)
ax.set_ylim(-5, 1)
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
ax.set_xlabel('x')
ax.set_ylabel('y')
plt.show()

3.7 3.5 Dot product

The dot product of two vectors

\[ u=(u_1,\ldots,u_n), \qquad v=(v_1,\ldots,v_n) \]

is

\[ u\cdot v=u_1v_1+u_2v_2+\cdots+u_nv_n. \]

The dot product takes two vectors and returns one number. It measures alignment.

The geometric identity is

\[ u\cdot v=\|u\|\,\|v\|\cos\theta, \]

where \(\theta\) is the angle between \(u\) and \(v\).

Therefore, for nonzero vectors,

\[ \cos\theta=\frac{u\cdot v}{\|u\|\,\|v\|}. \]

This formula is central in geometry, statistics, and machine learning.

3.7.1 Dot product interpretation

Suppose \(u\) and \(v\) are nonzero.

  • If \(u\cdot v>0\), the angle is acute and the vectors point generally in the same direction.
  • If \(u\cdot v=0\), the angle is \(90^\circ\) and the vectors are orthogonal.
  • If \(u\cdot v<0\), the angle is obtuse and the vectors point generally in opposite directions.

3.7.2 Example 3.5: Angle between two vectors

Let

\[ F=(1,2), \qquad v=(-1,3). \]

Then

\[ F\cdot v=(1)(-1)+(2)(3)=5, \]

and

\[ \|F\|=\sqrt{1^2+2^2}=\sqrt5, \qquad \|v\|=\sqrt{(-1)^2+3^2}=\sqrt{10}. \]

Thus

\[ \cos\theta=\frac{F\cdot v}{\|F\|\|v\|}=\frac{5}{\sqrt5\sqrt{10}}=\frac{1}{\sqrt2}. \]

So

\[ \theta=\cos^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt2}\right)=\frac{\pi}{4}. \]

NoteInterpretation

The angle is \(45^\circ\), so the two vectors are strongly aligned. The dot product is positive because they point generally in the same direction.

3.8 3.6 Orthogonality

Two vectors \(u\) and \(v\) are orthogonal if

\[ u\cdot v=0. \]

Orthogonality generalizes perpendicularity. In \(\mathbb R^2\) and \(\mathbb R^3\), it means the vectors meet at a right angle. In \(\mathbb R^n\), where we may not be able to draw the vectors, the equation \(u\cdot v=0\) is still meaningful.

3.8.1 Example 3.6: Checking orthogonality

Let

\[ u=(2,-1,3), \qquad v=(1,5,1). \]

Then

\[ u\cdot v=(2)(1)+(-1)(5)+(3)(1)=2-5+3=0. \]

Therefore \(u\) and \(v\) are orthogonal.

TipWhy orthogonality matters

Orthogonality is one of the main organizing ideas of applied mathematics. It appears in least squares, projections, Fourier series, PCA, QR factorization, residual analysis, and the geometry of independent directions in data.

3.9 3.7 Projection onto a direction

Projection answers a basic question:

How much of one vector points in the direction of another vector?

Let \(v\ne 0\). The vector projection of \(u\) onto \(v\) is

\[ \operatorname{proj}_v(u)=\frac{u\cdot v}{v\cdot v}v. \]

The scalar component of \(u\) in the direction of \(v\) is

\[ \operatorname{comp}_v(u)=\frac{u\cdot v}{\|v\|}. \]

If \(\widehat v=v/\|v\|\) is the unit vector in the direction of \(v\), then

\[ \operatorname{proj}_v(u)=(u\cdot \widehat v)\widehat v. \]

3.9.1 Example 3.7: Projecting \((1,2)\) onto \((-1,3)\)

Let

\[ F=(1,2), \qquad v=(-1,3). \]

Then

\[ F\cdot v=5, \qquad v\cdot v=(-1)^2+3^2=10. \]

Therefore

\[ \operatorname{proj}_v(F)=\frac{F\cdot v}{v\cdot v}v =\frac{5}{10}(-1,3) =\left(-\frac12,\frac32\right). \]

This is the part of \(F\) that lies in the direction of \(v\).

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

u = np.array([1.0, 2.0])
v = np.array([-1.0, 3.0])
proj = (u @ v) / (v @ v) * v
resid = u - proj
origin = np.array([0.0, 0.0])

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.quiver(*origin, *u, angles='xy', scale_units='xy', scale=1, width=0.008)
ax.quiver(*origin, *v, angles='xy', scale_units='xy', scale=1, width=0.006, alpha=0.5)
ax.quiver(*origin, *proj, angles='xy', scale_units='xy', scale=1, width=0.008)
ax.quiver(*proj, *resid, angles='xy', scale_units='xy', scale=1, width=0.006, alpha=0.8)

ax.text(u[0]+0.1, u[1], r'$u$', fontsize=12)
ax.text(v[0]-0.5, v[1], r'$v$', fontsize=12)
ax.text(proj[0]-0.75, proj[1]-0.15, r'$\operatorname{proj}_v(u)$', fontsize=12)
ax.text(proj[0]+resid[0]/2+0.05, proj[1]+resid[1]/2, r'$u-\operatorname{proj}_v(u)$', fontsize=11)

ax.set_xlim(-2, 2)
ax.set_ylim(-0.5, 3.5)
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
ax.set_xlabel('x')
ax.set_ylabel('y')
plt.show()

3.9.2 Projection creates an orthogonal decomposition

Let

\[ p=\operatorname{proj}_v(u), \qquad r=u-p. \]

Then

\[ u=p+r, \]

where \(p\) is parallel to \(v\) and \(r\) is orthogonal to \(v\).

To check this, compute

\[ r\cdot v=(u-p)\cdot v =u\cdot v-\left(\frac{u\cdot v}{v\cdot v}v\right)\cdot v =u\cdot v-\frac{u\cdot v}{v\cdot v}(v\cdot v)=0. \]

This orthogonal decomposition is the geometric foundation of least squares.

ImportantBridge to least squares

In linear regression, the fitted values are projections of the response vector onto a column space. The residual vector is orthogonal to every column of the design matrix. This is why dot products and projections are essential for statistics.

3.10 3.8 Work as a dot product

If a constant force \(F\) moves an object through a displacement \(d\), then the work done by the force is

\[ W=F\cdot d. \]

Only the component of the force in the direction of motion contributes to work.

3.10.1 Example 3.8: Work by a constant force

A constant force

\[ F=(-1,-3,4) \]

is applied while an object moves from

\[ A=(1,2,0) \]

to

\[ B=(2,0,1). \]

The displacement is

\[ d=B-A=(1,-2,1). \]

The work is

\[ W=F\cdot d=(-1)(1)+(-3)(-2)+(4)(1)=-1+6+4=9. \]

The positive result means that the force has a net component in the direction of motion.

3.11 3.9 Cosine similarity and data vectors

For nonzero vectors \(u\) and \(v\), the quantity

\[ \frac{u\cdot v}{\|u\|\|v\|} \]

is the cosine of the angle between them. In data science and AI, this value is often called cosine similarity.

Cosine similarity focuses on direction rather than magnitude:

  • similar direction: value near \(1\);
  • orthogonal or unrelated direction: value near \(0\);
  • opposite direction: value near \(-1\).

This is useful when the magnitude of a vector is less important than its pattern. For example, in text analysis, two documents may have different lengths but similar word-frequency directions.

3.11.1 Example 3.9: Cosine similarity of data vectors

Suppose two observations are

\[ x=(2,1,0,3), \qquad y=(4,2,0,6). \]

Then \(y=2x\), so the two observations point in exactly the same direction. Their cosine similarity is \(1\).

Now compare

\[ x=(2,1,0,3), \qquad z=(0,3,4,0). \]

The dot product is

\[ x\cdot z=(2)(0)+(1)(3)+(0)(4)+(3)(0)=3, \]

so the cosine similarity is positive but not close to \(1\).

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

vectors = np.array([
    [2, 1, 0, 3],
    [4, 2, 0, 6],
    [0, 3, 4, 0],
    [1, 0, 2, 1]
], dtype=float)

norms = np.linalg.norm(vectors, axis=1, keepdims=True)
unit_vectors = vectors / norms
cosine_matrix = unit_vectors @ unit_vectors.T

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
im = ax.imshow(cosine_matrix, vmin=-1, vmax=1)
ax.set_xticks(range(4))
ax.set_yticks(range(4))
ax.set_xticklabels(['x', 'y', 'z', 'w'])
ax.set_yticklabels(['x', 'y', 'z', 'w'])
for i in range(4):
    for j in range(4):
        ax.text(j, i, f'{cosine_matrix[i,j]:.2f}', ha='center', va='center')
ax.set_title('Cosine similarity matrix')
fig.colorbar(im, ax=ax, fraction=0.046, pad=0.04)
plt.show()

3.12 3.10 High-dimensional geometry: most random directions are nearly orthogonal

High-dimensional geometry can be surprising. In low dimensions, random vectors often have visibly meaningful angles. In high dimensions, randomly chosen directions are usually close to orthogonal.

This fact helps explain why high-dimensional data can be difficult to visualize and why algorithms must be designed carefully.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(2321)
num_pairs = 4000
dimensions = [2, 10, 100]

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
for d in dimensions:
    A = rng.normal(size=(num_pairs, d))
    B = rng.normal(size=(num_pairs, d))
    A = A / np.linalg.norm(A, axis=1, keepdims=True)
    B = B / np.linalg.norm(B, axis=1, keepdims=True)
    cosines = np.sum(A * B, axis=1)
    ax.hist(cosines, bins=40, alpha=0.45, density=True, label=f'd={d}')

ax.set_xlabel('cosine between random unit vectors')
ax.set_ylabel('density')
ax.set_title('Random directions in high dimension')
ax.legend()
plt.show()
NoteModern interpretation

In high-dimensional data spaces, distance and angle can behave differently from our three-dimensional intuition. This is one reason why normalization, projection, regularization, and dimensionality reduction are central in statistics and machine learning.

3.13 3.11 Norms beyond the Euclidean norm

The Euclidean norm is the main norm used in this chapter, but other norms are important in applied mathematics and data science.

For \(v=(v_1,\ldots,v_n)\):

\[ \|v\|_1=|v_1|+\cdots+|v_n| \]

is the \(\ell^1\) norm, and

\[ \|v\|_\infty=\max\{|v_1|,\ldots,|v_n|\} \]

is the \(\ell^\infty\) norm.

The Euclidean norm is

\[ \|v\|_2=\sqrt{v_1^2+\cdots+v_n^2}. \]

Different norms encode different ideas of size.

Norm Formula Common interpretation
\(\|v\|_1\) sum of absolute values sparsity, total variation, Manhattan distance
\(\|v\|_2\) square root of sum of squares Euclidean length, energy, least squares
\(\|v\|_\infty\) maximum absolute component worst-case component size 
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x = np.linspace(-1.2, 1.2, 400)
y = np.linspace(-1.2, 1.2, 400)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)
L1 = np.abs(X) + np.abs(Y)
L2 = np.sqrt(X**2 + Y**2)
Linf = np.maximum(np.abs(X), np.abs(Y))

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.contour(X, Y, L1, levels=[1], linewidths=2)
ax.contour(X, Y, L2, levels=[1], linewidths=2)
ax.contour(X, Y, Linf, levels=[1], linewidths=2)
ax.text(0.05, 1.05, r'$\|v\|_1=1$', fontsize=11)
ax.text(0.75, 0.70, r'$\|v\|_2=1$', fontsize=11)
ax.text(1.03, -0.95, r'$\|v\|_\infty=1$', fontsize=11)
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
ax.set_xlabel('x')
ax.set_ylabel('y')
ax.set_title('Unit balls for three norms')
plt.show()
ImportantWhy this matters later

Least-squares regression is built from the \(\ell^2\) norm. Lasso regression is built from the \(\ell^1\) norm. Robust and worst-case methods often use \(\ell^\infty\)-type constraints. Norms are not just geometric objects; they shape models.

3.14 3.12 Python implementation of core vector operations

The formulas in this chapter are simple enough to compute by hand, but they also form the basic operations used by numerical linear algebra and machine learning libraries.

import numpy as np

u = np.array([1.0, 2.0])
v = np.array([-1.0, 3.0])

norm_u = np.linalg.norm(u)
norm_v = np.linalg.norm(v)
dot_uv = u @ v
cos_theta = dot_uv / (norm_u * norm_v)
projection = (dot_uv / (v @ v)) * v
residual = u - projection

print('||u|| =', norm_u)
print('||v|| =', norm_v)
print('u dot v =', dot_uv)
print('cos(theta) =', cos_theta)
print('projection of u onto v =', projection)
print('residual =', residual)
print('residual dot v =', residual @ v)
||u|| = 2.23606797749979
||v|| = 3.1622776601683795
u dot v = 5.0
cos(theta) = 0.7071067811865475
projection of u onto v = [-0.5  1.5]
residual = [1.5 0.5]
residual dot v = 0.0

The last line should be approximately zero. This confirms that the residual \(u-\operatorname{proj}_v(u)\) is orthogonal to \(v\).

3.15 3.13 Worked example summary

3.15.1 Example 3.10: Full vector analysis

Let

\[ u=(2,-1,2), \qquad v=(1,2,2). \]

Step 1: Compute the norms.

\[ \|u\|=\sqrt{2^2+(-1)^2+2^2}=3, \]

and

\[ \|v\|=\sqrt{1^2+2^2+2^2}=3. \]

Step 2: Compute the dot product.

\[ u\cdot v=(2)(1)+(-1)(2)+(2)(2)=2-2+4=4. \]

Step 3: Find the angle.

\[ \cos\theta=\frac{u\cdot v}{\|u\|\|v\|}=\frac{4}{9}. \]

So

\[ \theta=\cos^{-1}\left(\frac49\right). \]

Step 4: Project \(u\) onto \(v\).

\[ \operatorname{proj}_v(u)=\frac{u\cdot v}{v\cdot v}v =\frac49(1,2,2) =\left(\frac49,\frac89,\frac89\right). \]

Step 5: Interpret.

The vector \(u\) has a component in the direction of \(v\) and a residual component perpendicular to \(v\). This is the same geometric idea that later appears in least-squares approximation.

## 3.1 Vectors as displacement, direction, and data

A vector in $\mathbb R^n$ is an ordered list

$$
v=(v_1,v_2,\ldots,v_n).
$$

Geometrically, we often draw a vector as an arrow.  Algebraically, we compute with its components.  The same component list can represent different kinds of objects:

| Context | Vector meaning |
|---|---|
| Geometry | displacement, direction, normal vector |
| Physics | force, velocity, acceleration |
| Statistics | data point, residual vector, coefficient vector |
| Machine learning | feature vector, weight vector, gradient vector |
| Optimization | search direction, update direction |

If $A=(a_1,\ldots,a_n)$ and $B=(b_1,\ldots,b_n)$ are points, the displacement from $A$ to $B$ is

$$
\overrightarrow{AB}=B-A=(b_1-a_1,\ldots,b_n-a_n).
$$

### Example 3.1: A displacement vector in space

Let

$$
A=(1,-2,3), \qquad B=(2,1,5).
$$

Then

$$
\overrightarrow{AB}=(2-1,1-(-2),5-3)=(1,3,2).
$$

This vector tells us how to move from $A$ to $B$: increase $x$ by $1$, increase $y$ by $3$, and increase $z$ by $2$.

::: {.callout-tip title='Point versus vector'}
The ordered triple $(1,3,2)$ can be interpreted as a point or as a vector.  As a point, it is a location.  As a vector, it is a displacement.  In calculus and applied mathematics, the interpretation matters as much as the arithmetic.
:::

## 3.2 Vector addition and scalar multiplication

If

$$
u=(u_1,\ldots,u_n), \qquad v=(v_1,\ldots,v_n),
$$

then vector addition is defined componentwise:

$$
u+v=(u_1+v_1,\ldots,u_n+v_n).
$$

If $c$ is a real number, scalar multiplication is

$$
cv=(cv_1,\ldots,cv_n).
$$

Geometrically:

- $u+v$ means follow displacement $u$ and then displacement $v$;
- $v-u$ means the displacement from the endpoint of $u$ to the endpoint of $v$;
- $cv$ stretches $v$ by the factor $|c|$;
- if $c<0$, then $cv$ also reverses direction.

### Example 3.2: Net displacement

A particle is displaced first by $(3,2)$ and then by $(2,-1)$.  Its net displacement is

$$
(3,2)+(2,-1)=(5,1).
$$

The final location depends on the initial location, but the net displacement does not.

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch03-vector-addition
#| fig-cap: 'Vector addition by following one displacement after another.'
#| fig-width: 6.5
#| fig-height: 4.5
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

u = np.array([3.0, 2.0])
v = np.array([2.0, -1.0])
w = u + v

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
origin = np.array([0.0, 0.0])

ax.quiver(*origin, *u, angles='xy', scale_units='xy', scale=1, width=0.008)
ax.quiver(*u, *v, angles='xy', scale_units='xy', scale=1, width=0.008)
ax.quiver(*origin, *w, angles='xy', scale_units='xy', scale=1, width=0.006, alpha=0.7)

ax.text(u[0]/2, u[1]/2 + 0.15, r'$u$', fontsize=12)
ax.text(u[0] + v[0]/2, u[1] + v[1]/2 - 0.35, r'$v$', fontsize=12)
ax.text(w[0]/2, w[1]/2 - 0.45, r'$u+v$', fontsize=12)

ax.set_xlim(-0.5, 6)
ax.set_ylim(-0.5, 3)
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
ax.set_xlabel('x')
ax.set_ylabel('y')
plt.show()
```

## 3.3 Norm, length, and unit vectors

The **Euclidean norm** of a vector $v=(v_1,\ldots,v_n)$ is

$$
\|v\|=\sqrt{v_1^2+v_2^2+\cdots+v_n^2}.
$$

This generalizes the Pythagorean theorem.  In $\mathbb R^2$, it gives the length of an arrow in the plane.  In $\mathbb R^3$, it gives the length of an arrow in space.  In $\mathbb R^n$, it gives the standard size of a data vector or parameter vector.

A vector $u$ is a **unit vector** if

$$
\|u\|=1.
$$

If $v\ne 0$, then the unit vector in the direction of $v$ is

$$
\widehat v=\frac{v}{\|v\|}.
$$

The vector $v$ can be decomposed as

$$
v=\|v\|\widehat v.
$$

This formula says that a nonzero vector is determined by two pieces of information: its magnitude and its direction.

### Example 3.3: Unit vector parallel to $(3,-4)$

Let

$$
v=(3,-4).
$$

Then

$$
\|v\|=\sqrt{3^2+(-4)^2}=5.
$$

Therefore a unit vector parallel to $v$ and pointing in the same direction is

$$
\widehat v=\frac{1}{5}(3,-4)=\left(\frac35,-\frac45\right).
$$

A unit vector parallel to $v$ but pointing in the opposite direction is

$$
-\widehat v=\left(-\frac35,\frac45\right).
$$

### Example 3.4: Unit vectors perpendicular to $(3,-4)$

In the plane, if $v=(a,b)$, then $( -b,a)$ and $(b,-a)$ are perpendicular to $v$.  For $v=(3,-4)$, two perpendicular vectors are

$$
(4,3), \qquad (-4,-3).
$$

Both have length $5$, so the corresponding unit vectors are

$$
\left(\frac45,\frac35\right), \qquad \left(-\frac45,-\frac35\right).
$$

::: {.callout-warning title='Common mistake'}
Do not confuse a vector with its norm.  The vector $(3,-4)$ has direction and magnitude.  The norm $\|(3,-4)\|=5$ is only a number.
:::

## 3.4 Distance as norm of displacement

The distance between two points is the length of the displacement vector connecting them.  If

$$
A=(a_1,\ldots,a_n), \qquad B=(b_1,\ldots,b_n),
$$

then

$$
\operatorname{dist}(A,B)=\|B-A\|=\sqrt{(b_1-a_1)^2+\cdots+(b_n-a_n)^2}.
$$

This is one of the most important bridges between geometry and computation.  In data science, distance is used in nearest-neighbor methods, clustering, anomaly detection, and similarity search.


## 3.5 Dot product

The **dot product** of two vectors

$$
u=(u_1,\ldots,u_n), \qquad v=(v_1,\ldots,v_n)
$$

is

$$
u\cdot v=u_1v_1+u_2v_2+\cdots+u_nv_n.
$$

The dot product takes two vectors and returns one number.  It measures alignment.

The geometric identity is

$$
u\cdot v=\|u\|\,\|v\|\cos\theta,
$$

where $\theta$ is the angle between $u$ and $v$.

Therefore, for nonzero vectors,

$$
\cos\theta=\frac{u\cdot v}{\|u\|\,\|v\|}.
$$

This formula is central in geometry, statistics, and machine learning.

### Dot product interpretation

Suppose $u$ and $v$ are nonzero.

- If $u\cdot v>0$, the angle is acute and the vectors point generally in the same direction.
- If $u\cdot v=0$, the angle is $90^\circ$ and the vectors are orthogonal.
- If $u\cdot v<0$, the angle is obtuse and the vectors point generally in opposite directions.

### Example 3.5: Angle between two vectors

Let

$$
F=(1,2), \qquad v=(-1,3).
$$

Then

$$
F\cdot v=(1)(-1)+(2)(3)=5,
$$

and

$$
\|F\|=\sqrt{1^2+2^2}=\sqrt5, \qquad \|v\|=\sqrt{(-1)^2+3^2}=\sqrt{10}.
$$

Thus

$$
\cos\theta=\frac{F\cdot v}{\|F\|\|v\|}=\frac{5}{\sqrt5\sqrt{10}}=\frac{1}{\sqrt2}.
$$

So

$$
\theta=\cos^{-1}\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt2}\right)=\frac{\pi}{4}.
$$

::: {.callout-note title='Interpretation'}
The angle is $45^\circ$, so the two vectors are strongly aligned.  The dot product is positive because they point generally in the same direction.
:::

## 3.6 Orthogonality

Two vectors $u$ and $v$ are **orthogonal** if

$$
u\cdot v=0.
$$

Orthogonality generalizes perpendicularity.  In $\mathbb R^2$ and $\mathbb R^3$, it means the vectors meet at a right angle.  In $\mathbb R^n$, where we may not be able to draw the vectors, the equation $u\cdot v=0$ is still meaningful.

### Example 3.6: Checking orthogonality

Let

$$
u=(2,-1,3), \qquad v=(1,5,1).
$$

Then

$$
u\cdot v=(2)(1)+(-1)(5)+(3)(1)=2-5+3=0.
$$

Therefore $u$ and $v$ are orthogonal.

::: {.callout-tip title='Why orthogonality matters'}
Orthogonality is one of the main organizing ideas of applied mathematics.  It appears in least squares, projections, Fourier series, PCA, QR factorization, residual analysis, and the geometry of independent directions in data.
:::

## 3.7 Projection onto a direction

Projection answers a basic question:

> How much of one vector points in the direction of another vector?

Let $v\ne 0$.  The vector projection of $u$ onto $v$ is

$$
\operatorname{proj}_v(u)=\frac{u\cdot v}{v\cdot v}v.
$$

The scalar component of $u$ in the direction of $v$ is

$$
\operatorname{comp}_v(u)=\frac{u\cdot v}{\|v\|}.
$$

If $\widehat v=v/\|v\|$ is the unit vector in the direction of $v$, then

$$
\operatorname{proj}_v(u)=(u\cdot \widehat v)\widehat v.
$$

### Example 3.7: Projecting $(1,2)$ onto $(-1,3)$

Let

$$
F=(1,2), \qquad v=(-1,3).
$$

Then

$$
F\cdot v=5, \qquad v\cdot v=(-1)^2+3^2=10.
$$

Therefore

$$
\operatorname{proj}_v(F)=\frac{F\cdot v}{v\cdot v}v
=\frac{5}{10}(-1,3)
=\left(-\frac12,\frac32\right).
$$

This is the part of $F$ that lies in the direction of $v$.

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch03-projection
#| fig-cap: 'Projection decomposes a vector into a part parallel to v and a residual perpendicular to v.'
#| fig-width: 6.5
#| fig-height: 5
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

u = np.array([1.0, 2.0])
v = np.array([-1.0, 3.0])
proj = (u @ v) / (v @ v) * v
resid = u - proj
origin = np.array([0.0, 0.0])

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.quiver(*origin, *u, angles='xy', scale_units='xy', scale=1, width=0.008)
ax.quiver(*origin, *v, angles='xy', scale_units='xy', scale=1, width=0.006, alpha=0.5)
ax.quiver(*origin, *proj, angles='xy', scale_units='xy', scale=1, width=0.008)
ax.quiver(*proj, *resid, angles='xy', scale_units='xy', scale=1, width=0.006, alpha=0.8)

ax.text(u[0]+0.1, u[1], r'$u$', fontsize=12)
ax.text(v[0]-0.5, v[1], r'$v$', fontsize=12)
ax.text(proj[0]-0.75, proj[1]-0.15, r'$\operatorname{proj}_v(u)$', fontsize=12)
ax.text(proj[0]+resid[0]/2+0.05, proj[1]+resid[1]/2, r'$u-\operatorname{proj}_v(u)$', fontsize=11)

ax.set_xlim(-2, 2)
ax.set_ylim(-0.5, 3.5)
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
ax.set_xlabel('x')
ax.set_ylabel('y')
plt.show()
```

### Projection creates an orthogonal decomposition

Let

$$
p=\operatorname{proj}_v(u), \qquad r=u-p.
$$

Then

$$
u=p+r,
$$

where $p$ is parallel to $v$ and $r$ is orthogonal to $v$.

To check this, compute

$$
r\cdot v=(u-p)\cdot v
=u\cdot v-\left(\frac{u\cdot v}{v\cdot v}v\right)\cdot v
=u\cdot v-\frac{u\cdot v}{v\cdot v}(v\cdot v)=0.
$$

This orthogonal decomposition is the geometric foundation of least squares.

::: {.callout-important title='Bridge to least squares'}
In linear regression, the fitted values are projections of the response vector onto a column space.  The residual vector is orthogonal to every column of the design matrix.  This is why dot products and projections are essential for statistics.
:::

## 3.8 Work as a dot product

If a constant force $F$ moves an object through a displacement $d$, then the work done by the force is

$$
W=F\cdot d.
$$

Only the component of the force in the direction of motion contributes to work.

### Example 3.8: Work by a constant force

A constant force

$$
F=(-1,-3,4)
$$

is applied while an object moves from

$$
A=(1,2,0)
$$

to

$$
B=(2,0,1).
$$

The displacement is

$$
d=B-A=(1,-2,1).
$$

The work is

$$
W=F\cdot d=(-1)(1)+(-3)(-2)+(4)(1)=-1+6+4=9.
$$

The positive result means that the force has a net component in the direction of motion.

## 3.9 Cosine similarity and data vectors

For nonzero vectors $u$ and $v$, the quantity

$$
\frac{u\cdot v}{\|u\|\|v\|}
$$

is the cosine of the angle between them.  In data science and AI, this value is often called **cosine similarity**.

Cosine similarity focuses on direction rather than magnitude:

- similar direction: value near $1$;
- orthogonal or unrelated direction: value near $0$;
- opposite direction: value near $-1$.

This is useful when the magnitude of a vector is less important than its pattern.  For example, in text analysis, two documents may have different lengths but similar word-frequency directions.

### Example 3.9: Cosine similarity of data vectors

Suppose two observations are

$$
x=(2,1,0,3), \qquad y=(4,2,0,6).
$$

Then $y=2x$, so the two observations point in exactly the same direction.  Their cosine similarity is $1$.

Now compare

$$
x=(2,1,0,3), \qquad z=(0,3,4,0).
$$

The dot product is

$$
x\cdot z=(2)(0)+(1)(3)+(0)(4)+(3)(0)=3,
$$

so the cosine similarity is positive but not close to $1$.

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch03-cosine-similarity
#| fig-cap: 'Cosine similarity measures direction rather than length.'
#| fig-width: 6.5
#| fig-height: 4.5
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

vectors = np.array([
    [2, 1, 0, 3],
    [4, 2, 0, 6],
    [0, 3, 4, 0],
    [1, 0, 2, 1]
], dtype=float)

norms = np.linalg.norm(vectors, axis=1, keepdims=True)
unit_vectors = vectors / norms
cosine_matrix = unit_vectors @ unit_vectors.T

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
im = ax.imshow(cosine_matrix, vmin=-1, vmax=1)
ax.set_xticks(range(4))
ax.set_yticks(range(4))
ax.set_xticklabels(['x', 'y', 'z', 'w'])
ax.set_yticklabels(['x', 'y', 'z', 'w'])
for i in range(4):
    for j in range(4):
        ax.text(j, i, f'{cosine_matrix[i,j]:.2f}', ha='center', va='center')
ax.set_title('Cosine similarity matrix')
fig.colorbar(im, ax=ax, fraction=0.046, pad=0.04)
plt.show()
```

## 3.10 High-dimensional geometry: most random directions are nearly orthogonal

High-dimensional geometry can be surprising.  In low dimensions, random vectors often have visibly meaningful angles.  In high dimensions, randomly chosen directions are usually close to orthogonal.

This fact helps explain why high-dimensional data can be difficult to visualize and why algorithms must be designed carefully.

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch03-high-dimensional-cosines
#| fig-cap: 'Cosines between random unit vectors concentrate near zero in high dimensions.'
#| fig-width: 6.5
#| fig-height: 4.5
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(2321)
num_pairs = 4000
dimensions = [2, 10, 100]

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
for d in dimensions:
    A = rng.normal(size=(num_pairs, d))
    B = rng.normal(size=(num_pairs, d))
    A = A / np.linalg.norm(A, axis=1, keepdims=True)
    B = B / np.linalg.norm(B, axis=1, keepdims=True)
    cosines = np.sum(A * B, axis=1)
    ax.hist(cosines, bins=40, alpha=0.45, density=True, label=f'd={d}')

ax.set_xlabel('cosine between random unit vectors')
ax.set_ylabel('density')
ax.set_title('Random directions in high dimension')
ax.legend()
plt.show()
```

::: {.callout-note title='Modern interpretation'}
In high-dimensional data spaces, distance and angle can behave differently from our three-dimensional intuition.  This is one reason why normalization, projection, regularization, and dimensionality reduction are central in statistics and machine learning.
:::

## 3.11 Norms beyond the Euclidean norm

The Euclidean norm is the main norm used in this chapter, but other norms are important in applied mathematics and data science.

For $v=(v_1,\ldots,v_n)$:

$$
\|v\|_1=|v_1|+\cdots+|v_n|
$$

is the **$\ell^1$ norm**, and

$$
\|v\|_\infty=\max\{|v_1|,\ldots,|v_n|\}
$$

is the **$\ell^\infty$ norm**.

The Euclidean norm is

$$
\|v\|_2=\sqrt{v_1^2+\cdots+v_n^2}.
$$

Different norms encode different ideas of size.

| Norm | Formula | Common interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| $\|v\|_1$ | sum of absolute values | sparsity, total variation, Manhattan distance |
| $\|v\|_2$ | square root of sum of squares | Euclidean length, energy, least squares |
| $\|v\|_\infty$ | maximum absolute component | worst-case component size |

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch03-norm-balls
#| fig-cap: 'Different norms produce different unit balls in the plane.'
#| fig-width: 6.5
#| fig-height: 5
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x = np.linspace(-1.2, 1.2, 400)
y = np.linspace(-1.2, 1.2, 400)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)
L1 = np.abs(X) + np.abs(Y)
L2 = np.sqrt(X**2 + Y**2)
Linf = np.maximum(np.abs(X), np.abs(Y))

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.contour(X, Y, L1, levels=[1], linewidths=2)
ax.contour(X, Y, L2, levels=[1], linewidths=2)
ax.contour(X, Y, Linf, levels=[1], linewidths=2)
ax.text(0.05, 1.05, r'$\|v\|_1=1$', fontsize=11)
ax.text(0.75, 0.70, r'$\|v\|_2=1$', fontsize=11)
ax.text(1.03, -0.95, r'$\|v\|_\infty=1$', fontsize=11)
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.3)
ax.set_xlabel('x')
ax.set_ylabel('y')
ax.set_title('Unit balls for three norms')
plt.show()
```

::: {.callout-important title='Why this matters later'}
Least-squares regression is built from the $\ell^2$ norm.  Lasso regression is built from the $\ell^1$ norm.  Robust and worst-case methods often use $\ell^\infty$-type constraints.  Norms are not just geometric objects; they shape models.
:::

## 3.12 Python implementation of core vector operations

The formulas in this chapter are simple enough to compute by hand, but they also form the basic operations used by numerical linear algebra and machine learning libraries.

```{python}
#| label: py-ch03-vector-operations
import numpy as np

u = np.array([1.0, 2.0])
v = np.array([-1.0, 3.0])

norm_u = np.linalg.norm(u)
norm_v = np.linalg.norm(v)
dot_uv = u @ v
cos_theta = dot_uv / (norm_u * norm_v)
projection = (dot_uv / (v @ v)) * v
residual = u - projection

print('||u|| =', norm_u)
print('||v|| =', norm_v)
print('u dot v =', dot_uv)
print('cos(theta) =', cos_theta)
print('projection of u onto v =', projection)
print('residual =', residual)
print('residual dot v =', residual @ v)
```

The last line should be approximately zero.  This confirms that the residual $u-\operatorname{proj}_v(u)$ is orthogonal to $v$.

## 3.13 Worked example summary

### Example 3.10: Full vector analysis

Let

$$
u=(2,-1,2), \qquad v=(1,2,2).
$$

**Step 1: Compute the norms.**

$$
\|u\|=\sqrt{2^2+(-1)^2+2^2}=3,
$$

and

$$
\|v\|=\sqrt{1^2+2^2+2^2}=3.
$$

**Step 2: Compute the dot product.**

$$
u\cdot v=(2)(1)+(-1)(2)+(2)(2)=2-2+4=4.
$$

**Step 3: Find the angle.**

$$
\cos\theta=\frac{u\cdot v}{\|u\|\|v\|}=\frac{4}{9}.
$$

So

$$
\theta=\cos^{-1}\left(\frac49\right).
$$

**Step 4: Project $u$ onto $v$.**

$$
\operatorname{proj}_v(u)=\frac{u\cdot v}{v\cdot v}v
=\frac49(1,2,2)
=\left(\frac49,\frac89,\frac89\right).
$$

**Step 5: Interpret.**

The vector $u$ has a component in the direction of $v$ and a residual component perpendicular to $v$.  This is the same geometric idea that later appears in least-squares approximation.

## 3.14 Mini-project: cosine similarity for short documents

A simple way to represent a document is by a vector of word counts.  Suppose our vocabulary is

$$
(\text{calculus},\text{data},\text{matrix},\text{gradient}).
$$

Three short documents might have count vectors

$$
d_1=(3,1,0,2), \qquad d_2=(2,1,0,3), \qquad d_3=(0,3,4,0).
$$

Cosine similarity compares their directions.

```{python}
#| label: py-ch03-document-similarity
import numpy as np

docs = np.array([
    [3, 1, 0, 2],
    [2, 1, 0, 3],
    [0, 3, 4, 0]
], dtype=float)

names = ['doc1', 'doc2', 'doc3']
unit_docs = docs / np.linalg.norm(docs, axis=1, keepdims=True)
sim = unit_docs @ unit_docs.T

for i, name_i in enumerate(names):
    for j, name_j in enumerate(names):
        print(f'{name_i} vs {name_j}: {sim[i, j]:.3f}')
```

Documents $d_1$ and $d_2$ are more similar to each other than either is to $d_3$.  This is a small example of the geometry behind search, clustering, and embedding models.

## Chapter summary

This chapter developed the core vector operations needed for multivariable calculus and its applications.

The central formulas are:

$$
u+v=(u_1+v_1,\ldots,u_n+v_n),
$$

$$
\|v\|=\sqrt{v_1^2+\cdots+v_n^2},
$$

$$
\widehat v=\frac{v}{\|v\|},
$$

$$
u\cdot v=u_1v_1+\cdots+u_nv_n,
$$

$$
u\cdot v=\|u\|\|v\|\cos\theta,
$$

and

$$
\operatorname{proj}_v(u)=\frac{u\cdot v}{v\cdot v}v.
$$

The most important conceptual message is that dot products and projections translate geometry into computation.  They measure alignment, define orthogonality, compute work, compare data vectors, and prepare the ground for tangent planes, gradients, least squares, optimization, and machine learning.
