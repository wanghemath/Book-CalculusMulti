8  Chapter 8. Partial Derivatives and the Gradient

Coordinate rates of change, sensitivity, and local information

8.1 Chapter overview

A function of one variable has one basic input direction: move left or move right on the number line. A function of several variables has many input directions. The first step is to measure change in the coordinate directions.

For a function \(f(x,y)\), the partial derivative \(f_x\) measures the instantaneous rate of change when \(x\) changes and \(y\) is held fixed. The partial derivative \(f_y\) measures the instantaneous rate of change when \(y\) changes and \(x\) is held fixed. These two numbers are not separate facts. Together they form the gradient vector

\[ \nabla f(x,y)=\langle f_x(x,y), f_y(x,y)\rangle. \]

The gradient is the local direction-information vector for a scalar field. It tells us how the output changes to first order near a point, how level curves are oriented, and how a loss function changes when model parameters change. This is why gradients appear everywhere in applied mathematics, statistics, machine learning, optimization, physics, imaging, and AI.

NoteTeaching-material connection

This chapter develops the material on partial derivatives, geometric traces, numerical derivative estimates, second partial derivatives, Clairaut’s theorem, and the gradient vector. It also uses the perspective that partial derivatives reduce multivariable differentiation to single-variable differentiation by holding all other variables fixed.

8.2 Learning goals

After studying this chapter, you should be able to:

  1. define \(f_x(a,b)\) and \(f_y(a,b)\) using limits;
  2. interpret partial derivatives as slopes of coordinate traces of a surface;
  3. compute partial derivatives by treating other variables as constants;
  4. estimate partial derivatives from data or finite differences;
  5. compute second partial derivatives and mixed partial derivatives;
  6. use Clairaut’s theorem when mixed partial derivatives are continuous;
  7. compute the gradient of functions on \(\mathbb R^2\), \(\mathbb R^3\), and \(\mathbb R^n\);
  8. explain why the gradient is perpendicular to level curves and level surfaces;
  9. connect gradients with sensitivity analysis, image processing, statistics, and machine learning.

8.3 8.1 From one derivative to many partial derivatives

For a single-variable function \(y=f(x)\), the derivative at \(x=a\) is

\[ f'(a)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}. \]

Geometrically, this is the slope of the tangent line at \(x=a\). For a two-variable function \(z=f(x,y)\), the graph is a surface. At a point \((a,b,f(a,b))\), there are many possible curves on the surface passing through that point. The easiest curves are the two coordinate traces:

  • the trace obtained by fixing \(y=b\) and allowing \(x\) to vary;
  • the trace obtained by fixing \(x=a\) and allowing \(y\) to vary.

The slopes of these two traces are the partial derivatives.

ImportantCore idea

A partial derivative is an ordinary derivative applied to a slice of a multivariable function.

8.4 8.2 Definitions of \(f_x\) and \(f_y\)

Let \(f\) be a function of two variables. The partial derivative of \(f\) with respect to \(x\) at \((a,b)\) is

\[ f_x(a,b)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(a+h,b)-f(a,b)}{h}, \]

provided the limit exists.

The partial derivative of \(f\) with respect to \(y\) at \((a,b)\) is

\[ f_y(a,b)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(a,b+h)-f(a,b)}{h}, \]

provided the limit exists.

Equivalently, define

\[ g(x)=f(x,b). \]

Then

\[ f_x(a,b)=g'(a). \]

Similarly, if

\[ k(y)=f(a,y), \]

then

\[ f_y(a,b)=k'(b). \]

The notation is flexible. If \(z=f(x,y)\), then

\[ f_x=\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}=\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}=D_xf, \qquad f_y=\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}=\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}=D_yf. \]

TipHow to compute partial derivatives

To compute \(f_x\), treat \(y\) as a constant and differentiate with respect to \(x\). To compute \(f_y\), treat \(x\) as a constant and differentiate with respect to \(y\).

8.5 8.3 Geometric interpretation: slopes of traces

Suppose the surface is \(z=f(x,y)\) and we focus on the point \((a,b,f(a,b))\).

The trace in the plane \(y=b\) is the single-variable curve

\[ z=f(x,b). \]

Its tangent slope at \(x=a\) is \(f_x(a,b)\). The trace in the plane \(x=a\) is

\[ z=f(a,y), \]

and its tangent slope at \(y=b\) is \(f_y(a,b)\).

8.5.1 Example 8.1: A surface and its coordinate traces

Let

\[ f(x,y)=4-\frac{x^2}{4}-y^2. \]

This is a downward-opening elliptic paraboloid. At the point \((1,1)\),

\[ f_x(x,y)=-\frac{x}{2}, \qquad f_y(x,y)=-2y. \]

Thus

\[ f_x(1,1)=-\frac12, \qquad f_y(1,1)=-2. \]

Interpretation:

  • if \(y=1\) is held fixed and \(x\) increases near \(x=1\), the height decreases at rate \(1/2\);
  • if \(x=1\) is held fixed and \(y\) increases near \(y=1\), the height decreases at rate \(2\).
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x = np.linspace(-4, 4, 120)
y = np.linspace(-2.2, 2.2, 120)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)
Z = 4 - X**2/4 - Y**2

a, b = 1.0, 1.0
z0 = 4 - a**2/4 - b**2

fig = plt.figure()
ax = fig.add_subplot(111, projection='3d')
ax.plot_surface(X, Y, Z, alpha=0.45, linewidth=0)

x_trace = np.linspace(-4, 4, 200)
y_trace = np.linspace(-2.2, 2.2, 200)
ax.plot(x_trace, b*np.ones_like(x_trace), 4 - x_trace**2/4 - b**2, linewidth=3)
ax.plot(a*np.ones_like(y_trace), y_trace, 4 - a**2/4 - y_trace**2, linewidth=3)
ax.scatter([a], [b], [z0], s=60)

ax.set_xlabel('x')
ax.set_ylabel('y')
ax.set_zlabel('z')
ax.set_title('Surface with coordinate traces')
plt.show()

8.6 8.4 Computing partial derivatives

8.6.1 Example 8.2: Polynomial, trigonometric, and exponential terms

Let

\[ f(x,y)=x^3+x^2\sin y-xe^y. \]

To compute \(f_x\), hold \(y\) constant:

\[ f_x(x,y)=3x^2+2x\sin y-e^y. \]

To compute \(f_y\), hold \(x\) constant:

\[ f_y(x,y)=x^2\cos y-xe^y. \]

At \((1,2)\),

\[ f_x(1,2)=3+2\sin 2-e^2, \]

and

\[ f_y(1,2)=\cos 2-e^2. \]

8.6.2 Example 8.3: A radial function

Let

\[ f(x,y)=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}. \]

For \((x,y)\ne(0,0)\),

\[ f_x(x,y)=\frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}, \qquad f_y(x,y)=\frac{y}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}. \]

At \((1,2)\),

\[ f_x(1,2)=\frac{1}{\sqrt5}, \qquad f_y(1,2)=\frac{2}{\sqrt5}. \]

At the origin, the formula is not valid because the denominator is zero. In fact, \(f_x(0,0)\) and \(f_y(0,0)\) exist, but the function is not differentiable at the origin. This reminds us that partial derivatives are only coordinate-direction information. They are important, but not always the whole story.

8.7 8.5 Partial derivatives from data

In applications, we often do not know a formula for \(f\). We may only have sampled values. Then partial derivatives are estimated by finite differences.

For example, if we know \(f(1,2)\), \(f(1.1,2)\), and \(f(1,2.15)\), then

\[ f_x(1,2)\approx \frac{f(1.1,2)-f(1,2)}{0.1}, \]

and

\[ f_y(1,2)\approx \frac{f(1,2.15)-f(1,2)}{0.15}. \]

This idea is fundamental in numerical differentiation, image processing, computer vision, and machine learning.

8.7.1 Example 8.4: Finite-difference estimates

Use

\[ f(x,y)=x^3+x^2\sin y-xe^y \]

and estimate \(f_x(1,2)\) and \(f_y(1,2)\) using forward differences.

Forward differences are simple, but they are not always very accurate. A better estimate often uses the centered difference

\[ f_x(a,b)\approx \frac{f(a+h,b)-f(a-h,b)}{2h}. \]

The same idea works for \(f_y\).

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt


def f_num(x, y):
    return x**3 + x**2*np.sin(y) - x*np.exp(y)

x0, y0 = 1.0, 2.0
exact = 3*x0**2 + 2*x0*np.sin(y0) - np.exp(y0)
h_values = 10.0**(-np.arange(1, 9))
forward_errors = []
centered_errors = []

for h in h_values:
    forward = (f_num(x0+h, y0) - f_num(x0, y0)) / h
    centered = (f_num(x0+h, y0) - f_num(x0-h, y0)) / (2*h)
    forward_errors.append(abs(forward - exact))
    centered_errors.append(abs(centered - exact))

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.loglog(h_values, forward_errors, marker='o', label='forward difference')
ax.loglog(h_values, centered_errors, marker='o', label='centered difference')
ax.set_xlabel('step size h')
ax.set_ylabel('absolute error')
ax.set_title('Finite-difference error for f_x(1,2)')
ax.legend()
ax.grid(True, which='both', alpha=0.3)
plt.show()

8.8 8.6 Image processing interpretation

An image can be viewed as a function

\[ I(x,y)=\text{pixel intensity at location }(x,y). \]

Then \(I_x\) measures horizontal change and \(I_y\) measures vertical change. Large values of \(|I_x|\) or \(|I_y|\) often indicate edges. This is the same calculus idea, but applied to a matrix of data instead of a symbolic formula.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

n = 120
x = np.linspace(-1, 1, n)
y = np.linspace(-1, 1, n)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)
image = np.exp(-8*((X+0.35)**2 + (Y-0.1)**2)) + 0.75*np.exp(-18*((X-0.3)**2 + (Y+0.25)**2))
image += 0.15*(X > 0.1)

dy, dx = np.gradient(image, y, x)
grad_mag = np.sqrt(dx**2 + dy**2)

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(11, 3.5))
axes[0].imshow(image, origin='lower', extent=[-1, 1, -1, 1])
axes[0].set_title('image intensity I')
axes[1].imshow(dx, origin='lower', extent=[-1, 1, -1, 1])
axes[1].set_title('horizontal derivative I_x')
axes[2].imshow(grad_mag, origin='lower', extent=[-1, 1, -1, 1])
axes[2].set_title('gradient magnitude')
for ax in axes:
    ax.set_xticks([])
    ax.set_yticks([])
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()
NoteModern interpretation

In symbolic calculus, \(f_x\) and \(f_y\) are formulas. In data analysis, they are often approximations computed from samples, grids, or pixels. The concept is the same: measure local change in coordinate directions.

8.9 8.7 Second partial derivatives

Partial derivatives can be differentiated again. If \(z=f(x,y)\), then the four second partial derivatives are

\[ f_{xx}=\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\right), \qquad f_{xy}=\frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\right), \]

\[ f_{yx}=\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\right), \qquad f_{yy}=\frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\right). \]

The derivatives \(f_{xy}\) and \(f_{yx}\) are called mixed partial derivatives.

8.9.1 Example 8.5: Second partials and mixed partials

Let

\[ f(x,y)=x^2+xy^3+\sin(xy). \]

First derivatives:

\[ f_x=2x+y^3+y\cos(xy), \]

\[ f_y=3xy^2+x\cos(xy). \]

Second derivatives:

\[ f_{xx}=2-y^2\sin(xy), \]

\[ f_{xy}=3y^2+\cos(xy)-xy\sin(xy), \]

\[ f_{yx}=3y^2+\cos(xy)-xy\sin(xy), \]

\[ f_{yy}=6xy-x^2\sin(xy). \]

Here the mixed partial derivatives are equal.

8.10 8.8 Clairaut’s theorem

The equality \(f_{xy}=f_{yx}\) is common, but it is not automatic for every possible function. The standard theorem is the following.

ImportantClairaut theorem

Suppose \(f\) is defined on a disk containing \((a,b)\). If \(f_{xy}\) and \(f_{yx}\) are both continuous on that disk, then

\[ f_{xy}(a,b)=f_{yx}(a,b). \]

In most elementary examples involving polynomials, exponentials, logarithms, and trigonometric functions on regions where the formulas are defined, the continuity hypothesis is satisfied. Then the order of mixed partial differentiation can be switched.

8.11 8.9 The gradient vector

The first partial derivatives form a vector.

For \(f(x,y)\),

\[ \nabla f(x,y)=\langle f_x(x,y), f_y(x,y)\rangle. \]

For \(f(x,y,z)\),

\[ \nabla f(x,y,z)=\langle f_x(x,y,z), f_y(x,y,z), f_z(x,y,z)\rangle. \]

More generally, for \(f(x_1, \ldots,x_n)\),

\[ \nabla f(x_1,\ldots,x_n) = \left\langle \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}, \ldots, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n} \right\rangle. \]

The symbol \(\nabla\) is pronounced del or nabla.

8.11.1 Example 8.6: Gradient of a two-variable function

Let

\[ f(x,y)=\sin(xy)+e^y. \]

Then

\[ f_x=y\cos(xy), \]

and

\[ f_y=x\cos(xy)+e^y. \]

Therefore

\[ \nabla f(x,y)=\langle y\cos(xy), x\cos(xy)+e^y\rangle. \]

At \((0,1)\),

\[ \nabla f(0,1)=\langle 1,e\rangle. \]

8.11.2 Example 8.7: Gradient of a three-variable function

Let

\[ f(x,y,z)=xyz. \]

Then

\[ f_x=yz, \qquad f_y=xz, \qquad f_z=xy. \]

Thus

\[ \nabla f(x,y,z)=\langle yz,xz,xy\rangle. \]

At \((1,2,3)\),

\[ \nabla f(1,2,3)=\langle 6,3,2\rangle. \]

8.12 8.10 The gradient and level curves

Level curves have equations

\[ f(x,y)=c. \]

The gradient \(\nabla f\) is perpendicular to the level curve at points where \(\nabla f\ne 0\).

Why? Suppose \(r(t)=\langle x(t),y(t)\rangle\) is a curve that stays on the level curve \(f(x,y)=c\). Then

\[ f(x(t),y(t))=c. \]

Differentiating with respect to \(t\) gives

\[ \nabla f(x(t),y(t))\cdot r'(t)=0. \]

The vector \(r'(t)\) is tangent to the level curve. Therefore the gradient is perpendicular to the tangent direction.

ImportantGeometric meaning of the gradient

The gradient points normal to level curves and level surfaces. It tells us which direction cuts most directly across the contours.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x = np.linspace(-4, 4, 250)
y = np.linspace(-2.3, 2.3, 250)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)
H = 4 - X**2/4 - Y**2

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
cs = ax.contour(X, Y, H, levels=[0, 1, 2, 3, 3.5], linewidths=1.2)
ax.clabel(cs, inline=True, fontsize=8)

points = np.array([[2, 0], [-2, 0], [0, 1], [0, -1], [1, np.sqrt(3)/2], [-1, -np.sqrt(3)/2]])
U = -points[:, 0] / 2
V = -2 * points[:, 1]
ax.quiver(points[:, 0], points[:, 1], U, V, angles='xy', scale_units='xy', scale=3)
ax.scatter(points[:, 0], points[:, 1], s=25)

ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
ax.set_xlabel('x')
ax.set_ylabel('y')
ax.set_title('Level curves and gradient vectors')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)
plt.show()

8.13 8.11 Gradient fields

For each point \((x,y)\), the gradient produces a vector \(\nabla f(x,y)\). The collection of these vectors is called a gradient field.

Gradient fields are important because they show local directions of increase. In later chapters, we will study vector fields more generally. Gradient fields are a special class of vector fields with strong structure.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x = np.linspace(-2.5, 2.5, 21)
y = np.linspace(-2.0, 2.0, 21)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)
Z = np.sin(X*Y) + np.exp(0.4*Y)
Fx = Y*np.cos(X*Y)
Fy = X*np.cos(X*Y) + 0.4*np.exp(0.4*Y)

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.contour(X, Y, Z, levels=20, linewidths=0.8)
ax.quiver(X, Y, Fx, Fy, angles='xy')
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
ax.set_xlabel('x')
ax.set_ylabel('y')
ax.set_title('Gradient field over contour map')
plt.show()

8.14 8.12 Directional derivatives as a preview

The next major idea is that we can measure rate of change in any unit direction \(u\). If

\[ u=\langle a,b\rangle \]

is a unit vector, the directional derivative is

\[ D_u f(x,y)=\nabla f(x,y)\cdot u. \]

This formula says that the gradient contains all first-order directional information. The coordinate partial derivatives are special cases:

\[ D_{\mathbf i}f=f_x, \qquad D_{\mathbf j}f=f_y. \]

A full geometric treatment of directional derivatives, maximum rate of change, and gradient descent appears later, but the key point begins here: the gradient is the vector that converts directions into rates of change.

8.14.1 Example 8.8: Directional derivative from a gradient

For

\[ f(x,y)=\sin(xy)+e^y, \]

we found

\[ \nabla f(0,1)=\langle 1,e\rangle. \]

Let \(v=\langle 2,3\rangle\). The corresponding unit vector is

\[ u=\frac{v}{\|v\|}=\left\langle\frac{2}{\sqrt{13}},\frac{3}{\sqrt{13}}\right\rangle. \]

Therefore

\[ D_u f(0,1) = \langle 1,e\rangle\cdot \left\langle\frac{2}{\sqrt{13}},\frac{3}{\sqrt{13}}\right\rangle = \frac{2+3e}{\sqrt{13}}. \]

8.15 8.13 Gradients in statistics and machine learning

In statistics and machine learning, a model often has parameters

\[ \theta=(\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_n), \]

and we minimize a loss function

\[ L(\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_n). \]

The partial derivative

\[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial \theta_k} \]

measures how sensitive the loss is to the parameter \(\theta_k\) while the other parameters are held fixed. The gradient

\[ \nabla L(\theta) \]

collects all parameter sensitivities into one vector.

For least-squares linear regression with data \((x_i,y_i)\) and model

\[ \widehat y_i=\beta_0+\beta_1x_i, \]

the loss is

\[ L(\beta_0,\beta_1)=\sum_{i=1}^m \left(y_i-(\beta_0+\beta_1x_i)\right)^2. \]

The partial derivatives are

\[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial \beta_0} = -2\sum_{i=1}^m \left(y_i-(\beta_0+\beta_1x_i)\right), \]

and

\[ \frac{\partial L}{\partial \beta_1} = -2\sum_{i=1}^m x_i\left(y_i-(\beta_0+\beta_1x_i)\right). \]

These two numbers form the gradient in the parameter plane.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(8)
x_data = np.linspace(-2, 2, 35)
y_data = 1.0 + 1.8*x_data + rng.normal(scale=0.3, size=x_data.shape)

b0 = np.linspace(-1, 3, 130)
b1 = np.linspace(0, 3.5, 130)
B0, B1 = np.meshgrid(b0, b1)
Loss = np.zeros_like(B0)
G0 = np.zeros_like(B0)
G1 = np.zeros_like(B1)

for x_i, y_i in zip(x_data, y_data):
    residual = y_i - (B0 + B1*x_i)
    Loss += residual**2
    G0 += -2*residual
    G1 += -2*x_i*residual

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
cs = ax.contour(B0, B1, Loss, levels=24)
ax.clabel(cs, inline=True, fontsize=8)
step = 16
ax.quiver(B0[::step, ::step], B1[::step, ::step], G0[::step, ::step], G1[::step, ::step], angles='xy', scale_units='xy', scale=350)
ax.set_xlabel(r'$\beta_0$')
ax.set_ylabel(r'$\beta_1$')
ax.set_title('Gradient vectors for a least-squares loss')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)
plt.show()

8.16 8.14 Common mistakes

WarningMistake 1: Differentiating every variable at once

When computing \(f_x\), only \(x\) varies. All other variables are constants. For example, if \(f(x,y)=x^2y\), then \(f_x=2xy\), not \(2xy+x^2\).

WarningMistake 2: Confusing partial derivatives with total change

The partial derivative \(f_x(a,b)\) measures change only in the \(x\) direction. It does not describe motion along an arbitrary path unless the path moves only in the \(x\) direction.

WarningMistake 3: Forgetting to normalize a direction vector

In the directional derivative formula \(D_u f=\nabla f\cdot u\), the vector \(u\) must be a unit vector. If a problem gives a nonunit direction vector, divide by its length first.

WarningMistake 4: Assuming mixed partials are always equal

Mixed partials are equal under hypotheses such as continuity near the point. For standard smooth examples this is usually true, but the theorem has a condition.

8.19 Chapter summary

Partial derivatives are the coordinate-direction derivatives of a multivariable function. They are ordinary derivatives applied to slices of the function. For \(f(x,y)\), the two first partial derivatives are \(f_x\) and \(f_y\). For a function of \(n\) variables, there are \(n\) first partial derivatives.

The gradient packages these partial derivatives into a vector:

\[ \nabla f=\left\langle \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}, \ldots, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n} \right\rangle. \]

This vector is perpendicular to level sets and contains the first-order sensitivity information of the function. In modern applications, the gradient is the basic object behind numerical differentiation, image processing, optimization, maximum likelihood, backpropagation, and gradient-based learning.

## Chapter overview

A function of one variable has one basic input direction: move left or move right on the number line. A function of several variables has many input directions. The first step is to measure change in the coordinate directions.

For a function $f(x,y)$, the partial derivative $f_x$ measures the instantaneous rate of change when $x$ changes and $y$ is held fixed. The partial derivative $f_y$ measures the instantaneous rate of change when $y$ changes and $x$ is held fixed. These two numbers are not separate facts. Together they form the **gradient vector**

$$
\nabla f(x,y)=\langle f_x(x,y), f_y(x,y)\rangle.
$$

The gradient is the local direction-information vector for a scalar field. It tells us how the output changes to first order near a point, how level curves are oriented, and how a loss function changes when model parameters change. This is why gradients appear everywhere in applied mathematics, statistics, machine learning, optimization, physics, imaging, and AI.

::: {.callout-note title='Teaching-material connection'}
This chapter develops the material on partial derivatives, geometric traces, numerical derivative estimates, second partial derivatives, Clairaut's theorem, and the gradient vector. It also uses the perspective that partial derivatives reduce multivariable differentiation to single-variable differentiation by holding all other variables fixed.
:::

## 8.1 From one derivative to many partial derivatives

For a single-variable function $y=f(x)$, the derivative at $x=a$ is

$$
f'(a)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}.
$$

Geometrically, this is the slope of the tangent line at $x=a$. For a two-variable function $z=f(x,y)$, the graph is a surface. At a point $(a,b,f(a,b))$, there are many possible curves on the surface passing through that point. The easiest curves are the two coordinate traces:

- the trace obtained by fixing $y=b$ and allowing $x$ to vary;
- the trace obtained by fixing $x=a$ and allowing $y$ to vary.

The slopes of these two traces are the partial derivatives.

::: {.callout-important title='Core idea'}
A partial derivative is an ordinary derivative applied to a slice of a multivariable function.
:::

## 8.2 Definitions of $f_x$ and $f_y$

Let $f$ be a function of two variables. The partial derivative of $f$ with respect to $x$ at $(a,b)$ is

$$
f_x(a,b)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(a+h,b)-f(a,b)}{h},
$$

provided the limit exists.

The partial derivative of $f$ with respect to $y$ at $(a,b)$ is

$$
f_y(a,b)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(a,b+h)-f(a,b)}{h},
$$

provided the limit exists.

Equivalently, define

$$
g(x)=f(x,b).
$$

Then

$$
f_x(a,b)=g'(a).
$$

Similarly, if

$$
k(y)=f(a,y),
$$

then

$$
f_y(a,b)=k'(b).
$$

The notation is flexible. If $z=f(x,y)$, then

$$
f_x=\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}=\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}=D_xf,
\qquad
f_y=\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}=\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}=D_yf.
$$

::: {.callout-tip title='How to compute partial derivatives'}
To compute $f_x$, treat $y$ as a constant and differentiate with respect to $x$. To compute $f_y$, treat $x$ as a constant and differentiate with respect to $y$.
:::

## 8.3 Geometric interpretation: slopes of traces

Suppose the surface is $z=f(x,y)$ and we focus on the point $(a,b,f(a,b))$.

The trace in the plane $y=b$ is the single-variable curve

$$
z=f(x,b).
$$

Its tangent slope at $x=a$ is $f_x(a,b)$. The trace in the plane $x=a$ is

$$
z=f(a,y),
$$

and its tangent slope at $y=b$ is $f_y(a,b)$.

### Example 8.1: A surface and its coordinate traces

Let

$$
f(x,y)=4-\frac{x^2}{4}-y^2.
$$

This is a downward-opening elliptic paraboloid. At the point $(1,1)$,

$$
f_x(x,y)=-\frac{x}{2},
\qquad
f_y(x,y)=-2y.
$$

Thus

$$
f_x(1,1)=-\frac12,
\qquad
f_y(1,1)=-2.
$$

Interpretation:

- if $y=1$ is held fixed and $x$ increases near $x=1$, the height decreases at rate $1/2$;
- if $x=1$ is held fixed and $y$ increases near $y=1$, the height decreases at rate $2$.

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch08-surface-traces
#| fig-cap: 'Coordinate traces of $f(x,y)=4-x^2/4-y^2$ through the point $(1,1)$. The partial derivatives are slopes of these traces.'
#| fig-width: 7
#| fig-height: 5
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x = np.linspace(-4, 4, 120)
y = np.linspace(-2.2, 2.2, 120)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)
Z = 4 - X**2/4 - Y**2

a, b = 1.0, 1.0
z0 = 4 - a**2/4 - b**2

fig = plt.figure()
ax = fig.add_subplot(111, projection='3d')
ax.plot_surface(X, Y, Z, alpha=0.45, linewidth=0)

x_trace = np.linspace(-4, 4, 200)
y_trace = np.linspace(-2.2, 2.2, 200)
ax.plot(x_trace, b*np.ones_like(x_trace), 4 - x_trace**2/4 - b**2, linewidth=3)
ax.plot(a*np.ones_like(y_trace), y_trace, 4 - a**2/4 - y_trace**2, linewidth=3)
ax.scatter([a], [b], [z0], s=60)

ax.set_xlabel('x')
ax.set_ylabel('y')
ax.set_zlabel('z')
ax.set_title('Surface with coordinate traces')
plt.show()
```

## 8.4 Computing partial derivatives

### Example 8.2: Polynomial, trigonometric, and exponential terms

Let

$$
f(x,y)=x^3+x^2\sin y-xe^y.
$$

To compute $f_x$, hold $y$ constant:

$$
f_x(x,y)=3x^2+2x\sin y-e^y.
$$

To compute $f_y$, hold $x$ constant:

$$
f_y(x,y)=x^2\cos y-xe^y.
$$

At $(1,2)$,

$$
f_x(1,2)=3+2\sin 2-e^2,
$$

and

$$
f_y(1,2)=\cos 2-e^2.
$$

### Example 8.3: A radial function

Let

$$
f(x,y)=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}.
$$

For $(x,y)\ne(0,0)$,

$$
f_x(x,y)=\frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}},
\qquad
f_y(x,y)=\frac{y}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}.
$$

At $(1,2)$,

$$
f_x(1,2)=\frac{1}{\sqrt5},
\qquad
f_y(1,2)=\frac{2}{\sqrt5}.
$$

At the origin, the formula is not valid because the denominator is zero. In fact, $f_x(0,0)$ and $f_y(0,0)$ exist, but the function is not differentiable at the origin. This reminds us that partial derivatives are only coordinate-direction information. They are important, but not always the whole story.

## 8.5 Partial derivatives from data

In applications, we often do not know a formula for $f$. We may only have sampled values. Then partial derivatives are estimated by finite differences.

For example, if we know $f(1,2)$, $f(1.1,2)$, and $f(1,2.15)$, then

$$
f_x(1,2)\approx \frac{f(1.1,2)-f(1,2)}{0.1},
$$

and

$$
f_y(1,2)\approx \frac{f(1,2.15)-f(1,2)}{0.15}.
$$

This idea is fundamental in numerical differentiation, image processing, computer vision, and machine learning.

### Example 8.4: Finite-difference estimates

Use

$$
f(x,y)=x^3+x^2\sin y-xe^y
$$

and estimate $f_x(1,2)$ and $f_y(1,2)$ using forward differences.

```{python}
#| label: tbl-ch08-finite-difference-estimates
import numpy as np


def f(x, y):
    return x**3 + x**2*np.sin(y) - x*np.exp(y)

point = (1.0, 2.0)
hx = 0.1
hy = 0.15

fx_forward = (f(point[0] + hx, point[1]) - f(*point)) / hx
fy_forward = (f(point[0], point[1] + hy) - f(*point)) / hy

fx_exact = 3*point[0]**2 + 2*point[0]*np.sin(point[1]) - np.exp(point[1])
fy_exact = point[0]**2*np.cos(point[1]) - point[0]*np.exp(point[1])

print('Forward estimate for f_x(1,2):', fx_forward)
print('Exact value of f_x(1,2):       ', fx_exact)
print('Forward estimate for f_y(1,2):', fy_forward)
print('Exact value of f_y(1,2):       ', fy_exact)
```

Forward differences are simple, but they are not always very accurate. A better estimate often uses the centered difference

$$
f_x(a,b)\approx \frac{f(a+h,b)-f(a-h,b)}{2h}.
$$

The same idea works for $f_y$.

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch08-finite-difference-error
#| fig-cap: 'Centered differences usually converge faster than forward differences for smooth functions.'
#| fig-width: 7
#| fig-height: 4.5
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt


def f_num(x, y):
    return x**3 + x**2*np.sin(y) - x*np.exp(y)

x0, y0 = 1.0, 2.0
exact = 3*x0**2 + 2*x0*np.sin(y0) - np.exp(y0)
h_values = 10.0**(-np.arange(1, 9))
forward_errors = []
centered_errors = []

for h in h_values:
    forward = (f_num(x0+h, y0) - f_num(x0, y0)) / h
    centered = (f_num(x0+h, y0) - f_num(x0-h, y0)) / (2*h)
    forward_errors.append(abs(forward - exact))
    centered_errors.append(abs(centered - exact))

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.loglog(h_values, forward_errors, marker='o', label='forward difference')
ax.loglog(h_values, centered_errors, marker='o', label='centered difference')
ax.set_xlabel('step size h')
ax.set_ylabel('absolute error')
ax.set_title('Finite-difference error for f_x(1,2)')
ax.legend()
ax.grid(True, which='both', alpha=0.3)
plt.show()
```

## 8.6 Image processing interpretation

An image can be viewed as a function

$$
I(x,y)=\text{pixel intensity at location }(x,y).
$$

Then $I_x$ measures horizontal change and $I_y$ measures vertical change. Large values of $|I_x|$ or $|I_y|$ often indicate edges. This is the same calculus idea, but applied to a matrix of data instead of a symbolic formula.

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch08-image-gradient
#| fig-cap: 'Finite differences applied to image-like data. Horizontal and vertical derivatives reveal edges.'
#| fig-width: 8
#| fig-height: 4.5
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

n = 120
x = np.linspace(-1, 1, n)
y = np.linspace(-1, 1, n)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)
image = np.exp(-8*((X+0.35)**2 + (Y-0.1)**2)) + 0.75*np.exp(-18*((X-0.3)**2 + (Y+0.25)**2))
image += 0.15*(X > 0.1)

dy, dx = np.gradient(image, y, x)
grad_mag = np.sqrt(dx**2 + dy**2)

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 3, figsize=(11, 3.5))
axes[0].imshow(image, origin='lower', extent=[-1, 1, -1, 1])
axes[0].set_title('image intensity I')
axes[1].imshow(dx, origin='lower', extent=[-1, 1, -1, 1])
axes[1].set_title('horizontal derivative I_x')
axes[2].imshow(grad_mag, origin='lower', extent=[-1, 1, -1, 1])
axes[2].set_title('gradient magnitude')
for ax in axes:
    ax.set_xticks([])
    ax.set_yticks([])
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()
```

::: {.callout-note title='Modern interpretation'}
In symbolic calculus, $f_x$ and $f_y$ are formulas. In data analysis, they are often approximations computed from samples, grids, or pixels. The concept is the same: measure local change in coordinate directions.
:::

## 8.7 Second partial derivatives

Partial derivatives can be differentiated again. If $z=f(x,y)$, then the four second partial derivatives are

$$
f_{xx}=\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\right),
\qquad
f_{xy}=\frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\right),
$$

$$
f_{yx}=\frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\right),
\qquad
f_{yy}=\frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\right).
$$

The derivatives $f_{xy}$ and $f_{yx}$ are called **mixed partial derivatives**.

### Example 8.5: Second partials and mixed partials

Let

$$
f(x,y)=x^2+xy^3+\sin(xy).
$$

First derivatives:

$$
f_x=2x+y^3+y\cos(xy),
$$

$$
f_y=3xy^2+x\cos(xy).
$$

Second derivatives:

$$
f_{xx}=2-y^2\sin(xy),
$$

$$
f_{xy}=3y^2+\cos(xy)-xy\sin(xy),
$$

$$
f_{yx}=3y^2+\cos(xy)-xy\sin(xy),
$$

$$
f_{yy}=6xy-x^2\sin(xy).
$$

Here the mixed partial derivatives are equal.

## 8.8 Clairaut's theorem

The equality $f_{xy}=f_{yx}$ is common, but it is not automatic for every possible function. The standard theorem is the following.

::: {.callout-important title='Clairaut theorem'}
Suppose $f$ is defined on a disk containing $(a,b)$. If $f_{xy}$ and $f_{yx}$ are both continuous on that disk, then

$$
f_{xy}(a,b)=f_{yx}(a,b).
$$
:::

In most elementary examples involving polynomials, exponentials, logarithms, and trigonometric functions on regions where the formulas are defined, the continuity hypothesis is satisfied. Then the order of mixed partial differentiation can be switched.

## 8.9 The gradient vector

The first partial derivatives form a vector.

For $f(x,y)$,

$$
\nabla f(x,y)=\langle f_x(x,y), f_y(x,y)\rangle.
$$

For $f(x,y,z)$,

$$
\nabla f(x,y,z)=\langle f_x(x,y,z), f_y(x,y,z), f_z(x,y,z)\rangle.
$$

More generally, for $f(x_1,
\ldots,x_n)$,

$$
\nabla f(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=
\left\langle
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1},
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2},
\ldots,
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}
\right\rangle.
$$

The symbol $\nabla$ is pronounced **del** or **nabla**.

### Example 8.6: Gradient of a two-variable function

Let

$$
f(x,y)=\sin(xy)+e^y.
$$

Then

$$
f_x=y\cos(xy),
$$

and

$$
f_y=x\cos(xy)+e^y.
$$

Therefore

$$
\nabla f(x,y)=\langle y\cos(xy), x\cos(xy)+e^y\rangle.
$$

At $(0,1)$,

$$
\nabla f(0,1)=\langle 1,e\rangle.
$$

### Example 8.7: Gradient of a three-variable function

Let

$$
f(x,y,z)=xyz.
$$

Then

$$
f_x=yz,
\qquad
f_y=xz,
\qquad
f_z=xy.
$$

Thus

$$
\nabla f(x,y,z)=\langle yz,xz,xy\rangle.
$$

At $(1,2,3)$,

$$
\nabla f(1,2,3)=\langle 6,3,2\rangle.
$$

## 8.10 The gradient and level curves

Level curves have equations

$$
f(x,y)=c.
$$

The gradient $\nabla f$ is perpendicular to the level curve at points where $\nabla f\ne 0$.

Why? Suppose $r(t)=\langle x(t),y(t)\rangle$ is a curve that stays on the level curve $f(x,y)=c$. Then

$$
f(x(t),y(t))=c.
$$

Differentiating with respect to $t$ gives

$$
\nabla f(x(t),y(t))\cdot r'(t)=0.
$$

The vector $r'(t)$ is tangent to the level curve. Therefore the gradient is perpendicular to the tangent direction.

::: {.callout-important title='Geometric meaning of the gradient'}
The gradient points normal to level curves and level surfaces. It tells us which direction cuts most directly across the contours.
:::

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch08-gradient-level-curves
```

## 8.12 Directional derivatives as a preview

The next major idea is that we can measure rate of change in any unit direction $u$. If

$$
u=\langle a,b\rangle
$$

is a unit vector, the directional derivative is

$$
D_u f(x,y)=\nabla f(x,y)\cdot u.
$$

This formula says that the gradient contains all first-order directional information. The coordinate partial derivatives are special cases:

$$
D_{\mathbf i}f=f_x,
\qquad
D_{\mathbf j}f=f_y.
$$

A full geometric treatment of directional derivatives, maximum rate of change, and gradient descent appears later, but the key point begins here: **the gradient is the vector that converts directions into rates of change**.

### Example 8.8: Directional derivative from a gradient

For

$$
f(x,y)=\sin(xy)+e^y,
$$

we found

$$
\nabla f(0,1)=\langle 1,e\rangle.
$$

Let $v=\langle 2,3\rangle$. The corresponding unit vector is

$$
u=\frac{v}{\|v\|}=\left\langle\frac{2}{\sqrt{13}},\frac{3}{\sqrt{13}}\right\rangle.
$$

Therefore

$$
D_u f(0,1)
=
\langle 1,e\rangle\cdot
\left\langle\frac{2}{\sqrt{13}},\frac{3}{\sqrt{13}}\right\rangle
=
\frac{2+3e}{\sqrt{13}}.
$$

## 8.13 Gradients in statistics and machine learning

In statistics and machine learning, a model often has parameters

$$
\theta=(\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_n),
$$

and we minimize a loss function

$$
L(\theta_1,\ldots,\theta_n).
$$

The partial derivative

$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial \theta_k}
$$

measures how sensitive the loss is to the parameter $\theta_k$ while the other parameters are held fixed. The gradient

$$
\nabla L(\theta)
$$

collects all parameter sensitivities into one vector.

For least-squares linear regression with data $(x_i,y_i)$ and model

$$
\widehat y_i=\beta_0+\beta_1x_i,
$$

the loss is

$$
L(\beta_0,\beta_1)=\sum_{i=1}^m \left(y_i-(\beta_0+\beta_1x_i)\right)^2.
$$

The partial derivatives are

$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial \beta_0}
= -2\sum_{i=1}^m \left(y_i-(\beta_0+\beta_1x_i)\right),
$$

and

$$
\frac{\partial L}{\partial \beta_1}
= -2\sum_{i=1}^m x_i\left(y_i-(\beta_0+\beta_1x_i)\right).
$$

These two numbers form the gradient in the parameter plane.

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch08-least-squares-gradient
#| fig-cap: 'The gradient of a least-squares loss points toward increasing loss; the negative gradient points downhill.'
#| fig-width: 7
#| fig-height: 5
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(8)
x_data = np.linspace(-2, 2, 35)
y_data = 1.0 + 1.8*x_data + rng.normal(scale=0.3, size=x_data.shape)

b0 = np.linspace(-1, 3, 130)
b1 = np.linspace(0, 3.5, 130)
B0, B1 = np.meshgrid(b0, b1)
Loss = np.zeros_like(B0)
G0 = np.zeros_like(B0)
G1 = np.zeros_like(B1)

for x_i, y_i in zip(x_data, y_data):
    residual = y_i - (B0 + B1*x_i)
    Loss += residual**2
    G0 += -2*residual
    G1 += -2*x_i*residual

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
cs = ax.contour(B0, B1, Loss, levels=24)
ax.clabel(cs, inline=True, fontsize=8)
step = 16
ax.quiver(B0[::step, ::step], B1[::step, ::step], G0[::step, ::step], G1[::step, ::step], angles='xy', scale_units='xy', scale=350)
ax.set_xlabel(r'$\beta_0$')
ax.set_ylabel(r'$\beta_1$')
ax.set_title('Gradient vectors for a least-squares loss')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)
plt.show()
```

## 8.14 Common mistakes

::: {.callout-warning title='Mistake 1: Differentiating every variable at once'}
When computing $f_x$, only $x$ varies. All other variables are constants. For example, if $f(x,y)=x^2y$, then $f_x=2xy$, not $2xy+x^2$.
:::

::: {.callout-warning title='Mistake 2: Confusing partial derivatives with total change'}
The partial derivative $f_x(a,b)$ measures change only in the $x$ direction. It does not describe motion along an arbitrary path unless the path moves only in the $x$ direction.
:::

::: {.callout-warning title='Mistake 3: Forgetting to normalize a direction vector'}
In the directional derivative formula $D_u f=\nabla f\cdot u$, the vector $u$ must be a unit vector. If a problem gives a nonunit direction vector, divide by its length first.
:::

::: {.callout-warning title='Mistake 4: Assuming mixed partials are always equal'}
Mixed partials are equal under hypotheses such as continuity near the point. For standard smooth examples this is usually true, but the theorem has a condition.
:::

## Chapter summary

Partial derivatives are the coordinate-direction derivatives of a multivariable function. They are ordinary derivatives applied to slices of the function. For $f(x,y)$, the two first partial derivatives are $f_x$ and $f_y$. For a function of $n$ variables, there are $n$ first partial derivatives.

The gradient packages these partial derivatives into a vector:

$$
\nabla f=\left\langle
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1},
\ldots,
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}
\right\rangle.
$$

This vector is perpendicular to level sets and contains the first-order sensitivity information of the function. In modern applications, the gradient is the basic object behind numerical differentiation, image processing, optimization, maximum likelihood, backpropagation, and gradient-based learning.
