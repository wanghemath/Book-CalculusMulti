5  Chapter 5: Cross Products, Curves, and Motion in Space

Orientation, area, space curves, arc length, curvature, velocity, and acceleration

In the previous chapters we learned how to describe points, vectors, lines, planes, and hyperplanes. This chapter adds two geometric tools that are essential for the rest of multivariable calculus.

The first tool is the cross product. Unlike the dot product, which returns a scalar, the cross product returns a vector. It encodes orientation, normal directions, area, and volume. It is the natural algebraic language for finding normal vectors to planes, surface orientations, torque, curl, and flux.

The second tool is the calculus of vector-valued functions. A curve in the plane or in space can be described by a position vector

\[ \mathbf r(t)=\langle x(t),y(t),z(t)\rangle. \]

Differentiating this vector gives velocity. Differentiating again gives acceleration. Integrating speed gives arc length. Differentiating the unit tangent vector gives curvature. These ideas connect geometry, physics, optimization paths, gradient descent trajectories, and data streams evolving through time.

This chapter is a bridge from vector geometry to vector calculus. Later, when we study line integrals, vector fields, Green’s theorem, Stokes’ theorem, and the divergence theorem, we will use the ideas here constantly.

5.1 Learning goals

After studying this chapter, you should be able to:

  1. compute cross products using determinants and component formulas;
  2. interpret the direction of \(\mathbf a\times \mathbf b\) using the right-hand rule;
  3. compute areas using \(|\mathbf a\times \mathbf b|\);
  4. compute volumes using the scalar triple product;
  5. describe curves using vector-valued functions;
  6. compute velocity, speed, and acceleration;
  7. compute arc length and reparameterize simple curves by arc length;
  8. compute unit tangent vectors and curvature;
  9. use Python to visualize orientation, curves, tangent vectors, curvature, and motion.

5.2 5.1 Why the cross product is needed

The dot product answers a question about alignment:

\[ \mathbf a\cdot \mathbf b=|\mathbf a||\mathbf b|\cos\theta. \]

It tells us whether two vectors point in similar, opposite, or perpendicular directions. But in three dimensions, we often need a vector that is perpendicular to two given vectors.

For example:

  • a plane is often described by a normal vector;
  • the orientation of a surface is described by a normal vector;
  • the area of a parallelogram spanned by two vectors is measured by a perpendicular vector;
  • the torque from a force is perpendicular to both the radius vector and the force vector;
  • the curl of a vector field is written using a cross product with the differential operator \(\nabla\).

The cross product is designed for exactly these tasks.

5.3 5.2 Definition and computation of the cross product

Let

\[ \mathbf a=\langle a_1,a_2,a_3\rangle, \qquad \mathbf b=\langle b_1,b_2,b_3\rangle. \]

The cross product \(\mathbf a\times \mathbf b\) is the vector

\[ \mathbf a\times \mathbf b = \left\langle a_2b_3-a_3b_2, a_3b_1-a_1b_3, a_1b_2-a_2b_1 \right\rangle. \]

It is often remembered by the determinant notation

\[ \mathbf a\times \mathbf b = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf i & \mathbf j & \mathbf k\\ a_1 & a_2 & a_3\\ b_1 & b_2 & b_3 \end{vmatrix}. \]

Expanding along the first row gives

\[ \mathbf a\times \mathbf b =(a_2b_3-a_3b_2)\mathbf i -(a_1b_3-a_3b_1)\mathbf j +(a_1b_2-a_2b_1)\mathbf k. \]

WarningThe cross product exists in this form only in \(\mathbb R^3\)

In this calculus course, the cross product is a special operation for three-dimensional vectors. In higher-dimensional linear algebra, the related ideas are expressed using determinants, exterior products, orientations, and differential forms.

5.3.1 Example 5.1: Compute a cross product

Let

\[ \mathbf a=\langle 1,2,3\rangle, \qquad \mathbf b=\langle 3,2,1\rangle. \]

Then

\[ \begin{aligned} \mathbf a\times \mathbf b &= \left\langle 2\cdot 1-3\cdot 2, 3\cdot 3-1\cdot 1, 1\cdot 2-2\cdot 3 \right\rangle\\ &=\langle -4,8,-4\rangle. \end{aligned} \]

This vector is perpendicular to both \(\mathbf a\) and \(\mathbf b\):

\[ \langle -4,8,-4\rangle\cdot \langle 1,2,3\rangle=-4+16-12=0, \]

and

\[ \langle -4,8,-4\rangle\cdot \langle 3,2,1\rangle=-12+16-4=0. \]

This same computation is a standard way to find a direction vector for a line perpendicular to two given vectors, or a normal vector to a plane spanned by two vectors.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

fig = plt.figure()
ax = fig.add_subplot(111, projection='3d')

a = np.array([1, 2, 3])
b = np.array([3, 2, 1])
c = np.cross(a, b)

origin = np.zeros(3)
for vector, label in [(a, 'a'), (b, 'b'), (c, 'a x b')]:
    ax.quiver(*origin, *vector, arrow_length_ratio=0.08)
    ax.text(*(vector * 1.05), label)

# Draw the parallelogram spanned by a and b.
parallelogram = np.array([origin, a, a + b, b, origin])
ax.plot(parallelogram[:, 0], parallelogram[:, 1], parallelogram[:, 2], linewidth=2)

ax.set_xlabel('x')
ax.set_ylabel('y')
ax.set_zlabel('z')
ax.set_title('Cross product as a normal vector')
ax.set_box_aspect([1, 1, 1])
plt.show()

5.4 5.3 Basic properties of the cross product

For vectors \(\mathbf a,\mathbf b,\mathbf c\) in \(\mathbb R^3\) and scalars \(\lambda\), the cross product satisfies:

\[ \mathbf a\times \mathbf b=-(\mathbf b\times \mathbf a), \]

\[ \mathbf a\times (\mathbf b+\mathbf c)=\mathbf a\times \mathbf b+\mathbf a\times \mathbf c, \]

\[ (\lambda \mathbf a)\times \mathbf b =\lambda(\mathbf a\times \mathbf b) =\mathbf a\times (\lambda \mathbf b), \]

\[ \mathbf a\times \mathbf a=\mathbf 0. \]

The first identity is especially important: order matters. Reversing the order reverses the direction.

5.4.1 Standard basis orientation

The standard basis vectors satisfy

\[ \mathbf i\times \mathbf j=\mathbf k, \qquad \mathbf j\times \mathbf k=\mathbf i, \qquad \mathbf k\times \mathbf i=\mathbf j. \]

Reversing the order changes the sign:

\[ \mathbf j\times \mathbf i=-\mathbf k, \qquad \mathbf k\times \mathbf j=-\mathbf i, \qquad \mathbf i\times \mathbf k=-\mathbf j. \]

This orientation is called the right-hand rule. If the fingers of your right hand rotate from \(\mathbf a\) toward \(\mathbf b\), then your thumb points in the direction of \(\mathbf a\times\mathbf b\).

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

fig = plt.figure()
ax = fig.add_subplot(111, projection='3d')

vectors = [np.array([1, 0, 0]), np.array([0, 1, 0]), np.array([0, 0, 1])]
labels = ['i', 'j', 'k']
origin = np.zeros(3)

for vector, label in zip(vectors, labels):
    ax.quiver(*origin, *vector, arrow_length_ratio=0.1)
    ax.text(*(1.12 * vector), label, fontsize=12)

ax.set_xlim(0, 1.3)
ax.set_ylim(0, 1.3)
ax.set_zlim(0, 1.3)
ax.set_xlabel('x')
ax.set_ylabel('y')
ax.set_zlabel('z')
ax.set_title('Right-handed orientation')
ax.set_box_aspect([1, 1, 1])
plt.show()

5.5 5.4 Area from the cross product

The magnitude of the cross product has a geometric meaning:

\[ |\mathbf a\times \mathbf b|=|\mathbf a||\mathbf b|\sin\theta, \]

where \(\theta\) is the angle between \(\mathbf a\) and \(\mathbf b\).

This is the area of the parallelogram spanned by \(\mathbf a\) and \(\mathbf b\).

Therefore:

\[ \text{Area of parallelogram}=|\mathbf a\times\mathbf b|, \]

and

\[ \text{Area of triangle}=\frac12|\mathbf a\times\mathbf b|. \]

5.5.1 Example 5.2: Area of a triangle

Find the area of the triangle with vertices

\[ O=(0,0,0),\qquad A=(1,2,3),\qquad B=(3,2,1). \]

The two side vectors from \(O\) are

\[ \overrightarrow{OA}=\langle 1,2,3\rangle, \qquad \overrightarrow{OB}=\langle 3,2,1\rangle. \]

We computed

\[ \overrightarrow{OA}\times \overrightarrow{OB}=\langle -4,8,-4\rangle. \]

Thus

\[ |\overrightarrow{OA}\times \overrightarrow{OB}| =\sqrt{(-4)^2+8^2+(-4)^2} =\sqrt{96}=4\sqrt 6. \]

So the area of the triangle is

\[ \frac12(4\sqrt6)=2\sqrt6. \]

This example is also a good reminder that a cross product gives both magnitude and orientation.

5.6 5.5 Normal vectors and planes

A plane in \(\mathbb R^3\) can be described using a point \(P_0=(x_0,y_0,z_0)\) and a normal vector

\[ \mathbf n=\langle A,B,C\rangle. \]

The equation is

\[ \mathbf n\cdot \langle x-x_0,y-y_0,z-z_0\rangle=0. \]

In standard form,

\[ A(x-x_0)+B(y-y_0)+C(z-z_0)=0. \]

If a plane is spanned by two nonparallel vectors \(\mathbf u\) and \(\mathbf v\), then a normal vector is

\[ \mathbf n=\mathbf u\times \mathbf v. \]

5.6.1 Example 5.3: Plane through three points

Find an equation of the plane through

\[ P=(1,2,3),\qquad Q=(2,2,5),\qquad R=(3,4,1). \]

First compute two direction vectors in the plane:

\[ \overrightarrow{PQ}=Q-P=\langle 1,0,2\rangle, \]

and

\[ \overrightarrow{PR}=R-P=\langle 2,2,-2\rangle. \]

A normal vector is

\[ \overrightarrow{PQ}\times \overrightarrow{PR} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf i & \mathbf j & \mathbf k\\ 1 & 0 & 2\\ 2 & 2 & -2 \end{vmatrix} =\langle -4,6,2\rangle. \]

We may divide by \(2\) and use

\[ \mathbf n=\langle -2,3,1\rangle. \]

Using point \(P=(1,2,3)\), the plane is

\[ -2(x-1)+3(y-2)+(z-3)=0. \]

Simplifying gives

\[ -2x+3y+z-7=0. \]

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

P = np.array([1, 2, 3])
Q = np.array([2, 2, 5])
R = np.array([3, 4, 1])
u = Q - P
v = R - P
n = np.cross(u, v)

s_vals = np.linspace(-0.5, 1.2, 8)
t_vals = np.linspace(-0.5, 1.2, 8)
S, T = np.meshgrid(s_vals, t_vals)
X = P[0] + S * u[0] + T * v[0]
Y = P[1] + S * u[1] + T * v[1]
Z = P[2] + S * u[2] + T * v[2]

fig = plt.figure()
ax = fig.add_subplot(111, projection='3d')
ax.plot_surface(X, Y, Z, alpha=0.35)
ax.scatter([P[0], Q[0], R[0]], [P[1], Q[1], R[1]], [P[2], Q[2], R[2]], s=50)

for point, label in [(P, 'P'), (Q, 'Q'), (R, 'R')]:
    ax.text(*(point + np.array([0.05, 0.05, 0.05])), label)

ax.quiver(*P, *u, arrow_length_ratio=0.08)
ax.quiver(*P, *v, arrow_length_ratio=0.08)
ax.quiver(*P, *(n / np.linalg.norm(n)), length=2.0, arrow_length_ratio=0.1)
ax.text(*(P + 2.1 * n / np.linalg.norm(n)), 'normal')

ax.set_xlabel('x')
ax.set_ylabel('y')
ax.set_zlabel('z')
ax.set_title('Plane and normal from a cross product')
ax.set_box_aspect([1, 1, 1])
plt.show()

5.7 5.6 Scalar triple product and volume

The scalar triple product of \(\mathbf a\), \(\mathbf b\), and \(\mathbf c\) is

\[ \mathbf a\cdot(\mathbf b\times\mathbf c). \]

Geometrically,

\[ |\mathbf a\cdot(\mathbf b\times\mathbf c)| \]

is the volume of the parallelepiped determined by \(\mathbf a\), \(\mathbf b\), and \(\mathbf c\).

Using a determinant,

\[ \mathbf a\cdot(\mathbf b\times\mathbf c) = \begin{vmatrix} a_1&a_2&a_3\\ b_1&b_2&b_3\\ c_1&c_2&c_3 \end{vmatrix}. \]

If the scalar triple product is zero, then the three vectors are coplanar.

5.7.1 Example 5.4: Test coplanarity

Let

\[ \mathbf a=\langle -3,0,6\rangle, \qquad \mathbf b=\langle 4,1,-7\rangle, \qquad \mathbf c=\langle 1,-2,-4\rangle. \]

Compute

\[ \mathbf a\cdot(\mathbf b\times\mathbf c) = \begin{vmatrix} -3&0&6\\ 4&1&-7\\ 1&-2&-4 \end{vmatrix}. \]

Expanding,

\[ \begin{aligned} \mathbf b\times\mathbf c &= \langle 1(-4)-(-7)(-2), (-7)(1)-4(-4), 4(-2)-1(1)\rangle\\ &=\langle -18,9,-9\rangle. \end{aligned} \]

Then

\[ \mathbf a\cdot(\mathbf b\times\mathbf c) =\langle -3,0,6\rangle\cdot\langle -18,9,-9\rangle =54+0-54=0. \]

Therefore the vectors are coplanar.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# A non-coplanar example for visualization.
a = np.array([2, 0, 1])
b = np.array([0, 2, 1])
c = np.array([1, 1, 3])
volume = abs(np.dot(a, np.cross(b, c)))

fig = plt.figure()
ax = fig.add_subplot(111, projection='3d')
origin = np.zeros(3)

for vector, label in [(a, 'a'), (b, 'b'), (c, 'c')]:
    ax.quiver(*origin, *vector, arrow_length_ratio=0.08)
    ax.text(*(1.05 * vector), label)

# Edges of the parallelepiped.
points = [origin, a, b, c, a+b, a+c, b+c, a+b+c]
edges = [(0,1),(0,2),(0,3),(1,4),(1,5),(2,4),(2,6),(3,5),(3,6),(4,7),(5,7),(6,7)]
for i, j in edges:
    p, q = points[i], points[j]
    ax.plot([p[0], q[0]], [p[1], q[1]], [p[2], q[2]], linewidth=1.5)

ax.set_title(f'Parallelepiped volume = {volume:.1f}')
ax.set_xlabel('x')
ax.set_ylabel('y')
ax.set_zlabel('z')
ax.set_box_aspect([1, 1, 1])
plt.show()

5.8 5.7 Vector-valued functions and space curves

A vector-valued function takes a scalar input and returns a vector:

\[ \mathbf r(t)=\langle x(t),y(t),z(t)\rangle. \]

The image of \(\mathbf r(t)\) is a curve in space. The parameter \(t\) may represent time, but it does not have to. It may simply be a variable that traces the curve.

5.8.1 Example 5.5: A helix

The curve

\[ \mathbf r(t)=\langle \cos t,\sin t,t\rangle \]

is a helix. Its projection onto the \(xy\)-plane is the unit circle, while the \(z\)-coordinate increases linearly.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

t = np.linspace(0, 6 * np.pi, 500)
x = np.cos(t)
y = np.sin(t)
z = t

fig = plt.figure()
ax = fig.add_subplot(111, projection='3d')
ax.plot(x, y, z, linewidth=2)

# Show a tangent vector at t0.
t0 = np.pi
p = np.array([np.cos(t0), np.sin(t0), t0])
v = np.array([-np.sin(t0), np.cos(t0), 1])
ax.quiver(*p, *v, length=1.3, normalize=True, arrow_length_ratio=0.12)
ax.text(*(p + v / np.linalg.norm(v) * 1.4), 'tangent')

ax.set_xlabel('x')
ax.set_ylabel('y')
ax.set_zlabel('z')
ax.set_title('A space curve and one tangent vector')
ax.set_box_aspect([1, 1, 3])
plt.show()

5.9 5.8 Derivatives and integrals of vector-valued functions

The derivative of

\[ \mathbf r(t)=\langle x(t),y(t),z(t)\rangle \]

is computed component by component:

\[ \mathbf r'(t)=\langle x'(t),y'(t),z'(t)\rangle. \]

Similarly,

\[ \int_a^b \mathbf r(t)\,dt = \left\langle \int_a^b x(t)\,dt, \int_a^b y(t)\,dt, \int_a^b z(t)\,dt \right\rangle. \]

The product rules are analogous to ordinary calculus:

\[ \frac{d}{dt}\left(\mathbf a(t)\cdot\mathbf b(t)\right) =\mathbf a'(t)\cdot\mathbf b(t)+\mathbf a(t)\cdot\mathbf b'(t), \]

and

\[ \frac{d}{dt}\left(\mathbf a(t)\times\mathbf b(t)\right) =\mathbf a'(t)\times\mathbf b(t)+\mathbf a(t)\times\mathbf b'(t). \]

5.9.1 Example 5.6: Differentiate a vector function

Let

\[ \mathbf r(t)=\langle t^3,\tfrac12t,t^2-4t\rangle. \]

Then

\[ \mathbf r'(t)=\langle 3t^2,\tfrac12,2t-4\rangle, \]

and

\[ \mathbf r''(t)=\langle 6t,0,2\rangle. \]

At \(t=1\),

\[ \mathbf r'(1)=\langle 3,\tfrac12,-2\rangle, \qquad \mathbf r''(1)=\langle 6,0,2\rangle. \]

5.10 5.9 Motion in space: velocity, speed, and acceleration

Suppose a particle moves through space with position vector

\[ \mathbf r(t)=\langle x(t),y(t),z(t)\rangle. \]

The velocity vector is

\[ \mathbf v(t)=\mathbf r'(t). \]

The speed is the magnitude of the velocity vector:

\[ \text{speed}=|\mathbf v(t)|=|\mathbf r'(t)|. \]

The acceleration vector is

\[ \mathbf a(t)=\mathbf v'(t)=\mathbf r''(t). \]

ImportantVector versus scalar quantities

Velocity is a vector because it has magnitude and direction. Speed is a scalar because it is only the magnitude of velocity.

5.10.1 Example 5.7: Motion on a helix

Let

\[ \mathbf r(t)=\langle \sin t,\cos t,t\rangle. \]

Then

\[ \mathbf v(t)=\mathbf r'(t)=\langle \cos t,-\sin t,1\rangle, \]

and

\[ \mathbf a(t)=\mathbf r''(t)=\langle -\sin t,-\cos t,0\rangle. \]

The speed is

\[ |\mathbf v(t)|=\sqrt{\cos^2t+\sin^2t+1}=\sqrt2. \]

So the particle moves with constant speed even though its direction is changing.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

t = np.linspace(0, 4 * np.pi, 400)
x = np.sin(t)
y = np.cos(t)
z = t

fig = plt.figure()
ax = fig.add_subplot(111, projection='3d')
ax.plot(x, y, z, linewidth=2)

for t0 in [np.pi/3, 4*np.pi/3, 7*np.pi/3]:
    p = np.array([np.sin(t0), np.cos(t0), t0])
    v = np.array([np.cos(t0), -np.sin(t0), 1])
    a = np.array([-np.sin(t0), -np.cos(t0), 0])
    ax.quiver(*p, *(v / np.linalg.norm(v)), length=0.9, arrow_length_ratio=0.12)
    ax.quiver(*p, *(a / np.linalg.norm(a)), length=0.7, arrow_length_ratio=0.12)

ax.set_xlabel('x')
ax.set_ylabel('y')
ax.set_zlabel('z')
ax.set_title('Velocity and acceleration along a helix')
ax.set_box_aspect([1, 1, 3])
plt.show()

5.11 5.10 Recovering velocity and position from acceleration

Because

\[ \mathbf a(t)=\mathbf v'(t), \]

we can recover velocity by integration:

\[ \mathbf v(t)=\mathbf v(0)+\int_0^t \mathbf a(u)\,du. \]

Because

\[ \mathbf v(t)=\mathbf r'(t), \]

we can recover position by another integration:

\[ \mathbf r(t)=\mathbf r(0)+\int_0^t \mathbf v(u)\,du. \]

5.11.1 Example 5.8: From acceleration to position

A particle starts at

\[ \mathbf r(0)=\langle 0,0,1\rangle \]

with initial velocity

\[ \mathbf v(0)=\mathbf i+\mathbf k=\langle 1,0,1\rangle. \]

Its acceleration is

\[ \mathbf a(t)=\langle 2t,1,6t\rangle. \]

Then

\[ \mathbf v(t) =\langle 1,0,1\rangle+ \int_0^t \langle 2u,1,6u\rangle\,du =\langle 1+t^2,t,1+3t^2\rangle. \]

Now integrate again:

\[ \begin{aligned} \mathbf r(t) &=\langle 0,0,1\rangle+ \int_0^t \langle 1+u^2,u,1+3u^2\rangle\,du\\ &=\left\langle t+\frac{t^3}{3},\frac{t^2}{2},1+t+t^3\right\rangle. \end{aligned} \]

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

t = np.linspace(0, 2.5, 300)
x = t + t**3 / 3
y = t**2 / 2
z = 1 + t + t**3

fig = plt.figure()
ax = fig.add_subplot(111, projection='3d')
ax.plot(x, y, z, linewidth=2)
ax.scatter([x[0]], [y[0]], [z[0]], s=50)
ax.text(x[0], y[0], z[0], 'start')
ax.scatter([x[-1]], [y[-1]], [z[-1]], s=50)
ax.text(x[-1], y[-1], z[-1], 'end')

ax.set_xlabel('x')
ax.set_ylabel('y')

5.12 5.11 Arc length

Suppose a curve is given by

\[ \mathbf r(t)=\langle x(t),y(t),z(t)\rangle, \qquad a\le t\le b, \]

and assume the curve is traversed exactly once. The length of the curve is

\[ L= \int_a^b |\mathbf r'(t)|\,dt. \]

Equivalently,

\[ L= \int_a^b \sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2+ \left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)^2+ \left(\frac{dz}{dt}\right)^2}\,dt. \]

5.12.1 Example 5.9: Length of a circular helix

Let

\[ \mathbf r(t)=\langle \cos t,\sin t,t\rangle, \qquad 0\le t\le 2\pi. \]

Then

\[ \mathbf r'(t)=\langle -\sin t,\cos t,1\rangle, \]

so

\[ |\mathbf r'(t)|=\sqrt{\sin^2t+\cos^2t+1}=\sqrt2. \]

Therefore

\[ L=\int_0^{2\pi}\sqrt2\,dt=2\sqrt2\pi. \]

5.13 5.12 Arc length parameterization

The arc length function from \(t=a\) to \(t\) is

\[ s(t)=\int_a^t |\mathbf r'(u)|\,du. \]

When \(s(t)\) can be solved for \(t\) as a function of \(s\), we can reparameterize the curve by arc length. This is useful because the new parameter directly measures distance traveled along the curve.

5.13.1 Example 5.10: Reparameterize a helix by arc length

For

\[ \mathbf r(t)=\langle \cos t,\sin t,t\rangle, \qquad t\ge 0, \]

we have \(|\mathbf r'(t)|=\sqrt2\). Thus

\[ s(t)=\int_0^t \sqrt2\,du=\sqrt2t. \]

Solving for \(t\) gives

\[ t=\frac{s}{\sqrt2}. \]

Therefore the arc length parameterization is

\[ \mathbf r(s)= \left\langle \cos\left(\frac{s}{\sqrt2}\right), \sin\left(\frac{s}{\sqrt2}\right), \frac{s}{\sqrt2} \right\rangle. \]

With this parameterization,

\[ \left|\frac{d\mathbf r}{ds}\right|=1. \]

That is why an arc length parameterization is also called a unit-speed parameterization.

5.14 5.13 Unit tangent vector

For a smooth curve with \(\mathbf r'(t)\ne \mathbf 0\), the unit tangent vector is

\[ \mathbf T(t)=\frac{\mathbf r'(t)}{|\mathbf r'(t)|}. \]

This vector points in the direction of motion, but has length \(1\).

For the helix

\[ \mathbf r(t)=\langle \cos t,\sin t,t\rangle, \]

we have

\[ \mathbf T(t)=\frac{1}{\sqrt2}\langle -\sin t,\cos t,1\rangle. \]

5.15 5.14 Curvature

Curvature measures how quickly a curve changes direction. A straight line has curvature \(0\). A small circle has larger curvature than a large circle.

The conceptual definition is

\[ \kappa=\left|\frac{d\mathbf T}{ds}\right|, \]

where \(s\) is arc length.

A practical formula is

\[ \kappa(t)=\frac{|\mathbf T'(t)|}{|\mathbf r'(t)|}. \]

For many computations in \(\mathbb R^3\), the most convenient formula is

\[ \kappa(t)=\frac{|\mathbf r'(t)\times\mathbf r''(t)|}{|\mathbf r'(t)|^3}. \]

5.15.1 Example 5.11: Curvature of a circle

Let a circle of radius \(a\) be parameterized by

\[ \mathbf r(t)=\langle a\cos t,a\sin t,0\rangle. \]

Then

\[ \mathbf r'(t)=\langle -a\sin t,a\cos t,0\rangle, \]

and

\[ \mathbf r''(t)=\langle -a\cos t,-a\sin t,0\rangle. \]

We have

\[ |\mathbf r'(t)|=a, \]

and

\[ |\mathbf r'(t)\times\mathbf r''(t)|=a^2. \]

Therefore

\[ \kappa(t)=\frac{a^2}{a^3}=\frac1a. \]

Thus a circle with larger radius has smaller curvature.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

theta = np.linspace(0, 2 * np.pi, 400)
radii = [0.7, 1.2, 2.0]

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
for radius in radii:
    x = radius * np.cos(theta)
    y = radius * np.sin(theta)
    ax.plot(x, y, label=f'radius {radius}, curvature {1/radius:.2f}')

ax.set_aspect('equal')
ax.set_xlabel('x')
ax.set_ylabel('y')
ax.set_title('Curvature of circles')
ax.legend()
plt.show()

5.15.2 Example 5.12: Curvature of the circular helix

Let

\[ \mathbf r(t)=\langle \cos t,\sin t,t\rangle. \]

Then

\[ \mathbf r'(t)=\langle -\sin t,\cos t,1\rangle, \]

and

\[ \mathbf r''(t)=\langle -\cos t,-\sin t,0\rangle. \]

Compute the cross product:

\[ \mathbf r'(t)\times \mathbf r''(t) =\langle \sin t,-\cos t,1\rangle. \]

Thus

\[ |\mathbf r'(t)\times \mathbf r''(t)|=\sqrt{\sin^2t+\cos^2t+1}=\sqrt2, \]

and

\[ |\mathbf r'(t)|=\sqrt2. \]

Therefore

\[ \kappa(t)=\frac{\sqrt2}{(\sqrt2)^3}=\frac12. \]

The helix has constant curvature.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

t = np.linspace(0, 5 * np.pi, 500)
r = np.column_stack([np.cos(t), np.sin(t), t])
rp = np.column_stack([-np.sin(t), np.cos(t), np.ones_like(t)])
rpp = np.column_stack([-np.cos(t), -np.sin(t), np.zeros_like(t)])
curvature = np.linalg.norm(np.cross(rp, rpp), axis=1) / np.linalg.norm(rp, axis=1)**3

fig = plt.figure()

curve = ax.scatter(r[:, 0], r[:, 1], r[:, 2], c=curvature, s=8)
fig.colorbar(curve, ax=ax, shrink=0.65, label='curvature')
ax.set_xlabel('x')
ax.set_ylabel('y')
ax.set_zlabel('z')
ax.set_title('Curvature along a helix')
ax.set_box_aspect([1, 1, 3])
plt.show()

5.16 5.15 Finding when speed is minimized

In applications, a trajectory may have variable speed. Since speed is nonnegative, minimizing speed is equivalent to minimizing the square of speed.

5.16.1 Example 5.13: Minimum speed

Let

\[ \mathbf r(t)=\langle t^2+1,3t,t^2-4t\rangle. \]

Then

\[ \mathbf v(t)=\mathbf r'(t)=\langle 2t,3,2t-4\rangle. \]

The speed is

\[ |\mathbf v(t)|=\sqrt{(2t)^2+3^2+(2t-4)^2}. \]

Instead of minimizing this square root, minimize

\[ |\mathbf v(t)|^2=4t^2+9+(2t-4)^2. \]

Simplify:

\[ |\mathbf v(t)|^2=4t^2+9+4t^2-16t+16=8t^2-16t+25. \]

This quadratic is minimized when

\[ t=\frac{-(-16)}{2\cdot 8}=1. \]

Therefore the speed is minimized at \(t=1\).

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

t = np.linspace(-1, 3, 400)
speed = np.sqrt((2*t)**2 + 3**2 + (2*t - 4)**2)

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.plot(t, speed, linewidth=2)
ax.axvline(1, linestyle='--')
ax.scatter([1], [np.sqrt(17)], s=50)
ax.set_xlabel('t')
ax.set_ylabel('speed')
ax.set_title('Speed along a trajectory')
plt.show()

5.17 5.16 Modern viewpoint: trajectories in data and optimization

A vector-valued function does not need to describe a physical particle. It can describe any time-indexed or parameter-indexed state.

5.17.1 Example: gradient descent as a curve

In machine learning, an optimization algorithm generates a sequence of model parameters

\[ \theta_0,\theta_1,\theta_2,\dots \]

where each \(\theta_k\) may be a vector in a very high-dimensional space. If we interpolate these points, we can view training as a curve in parameter space.

The velocity of this curve describes how fast the parameters are changing. The curvature describes how sharply the path bends. A highly curved path may indicate unstable optimization, poor scaling, or a loss landscape with narrow valleys.

5.17.2 Example: data streams

A time series with several measurements at each time can be represented as

\[ \mathbf x(t)=\langle x_1(t),x_2(t),\dots,x_n(t)\rangle. \]

Then

\[ \mathbf x'(t) \]

measures the instantaneous change of all features. This is useful in dynamical systems, finance, biology, robotics, and sensor data.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# A toy loss surface and gradient descent path.
def grad(theta):
    x, y = theta
    return np.array([2*x + 0.8*y, 0.8*x + 6*y])

theta = np.array([3.0, 2.0])
path = [theta.copy()]
eta = 0.12
for _ in range(35):
    theta = theta - eta * grad(theta)
    path.append(theta.copy())
path = np.array(path)

x = np.linspace(-3.5, 3.5, 200)
y = np.linspace(-2.5, 2.5, 200)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)
Z = X**2 + 0.8*X*Y + 3*Y**2

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.contour(X, Y, Z, levels=25)
ax.plot(path[:, 0], path[:, 1], marker='o', markersize=3)
ax.set_xlabel('parameter 1')
ax.set_ylabel('parameter 2')
ax.set_title('Gradient descent trajectory')
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
plt.show()

## 5.1 Why the cross product is needed

The dot product answers a question about **alignment**:

$$
\mathbf a\cdot \mathbf b=|\mathbf a||\mathbf b|\cos\theta.
$$

It tells us whether two vectors point in similar, opposite, or perpendicular directions. But in three dimensions, we often need a vector that is **perpendicular to two given vectors**.

For example:

- a plane is often described by a normal vector;
- the orientation of a surface is described by a normal vector;
- the area of a parallelogram spanned by two vectors is measured by a perpendicular vector;
- the torque from a force is perpendicular to both the radius vector and the force vector;
- the curl of a vector field is written using a cross product with the differential operator $\nabla$.

The cross product is designed for exactly these tasks.

## 5.2 Definition and computation of the cross product

Let

$$
\mathbf a=\langle a_1,a_2,a_3\rangle,
\qquad
\mathbf b=\langle b_1,b_2,b_3\rangle.
$$

The **cross product** $\mathbf a\times \mathbf b$ is the vector

$$
\mathbf a\times \mathbf b
=
\left\langle
 a_2b_3-a_3b_2,
 a_3b_1-a_1b_3,
 a_1b_2-a_2b_1
\right\rangle.
$$

It is often remembered by the determinant notation

$$
\mathbf a\times \mathbf b
=
\begin{vmatrix}
\mathbf i & \mathbf j & \mathbf k\\
a_1 & a_2 & a_3\\
b_1 & b_2 & b_3
\end{vmatrix}.
$$

Expanding along the first row gives

$$
\mathbf a\times \mathbf b
=(a_2b_3-a_3b_2)\mathbf i
-(a_1b_3-a_3b_1)\mathbf j
+(a_1b_2-a_2b_1)\mathbf k.
$$

::: {.callout-warning}
## The cross product exists in this form only in $\mathbb R^3$

In this calculus course, the cross product is a special operation for three-dimensional vectors. In higher-dimensional linear algebra, the related ideas are expressed using determinants, exterior products, orientations, and differential forms.
:::

### Example 5.1: Compute a cross product

Let

$$
\mathbf a=\langle 1,2,3\rangle,
\qquad
\mathbf b=\langle 3,2,1\rangle.
$$

Then

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf a\times \mathbf b
&=
\left\langle
2\cdot 1-3\cdot 2,
3\cdot 3-1\cdot 1,
1\cdot 2-2\cdot 3
\right\rangle\\
&=\langle -4,8,-4\rangle.
\end{aligned}
$$

This vector is perpendicular to both $\mathbf a$ and $\mathbf b$:

$$
\langle -4,8,-4\rangle\cdot \langle 1,2,3\rangle=-4+16-12=0,
$$

and

$$
\langle -4,8,-4\rangle\cdot \langle 3,2,1\rangle=-12+16-4=0.
$$

This same computation is a standard way to find a direction vector for a line perpendicular to two given vectors, or a normal vector to a plane spanned by two vectors.

## 5.3 Basic properties of the cross product

For vectors $\mathbf a,\mathbf b,\mathbf c$ in $\mathbb R^3$ and scalars $\lambda$, the cross product satisfies:

$$
\mathbf a\times \mathbf b=-(\mathbf b\times \mathbf a),
$$

$$
\mathbf a\times (\mathbf b+\mathbf c)=\mathbf a\times \mathbf b+\mathbf a\times \mathbf c,
$$

$$
(\lambda \mathbf a)\times \mathbf b
=\lambda(\mathbf a\times \mathbf b)
=\mathbf a\times (\lambda \mathbf b),
$$

$$
\mathbf a\times \mathbf a=\mathbf 0.
$$

The first identity is especially important: **order matters**. Reversing the order reverses the direction.

### Standard basis orientation

The standard basis vectors satisfy

$$
\mathbf i\times \mathbf j=\mathbf k,
\qquad
\mathbf j\times \mathbf k=\mathbf i,
\qquad
\mathbf k\times \mathbf i=\mathbf j.
$$

Reversing the order changes the sign:

$$
\mathbf j\times \mathbf i=-\mathbf k,
\qquad
\mathbf k\times \mathbf j=-\mathbf i,
\qquad
\mathbf i\times \mathbf k=-\mathbf j.
$$

This orientation is called the **right-hand rule**. If the fingers of your right hand rotate from $\mathbf a$ toward $\mathbf b$, then your thumb points in the direction of $\mathbf a\times\mathbf b$.

## 5.4 Area from the cross product

The magnitude of the cross product has a geometric meaning:

$$
|\mathbf a\times \mathbf b|=|\mathbf a||\mathbf b|\sin\theta,
$$

where $\theta$ is the angle between $\mathbf a$ and $\mathbf b$.

This is the area of the parallelogram spanned by $\mathbf a$ and $\mathbf b$.

Therefore:

$$
\text{Area of parallelogram}=|\mathbf a\times\mathbf b|,
$$

and

$$
\text{Area of triangle}=\frac12|\mathbf a\times\mathbf b|.
$$

### Example 5.2: Area of a triangle

Find the area of the triangle with vertices

$$
O=(0,0,0),\qquad A=(1,2,3),\qquad B=(3,2,1).
$$

The two side vectors from $O$ are

$$
\overrightarrow{OA}=\langle 1,2,3\rangle,
\qquad
\overrightarrow{OB}=\langle 3,2,1\rangle.
$$

We computed

$$
\overrightarrow{OA}\times \overrightarrow{OB}=\langle -4,8,-4\rangle.
$$

Thus

$$
|\overrightarrow{OA}\times \overrightarrow{OB}|
=\sqrt{(-4)^2+8^2+(-4)^2}
=\sqrt{96}=4\sqrt 6.
$$

So the area of the triangle is

$$
\frac12(4\sqrt6)=2\sqrt6.
$$

This example is also a good reminder that a cross product gives both **magnitude** and **orientation**.

## 5.5 Normal vectors and planes

A plane in $\mathbb R^3$ can be described using a point $P_0=(x_0,y_0,z_0)$ and a normal vector

$$
\mathbf n=\langle A,B,C\rangle.
$$

The equation is

$$
\mathbf n\cdot \langle x-x_0,y-y_0,z-z_0\rangle=0.
$$

In standard form,

$$
A(x-x_0)+B(y-y_0)+C(z-z_0)=0.
$$

If a plane is spanned by two nonparallel vectors $\mathbf u$ and $\mathbf v$, then a normal vector is

$$
\mathbf n=\mathbf u\times \mathbf v.
$$

### Example 5.3: Plane through three points

Find an equation of the plane through

$$
P=(1,2,3),\qquad Q=(2,2,5),\qquad R=(3,4,1).
$$

First compute two direction vectors in the plane:

$$
\overrightarrow{PQ}=Q-P=\langle 1,0,2\rangle,
$$

and

$$
\overrightarrow{PR}=R-P=\langle 2,2,-2\rangle.
$$

A normal vector is

$$
\overrightarrow{PQ}\times \overrightarrow{PR}
=
\begin{vmatrix}
\mathbf i & \mathbf j & \mathbf k\\
1 & 0 & 2\\
2 & 2 & -2
\end{vmatrix}
=\langle -4,6,2\rangle.
$$

$$

Simplifying gives

$$
-2x+3y+z-7=0.
$$

## 5.6 Scalar triple product and volume

The **scalar triple product** of $\mathbf a$, $\mathbf b$, and $\mathbf c$ is

$$
\mathbf a\cdot(\mathbf b\times\mathbf c).
$$

Geometrically,

$$
|\mathbf a\cdot(\mathbf b\times\mathbf c)|
$$

is the volume of the parallelepiped determined by $\mathbf a$, $\mathbf b$, and $\mathbf c$.

Using a determinant,

$$
\mathbf a\cdot(\mathbf b\times\mathbf c)
=
\begin{vmatrix}
a_1&a_2&a_3\\
b_1&b_2&b_3\\
c_1&c_2&c_3
\end{vmatrix}.
$$

If the scalar triple product is zero, then the three vectors are coplanar.

### Example 5.4: Test coplanarity

Let

$$
\mathbf a=\langle -3,0,6\rangle,
\qquad
\mathbf b=\langle 4,1,-7\rangle,
\qquad
\mathbf c=\langle 1,-2,-4\rangle.
$$

Compute

$$
\mathbf a\cdot(\mathbf b\times\mathbf c)
=
\begin{vmatrix}
-3&0&6\\
4&1&-7\\
1&-2&-4
\end{vmatrix}.
$$

Expanding,

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf b\times\mathbf c
&=
\langle 1(-4)-(-7)(-2),
(-7)(1)-4(-4),
4(-2)-1(1)\rangle\\
&=\langle -18,9,-9\rangle.
\end{aligned}
$$

Then

$$
\mathbf a\cdot(\mathbf b\times\mathbf c)
=\langle -3,0,6\rangle\cdot\langle -18,9,-9\rangle
=54+0-54=0.
$$

Therefore the vectors are coplanar.

## 5.8 Derivatives and integrals of vector-valued functions

The derivative of

$$
\mathbf r(t)=\langle x(t),y(t),z(t)\rangle
$$

is computed component by component:

$$
\mathbf r'(t)=\langle x'(t),y'(t),z'(t)\rangle.
$$

Similarly,

$$
\int_a^b \mathbf r(t)\,dt
=
\left\langle
\int_a^b x(t)\,dt,
\int_a^b y(t)\,dt,
\int_a^b z(t)\,dt
\right\rangle.
$$

The product rules are analogous to ordinary calculus:

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\left(\mathbf a(t)\cdot\mathbf b(t)\right)
=\mathbf a'(t)\cdot\mathbf b(t)+\mathbf a(t)\cdot\mathbf b'(t),
$$

and

$$
\frac{d}{dt}\left(\mathbf a(t)\times\mathbf b(t)\right)
=\mathbf a'(t)\times\mathbf b(t)+\mathbf a(t)\times\mathbf b'(t).
$$

### Example 5.6: Differentiate a vector function

Let

$$
\mathbf r(t)=\langle t^3,\tfrac12t,t^2-4t\rangle.
$$

Then

$$
\mathbf r'(t)=\langle 3t^2,\tfrac12,2t-4\rangle,
$$

and

$$
\mathbf r''(t)=\langle 6t,0,2\rangle.
$$

At $t=1$,

$$
\mathbf r'(1)=\langle 3,\tfrac12,-2\rangle,
\qquad
\mathbf r''(1)=\langle 6,0,2\rangle.
$$

## 5.9 Motion in space: velocity, speed, and acceleration

Suppose a particle moves through space with position vector

$$
\mathbf r(t)=\langle x(t),y(t),z(t)\rangle.
$$

The **velocity vector** is

$$
\mathbf v(t)=\mathbf r'(t).
$$

The **speed** is the magnitude of the velocity vector:

$$
\text{speed}=|\mathbf v(t)|=|\mathbf r'(t)|.
$$

The **acceleration vector** is

$$
\mathbf a(t)=\mathbf v'(t)=\mathbf r''(t).
$$

::: {.callout-important}
## Vector versus scalar quantities

Velocity is a vector because it has magnitude and direction. Speed is a scalar because it is only the magnitude of velocity.
:::

### Example 5.7: Motion on a helix

Let

$$
\mathbf r(t)=\langle \sin t,\cos t,t\rangle.
$$

Then

$$
\mathbf v(t)=\mathbf r'(t)=\langle \cos t,-\sin t,1\rangle,
$$

and

$$
\mathbf a(t)=\mathbf r''(t)=\langle -\sin t,-\cos t,0\rangle.
$$

The speed is

$$
|\mathbf v(t)|=\sqrt{\cos^2t+\sin^2t+1}=\sqrt2.
$$

So the particle moves with constant speed even though its direction is changing.

## 5.10 Recovering velocity and position from acceleration

Because

$$
\mathbf a(t)=\mathbf v'(t),
$$

we can recover velocity by integration:

$$
\mathbf v(t)=\mathbf v(0)+\int_0^t \mathbf a(u)\,du.
$$

Because

$$
\mathbf v(t)=\mathbf r'(t),
$$

we can recover position by another integration:

$$
\mathbf r(t)=\mathbf r(0)+\int_0^t \mathbf v(u)\,du.
$$

### Example 5.8: From acceleration to position

A particle starts at

$$
\mathbf r(0)=\langle 0,0,1\rangle
$$

with initial velocity

$$
\mathbf v(0)=\mathbf i+\mathbf k=\langle 1,0,1\rangle.
$$

Its acceleration is

$$
\mathbf a(t)=\langle 2t,1,6t\rangle.
$$

Then

$$
\mathbf v(t)
=\langle 1,0,1\rangle+
\int_0^t \langle 2u,1,6u\rangle\,du
=\langle 1+t^2,t,1+3t^2\rangle.
$$

Now integrate again:

$$
\begin{aligned}
\mathbf r(t)
&=\langle 0,0,1\rangle+
\int_0^t \langle 1+u^2,u,1+3u^2\rangle\,du\\
&=\left\langle t+\frac{t^3}{3},\frac{t^2}{2},1+t+t^3\right\rangle.
\end{aligned}
$$

```{python}
#| fig-height: 6
```

## 5.11 Arc length

Suppose a curve is given by

$$
\mathbf r(t)=\langle x(t),y(t),z(t)\rangle,
\qquad a\le t\le b,
$$

and assume the curve is traversed exactly once. The length of the curve is

$$
L=
\int_a^b |\mathbf r'(t)|\,dt.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
L=
\int_a^b
\sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2+
\left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)^2+
\left(\frac{dz}{dt}\right)^2}\,dt.
$$

### Example 5.9: Length of a circular helix

Let

$$
\mathbf r(t)=\langle \cos t,\sin t,t\rangle,
\qquad 0\le t\le 2\pi.
$$

Then

$$
\mathbf r'(t)=\langle -\sin t,\cos t,1\rangle,
$$

so

$$
|\mathbf r'(t)|=\sqrt{\sin^2t+\cos^2t+1}=\sqrt2.
$$

Therefore

$$
L=\int_0^{2\pi}\sqrt2\,dt=2\sqrt2\pi.
$$

## 5.12 Arc length parameterization

The arc length function from $t=a$ to $t$ is

$$
s(t)=\int_a^t |\mathbf r'(u)|\,du.
$$

When $s(t)$ can be solved for $t$ as a function of $s$, we can reparameterize the curve by arc length. This is useful because the new parameter directly measures distance traveled along the curve.

### Example 5.10: Reparameterize a helix by arc length

For

$$
\mathbf r(t)=\langle \cos t,\sin t,t\rangle,
\qquad t\ge 0,
$$

we have $|\mathbf r'(t)|=\sqrt2$. Thus

$$
s(t)=\int_0^t \sqrt2\,du=\sqrt2t.
$$

Solving for $t$ gives

$$
t=\frac{s}{\sqrt2}.
$$

Therefore the arc length parameterization is

$$
\mathbf r(s)=
\left\langle
\cos\left(\frac{s}{\sqrt2}\right),
\sin\left(\frac{s}{\sqrt2}\right),
\frac{s}{\sqrt2}
\right\rangle.
$$

With this parameterization,

$$
\left|\frac{d\mathbf r}{ds}\right|=1.
$$

That is why an arc length parameterization is also called a **unit-speed parameterization**.

## 5.13 Unit tangent vector

For a smooth curve with $\mathbf r'(t)\ne \mathbf 0$, the **unit tangent vector** is

$$
\mathbf T(t)=\frac{\mathbf r'(t)}{|\mathbf r'(t)|}.
$$

This vector points in the direction of motion, but has length $1$.

For the helix

$$
\mathbf r(t)=\langle \cos t,\sin t,t\rangle,
$$

we have

$$
\mathbf T(t)=\frac{1}{\sqrt2}\langle -\sin t,\cos t,1\rangle.
$$

## 5.14 Curvature

Curvature measures how quickly a curve changes direction. A straight line has curvature $0$. A small circle has larger curvature than a large circle.

The conceptual definition is

$$
\kappa=\left|\frac{d\mathbf T}{ds}\right|,
$$

where $s$ is arc length.

A practical formula is

$$
\kappa(t)=\frac{|\mathbf T'(t)|}{|\mathbf r'(t)|}.
$$

For many computations in $\mathbb R^3$, the most convenient formula is

$$
\kappa(t)=\frac{|\mathbf r'(t)\times\mathbf r''(t)|}{|\mathbf r'(t)|^3}.
$$

### Example 5.11: Curvature of a circle

Let a circle of radius $a$ be parameterized by

$$
\mathbf r(t)=\langle a\cos t,a\sin t,0\rangle.
$$

Then

$$
\mathbf r'(t)=\langle -a\sin t,a\cos t,0\rangle,
$$

and

$$
\mathbf r''(t)=\langle -a\cos t,-a\sin t,0\rangle.
$$

We have

$$
|\mathbf r'(t)|=a,
$$

and

$$
|\mathbf r'(t)\times\mathbf r''(t)|=a^2.
$$

Therefore

$$
\kappa(t)=\frac{a^2}{a^3}=\frac1a.
$$

Thus a circle with larger radius has smaller curvature.

### Example 5.12: Curvature of the circular helix

Let

$$
\mathbf r(t)=\langle \cos t,\sin t,t\rangle.
$$

Then

$$
\mathbf r'(t)=\langle -\sin t,\cos t,1\rangle,
$$

and

$$
\mathbf r''(t)=\langle -\cos t,-\sin t,0\rangle.
$$

Compute the cross product:

$$
\mathbf r'(t)\times \mathbf r''(t)
=\langle \sin t,-\cos t,1\rangle.
$$

Thus

$$
|\mathbf r'(t)\times \mathbf r''(t)|=\sqrt{\sin^2t+\cos^2t+1}=\sqrt2,
$$

and

$$
|\mathbf r'(t)|=\sqrt2.
$$

Therefore

$$
\kappa(t)=\frac{\sqrt2}{(\sqrt2)^3}=\frac12.
$$

The helix has constant curvature.

## 5.15 Finding when speed is minimized

In applications, a trajectory may have variable speed. Since speed is nonnegative, minimizing speed is equivalent to minimizing the square of speed.

### Example 5.13: Minimum speed

Let

$$
\mathbf r(t)=\langle t^2+1,3t,t^2-4t\rangle.
$$

Then

$$
\mathbf v(t)=\mathbf r'(t)=\langle 2t,3,2t-4\rangle.
$$

The speed is

$$
|\mathbf v(t)|=\sqrt{(2t)^2+3^2+(2t-4)^2}.
$$

Instead of minimizing this square root, minimize

$$
|\mathbf v(t)|^2=4t^2+9+(2t-4)^2.
$$

Simplify:

$$
|\mathbf v(t)|^2=4t^2+9+4t^2-16t+16=8t^2-16t+25.
$$

This quadratic is minimized when

$$
t=\frac{-(-16)}{2\cdot 8}=1.
$$

Therefore the speed is minimized at $t=1$.

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch05-speed-minimum
#| fig-cap: 'The speed of $\mathbf r(t)=\langle t^2+1,3t,t^2-4t\rangle$ is minimized at $t=1$.'

#| fig-height: 4.5

import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
```

## 5.16 Modern viewpoint: trajectories in data and optimization

A vector-valued function does not need to describe a physical particle. It can describe any time-indexed or parameter-indexed state.

### Example: gradient descent as a curve

In machine learning, an optimization algorithm generates a sequence of model parameters

$$
\theta_0,\theta_1,\theta_2,\dots
$$

where each $\theta_k$ may be a vector in a very high-dimensional space. If we interpolate these points, we can view training as a curve in parameter space.

The velocity of this curve describes how fast the parameters are changing. The curvature describes how sharply the path bends. A highly curved path may indicate unstable optimization, poor scaling, or a loss landscape with narrow valleys.

### Example: data streams

A time series with several measurements at each time can be represented as

$$
\mathbf x(t)=\langle x_1(t),x_2(t),\dots,x_n(t)\rangle.
$$

Then

$$
\mathbf x'(t)
$$

measures the instantaneous change of all features. This is useful in dynamical systems, finance, biology, robotics, and sensor data.

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch05-optimization-path
#| fig-cap: 'An optimization path can be viewed as a vector-valued trajectory in parameter space.'
#| fig-width: 7
#| fig-height: 6

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# A toy loss surface and gradient descent path.
def grad(theta):
    x, y = theta
    return np.array([2*x + 0.8*y, 0.8*x + 6*y])

theta = np.array([3.0, 2.0])
path = [theta.copy()]
eta = 0.12
for _ in range(35):
    theta = theta - eta * grad(theta)
    path.append(theta.copy())
path = np.array(path)

x = np.linspace(-3.5, 3.5, 200)
y = np.linspace(-2.5, 2.5, 200)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)
Z = X**2 + 0.8*X*Y + 3*Y**2

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.contour(X, Y, Z, levels=25)
ax.plot(path[:, 0], path[:, 1], marker='o', markersize=3)
ax.set_xlabel('parameter 1')
ax.set_ylabel('parameter 2')
ax.set_title('Gradient descent trajectory')
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
plt.show()
```

## 5.19 Chapter project: tracking motion from data

Suppose a sensor records the position of an object at equally spaced times. The data are noisy, so you do not know $\mathbf r(t)$ exactly. Your goal is to estimate velocity, speed, acceleration, and curvature from the data.

A possible workflow:

1. Load or simulate position data $\mathbf r(t_k)$.
2. Estimate velocity using finite differences.
3. Estimate speed as the norm of velocity.
4. Estimate acceleration using finite differences again.
5. Estimate curvature using

$$
\kappa(t)=\frac{|\mathbf r'(t)\times\mathbf r''(t)|}{|\mathbf r'(t)|^3}.
$$

6. Plot the trajectory and color it by speed or curvature.

This project connects calculus to robotics, sports tracking, physics simulations, autonomous systems, and time-dependent data analysis.

## 5.20 What comes next

The cross product and space curves are not isolated topics. They prepare us for the main constructions of vector calculus.

In later chapters:

- line integrals use $ds=|\mathbf r'(t)|dt$;
- work integrals use $\mathbf F(\mathbf r(t))\cdot \mathbf r'(t)$;
- surface normals often come from cross products;
- curl is remembered as $\nabla\times\mathbf F$;
- Stokes' theorem relates circulation around a boundary curve to curl through a surface.

The ideas of this chapter therefore form the geometric foundation for the second half of the book.
