22  Chapter 22. Curl, Divergence, and Surface Integrals

Local rotation, local expansion, oriented surfaces, and flux

The previous chapters developed line integrals and Green’s theorem. A line integral measures how a scalar or vector field accumulates along a curve. Green’s theorem then showed that circulation or flux around a closed plane curve can be computed by integrating a local derivative over the enclosed region.

This chapter moves that idea into three-dimensional space. Instead of curves enclosing planar regions, we now study surfaces in space. Instead of asking only how much a vector field pushes along a path, we ask how much it rotates near a point, how much it expands out of a point, and how much it flows through a surface.

The central objects are:

  • curl, which measures local rotational tendency;
  • divergence, which measures local source or sink strength;
  • surface integrals, which accumulate scalar quantities over curved surfaces;
  • flux integrals, which measure flow through oriented surfaces.

These concepts are essential for vector calculus, physics, partial differential equations, probability flow, optimization dynamics, and geometric data analysis. They also prepare the way for Stokes’ theorem and the divergence theorem in the next chapter.

22.1 Learning goals

After studying this chapter, you should be able to:

  1. compute the curl of a vector field in space;
  2. interpret curl as local rotational tendency;
  3. compute the divergence of a vector field;
  4. interpret divergence as local expansion, compression, source, or sink behavior;
  5. use the identities \(\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times F)=0\) and \(\nabla\times(\nabla f)=0\) when appropriate;
  6. parameterize surfaces and compute tangent vectors;
  7. compute surface area using \(|r_u\times r_v|\);
  8. compute scalar surface integrals;
  9. orient a surface and compute flux integrals;
  10. use graph formulas for surface area and flux;
  11. approximate curl, divergence, surface area, and flux numerically using Python.
NoteThe main theme

Curl and divergence are local differential measurements. Surface integrals and flux integrals are global accumulations. The great theorems of vector calculus explain when local rotation or local expansion determines a global boundary integral.

22.2 The differential operator \(\nabla\)

For a scalar function \(f(x,y,z)\), the gradient is

\[ \nabla f=\left\langle f_x,f_y,f_z\right\rangle. \]

For a vector field

\[ F(x,y,z)=\langle P(x,y,z),Q(x,y,z),R(x,y,z)\rangle, \]

we use the formal differential operator

\[ \nabla=\left\langle \frac{\partial}{\partial x},\frac{\partial}{\partial y},\frac{\partial}{\partial z}\right\rangle. \]

This notation is powerful because it lets us remember several vector-calculus operations using familiar vector algebra:

\[ \nabla f \quad \text{gradient}, \]

\[ \nabla\cdot F \quad \text{divergence}, \]

\[ \nabla\times F \quad \text{curl}. \]

The notation is formal: \(\nabla\) is not an ordinary vector of numbers. Its components are derivative operators. But the dot-product and cross-product notation correctly organizes the formulas.

22.3 Curl

Let

\[ F=\langle P,Q,R\rangle \]

be a vector field in space. The curl of \(F\) is the vector field

\[ \nabla\times F = \left\langle R_y-Q_z, P_z-R_x, Q_x-P_y \right\rangle. \]

Equivalently,

\[ \nabla\times F = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf i & \mathbf j & \mathbf k\\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\\ P & Q & R \end{vmatrix}. \]

Curl measures the infinitesimal tendency of the field to rotate. Its direction is the axis of rotation determined by the right-hand rule, and its magnitude measures the local strength of the rotation.

ImportantCurl in the plane

A two-dimensional vector field

\[ F(x,y)=\langle P(x,y),Q(x,y)\rangle \]

can be treated as the three-dimensional field \(\langle P,Q,0\rangle\). Then

\[ \nabla\times F = \left\langle 0,0,Q_x-P_y\right\rangle. \]

The scalar quantity

\[ Q_x-P_y \]

is often called the scalar curl of the plane field. It is exactly the integrand that appears in Green’s theorem in circulation form.

22.3.1 Example: a rotating field

Let

\[ F(x,y,z)=\langle -y,x,0\rangle. \]

Then

\[ P=-y, \qquad Q=x, \qquad R=0. \]

Therefore

\[ \nabla\times F =\left\langle 0-0, 0-0, 1-(-1) \right\rangle =\langle 0,0,2\rangle. \]

The field rotates counterclockwise in the \(xy\)-plane. The curl points in the positive \(z\)-direction.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x = np.linspace(-2, 2, 21)
y = np.linspace(-2, 2, 21)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)
U = -Y
V = X
speed = np.sqrt(U**2 + V**2)

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.quiver(X, Y, U, V, speed, angles='xy')
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
ax.set_xlabel(r'$x$')
ax.set_ylabel(r'$y$')
ax.set_title(r'Rotational field $F=\langle -y,x\rangle$')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)
plt.show()

22.3.2 Example: curl of a three-dimensional field

Let

\[ F(x,y,z)=\langle yz,xz,xy+e^z\rangle. \]

Then

\[ P=yz, \qquad Q=xz, \qquad R=xy+e^z. \]

Compute:

\[ R_y=x, \qquad Q_z=x, \]

\[ P_z=y, \qquad R_x=y, \]

\[ Q_x=z, \qquad P_y=z. \]

Hence

\[ \nabla\times F=\langle x-x,y-y,z-z\rangle=\langle 0,0,0\rangle. \]

This zero curl suggests that the field may be conservative on a suitable simply connected domain. In fact,

\[ F=\nabla f, \qquad f(x,y,z)=xyz+e^z. \]

22.4 Divergence

Let

\[ F=\langle P,Q,R\rangle. \]

The divergence of \(F\) is the scalar field

\[ \nabla\cdot F = P_x+Q_y+R_z. \]

Divergence measures the local tendency of a vector field to flow outward from a point. Positive divergence indicates local source-like behavior. Negative divergence indicates local sink-like behavior. Zero divergence indicates local incompressibility.

ImportantDivergence is a scalar field

Curl returns a vector field in three dimensions. Divergence returns a scalar field:

\[ \nabla\times F \in \mathbb R^3, \qquad \nabla\cdot F \in \mathbb R. \]

22.4.1 Example: a source field

Let

\[ F(x,y,z)=\langle x,y,z\rangle. \]

Then

\[ \nabla\cdot F=1+1+1=3. \]

Everywhere, the field points away from the origin. A small ball centered at any point experiences net outward flow.

22.4.2 Example: an incompressible rotational field

For

\[ F(x,y,z)=\langle -y,x,0\rangle, \]

we get

\[ \nabla\cdot F=0+0+0=0. \]

The field rotates, but it does not locally expand or compress area. It has curl but no divergence.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x = np.linspace(-2, 2, 17)
y = np.linspace(-2, 2, 17)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(10, 4))

U1, V1 = X, Y
axes[0].quiver(X, Y, U1, V1, angles='xy')
axes[0].set_title(r'Source field $\langle x,y\rangle$')
axes[0].set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
axes[0].grid(True, alpha=0.25)

U2, V2 = -Y, X
axes[1].quiver(X, Y, U2, V2, angles='xy')
axes[1].set_title(r'Rotational field $\langle -y,x\rangle$')
axes[1].set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
axes[1].grid(True, alpha=0.25)

for ax in axes:
    ax.set_xlabel(r'$x$')
    ax.set_ylabel(r'$y$')

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

22.5 Two fundamental identities

For sufficiently smooth functions and fields, two identities occur throughout vector calculus.

First, the curl of a gradient is zero:

\[ \nabla\times(\nabla f)=0. \]

This says that gradient fields have no local rotation. It is the three-dimensional version of the conservative-field test.

Second, the divergence of a curl is zero:

\[ \nabla\cdot(\nabla\times F)=0. \]

This says that a curl field has no local source or sink.

WarningSmoothness and domain matter

The identities above require enough differentiability. Also, equations such as \(\nabla\times F=0\) guarantee a global potential only under suitable assumptions on the domain. Holes and singularities can create global circulation even when local curl is zero away from the singularity.

22.6 Numerical curl and divergence on a grid

In applications, a vector field may be sampled rather than given by a formula. For example, a velocity field may come from fluid simulation, a weather model, image motion, or a learned dynamical system. We can approximate divergence and curl by finite differences.

For a plane field \(F=\langle P,Q\rangle\), approximate

\[ \nabla\cdot F=P_x+Q_y, \]

and

\[ \operatorname{curl}_z F=Q_x-P_y. \]

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

x = np.linspace(-2, 2, 80)
y = np.linspace(-2, 2, 80)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)
P = -Y + 0.35*X
Q = X - 0.2*Y

dx = x[1] - x[0]
dy = y[1] - y[0]
P_y, P_x = np.gradient(P, dy, dx)
Q_y, Q_x = np.gradient(Q, dy, dx)
Div = P_x + Q_y
Curl = Q_x - P_y

fig, axes = plt.subplots(1, 2, figsize=(10, 4))
cf0 = axes[0].contourf(X, Y, Div, levels=20)
axes[0].set_title(r'Approximate divergence')
axes[0].set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
fig.colorbar(cf0, ax=axes[0])

cf1 = axes[1].contourf(X, Y, Curl, levels=20)
axes[1].set_title(r'Approximate scalar curl')
axes[1].set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
fig.colorbar(cf1, ax=axes[1])

for ax in axes:
    ax.set_xlabel(r'$x$')
    ax.set_ylabel(r'$y$')

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

This example is deliberately simple: the field has nearly constant divergence and curl. In real data, the plots may reveal sources, sinks, vortices, and boundaries.

22.7 Surfaces and parameterizations

A curve is described by one parameter:

\[ r(t)=\langle x(t),y(t),z(t)\rangle. \]

A surface is described by two parameters:

\[ r(u,v)=\langle x(u,v),y(u,v),z(u,v)\rangle, \qquad (u,v)\in D. \]

The parameter domain \(D\) lies in the \(uv\)-plane. The map \(r\) bends and stretches this flat parameter region into a surface \(S\) in space.

For a parameterized surface, the tangent vectors are

\[ r_u=\frac{\partial r}{\partial u}, \qquad r_v=\frac{\partial r}{\partial v}. \]

The cross product

\[ r_u\times r_v \]

is normal to the surface. Its magnitude gives the local area scaling factor:

\[ dS=|r_u\times r_v|\,du\,dv. \]

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

u = np.linspace(-1.5, 1.5, 35)
v = np.linspace(-1.5, 1.5, 35)
U, V = np.meshgrid(u, v)
X = U
Y = V
Z = 0.35*(U**2 + V**2)

fig = plt.figure(figsize=(8, 5))
ax = fig.add_subplot(111, projection='3d')
ax.plot_surface(X, Y, Z, alpha=0.75, linewidth=0)

# normals for z = g(x,y): <-g_x, -g_y, 1>
pts = np.linspace(-1.2, 1.2, 5)
PX, PY = np.meshgrid(pts, pts)
PZ = 0.35*(PX**2 + PY**2)
NX = -0.7*PX
NY = -0.7*PY
NZ = np.ones_like(PX)
length = np.sqrt(NX**2 + NY**2 + NZ**2)
NX, NY, NZ = NX/length, NY/length, NZ/length
ax.quiver(PX, PY, PZ, NX, NY, NZ, length=0.35)

ax.set_xlabel(r'$x$')
ax.set_ylabel(r'$y$')
ax.set_zlabel(r'$z$')
ax.set_title('Surface normals on a paraboloid')
plt.show()

22.8 Surface area

The area of a parameterized surface

\[ r(u,v), \qquad (u,v)\in D, \]

is

\[ A(S)=\iint_D |r_u\times r_v|\,dA. \]

This formula is the surface analog of arc length. For curves, \(|r'(t)|\) measures how much the parameter stretches length. For surfaces, \(|r_u\times r_v|\) measures how much the two-parameter map stretches area.

22.8.1 Graph surfaces

If a surface is a graph

\[ z=g(x,y), \qquad (x,y)\in D, \]

we can parameterize it as

\[ r(x,y)=\langle x,y,g(x,y)\rangle. \]

Then

\[ r_x=\langle 1,0,g_x\rangle, \qquad r_y=\langle 0,1,g_y\rangle, \]

so

\[ r_x\times r_y=\langle -g_x,-g_y,1\rangle. \]

Therefore

\[ dS=\sqrt{1+g_x^2+g_y^2}\,dA, \]

and

\[ A(S)=\iint_D \sqrt{1+g_x^2+g_y^2}\,dA. \]

22.8.2 Worked example: area of a plane patch

Find the area of the part of the plane

\[ z=8+2x+5y \]

above the rectangle

\[ 0\le x\le 3, \qquad 1\le y\le 6. \]

Here

\[ g_x=2, \qquad g_y=5. \]

Thus

\[ \sqrt{1+g_x^2+g_y^2} =\sqrt{1+4+25} =\sqrt{30}. \]

The rectangle has area \(15\), so

\[ A(S)=15\sqrt{30}. \]

22.9 Scalar surface integrals

If \(f(x,y,z)\) is a scalar function defined on a surface \(S\), then the scalar surface integral is

\[ \iint_S f(x,y,z)\,dS. \]

For a parameterized surface \(r(u,v)\),

\[ \iint_S f\,dS = \iint_D f(r(u,v))|r_u\times r_v|\,du\,dv. \]

If \(f\) is a surface density, then this integral gives the total mass of the surface. If \(f=1\), the integral gives surface area.

22.9.1 Worked example: surface integral over a paraboloid

Let \(S\) be the graph

\[ z=x^2+y^2 \]

over the disk \(x^2+y^2\le 1\). Compute

\[ \iint_S z\,dS. \]

Since \(g(x,y)=x^2+y^2\), we have

\[ g_x=2x, \qquad g_y=2y, \]

so

\[ dS=\sqrt{1+4x^2+4y^2}\,dA. \]

The integral becomes

\[ \iint_D (x^2+y^2)\sqrt{1+4x^2+4y^2}\,dA. \]

In polar coordinates,

\[ \iint_S z\,dS = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 r^2\sqrt{1+4r^2}\,r\,dr\,d\theta. \]

That is

\[ 2\pi\int_0^1 r^3\sqrt{1+4r^2}\,dr. \]

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

r = np.linspace(0, 1, 80)
theta = np.linspace(0, 2*np.pi, 120)
R, T = np.meshgrid(r, theta)
X = R*np.cos(T)
Y = R*np.sin(T)
Z = R**2

fig = plt.figure(figsize=(8, 5))
ax = fig.add_subplot(111, projection='3d')
surf = ax.plot_surface(X, Y, Z, cmap='viridis', linewidth=0, alpha=0.9)
ax.set_xlabel(r'$x$')
ax.set_ylabel(r'$y$')
ax.set_zlabel(r'$z$')
ax.set_title(r'Paraboloid $z=x^2+y^2$ over the unit disk')
fig.colorbar(surf, ax=ax, shrink=0.65, label=r'$z$')
plt.show()

22.10 Orientation of surfaces

A surface is orientable if it is possible to choose a continuous unit normal vector \(n\) at every point of the surface. Choosing one of the two possible normal directions gives an orientation.

For a parameterized surface \(r(u,v)\), the vector

\[ r_u\times r_v \]

defines one orientation. Reversing the parameter order changes the normal direction:

\[ r_v\times r_u=-(r_u\times r_v). \]

For a closed surface, the standard orientation is outward. For an open surface, the orientation must be specified, for example upward, downward, outward, or inward.

WarningOrientation affects flux

Scalar surface integrals do not depend on orientation because \(dS\) is positive. Flux integrals do depend on orientation because changing \(n\) to \(-n\) changes the sign of \(F\cdot n\).

22.11 Flux integrals

Let \(F\) be a vector field and let \(S\) be an oriented surface with unit normal vector \(n\). The flux of \(F\) through \(S\) is

\[ \iint_S F\cdot n\,dS. \]

This measures the net amount of the field passing through the surface in the chosen normal direction.

Using a parameterization \(r(u,v)\), we have

\[ n\,dS=(r_u\times r_v)\,du\,dv \]

for the orientation determined by \(r_u\times r_v\). Therefore

\[ \iint_S F\cdot n\,dS = \iint_D F(r(u,v))\cdot(r_u\times r_v)\,du\,dv. \]

This formula is one of the most important computational tools in vector calculus.

22.11.1 Graph formula for flux

Suppose

\[ S: z=g(x,y), \qquad (x,y)\in D, \]

with upward orientation. Then

\[ r_x\times r_y=\langle -g_x,-g_y,1\rangle. \]

If

\[ F=\langle P,Q,R\rangle, \]

then

\[ \iint_S F\cdot n\,dS = \iint_D \left(-P g_x-Q g_y+R\right)dA, \]

where \(P,Q,R\) are evaluated at \((x,y,g(x,y))\).

For downward orientation, the sign changes.

22.11.2 Worked example: flux through a graph

Let

\[ F(x,y,z)=\langle x,y,z\rangle, \]

and let \(S\) be the upper paraboloid

\[ z=1-x^2-y^2, \qquad x^2+y^2\le 1, \]

with upward orientation.

Here

\[ g_x=-2x, \qquad g_y=-2y. \]

The upward flux integrand is

\[ -Pg_x-Qg_y+R = -x(-2x)-y(-2y)+(1-x^2-y^2). \]

Thus

\[ -Pg_x-Qg_y+R =1+x^2+y^2. \]

Therefore

\[ \iint_S F\cdot n\,dS =\iint_D (1+x^2+y^2)\,dA. \]

In polar coordinates,

\[ \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 (1+r^2)r\,dr\,d\theta =2\pi\left[\frac{r^2}{2}+\frac{r^4}{4}\right]_0^1 =\frac{3\pi}{2}. \]

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

r = np.linspace(0, 1, 120)
theta = np.linspace(0, 2*np.pi, 240)
R, T = np.meshgrid(r, theta)
X = R*np.cos(T)
Y = R*np.sin(T)
Z = 1 - R**2

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 6))
cf = ax.contourf(X, Y, Z, levels=20)
fig.colorbar(cf, ax=ax, label=r'$z=1-x^2-y^2$')

pts = np.linspace(-0.8, 0.8, 7)
PX, PY = np.meshgrid(pts, pts)
mask = PX**2 + PY**2 <= 1
PX = PX[mask]
PY = PY[mask]
# Upward normal for z=1-x^2-y^2 is <2x,2y,1>.
# We draw its horizontal projection <2x,2y>.
ax.quiver(PX, PY, 2*PX, 2*PY, angles='xy', scale_units='xy', scale=8)
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
ax.set_xlabel(r'$x$')
ax.set_ylabel(r'$y$')
ax.set_title('Projected upward normals on the paraboloid')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)
plt.show()

22.12 Flux through standard surfaces

For standard surfaces, it is often better to use geometry than algebra.

22.12.1 Sphere

The sphere of radius \(a\) centered at the origin can be parameterized by

\[ r(\phi,\theta) =\langle a\sin\phi\cos\theta, a\sin\phi\sin\theta, a\cos\phi\rangle, \]

where

\[ 0\le \phi\le \pi, \qquad 0\le \theta\le 2\pi. \]

The outward unit normal is

\[ n=\frac{1}{a}\langle x,y,z\rangle. \]

If \(F=\langle x,y,z\rangle\), then on the sphere,

\[ F\cdot n =\langle x,y,z\rangle\cdot \frac{1}{a}\langle x,y,z\rangle =\frac{x^2+y^2+z^2}{a} =a. \]

Since the surface area is \(4\pi a^2\), the flux is

\[ \iint_S F\cdot n\,dS =a(4\pi a^2)=4\pi a^3. \]

For the sphere \(x^2+y^2+z^2=4\), we have \(a=2\), so the flux is

\[ 32\pi. \]

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

a = 2.0
theta = np.linspace(0, 2*np.pi, 300)
xc = a*np.cos(theta)
yc = a*np.sin(theta)

sample = np.linspace(0, 2*np.pi, 16, endpoint=False)
xs = a*np.cos(sample)
ys = a*np.sin(sample)
ux = np.cos(sample)
uy = np.sin(sample)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(6, 6))
ax.plot(xc, yc)
ax.quiver(xs, ys, ux, uy, angles='xy', scale_units='xy', scale=4)
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
ax.set_xlabel(r'$x$')
ax.set_ylabel(r'$y$')
ax.set_title('Radial outward normals on a sphere: cross-section view')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)
plt.show()

22.12.2 Cylinder

A circular cylinder of radius \(a\) can be parameterized by

\[ r(\theta,z)=\langle a\cos\theta,a\sin\theta,z\rangle. \]

Then

\[ r_\theta=\langle -a\sin\theta,a\cos\theta,0\rangle, \qquad r_z=\langle 0,0,1\rangle. \]

The cross product

\[ r_z\times r_\theta=\langle -a\cos\theta,-a\sin\theta,0\rangle \]

points inward, while

\[ r_\theta\times r_z=\langle a\cos\theta,a\sin\theta,0\rangle \]

points outward. Always check orientation.

22.13 Numerical surface integration

For computational work, a parameterized surface integral is usually approximated by sampling a grid in parameter space. If

\[ r(u,v), \qquad (u,v)\in D, \]

then

\[ \iint_S f\,dS \approx \sum_{i,j} f(r(u_i,v_j))|r_u(u_i,v_j)\times r_v(u_i,v_j)|\Delta u\Delta v. \]

Similarly,

\[ \iint_S F\cdot n\,dS \approx \sum_{i,j} F(r(u_i,v_j))\cdot(r_u(u_i,v_j)\times r_v(u_i,v_j))\Delta u\Delta v. \]

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Approximate surface area of z = x^2 + y^2 over [-1,1] x [-1,1]
n = 250
x = np.linspace(-1, 1, n)
y = np.linspace(-1, 1, n)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)
g_x = 2*X
g_y = 2*Y
area_density = np.sqrt(1 + g_x**2 + g_y**2)
dx = x[1] - x[0]
dy = y[1] - y[0]
area_est = area_density.sum()*dx*dy

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 5))
cs = ax.contourf(X, Y, area_density, levels=25)
fig.colorbar(cs, ax=ax, label=r'$\sqrt{1+g_x^2+g_y^2}$')
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
ax.set_xlabel(r'$x$')
ax.set_ylabel(r'$y$')
ax.set_title(f'Area-density plot; estimated area = {area_est:.4f}')
plt.show()

The code computes an approximate surface area by summing the area-density factor over a grid. This is the same Riemann-sum idea that underlies all definite integrals, now applied to surfaces.

22.14 Curl, divergence, and flux in applied modeling

22.14.1 Fluid velocity fields

If \(F\) is a fluid velocity field, then:

  • \(\nabla\times F\) measures local swirling;
  • \(\nabla\cdot F\) measures local expansion or compression;
  • \(\iint_S F\cdot n\,dS\) measures the rate of flow through a surface.

These ideas appear in incompressible flow, fluid simulation, weather models, and diffusion processes.

22.14.2 Probability flow

In probability and statistics, a time-dependent density can move under a velocity field. Divergence helps describe whether probability mass is locally spreading or concentrating. In continuous normalizing flows and score-based generative modeling, vector fields and Jacobians are central tools.

22.14.3 Optimization and machine learning

Gradient descent is driven by a vector field \(-\nabla L\). Such a field has zero curl when \(L\) is smooth. More general training dynamics may include momentum, stochastic noise, constraints, or game-theoretic interactions. These dynamics can show rotational behavior, which is naturally detected by curl-like measurements.

22.14.4 Geometry and data

Surface integrals appear when data lie on curved manifolds rather than flat Euclidean spaces. Area elements and Jacobian factors explain why densities change under parameterization and why sampling uniformly in parameter coordinates does not always sample uniformly on a surface.

22.15 Common mistakes

  1. Forgetting that curl is a vector in space. In three dimensions, \(\nabla\times F\) has three components.

  2. Confusing scalar curl with full curl. In the plane, \(Q_x-P_y\) is the \(z\)-component of the three-dimensional curl of \(\langle P,Q,0\rangle\).

  3. Forgetting the area factor. A surface integral is not usually just an ordinary double integral over the parameter domain. The factor \(|r_u\times r_v|\) is essential.

  4. Using the wrong orientation. Flux changes sign when the normal direction is reversed.

  5. Using graph formulas outside their setting. The formula for \(z=g(x,y)\) applies only when the surface is represented as a graph over the \(xy\)-plane.

  6. Treating \(dS\) like \(dA\). For flat horizontal surfaces, \(dS=dA\). For curved surfaces, \(dS\) includes an area-stretching factor.

The previous chapters developed line integrals and Green's theorem.  A line integral measures how a scalar or vector field accumulates along a curve.  Green's theorem then showed that circulation or flux around a closed plane curve can be computed by integrating a local derivative over the enclosed region.

This chapter moves that idea into three-dimensional space.  Instead of curves enclosing planar regions, we now study surfaces in space.  Instead of asking only how much a vector field pushes along a path, we ask how much it rotates near a point, how much it expands out of a point, and how much it flows through a surface.

The central objects are:

- **curl**, which measures local rotational tendency;
- **divergence**, which measures local source or sink strength;
- **surface integrals**, which accumulate scalar quantities over curved surfaces;
- **flux integrals**, which measure flow through oriented surfaces.

These concepts are essential for vector calculus, physics, partial differential equations, probability flow, optimization dynamics, and geometric data analysis.  They also prepare the way for Stokes' theorem and the divergence theorem in the next chapter.

## The differential operator $\nabla$

For a scalar function $f(x,y,z)$, the gradient is

$$
\nabla f=\left\langle f_x,f_y,f_z\right\rangle.
$$

For a vector field

$$
F(x,y,z)=\langle P(x,y,z),Q(x,y,z),R(x,y,z)\rangle,
$$

we use the formal differential operator

$$
\nabla=\left\langle \frac{\partial}{\partial x},\frac{\partial}{\partial y},\frac{\partial}{\partial z}\right\rangle.
$$

This notation is powerful because it lets us remember several vector-calculus operations using familiar vector algebra:

$$
\nabla f \quad \text{gradient},
$$

$$
\nabla\cdot F \quad \text{divergence},
$$

$$
\nabla\times F \quad \text{curl}.
$$

The notation is formal: $\nabla$ is not an ordinary vector of numbers.  Its components are derivative operators.  But the dot-product and cross-product notation correctly organizes the formulas.

## Curl

Let

$$
F=\langle P,Q,R\rangle
$$

be a vector field in space.  The **curl** of $F$ is the vector field

$$
\nabla\times F
=
\left\langle
R_y-Q_z,
P_z-R_x,
Q_x-P_y
\right\rangle.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
\nabla\times F
=
\begin{vmatrix}
\mathbf i & \mathbf j & \mathbf k\\
\frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\\
P & Q & R
\end{vmatrix}.
$$

Curl measures the infinitesimal tendency of the field to rotate.  Its direction is the axis of rotation determined by the right-hand rule, and its magnitude measures the local strength of the rotation.

::: {.callout-important}
## Curl in the plane

A two-dimensional vector field

$$
F(x,y)=\langle P(x,y),Q(x,y)\rangle
$$

can be treated as the three-dimensional field $\langle P,Q,0\rangle$.  Then

$$
\nabla\times F
=
\left\langle 0,0,Q_x-P_y\right\rangle.
$$

The scalar quantity

$$
Q_x-P_y
$$

is often called the scalar curl of the plane field.  It is exactly the integrand that appears in Green's theorem in circulation form.
:::

### Example: a rotating field

Let

$$
F(x,y,z)=\langle -y,x,0\rangle.
$$

Then

$$
P=-y,
\qquad
Q=x,
\qquad
R=0.
$$

Therefore

$$
\nabla\times F
=\left\langle
0-0,
0-0,
1-(-1)
\right\rangle
=\langle 0,0,2\rangle.
$$

The field rotates counterclockwise in the $xy$-plane.  The curl points in the positive $z$-direction.

### Example: curl of a three-dimensional field

Let

$$
F(x,y,z)=\langle yz,xz,xy+e^z\rangle.
$$

Then

$$
P=yz,
\qquad
Q=xz,
\qquad
R=xy+e^z.
$$

Compute:

$$
R_y=x,
\qquad
Q_z=x,
$$

$$
P_z=y,
\qquad
R_x=y,
$$

$$
Q_x=z,
\qquad
P_y=z.
$$

Hence

$$
\nabla\times F=\langle x-x,y-y,z-z\rangle=\langle 0,0,0\rangle.
$$

This zero curl suggests that the field may be conservative on a suitable simply connected domain.  In fact,

$$
F=\nabla f,
\qquad
f(x,y,z)=xyz+e^z.
$$

## Divergence

Let

$$
F=\langle P,Q,R\rangle.
$$

The **divergence** of $F$ is the scalar field

$$
\nabla\cdot F
=
P_x+Q_y+R_z.
$$

Divergence measures the local tendency of a vector field to flow outward from a point.  Positive divergence indicates local source-like behavior.  Negative divergence indicates local sink-like behavior.  Zero divergence indicates local incompressibility.

::: {.callout-important}
## Divergence is a scalar field

Curl returns a vector field in three dimensions.  Divergence returns a scalar field:

$$
\nabla\times F \in \mathbb R^3,
\qquad
\nabla\cdot F \in \mathbb R.
$$
:::

### Example: a source field

Let

$$
F(x,y,z)=\langle x,y,z\rangle.
$$

Then

$$
\nabla\cdot F=1+1+1=3.
$$

Everywhere, the field points away from the origin.  A small ball centered at any point experiences net outward flow.

### Example: an incompressible rotational field

For

$$
F(x,y,z)=\langle -y,x,0\rangle,
$$

we get

$$
\nabla\cdot F=0+0+0=0.
$$

The field rotates, but it does not locally expand or compress area.  It has curl but no divergence.

## Two fundamental identities

For sufficiently smooth functions and fields, two identities occur throughout vector calculus.

First, the curl of a gradient is zero:

$$
\nabla\times(\nabla f)=0.
$$

This says that gradient fields have no local rotation.  It is the three-dimensional version of the conservative-field test.

Second, the divergence of a curl is zero:

$$
\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times F)=0.
$$

This says that a curl field has no local source or sink.

::: {.callout-warning}
## Smoothness and domain matter

The identities above require enough differentiability.  Also, equations such as $\nabla\times F=0$ guarantee a global potential only under suitable assumptions on the domain.  Holes and singularities can create global circulation even when local curl is zero away from the singularity.
:::

## Numerical curl and divergence on a grid

In applications, a vector field may be sampled rather than given by a formula.  For example, a velocity field may come from fluid simulation, a weather model, image motion, or a learned dynamical system.  We can approximate divergence and curl by finite differences.

For a plane field $F=\langle P,Q\rangle$, approximate

$$
\nabla\cdot F=P_x+Q_y,
$$

and

$$
\operatorname{curl}_z F=Q_x-P_y.
$$

```{python}

Q = X - 0.2*Y

dy = y[1] - y[0]
```

## Surfaces and parameterizations

A curve is described by one parameter:

$$
r(t)=\langle x(t),y(t),z(t)\rangle.
$$

A surface is described by two parameters:

$$
r(u,v)=\langle x(u,v),y(u,v),z(u,v)\rangle,
\qquad
(u,v)\in D.
$$

The parameter domain $D$ lies in the $uv$-plane.  The map $r$ bends and stretches this flat parameter region into a surface $S$ in space.

For a parameterized surface, the tangent vectors are

$$
r_u=\frac{\partial r}{\partial u},
\qquad
r_v=\frac{\partial r}{\partial v}.
$$

The cross product

$$
r_u\times r_v
$$

is normal to the surface.  Its magnitude gives the local area scaling factor:

$$
dS=|r_u\times r_v|\,du\,dv.
$$

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch22-parametric-surface-normals
#| fig-cap: 'For a parameterized surface, the cross product $r_u\times r_v$ gives a normal direction and the local area scale.'
#| fig-width: 7
#| fig-height: 5
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

u = np.linspace(-1.5, 1.5, 35)
v = np.linspace(-1.5, 1.5, 35)
U, V = np.meshgrid(u, v)
X = U
Y = V
Z = 0.35*(U**2 + V**2)

fig = plt.figure(figsize=(8, 5))
ax = fig.add_subplot(111, projection='3d')
ax.plot_surface(X, Y, Z, alpha=0.75, linewidth=0)

# normals for z = g(x,y): <-g_x, -g_y, 1>

PZ = 0.35*(PX**2 + PY**2)
NX = -0.7*PX
NY = -0.7*PY

length = np.sqrt(NX**2 + NY**2 + NZ**2)
NX, NY, NZ = NX/length, NY/length, NZ/length
ax.quiver(PX, PY, PZ, NX, NY, NZ, length=0.35)

ax.set_xlabel(r'$x$')
ax.set_ylabel(r'$y$')
ax.set_zlabel(r'$z$')
```

## Surface area

The area of a parameterized surface

$$
r(u,v),
\qquad
(u,v)\in D,
$$

is

$$
A(S)=\iint_D |r_u\times r_v|\,dA.
$$

This formula is the surface analog of arc length.  For curves, $|r'(t)|$ measures how much the parameter stretches length.  For surfaces, $|r_u\times r_v|$ measures how much the two-parameter map stretches area.

### Graph surfaces

If a surface is a graph

$$
z=g(x,y),
\qquad
(x,y)\in D,
$$

we can parameterize it as

$$
r(x,y)=\langle x,y,g(x,y)\rangle.
$$

Then

$$
r_x=\langle 1,0,g_x\rangle,
\qquad
r_y=\langle 0,1,g_y\rangle,
$$

so

$$
r_x\times r_y=\langle -g_x,-g_y,1\rangle.
$$

Therefore

$$
dS=\sqrt{1+g_x^2+g_y^2}\,dA,
$$

and

$$
A(S)=\iint_D \sqrt{1+g_x^2+g_y^2}\,dA.
$$

### Worked example: area of a plane patch

Find the area of the part of the plane

$$
z=8+2x+5y
$$

above the rectangle

$$
0\le x\le 3,
\qquad
1\le y\le 6.
$$

Here

$$
g_x=2,
\qquad
g_y=5.
$$

Thus

$$
\sqrt{1+g_x^2+g_y^2}
=\sqrt{1+4+25}
=\sqrt{30}.
$$

The rectangle has area $15$, so

$$
A(S)=15\sqrt{30}.
$$

## Scalar surface integrals

If $f(x,y,z)$ is a scalar function defined on a surface $S$, then the scalar surface integral is

$$
\iint_S f(x,y,z)\,dS.
$$

For a parameterized surface $r(u,v)$,

$$
\iint_S f\,dS
=
\iint_D f(r(u,v))|r_u\times r_v|\,du\,dv.
$$

If $f$ is a surface density, then this integral gives the total mass of the surface.  If $f=1$, the integral gives surface area.

### Worked example: surface integral over a paraboloid

Let $S$ be the graph

$$
z=x^2+y^2
$$

over the disk $x^2+y^2\le 1$.  Compute

$$
\iint_S z\,dS.
$$

Since $g(x,y)=x^2+y^2$, we have

$$
g_x=2x,
\qquad
g_y=2y,
$$

so

$$
dS=\sqrt{1+4x^2+4y^2}\,dA.
$$

The integral becomes

$$
\iint_D (x^2+y^2)\sqrt{1+4x^2+4y^2}\,dA.
$$

In polar coordinates,

$$
\iint_S z\,dS
=
\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 r^2\sqrt{1+4r^2}\,r\,dr\,d\theta.
$$

That is

$$
2\pi\int_0^1 r^3\sqrt{1+4r^2}\,dr.
$$

## Orientation of surfaces

A surface is **orientable** if it is possible to choose a continuous unit normal vector $n$ at every point of the surface.  Choosing one of the two possible normal directions gives an **orientation**.

For a parameterized surface $r(u,v)$, the vector

$$
r_u\times r_v
$$

defines one orientation.  Reversing the parameter order changes the normal direction:

$$
r_v\times r_u=-(r_u\times r_v).
$$

For a closed surface, the standard orientation is **outward**.  For an open surface, the orientation must be specified, for example upward, downward, outward, or inward.

::: {.callout-warning}
## Orientation affects flux

Scalar surface integrals do not depend on orientation because $dS$ is positive.  Flux integrals do depend on orientation because changing $n$ to $-n$ changes the sign of $F\cdot n$.
:::

## Flux integrals

Let $F$ be a vector field and let $S$ be an oriented surface with unit normal vector $n$.  The **flux** of $F$ through $S$ is

$$
\iint_S F\cdot n\,dS.
$$

This measures the net amount of the field passing through the surface in the chosen normal direction.

Using a parameterization $r(u,v)$, we have

$$
n\,dS=(r_u\times r_v)\,du\,dv
$$

for the orientation determined by $r_u\times r_v$.  Therefore

$$
\iint_S F\cdot n\,dS
=
\iint_D F(r(u,v))\cdot(r_u\times r_v)\,du\,dv.
$$

This formula is one of the most important computational tools in vector calculus.

### Graph formula for flux

Suppose

$$
S: z=g(x,y),
\qquad
(x,y)\in D,
$$

with upward orientation.  Then

$$
r_x\times r_y=\langle -g_x,-g_y,1\rangle.
$$

If

$$
F=\langle P,Q,R\rangle,
$$

then

$$
\iint_S F\cdot n\,dS
=
\iint_D \left(-P g_x-Q g_y+R\right)dA,
$$

where $P,Q,R$ are evaluated at $(x,y,g(x,y))$.

For downward orientation, the sign changes.

### Worked example: flux through a graph

Let

$$
F(x,y,z)=\langle x,y,z\rangle,
$$

and let $S$ be the upper paraboloid

$$
z=1-x^2-y^2,
\qquad
x^2+y^2\le 1,
$$

with upward orientation.

Here

$$
g_x=-2x,
\qquad
g_y=-2y.
$$

The upward flux integrand is

$$
-Pg_x-Qg_y+R
= -x(-2x)-y(-2y)+(1-x^2-y^2).
$$

Thus

$$
-Pg_x-Qg_y+R
=1+x^2+y^2.
$$

Therefore

$$
\iint_S F\cdot n\,dS
=\iint_D (1+x^2+y^2)\,dA.
$$

In polar coordinates,

$$
\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 (1+r^2)r\,dr\,d\theta
=2\pi\left[\frac{r^2}{2}+\frac{r^4}{4}\right]_0^1
=\frac{3\pi}{2}.
$$

## Flux through standard surfaces

For standard surfaces, it is often better to use geometry than algebra.

### Sphere

The sphere of radius $a$ centered at the origin can be parameterized by

$$
r(\phi,\theta)
=\langle a\sin\phi\cos\theta,
a\sin\phi\sin\theta,
a\cos\phi\rangle,
$$

where

$$
0\le \phi\le \pi,
\qquad
0\le \theta\le 2\pi.
$$

The outward unit normal is

$$
n=\frac{1}{a}\langle x,y,z\rangle.
$$

If $F=\langle x,y,z\rangle$, then on the sphere,

$$
F\cdot n
=\langle x,y,z\rangle\cdot \frac{1}{a}\langle x,y,z\rangle
=\frac{x^2+y^2+z^2}{a}
=a.
$$

Since the surface area is $4\pi a^2$, the flux is

$$
\iint_S F\cdot n\,dS
=a(4\pi a^2)=4\pi a^3.
$$

For the sphere $x^2+y^2+z^2=4$, we have $a=2$, so the flux is

$$
32\pi.
$$

### Cylinder

A circular cylinder of radius $a$ can be parameterized by

$$
r(\theta,z)=\langle a\cos\theta,a\sin\theta,z\rangle.
$$

Then

$$
r_\theta=\langle -a\sin\theta,a\cos\theta,0\rangle,
\qquad
r_z=\langle 0,0,1\rangle.
$$

The cross product

$$
r_z\times r_\theta=\langle -a\cos\theta,-a\sin\theta,0\rangle
$$

points inward, while

$$
r_\theta\times r_z=\langle a\cos\theta,a\sin\theta,0\rangle
$$

points outward.  Always check orientation.

## Numerical surface integration

For computational work, a parameterized surface integral is usually approximated by sampling a grid in parameter space.  If

$$
r(u,v),
\qquad
(u,v)\in D,
$$

then

$$
\iint_S f\,dS
\approx
\sum_{i,j} f(r(u_i,v_j))|r_u(u_i,v_j)\times r_v(u_i,v_j)|\Delta u\Delta v.
$$

Similarly,

$$
\iint_S F\cdot n\,dS
\approx
\sum_{i,j} F(r(u_i,v_j))\cdot(r_u(u_i,v_j)\times r_v(u_i,v_j))\Delta u\Delta v.
$$

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch22-numerical-surface-integration
#| fig-cap: 'Numerical surface integration samples the parameter domain and multiplies by the local area scale.'
#| fig-width: 7
#| fig-height: 5
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

# Approximate surface area of z = x^2 + y^2 over [-1,1] x [-1,1]
n = 250
x = np.linspace(-1, 1, n)
y = np.linspace(-1, 1, n)
X, Y = np.meshgrid(x, y)
g_x = 2*X
g_y = 2*Y
area_density = np.sqrt(1 + g_x**2 + g_y**2)
dx = x[1] - x[0]
dy = y[1] - y[0]
area_est = area_density.sum()*dx*dy

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(7, 5))
cs = ax.contourf(X, Y, area_density, levels=25)
fig.colorbar(cs, ax=ax, label=r'$\sqrt{1+g_x^2+g_y^2}$')
ax.set_aspect('equal', adjustable='box')
ax.set_xlabel(r'$x$')
ax.set_ylabel(r'$y$')
ax.set_title(f'Area-density plot; estimated area = {area_est:.4f}')
plt.show()
```

The code computes an approximate surface area by summing the area-density factor over a grid.  This is the same Riemann-sum idea that underlies all definite integrals, now applied to surfaces.

## Curl, divergence, and flux in applied modeling

### Fluid velocity fields

If $F$ is a fluid velocity field, then:

- $\nabla\times F$ measures local swirling;
- $\nabla\cdot F$ measures local expansion or compression;
- $\iint_S F\cdot n\,dS$ measures the rate of flow through a surface.

These ideas appear in incompressible flow, fluid simulation, weather models, and diffusion processes.

### Probability flow

In probability and statistics, a time-dependent density can move under a velocity field.  Divergence helps describe whether probability mass is locally spreading or concentrating.  In continuous normalizing flows and score-based generative modeling, vector fields and Jacobians are central tools.

### Optimization and machine learning

Gradient descent is driven by a vector field $-\nabla L$.  Such a field has zero curl when $L$ is smooth.  More general training dynamics may include momentum, stochastic noise, constraints, or game-theoretic interactions.  These dynamics can show rotational behavior, which is naturally detected by curl-like measurements.

### Geometry and data

Surface integrals appear when data lie on curved manifolds rather than flat Euclidean spaces.  Area elements and Jacobian factors explain why densities change under parameterization and why sampling uniformly in parameter coordinates does not always sample uniformly on a surface.

## Common mistakes

1. **Forgetting that curl is a vector in space.**  In three dimensions, $\nabla\times F$ has three components.

2. **Confusing scalar curl with full curl.**  In the plane, $Q_x-P_y$ is the $z$-component of the three-dimensional curl of $\langle P,Q,0\rangle$.

3. **Forgetting the area factor.**  A surface integral is not usually just an ordinary double integral over the parameter domain.  The factor $|r_u\times r_v|$ is essential.

4. **Using the wrong orientation.**  Flux changes sign when the normal direction is reversed.

5. **Using graph formulas outside their setting.**  The formula for $z=g(x,y)$ applies only when the surface is represented as a graph over the $xy$-plane.

6. **Treating $dS$ like $dA$.**  For flat horizontal surfaces, $dS=dA$.  For curved surfaces, $dS$ includes an area-stretching factor.

## Chapter summary

This chapter introduced the local derivative operations and surface integrals needed for the final theorems of vector calculus.

- Curl measures local rotation.
- Divergence measures local source or sink behavior.
- In the plane, scalar curl is $Q_x-P_y$.
- In space, curl is $\nabla\times F$ and divergence is $\nabla\cdot F$.
- The identity $\nabla\times(\nabla f)=0$ says gradient fields have no local rotation.
- The identity $\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times F)=0$ says curl fields have no local source or sink.
- A parameterized surface $r(u,v)$ has tangent vectors $r_u$ and $r_v$.
- The vector $r_u\times r_v$ gives a normal direction and an area scale.
- Scalar surface integrals accumulate scalar quantities over curved surfaces.
- Flux integrals measure how much a vector field passes through an oriented surface.
- Orientation matters for flux but not for scalar surface integrals.
