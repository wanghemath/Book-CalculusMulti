17  Chapter 17. Cylindrical and Spherical Coordinates

Triple integrals with symmetry, radial geometry, and modern applications

Rectangular coordinates are natural for boxes, planes, and regions bounded by simple inequalities in \(x\), \(y\), and \(z\). Many important three-dimensional regions, however, are not rectangular. Cylinders, cones, spheres, shells, balls, hemispheres, and rotationally symmetric solids are often much easier to describe using coordinates adapted to their geometry.

This chapter develops two coordinate systems for triple integrals:

  • cylindrical coordinates \((r, heta,z)\), which use polar coordinates in the \(xy\)-plane and keep the vertical coordinate \(z\);
  • spherical coordinates \((\rho, heta,\phi)\), which describe a point by distance from the origin and two angles.

The goal is not simply to memorize formulas. The goal is to learn how to choose coordinates that make geometry, integration, probability, and computation simpler.

In modern applied mathematics, statistics, data science, and machine learning, these coordinates appear whenever a model has radial symmetry, rotational symmetry, distance-based structure, isotropic noise, or Gaussian geometry.

17.1 Learning goals

After studying this chapter, you should be able to:

  1. Convert points and equations among rectangular, cylindrical, and spherical coordinates.
  2. Recognize common coordinate surfaces: cylinders, cones, spheres, and half-planes.
  3. Use the volume elements \(dV = r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta\) and \(dV = \rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta\).
  4. Set up and evaluate triple integrals in cylindrical coordinates.
  5. Set up and evaluate triple integrals in spherical coordinates.
  6. Choose an efficient coordinate system by reading the symmetry of the region and integrand.
  7. Use coordinate transformations in probability, statistics, and simulation.

17.2 17.1 Why new coordinates are needed

A coordinate system is a language for describing points. In rectangular coordinates, a point is described by three signed distances:

\[ (x,y,z). \]

This is perfect for a rectangular box such as

\[ 0 \le x \le 2, \qquad 1 \le y \le 4, \qquad -1 \le z \le 3. \]

But consider the cylinder

\[ x^2+y^2 \le 4, \]

or the sphere

\[ x^2+y^2+z^2 \le 9. \]

These regions are simple geometrically but awkward in rectangular bounds. The equations already contain distance expressions. That is the signal that radial coordinates may simplify the problem.

TipCoordinate choice principle

Use a coordinate system whose coordinate surfaces resemble the boundary surfaces of the region.

  • Cylinders suggest cylindrical coordinates.
  • Spheres suggest spherical coordinates.
  • Cones often work well in either cylindrical or spherical coordinates.
  • Planes and boxes often work well in rectangular coordinates.

17.3 17.2 Cylindrical coordinates

Cylindrical coordinates combine polar coordinates in the \(xy\)-plane with the usual vertical coordinate \(z\):

\[ x = r\cos\theta, \qquad y = r\sin\theta, \qquad z = z. \]

Here

\[ r \ge 0, \qquad 0 \le \theta \le 2\pi, \]

unless a smaller angular interval is specified.

The inverse relations are

\[ r^2 = x^2+y^2, \qquad \tan\theta = \frac{y}{x}, \qquad z=z. \]

The expression \(r\) is the distance from the point \((x,y,z)\) to the \(z\)-axis, not the distance to the origin in space.

17.3.1 Coordinate surfaces in cylindrical coordinates

The coordinate surfaces are especially important:

Cylindrical equation Geometric meaning
\(r=r_0\) a cylinder of radius \(r_0\) centered on the \(z\)-axis
\(\theta=\theta_0\) a vertical half-plane through the \(z\)-axis
\(z=z_0\) a horizontal plane parallel to the \(xy\)-plane

For example, \(r=3\) means \(x^2+y^2=9\), a vertical cylinder. The equation \(z=r\) means

\[ z = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}, \]

which is a cone opening upward.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

fig = plt.figure(figsize=(7, 6))

X_cyl = R*np.cos(Theta)
Y_cyl = R*np.sin(Theta)
ax.plot_surface(X_cyl, Y_cyl, Z, alpha=0.25, linewidth=0)

Z_cone = R2
ax.plot_surface(X_cone, Y_cone, Z_cone, alpha=0.25, linewidth=0)

# Horizontal plane z = 2.5

X_plane, Y_plane = np.meshgrid(x, y)
Z_plane = 2.5*np.ones_like(X_plane)
ax.plot_surface(X_plane, Y_plane, Z_plane, alpha=0.18, linewidth=0)

ax.set_ylabel('y')

17.4 17.3 The cylindrical volume element

In rectangular coordinates, a small box has volume

\[ dV = dx\,dy\,dz. \]

In cylindrical coordinates, the small coordinate box has dimensions approximately

\[ dr, \qquad r\,d\theta, \qquad dz. \]

Therefore,

\[ dV = r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta. \]

The factor \(r\) is the same factor that appears in polar coordinates. It accounts for the fact that an angular change \(d\theta\) sweeps out a longer arc when \(r\) is larger.

ImportantCylindrical integration formula

If \(x=r\cos\theta\), \(y=r\sin\theta\), and \(z=z\), then

\[ \iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV = \int_{\theta=\alpha}^{\beta}\int_{r=h_1(\theta)}^{h_2(\theta)} \int_{z=g_1(r,\theta)}^{g_2(r,\theta)} f(r\cos\theta,r\sin\theta,z)\,r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta. \]

The extra factor \(r\) is not optional. It is part of the volume element.

17.5 17.4 Worked examples in cylindrical coordinates

17.5.1 Example 1: Convert a point

Find rectangular coordinates for the point with cylindrical coordinates

\[ (r,\theta,z)=\left(2,\frac{2\pi}{3},3\right). \]

Using \(x=r\cos\theta\) and \(y=r\sin\theta\),

\[ x = 2\cos\left(\frac{2\pi}{3}\right) = -1, \]

\[ y = 2\sin\left(\frac{2\pi}{3}\right) = \sqrt{3}, \]

and \(z=3\). Thus the rectangular coordinates are

\[ (-1,\sqrt{3},3). \]

17.5.2 Example 2: A cone below a plane

Find the volume of the solid above the cone \(z=r\) and below the plane \(z=1\).

The cone and plane intersect when \(r=1\). The region is

\[ 0 \le \theta \le 2\pi, \qquad 0 \le r \le 1, \qquad r \le z \le 1. \]

Therefore,

\[ V = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1\int_r^1 r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta. \]

Compute:

\[ V = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 r(1-r)\,dr\,d\theta = 2\pi\left[\frac{r^2}{2}-\frac{r^3}{3}\right]_0^1 = \frac{\pi}{3}. \]

This is the familiar volume of a cone with radius \(1\) and height \(1\).

17.5.3 Example 3: A cylinder above a paraboloid

Let \(E\) be the solid inside the cylinder \(x^2+y^2 \le 1\), above the paraboloid

\[ z = 1-x^2-y^2, \]

and below the plane \(z=3\). Find the volume of \(E\).

In cylindrical coordinates,

\[ x^2+y^2 = r^2, \]

so the lower surface is \(z=1-r^2\). The bounds are

\[ 0 \le \theta \le 2\pi, \qquad 0 \le r \le 1, \qquad 1-r^2 \le z \le 3. \]

Thus

\[ V = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1\int_{1-r^2}^{3} r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta. \]

Compute:

\[ V = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 r(2+r^2)\,dr\,d\theta = 2\pi\left[ r^2+\frac{r^4}{4}\right]_0^1 = \frac{5\pi}{2}. \]

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

fig = plt.figure(figsize=(7, 6))
ax = fig.add_subplot(111, projection='3d')

theta = np.linspace(0, 2*np.pi, 100)
r = np.linspace(0, 1, 60)
Theta, R = np.meshgrid(theta, r)
X = R*np.cos(Theta)
Y = R*np.sin(Theta)
Z_lower = 1 - R**2
Z_upper = 3*np.ones_like(R)

ax.plot_surface(X, Y, Z_lower, alpha=0.45, linewidth=0)
ax.plot_surface(X, Y, Z_upper, alpha=0.18, linewidth=0)

# side cylinder
z_side = np.linspace(0, 3, 60)
Theta_s, Z_s = np.meshgrid(theta, z_side)
X_s = np.cos(Theta_s)
Y_s = np.sin(Theta_s)
ax.plot_surface(X_s, Y_s, Z_s, alpha=0.15, linewidth=0)

ax.set_xlabel('x')
ax.set_ylabel('y')
ax.set_zlabel('z')
ax.set_title('Cylinder, paraboloid, and plane')
ax.set_box_aspect((1, 1, 1.2))
plt.show()

17.5.4 Example 4: Mass with radial density

Suppose the same solid \(E\) has density

\[ \delta(x,y,z)=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}. \]

In cylindrical coordinates, the density becomes

\[ \delta = r. \]

Mass is density integrated over volume:

\[ M = \iiint_E \delta\,dV. \]

Thus

\[ M = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1\int_{1-r^2}^{3} r\cdot r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta. \]

The first \(r\) is the density and the second \(r\) is the Jacobian factor from \(dV\).

So

\[ M = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 r^2(2+r^2)\,dr\,d\theta = 2\pi\left[\frac{2r^3}{3}+\frac{r^5}{5}\right]_0^1 = \frac{26\pi}{15}. \]

This example shows why it is useful to write every factor carefully: one factor may come from the density, another from the coordinate transformation.

17.5.5 Example 5: Converting a rectangular integral

Evaluate

\[ \int_{-1}^{1}\int_{-\sqrt{1-x^2}}^{\sqrt{1-x^2}}\int_{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}^{1} (x^2+y^2)\,dz\,dy\,dx. \]

The projection onto the \(xy\)-plane is the unit disk. The lower surface is the cone \(z=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}=r\), and the upper surface is \(z=1\). The integrand becomes \(r^2\). Therefore,

\[ \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1\int_r^1 r^2\,r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta. \]

Compute:

\[ \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 r^3(1-r)\,dr\,d\theta = 2\pi\left(\frac14-\frac15\right) = \frac{\pi}{10}. \]

17.6 17.5 Spherical coordinates

Spherical coordinates are designed for regions with spherical or conical symmetry. A point is described by

\[ (\rho,\theta,\phi), \]

where:

  • \(\rho\) is the distance from the origin;
  • \(\theta\) is the angle in the \(xy\)-plane, measured from the positive \(x\)-axis;
  • \(\phi\) is the angle from the positive \(z\)-axis.

The conversion formulas are

\[ x = \rho\sin\phi\cos\theta, \qquad y = \rho\sin\phi\sin\theta, \qquad z = \rho\cos\phi. \]

The usual ranges are

\[ \rho \ge 0, \qquad 0 \le \theta \le 2\pi, \qquad 0 \le \phi \le \pi. \]

The inverse relations are

\[ \rho^2 = x^2+y^2+z^2, \qquad r = \rho\sin\phi, \qquad z = \rho\cos\phi. \]

The relation between cylindrical and spherical coordinates is

\[ r = \rho\sin\phi, \qquad z=\rho\cos\phi, \qquad \theta=\theta. \]

17.6.1 Coordinate surfaces in spherical coordinates

Spherical equation Geometric meaning
\(\rho=\rho_0\) a sphere of radius \(\rho_0\) centered at the origin
\(\theta=\theta_0\) a vertical half-plane through the \(z\)-axis
\(\phi=\phi_0\) a cone with vertex at the origin

For example, \(\rho=2\) is the sphere \(x^2+y^2+z^2=4\). The equation \(\phi=\pi/4\) is a cone around the positive \(z\)-axis.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

fig = plt.figure(figsize=(7, 6))
ax = fig.add_subplot(111, projection='3d')

# Sphere rho = 2
u = np.linspace(0, 2*np.pi, 80)
v = np.linspace(0, np.pi, 50)
U, V = np.meshgrid(u, v)
rho = 2
X_sphere = rho*np.sin(V)*np.cos(U)
Y_sphere = rho*np.sin(V)*np.sin(U)
Z_sphere = rho*np.cos(V)
ax.plot_surface(X_sphere, Y_sphere, Z_sphere, alpha=0.20, linewidth=0)

# Cone phi = pi/4
rho_grid = np.linspace(0, 2.4, 60)
theta_grid = np.linspace(0, 2*np.pi, 80)
Rho, Theta = np.meshgrid(rho_grid, theta_grid)
Phi = np.pi/4
X_cone = Rho*np.sin(Phi)*np.cos(Theta)
Y_cone = Rho*np.sin(Phi)*np.sin(Theta)
Z_cone = Rho*np.cos(Phi)
ax.plot_surface(X_cone, Y_cone, Z_cone, alpha=0.35, linewidth=0)

ax.set_xlabel('x')
ax.set_ylabel('y')
ax.set_zlabel('z')
ax.set_title('Spherical coordinate surfaces')
ax.set_box_aspect((1, 1, 1))
plt.show()

17.7 17.6 The spherical volume element

A small spherical coordinate box has approximate side lengths

\[ d\rho, \qquad \rho\,d\phi, \qquad \rho\sin\phi\,d\theta. \]

Therefore,

\[ dV = \rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta. \]

ImportantSpherical integration formula

If

\[ x=\rho\sin\phi\cos\theta, \qquad y=\rho\sin\phi\sin\theta, \qquad z=\rho\cos\phi, \]

then

\[ \iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV = \int \int \int f(\rho\sin\phi\cos\theta,\rho\sin\phi\sin\theta,\rho\cos\phi) \rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta. \]

Again, the factor \(\rho^2\sin\phi\) is not optional. It is the three-dimensional Jacobian factor for spherical coordinates.

17.8 17.7 Worked examples in spherical coordinates

17.8.1 Example 6: Volume of the unit ball

Let \(B\) be the unit ball

\[ x^2+y^2+z^2 \le 1. \]

In spherical coordinates, this is simply

\[ 0 \le \rho \le 1, \qquad 0 \le \phi \le \pi, \qquad 0 \le \theta \le 2\pi. \]

Therefore,

\[ V = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^\pi\int_0^1 \rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta. \]

Compute:

\[ V = \left(\int_0^{2\pi} d\theta\right) \left(\int_0^\pi \sin\phi\,d\phi\right) \left(\int_0^1 \rho^2\,d\rho\right) = (2\pi)(2)\left(\frac13\right) = \frac{4\pi}{3}. \]

17.8.2 Example 7: A solid inside a sphere and above a cone

Find the volume of the solid inside the sphere

\[ x^2+y^2+z^2=8 \]

and above the cone

\[ z=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}. \]

In spherical coordinates, the sphere is

\[ \rho = \sqrt{8}. \]

The cone is found by writing \(z=r\). Since \(z=\rho\cos\phi\) and \(r=\rho\sin\phi\),

\[ \rho\cos\phi = \rho\sin\phi. \]

Away from the origin, this gives

\[ \phi = \frac{\pi}{4}. \]

The region above the cone and inside the sphere is therefore

\[ 0 \le \theta \le 2\pi, \qquad 0 \le \phi \le \frac{\pi}{4}, \qquad 0 \le \rho \le \sqrt{8}. \]

Thus

\[ V = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{\pi/4}\int_0^{\sqrt{8}} \rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta. \]

Compute:

\[ V = (2\pi)\left(1-\frac{1}{\sqrt2}\right) \left(\frac{(\sqrt8)^3}{3}\right) = \frac{32\pi}{3}(\sqrt2-1). \]

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

fig = plt.figure(figsize=(7, 6))
ax = fig.add_subplot(111, projection='3d')

theta = np.linspace(0, 2*np.pi, 100)
phi = np.linspace(0, np.pi/4, 50)
Theta, Phi = np.meshgrid(theta, phi)
Rho = np.sqrt(8)
X = Rho*np.sin(Phi)*np.cos(Theta)
Y = Rho*np.sin(Phi)*np.sin(Theta)
Z = Rho*np.cos(Phi)
ax.plot_surface(X, Y, Z, alpha=0.35, linewidth=0)

rho_vals = np.linspace(0, np.sqrt(8), 60)
Theta2, Rho2 = np.meshgrid(theta, rho_vals)
Phi2 = np.pi/4
X_cone = Rho2*np.sin(Phi2)*np.cos(Theta2)
Y_cone = Rho2*np.sin(Phi2)*np.sin(Theta2)
Z_cone = Rho2*np.cos(Phi2)
ax.plot_surface(X_cone, Y_cone, Z_cone, alpha=0.25, linewidth=0)

ax.set_xlabel('x')
ax.set_ylabel('y')
ax.set_zlabel('z')
ax.set_title('Sphere-cone solid')
ax.set_box_aspect((1, 1, 1))
plt.show()

17.8.3 Example 8: A spherical shell in the first octant

Let \(E\) be the part of the spherical shell

\[ 1 \le x^2+y^2+z^2 \le 4 \]

in the first octant. Find its volume.

The first octant means

\[ x\ge 0, \qquad y\ge 0, \qquad z\ge 0. \]

In spherical coordinates this becomes

\[ 0 \le \theta \le \frac{\pi}{2}, \qquad 0 \le \phi \le \frac{\pi}{2}. \]

The shell condition is

\[ 1 \le \rho^2 \le 4, \]

so

\[ 1 \le \rho \le 2. \]

Hence

\[ V = \int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^{\pi/2}\int_1^2 \rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta. \]

Compute:

\[ V = \left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right)(1)\left(\frac{2^3-1^3}{3}\right) = \frac{7\pi}{6}. \]

This result also makes geometric sense: the full shell has volume

\[ \frac{4\pi}{3}(2^3-1^3)=\frac{28\pi}{3}, \]

and the first octant is one eighth of it:

\[ \frac18\cdot \frac{28\pi}{3}=\frac{7\pi}{6}. \]

17.9 17.8 Choosing among rectangular, cylindrical, and spherical coordinates

Coordinate choice is part of problem solving. A good choice makes the region and integrand simple. A poor choice may make a straightforward problem unnecessarily complicated.

Geometry or expression Recommended coordinates Reason
Box with constant \(x,y,z\) bounds rectangular boundaries already align with coordinate planes
Cylinder \(x^2+y^2=R^2\) cylindrical becomes \(r=R\)
Cone \(z=c\sqrt{x^2+y^2}\) cylindrical or spherical becomes \(z=cr\) or \(\phi=\) constant
Sphere \(x^2+y^2+z^2=R^2\) spherical becomes \(\rho=R\)
Radial integrand \(f(x^2+y^2)\) cylindrical becomes \(f(r^2)\)
Radial integrand \(f(x^2+y^2+z^2)\) spherical becomes \(f(\rho^2)\)
First octant of a ball spherical constant angle bounds
Solid of revolution around the \(z\)-axis cylindrical natural \(r\) and \(z\) description
NoteA practical workflow
  1. Sketch or identify the boundary surfaces.
  2. Translate the boundary equations into candidate coordinate systems.
  3. Choose the system where most bounds become constants or simple functions.
  4. Rewrite the integrand.
  5. Include the correct volume element.

17.10 17.9 Numerical integration in cylindrical and spherical coordinates

The coordinate formulas are useful not only for exact integration but also for computation. A numerical method works best when the region has simple bounds.

For example, the sphere-cone solid from Example 7 is awkward in rectangular coordinates but simple in spherical coordinates:

\[ 0 \le \theta \le 2\pi, \qquad 0 \le \phi \le \frac{\pi}{4}, \qquad 0 \le \rho \le \sqrt8. \]

The following code approximates its volume by a grid-based spherical-coordinate sum.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

n_theta = 80
n_phi = 50
n_rho = 60

theta_edges = np.linspace(0, 2*np.pi, n_theta + 1)
phi_edges = np.linspace(0, np.pi/4, n_phi + 1)
rho_edges = np.linspace(0, np.sqrt(8), n_rho + 1)

theta_mid = 0.5*(theta_edges[:-1] + theta_edges[1:])
phi_mid = 0.5*(phi_edges[:-1] + phi_edges[1:])
rho_mid = 0.5*(rho_edges[:-1] + rho_edges[1:])

dtheta = theta_edges[1] - theta_edges[0]
dphi = phi_edges[1] - phi_edges[0]
drho = rho_edges[1] - rho_edges[0]

Theta, Phi, Rho = np.meshgrid(theta_mid, phi_mid, rho_mid, indexing='ij')
volume_approx = np.sum(Rho**2*np.sin(Phi))*drho*dphi*dtheta
volume_exact = (32*np.pi/3)*(np.sqrt(2)-1)

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.bar(['grid approximation', 'exact value'], [volume_approx, volume_exact])
ax.set_ylabel('volume')
ax.set_title('Spherical-coordinate volume approximation')
plt.show()

print(f'Grid approximation: {volume_approx:.8f}')
print(f'Exact value:        {volume_exact:.8f}')
print(f'Absolute error:     {abs(volume_approx-volume_exact):.3e}')
Grid approximation: 13.87960848
Exact value:        13.88042970
Absolute error:     8.212e-04

17.11 17.10 Coordinate transformations and probability

Triple integrals are also probability integrals. If \(f(x,y,z)\) is a joint density, then

\[ P((X,Y,Z)\in E)=\iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV. \]

When the density is radially symmetric, spherical coordinates are natural. For example, the three-dimensional standard normal density is

\[ f(x,y,z)=\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{3/2}}e^{-(x^2+y^2+z^2)/2}. \]

Since

\[ x^2+y^2+z^2 = \rho^2, \]

spherical coordinates transform the density into a radial expression:

\[ f = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{3/2}}e^{-\rho^2/2}. \]

The probability mass in a ball of radius \(R\) is

\[ P(\rho\le R) = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^\pi\int_0^R \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{3/2}}e^{-\rho^2/2}\rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta. \]

The angular integrals simplify:

\[ P(\rho\le R) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}}\int_0^R \rho^2 e^{-\rho^2/2}\,d\rho. \]

This is a major idea in statistics: a rotationally symmetric density becomes a one-dimensional radial integral after integrating out the angles.

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rho = np.linspace(0, 5, 400)
radial_density = np.sqrt(2/np.pi) * rho**2 * np.exp(-rho**2/2)

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.plot(rho, radial_density)
ax.set_xlabel(r'$\rho$')
ax.set_ylabel('radial density')
ax.set_title('Radial distribution for a 3D standard normal vector')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)
plt.show()

17.12 17.11 Sampling uniformly from a ball

Coordinate transformations matter in simulation. To sample uniformly from the unit ball in \(\mathbb R^3\), it is not correct to choose \(\rho\) uniformly from \([0,1]\). The reason is that the volume element contains \(\rho^2\).

A correct method is:

\[ \theta = 2\pi U_1, \qquad \cos\phi = 2U_2-1, \qquad \rho = U_3^{1/3}, \]

where \(U_1,U_2,U_3\) are independent uniform random variables on \([0,1]\).

The power \(1/3\) appears because the volume of a ball of radius \(r\) is proportional to \(r^3\).

import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(17)
n = 5000
U1 = rng.random(n)
U2 = rng.random(n)
U3 = rng.random(n)

theta = 2*np.pi*U1
cos_phi = 2*U2 - 1
phi = np.arccos(cos_phi)
rho_correct = U3**(1/3)
rho_naive = U3

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.hist(rho_correct, bins=35, density=True, alpha=0.55, label='correct rho')
ax.hist(rho_naive, bins=35, density=True, alpha=0.35, label='naive uniform rho')
ax.set_xlabel(r'$\rho$')
ax.set_ylabel('density')
ax.set_title('Radial distribution in unit ball sampling')
ax.legend()
plt.show()

The correct radial distribution places more points near the boundary because there is more volume near the boundary. This is not a bug; it is exactly what uniform volume sampling requires.

17.13 17.12 Common mistakes

17.13.1 Mistake 1: Forgetting the Jacobian factor

In cylindrical coordinates, the volume element is \(r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta\), not just \(dz\,dr\,d\theta\).

In spherical coordinates, the volume element is \(\rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta\), not just \(d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta\).

17.13.2 Mistake 2: Confusing \(r\) and \(\rho\)

In cylindrical coordinates,

\[ r = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}. \]

In spherical coordinates,

\[ \rho = \sqrt{x^2+y^2+z^2}. \]

Thus \(r\) is distance to the \(z\)-axis, while \(\rho\) is distance to the origin.

17.13.3 Mistake 3: Confusing \(\theta\) and \(\phi\)

The angle \(\theta\) is measured in the \(xy\)-plane. The angle \(\phi\) is measured down from the positive \(z\)-axis.

17.13.4 Mistake 4: Writing bounds before understanding the geometry

Do not begin by guessing bounds. First translate the boundary surfaces. Then decide which coordinate system makes them simple.

## 17.1 Why new coordinates are needed

A coordinate system is a language for describing points. In rectangular coordinates, a point is described by three signed distances:

$$
(x,y,z).
$$

This is perfect for a rectangular box such as

$$
0 \le x \le 2, \qquad 1 \le y \le 4, \qquad -1 \le z \le 3.
$$

But consider the cylinder

$$
x^2+y^2 \le 4,
$$

or the sphere

$$
x^2+y^2+z^2 \le 9.
$$

These regions are simple geometrically but awkward in rectangular bounds. The equations already contain distance expressions. That is the signal that radial coordinates may simplify the problem.

::: {.callout-tip}
## Coordinate choice principle
Use a coordinate system whose coordinate surfaces resemble the boundary surfaces of the region.

- Cylinders suggest cylindrical coordinates.
- Spheres suggest spherical coordinates.
- Cones often work well in either cylindrical or spherical coordinates.
- Planes and boxes often work well in rectangular coordinates.
:::

## 17.2 Cylindrical coordinates

Cylindrical coordinates combine polar coordinates in the $xy$-plane with the usual vertical coordinate $z$:

$$
x = r\cos\theta, \qquad y = r\sin\theta, \qquad z = z.
$$

Here

$$
r \ge 0, \qquad 0 \le \theta \le 2\pi,
$$

unless a smaller angular interval is specified.

The inverse relations are

$$
r^2 = x^2+y^2, \qquad \tan\theta = \frac{y}{x}, \qquad z=z.
$$

The expression $r$ is the distance from the point $(x,y,z)$ to the $z$-axis, not the distance to the origin in space.

### Coordinate surfaces in cylindrical coordinates

The coordinate surfaces are especially important:

| Cylindrical equation | Geometric meaning |
|---|---|
| $r=r_0$ | a cylinder of radius $r_0$ centered on the $z$-axis |
| $\theta=\theta_0$ | a vertical half-plane through the $z$-axis |
| $z=z_0$ | a horizontal plane parallel to the $xy$-plane |

For example, $r=3$ means $x^2+y^2=9$, a vertical cylinder. The equation $z=r$ means

$$
z = \sqrt{x^2+y^2},
$$

which is a cone opening upward.

## 17.3 The cylindrical volume element

In rectangular coordinates, a small box has volume

$$
dV = dx\,dy\,dz.
$$

In cylindrical coordinates, the small coordinate box has dimensions approximately

$$
dr, \qquad r\,d\theta, \qquad dz.
$$

Therefore,

$$
dV = r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta.
$$

The factor $r$ is the same factor that appears in polar coordinates. It accounts for the fact that an angular change $d\theta$ sweeps out a longer arc when $r$ is larger.

::: {.callout-important}
## Cylindrical integration formula
If $x=r\cos\theta$, $y=r\sin\theta$, and $z=z$, then

$$
\iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV
= \int_{\theta=\alpha}^{\beta}\int_{r=h_1(\theta)}^{h_2(\theta)}
\int_{z=g_1(r,\theta)}^{g_2(r,\theta)}
f(r\cos\theta,r\sin\theta,z)\,r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta.
$$
:::

The extra factor $r$ is not optional. It is part of the volume element.

## 17.4 Worked examples in cylindrical coordinates

### Example 1: Convert a point

Find rectangular coordinates for the point with cylindrical coordinates

$$
(r,\theta,z)=\left(2,\frac{2\pi}{3},3\right).
$$

Using $x=r\cos\theta$ and $y=r\sin\theta$,

$$
x = 2\cos\left(\frac{2\pi}{3}\right) = -1,
$$

$$
y = 2\sin\left(\frac{2\pi}{3}\right) = \sqrt{3},
$$

and $z=3$. Thus the rectangular coordinates are

$$
(-1,\sqrt{3},3).
$$

### Example 2: A cone below a plane

Find the volume of the solid above the cone $z=r$ and below the plane $z=1$.

The cone and plane intersect when $r=1$. The region is

$$
0 \le \theta \le 2\pi, \qquad 0 \le r \le 1, \qquad r \le z \le 1.
$$

Therefore,

$$
V
= \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1\int_r^1 r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta.
$$

Compute:

$$
V
= \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 r(1-r)\,dr\,d\theta
= 2\pi\left[\frac{r^2}{2}-\frac{r^3}{3}\right]_0^1
= \frac{\pi}{3}.
$$

This is the familiar volume of a cone with radius $1$ and height $1$.

### Example 3: A cylinder above a paraboloid

Let $E$ be the solid inside the cylinder $x^2+y^2 \le 1$, above the paraboloid

$$
z = 1-x^2-y^2,
$$

and below the plane $z=3$. Find the volume of $E$.

In cylindrical coordinates,

$$
x^2+y^2 = r^2,
$$

so the lower surface is $z=1-r^2$. The bounds are

$$
0 \le \theta \le 2\pi, \qquad 0 \le r \le 1, \qquad 1-r^2 \le z \le 3.
$$

Thus

$$
V
= \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1\int_{1-r^2}^{3} r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta.
$$

Compute:

$$
V
= \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 r(2+r^2)\,dr\,d\theta
= 2\pi\left[ r^2+\frac{r^4}{4}\right]_0^1
= \frac{5\pi}{2}.
$$

### Example 4: Mass with radial density

Suppose the same solid $E$ has density

$$
\delta(x,y,z)=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}.
$$

In cylindrical coordinates, the density becomes

$$
\delta = r.
$$

Mass is density integrated over volume:

$$
M = \iiint_E \delta\,dV.
$$

Thus

$$
M
= \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1\int_{1-r^2}^{3} r\cdot r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta.
$$

The first $r$ is the density and the second $r$ is the Jacobian factor from $dV$.

So

$$
M
= \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 r^2(2+r^2)\,dr\,d\theta
= 2\pi\left[\frac{2r^3}{3}+\frac{r^5}{5}\right]_0^1
= \frac{26\pi}{15}.
$$

This example shows why it is useful to write every factor carefully: one factor may come from the density, another from the coordinate transformation.

### Example 5: Converting a rectangular integral

Evaluate

$$
\int_{-1}^{1}\int_{-\sqrt{1-x^2}}^{\sqrt{1-x^2}}\int_{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}^{1} (x^2+y^2)\,dz\,dy\,dx.
$$

The projection onto the $xy$-plane is the unit disk. The lower surface is the cone $z=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}=r$, and the upper surface is $z=1$. The integrand becomes $r^2$. Therefore,

$$
\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1\int_r^1 r^2\,r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta.
$$

Compute:

$$
\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 r^3(1-r)\,dr\,d\theta
= 2\pi\left(\frac14-\frac15\right)
= \frac{\pi}{10}.
$$

## 17.5 Spherical coordinates

Spherical coordinates are designed for regions with spherical or conical symmetry. A point is described by

$$
(\rho,\theta,\phi),
$$

where:

- $\rho$ is the distance from the origin;
- $\theta$ is the angle in the $xy$-plane, measured from the positive $x$-axis;
- $\phi$ is the angle from the positive $z$-axis.

The conversion formulas are

$$
x = \rho\sin\phi\cos\theta,
\qquad
 y = \rho\sin\phi\sin\theta,
\qquad
 z = \rho\cos\phi.
$$

The usual ranges are

$$
\rho \ge 0, \qquad 0 \le \theta \le 2\pi, \qquad 0 \le \phi \le \pi.
$$

The inverse relations are

$$
\rho^2 = x^2+y^2+z^2,
\qquad
r = \rho\sin\phi,
\qquad
z = \rho\cos\phi.
$$

The relation between cylindrical and spherical coordinates is

$$
r = \rho\sin\phi, \qquad z=\rho\cos\phi, \qquad \theta=\theta.
$$

### Coordinate surfaces in spherical coordinates

| Spherical equation | Geometric meaning |
|---|---|
| $\rho=\rho_0$ | a sphere of radius $\rho_0$ centered at the origin |
| $\theta=\theta_0$ | a vertical half-plane through the $z$-axis |
| $\phi=\phi_0$ | a cone with vertex at the origin |

For example, $\rho=2$ is the sphere $x^2+y^2+z^2=4$. The equation $\phi=\pi/4$ is a cone around the positive $z$-axis.

## 17.6 The spherical volume element

A small spherical coordinate box has approximate side lengths

$$
d\rho, \qquad \rho\,d\phi, \qquad \rho\sin\phi\,d\theta.
$$

Therefore,

$$
dV = \rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta.
$$

::: {.callout-important}
## Spherical integration formula
If

$$
x=\rho\sin\phi\cos\theta, \qquad
 y=\rho\sin\phi\sin\theta, \qquad
 z=\rho\cos\phi,
$$

then

$$
\iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV
= \int \int \int
f(\rho\sin\phi\cos\theta,\rho\sin\phi\sin\theta,\rho\cos\phi)
\rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta.
$$
:::

Again, the factor $\rho^2\sin\phi$ is not optional. It is the three-dimensional Jacobian factor for spherical coordinates.

## 17.7 Worked examples in spherical coordinates

### Example 6: Volume of the unit ball

Let $B$ be the unit ball

$$
x^2+y^2+z^2 \le 1.
$$

In spherical coordinates, this is simply

$$
0 \le \rho \le 1, \qquad 0 \le \phi \le \pi, \qquad 0 \le \theta \le 2\pi.
$$

Therefore,

$$
V
= \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^\pi\int_0^1 \rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta.
$$

Compute:

$$
V
= \left(\int_0^{2\pi} d\theta\right)
  \left(\int_0^\pi \sin\phi\,d\phi\right)
  \left(\int_0^1 \rho^2\,d\rho\right)
= (2\pi)(2)\left(\frac13\right)
= \frac{4\pi}{3}.
$$

### Example 7: A solid inside a sphere and above a cone

Find the volume of the solid inside the sphere

$$
x^2+y^2+z^2=8
$$

and above the cone

$$
z=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}.
$$

In spherical coordinates, the sphere is

$$
\rho = \sqrt{8}.
$$

The cone is found by writing $z=r$. Since $z=\rho\cos\phi$ and $r=\rho\sin\phi$,

$$
\rho\cos\phi = \rho\sin\phi.
$$

Away from the origin, this gives

$$
\phi = \frac{\pi}{4}.
$$

The region above the cone and inside the sphere is therefore

$$
0 \le \theta \le 2\pi, \qquad 0 \le \phi \le \frac{\pi}{4}, \qquad 0 \le \rho \le \sqrt{8}.
$$

Thus

$$
V
= \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{\pi/4}\int_0^{\sqrt{8}}
\rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta.
$$

Compute:

$$
V
= (2\pi)\left(1-\frac{1}{\sqrt2}\right)
\left(\frac{(\sqrt8)^3}{3}\right)
= \frac{32\pi}{3}(\sqrt2-1).
$$

```{python}
Y_cone = Rho2*np.sin(Phi2)*np.sin(Theta2)
Z_cone = Rho2*np.cos(Phi2)
ax.plot_surface(X_cone, Y_cone, Z_cone, alpha=0.25, linewidth=0)

ax.set_xlabel('x')
ax.set_ylabel('y')
ax.set_zlabel('z')
ax.set_title('Sphere-cone solid')
ax.set_box_aspect((1, 1, 1))
plt.show()
```

### Example 8: A spherical shell in the first octant

Let $E$ be the part of the spherical shell

$$
1 \le x^2+y^2+z^2 \le 4
$$

in the first octant. Find its volume.

The first octant means

$$
x\ge 0, \qquad y\ge 0, \qquad z\ge 0.
$$

In spherical coordinates this becomes

$$
0 \le \theta \le \frac{\pi}{2}, \qquad 0 \le \phi \le \frac{\pi}{2}.
$$

The shell condition is

$$
1 \le \rho^2 \le 4,
$$

so

$$
1 \le \rho \le 2.
$$

Hence

$$
V
= \int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^{\pi/2}\int_1^2
\rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta.
$$

Compute:

$$
V
= \left(\frac{\pi}{2}\right)(1)\left(\frac{2^3-1^3}{3}\right)
= \frac{7\pi}{6}.
$$

This result also makes geometric sense: the full shell has volume

$$
\frac{4\pi}{3}(2^3-1^3)=\frac{28\pi}{3},
$$

and the first octant is one eighth of it:

$$
\frac18\cdot \frac{28\pi}{3}=\frac{7\pi}{6}.
$$

## 17.8 Choosing among rectangular, cylindrical, and spherical coordinates

Coordinate choice is part of problem solving. A good choice makes the region and integrand simple. A poor choice may make a straightforward problem unnecessarily complicated.

| Geometry or expression | Recommended coordinates | Reason |
|---|---|---|
| Box with constant $x,y,z$ bounds | rectangular | boundaries already align with coordinate planes |
| Cylinder $x^2+y^2=R^2$ | cylindrical | becomes $r=R$ |
| Cone $z=c\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$ | cylindrical or spherical | becomes $z=cr$ or $\phi=$ constant |
| Sphere $x^2+y^2+z^2=R^2$ | spherical | becomes $\rho=R$ |
| Radial integrand $f(x^2+y^2)$ | cylindrical | becomes $f(r^2)$ |
| Radial integrand $f(x^2+y^2+z^2)$ | spherical | becomes $f(\rho^2)$ |
| First octant of a ball | spherical | constant angle bounds |
| Solid of revolution around the $z$-axis | cylindrical | natural $r$ and $z$ description |

::: {.callout-note}
## A practical workflow
1. Sketch or identify the boundary surfaces.
2. Translate the boundary equations into candidate coordinate systems.
3. Choose the system where most bounds become constants or simple functions.
4. Rewrite the integrand.
5. Include the correct volume element.
:::

## 17.9 Numerical integration in cylindrical and spherical coordinates

The coordinate formulas are useful not only for exact integration but also for computation. A numerical method works best when the region has simple bounds.

For example, the sphere-cone solid from Example 7 is awkward in rectangular coordinates but simple in spherical coordinates:

$$
0 \le \theta \le 2\pi, \qquad 0 \le \phi \le \frac{\pi}{4}, \qquad 0 \le \rho \le \sqrt8.
$$

The following code approximates its volume by a grid-based spherical-coordinate sum.

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch17-spherical-grid-volume
#| fig-cap: 'A grid approximation in spherical coordinates for the sphere-cone solid.'
#| fig-width: 7
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

n_theta = 80
n_phi = 50
n_rho = 60

theta_edges = np.linspace(0, 2*np.pi, n_theta + 1)
phi_edges = np.linspace(0, np.pi/4, n_phi + 1)
rho_edges = np.linspace(0, np.sqrt(8), n_rho + 1)

theta_mid = 0.5*(theta_edges[:-1] + theta_edges[1:])
phi_mid = 0.5*(phi_edges[:-1] + phi_edges[1:])
rho_mid = 0.5*(rho_edges[:-1] + rho_edges[1:])

dtheta = theta_edges[1] - theta_edges[0]
dphi = phi_edges[1] - phi_edges[0]
drho = rho_edges[1] - rho_edges[0]

Theta, Phi, Rho = np.meshgrid(theta_mid, phi_mid, rho_mid, indexing='ij')
volume_approx = np.sum(Rho**2*np.sin(Phi))*drho*dphi*dtheta
volume_exact = (32*np.pi/3)*(np.sqrt(2)-1)

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.bar(['grid approximation', 'exact value'], [volume_approx, volume_exact])
ax.set_ylabel('volume')
ax.set_title('Spherical-coordinate volume approximation')
plt.show()

print(f'Grid approximation: {volume_approx:.8f}')
print(f'Exact value:        {volume_exact:.8f}')
print(f'Absolute error:     {abs(volume_approx-volume_exact):.3e}')
```

## 17.10 Coordinate transformations and probability

Triple integrals are also probability integrals. If $f(x,y,z)$ is a joint density, then

$$
P((X,Y,Z)\in E)=\iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV.
$$

When the density is radially symmetric, spherical coordinates are natural. For example, the three-dimensional standard normal density is

$$
f(x,y,z)=\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{3/2}}e^{-(x^2+y^2+z^2)/2}.
$$

Since

$$
x^2+y^2+z^2 = \rho^2,
$$

spherical coordinates transform the density into a radial expression:

$$
f = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{3/2}}e^{-\rho^2/2}.
$$

The probability mass in a ball of radius $R$ is

$$
P(\rho\le R)
= \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^\pi\int_0^R
\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{3/2}}e^{-\rho^2/2}\rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta.
$$

The angular integrals simplify:

$$
P(\rho\le R)
= \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}}\int_0^R \rho^2 e^{-\rho^2/2}\,d\rho.
$$

This is a major idea in statistics: a rotationally symmetric density becomes a one-dimensional radial integral after integrating out the angles.

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch17-radial-normal-density
#| fig-cap: 'The radial density for a three-dimensional standard normal distribution.'
#| fig-width: 7
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rho = np.linspace(0, 5, 400)
radial_density = np.sqrt(2/np.pi) * rho**2 * np.exp(-rho**2/2)

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.plot(rho, radial_density)
ax.set_xlabel(r'$\rho$')
ax.set_ylabel('radial density')
ax.set_title('Radial distribution for a 3D standard normal vector')
ax.grid(True, alpha=0.25)
plt.show()
```

## 17.11 Sampling uniformly from a ball

Coordinate transformations matter in simulation. To sample uniformly from the unit ball in $\mathbb R^3$, it is not correct to choose $\rho$ uniformly from $[0,1]$. The reason is that the volume element contains $\rho^2$.

A correct method is:

$$
\theta = 2\pi U_1, \qquad \cos\phi = 2U_2-1, \qquad \rho = U_3^{1/3},
$$

where $U_1,U_2,U_3$ are independent uniform random variables on $[0,1]$.

The power $1/3$ appears because the volume of a ball of radius $r$ is proportional to $r^3$.

```{python}
#| label: fig-ch17-uniform-ball-sampling
#| fig-cap: 'Uniform sampling in a ball requires the radial transformation rho = U^(1/3).'
#| fig-width: 7
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

rng = np.random.default_rng(17)
n = 5000
U1 = rng.random(n)
U2 = rng.random(n)
U3 = rng.random(n)

theta = 2*np.pi*U1
cos_phi = 2*U2 - 1
phi = np.arccos(cos_phi)
rho_correct = U3**(1/3)
rho_naive = U3

fig, ax = plt.subplots()
ax.hist(rho_correct, bins=35, density=True, alpha=0.55, label='correct rho')
ax.hist(rho_naive, bins=35, density=True, alpha=0.35, label='naive uniform rho')
ax.set_xlabel(r'$\rho$')
ax.set_ylabel('density')
ax.set_title('Radial distribution in unit ball sampling')
ax.legend()
plt.show()
```

The correct radial distribution places more points near the boundary because there is more volume near the boundary. This is not a bug; it is exactly what uniform volume sampling requires.

## 17.12 Common mistakes

### Mistake 1: Forgetting the Jacobian factor

In cylindrical coordinates, the volume element is $r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta$, not just $dz\,dr\,d\theta$.

In spherical coordinates, the volume element is $\rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta$, not just $d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta$.

### Mistake 2: Confusing $r$ and $\rho$

In cylindrical coordinates,

$$
r = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}.
$$

In spherical coordinates,

$$
\rho = \sqrt{x^2+y^2+z^2}.
$$

Thus $r$ is distance to the $z$-axis, while $\rho$ is distance to the origin.

### Mistake 3: Confusing $\theta$ and $\phi$

The angle $\theta$ is measured in the $xy$-plane. The angle $\phi$ is measured down from the positive $z$-axis.

### Mistake 4: Writing bounds before understanding the geometry

Do not begin by guessing bounds. First translate the boundary surfaces. Then decide which coordinate system makes them simple.

## 17.13 Chapter summary

Cylindrical coordinates are defined by

$$
x=r\cos\theta, \qquad y=r\sin\theta, \qquad z=z,
$$

with volume element

$$
dV = r\,dz\,dr\,d\theta.
$$

Spherical coordinates are defined by

$$
x=\rho\sin\phi\cos\theta, \qquad
 y=\rho\sin\phi\sin\theta, \qquad
 z=\rho\cos\phi,
$$

with volume element

$$
dV = \rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta.
$$

The main problem-solving skill is choosing coordinates that match the region and integrand. Cylindrical coordinates simplify cylinders, cones, and axial symmetry. Spherical coordinates simplify spheres, balls, shells, cones, and radial functions.

These coordinate systems are not only tools for hand integration. They are also important in numerical integration, simulation, probability, isotropic models, radial kernels, and high-dimensional statistical thinking.
