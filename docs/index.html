Welcome

Multivariable Calculus in the Age of Data, AI, and Applied Mathematics is a modern textbook for students who want to use calculus as a language for geometry, computation, statistics, machine learning, and applied mathematical modeling.

The guiding idea is simple:

Multivariable calculus studies functions with many inputs, and the modern quantitative world is built from functions with many inputs.

A point in \(\mathbb R^n\) can represent a location in space, a data record, a parameter vector, a probability model, a neural-network weight vector, or the state of a dynamical system. A derivative becomes a gradient, a Jacobian, or a Hessian. An integral becomes volume, mass, probability, expectation, flux, or accumulated work. Vector calculus becomes the language of flow, conservation, circulation, and change over regions and surfaces.

This book develops the classical core of multivariable calculus while making the computational and data-centered viewpoint explicit from the beginning.

Who this book is for

This book is designed for students preparing for graduate study or advanced undergraduate work in:

  • applied mathematics,
  • statistics,
  • data science,
  • artificial intelligence,
  • machine learning,
  • quantitative modeling,
  • scientific computing,
  • engineering and physical sciences,
  • mathematical foundations of modern computation.

The expected background is single-variable calculus and basic algebra. Linear algebra is introduced and reinforced where needed, especially through vectors, projections, hyperplanes, Jacobians, Hessians, quadratic forms, and optimization.

What makes the book modern

Classical multivariable calculus is usually organized around geometry, differentiation, integration, and vector fields. Those topics remain the foundation of this book. The modern emphasis is that each idea is also connected to computation and modeling.

For example:

  • vectors are used for geometry, data, parameters, velocity, and force;
  • dot products explain angles, projections, similarity, and work;
  • gradients explain direction of fastest increase, optimization, likelihood, and learning;
  • Jacobians explain coordinate changes, sensitivity, and backpropagation;
  • Hessians explain curvature, second-order approximation, and uncertainty;
  • multiple integrals explain volume, probability, expectation, density, and mass;
  • Green’s theorem, Stokes’ theorem, and the divergence theorem explain circulation, flux, and conservation laws.

The book includes Python examples, numerical experiments, interactive visualizations, and applications to statistics and machine learning throughout.

  3. Run the Python code chunks or the companion notebook lab.

  5. Complete the conceptual and computational exercises.

Learning philosophy

The goal is not only to compute derivatives and integrals. The goal is to see the structure behind the computations:

  • local linearity behind derivatives,
  • accumulation behind integrals,
  • geometry behind optimization,
  • curvature behind uncertainty,
  • coordinates behind transformations,
  • fields behind flows and conservation laws.

A student who finishes the book should be ready to enter advanced courses in applied mathematics, probability, statistics, machine learning, optimization, scientific computing, and mathematical modeling with a clear geometric and computational foundation.
