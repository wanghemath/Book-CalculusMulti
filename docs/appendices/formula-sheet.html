30  Formula Sheet

Core formulas for multivariable calculus, computation, probability, statistics, and machine learning

31 Formula Sheet

This appendix collects the central formulas used throughout the book. It is designed as a quick reference, not as a substitute for understanding the geometry behind each formula. The best way to use it is to identify the object first: point, vector, curve, surface, scalar field, vector field, density, loss function, or constraint.

31.1 1. Euclidean Space and Vector Geometry

31.1.1 Points, vectors, distance, and norm

For points \(a=(a_1, \dots,a_n)\) and \(b=(b_1,\dots,b_n)\) in \(\mathbb R^n\),

\[ b-a=(b_1-a_1,\dots,b_n-a_n) \]

is the displacement vector from \(a\) to \(b\).

\[ \|v\|=\sqrt{v_1^2+\cdots+v_n^2} \]

\[ d(a,b)=\|b-a\|=\sqrt{(b_1-a_1)^2+\cdots+(b_n-a_n)^2} \]

The unit vector in the direction of a nonzero vector \(v\) is

\[ \widehat v=\frac{v}{\|v\|}. \]

A sphere in \(\mathbb R^3\) with center \((h,k,\ell)\) and radius \(r\) has equation

\[ (x-h)^2+(y-k)^2+(z-\ell)^2=r^2. \]

More generally, the sphere in \(\mathbb R^n\) centered at \(a\) with radius \(r\) is

\[ \|x-a\|=r. \]

31.1.2 Dot product, angles, orthogonality, and projections

For \(u,v\in \mathbb R^n\),

\[ u\cdot v=u_1v_1+\cdots+u_nv_n. \]

\[ u\cdot v=\|u\|\|v\|\cos\theta. \]

Thus,

\[ \cos\theta=\frac{u\cdot v}{\|u\|\|v\|}. \]

Vectors \(u\) and \(v\) are orthogonal if and only if

\[ u\cdot v=0. \]

The scalar projection of \(u\) onto \(v\) is

\[ \operatorname{comp}_v(u)=\frac{u\cdot v}{\|v\|}. \]

The vector projection of \(u\) onto \(v\) is

\[ \operatorname{proj}_v(u)=\frac{u\cdot v}{v\cdot v}v. \]

The component of \(u\) perpendicular to \(v\) is

\[ u-\operatorname{proj}_v(u). \]

Work by a constant force \(F\) along displacement \(d\) is

\[ W=F\cdot d. \]

31.2 2. Lines, Planes, Hyperplanes, and Cross Products

31.2.1 Lines and line segments

A line through a point \(p\) in direction \(v\ne 0\) is

\[ r(t)=p+tv,\qquad -\infty<t<\infty. \]

The line segment from \(a\) to \(b\) is

\[ r(t)=(1-t)a+tb=a+t(b-a),\qquad 0\le t\le 1. \]

The symmetric form of a line in \(\mathbb R^3\) is

\[ \frac{x-x_0}{a}=\frac{y-y_0}{b}=\frac{z-z_0}{c} \]

when the direction vector is \((a,b,c)\) and the nonzero denominators are meaningful.

31.2.2 Planes and hyperplanes

A plane through \(p=(x_0,y_0,z_0)\) with normal vector \(n=(A,B,C)\) is

\[ A(x-x_0)+B(y-y_0)+C(z-z_0)=0. \]

Equivalently,

\[ n\cdot (x-p)=0. \]

A parametric plane through \(p\) spanned by two nonparallel vectors \(u\) and \(v\) is

\[ r(s,t)=p+su+tv. \]

A hyperplane in \(\mathbb R^n\) has the form

\[ w\cdot x=b. \]

The distance from a point \(q\) to the plane \(n\cdot(x-p)=0\) is

\[ \operatorname{dist}(q,\text{plane})=\frac{|n\cdot(q-p)|}{\|n\|}. \]

The angle \(\theta\) between two planes with normal vectors \(n_1\) and \(n_2\) satisfies

\[ \cos\theta=\frac{|n_1\cdot n_2|}{\|n_1\|\|n_2\|}. \]

31.2.3 Cross product and triple product

For \(u=(u_1,u_2,u_3)\) and \(v=(v_1,v_2,v_3)\),

\[ u\times v= \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf i & \mathbf j & \mathbf k\\ u_1 & u_2 & u_3\\ v_1 & v_2 & v_3 \end{vmatrix}. \]

Equivalently,

\[ u\times v=(u_2v_3-u_3v_2,\,u_3v_1-u_1v_3,\,u_1v_2-u_2v_1). \]

The magnitude is

\[ \|u\times v\|=\|u\|\|v\|\sin\theta. \]

Area of parallelogram spanned by \(u\) and \(v\):

\[ A=\|u\times v\|. \]

Area of triangle with side vectors \(u\) and \(v\):

\[ A=\frac12\|u\times v\|. \]

The scalar triple product is

\[ u\cdot(v\times w)= \begin{vmatrix} u_1&u_2&u_3\\ v_1&v_2&v_3\\ w_1&w_2&w_3 \end{vmatrix}. \]

The volume of the parallelepiped spanned by \(u,v,w\) is

\[ V=|u\cdot(v\times w)|. \]

31.3 3. Curves and Motion in Space

For a vector-valued curve

\[ r(t)=(x(t),y(t),z(t)), \]

velocity, speed, and acceleration are

\[ v(t)=r'(t),\qquad \text{speed}=\|r'(t)\|,\qquad a(t)=r''(t). \]

Arc length from \(t=a\) to \(t=b\) is

\[ L=\int_a^b \|r'(t)\|\,dt. \]

For a plane curve \(r(t)=(x(t),y(t))\),

\[ L=\int_a^b \sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2+\left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)^2}\,dt. \]

For a space curve \(r(t)=(x(t),y(t),z(t))\),

\[ L=\int_a^b \sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2+\left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)^2+\left(\frac{dz}{dt}\right)^2}\,dt. \]

The unit tangent vector is

\[ T(t)=\frac{r'(t)}{\|r'(t)\|}. \]

Curvature is

\[ \kappa(t)=\frac{\|T'(t)\|}{\|r'(t)\|}. \]

When \(r'(t)\times r''(t)\ne 0\),

\[ \kappa(t)=\frac{\|r'(t)\times r''(t)\|}{\|r'(t)\|^3}. \]

31.4 4. Functions, Limits, Partial Derivatives, and Gradients

31.4.1 Scalar fields and level sets

A scalar field is a function

\[ f:\mathbb R^n\to \mathbb R. \]

For \(f(x,y)\), the graph is

\[ z=f(x,y). \]

Level curves satisfy

\[ f(x,y)=c. \]

Level surfaces satisfy

\[ f(x,y,z)=c. \]

31.4.2 Partial derivatives

For \(f(x,y)\),

\[ f_x(a,b)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(a+h,b)-f(a,b)}{h}, \]

\[ f_y(a,b)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(a,b+h)-f(a,b)}{h}. \]

Second partial derivatives are

\[ f_{xx},\qquad f_{xy},\qquad f_{yx},\qquad f_{yy}. \]

If \(f_{xy}\) and \(f_{yx}\) are continuous near a point, then

\[ f_{xy}=f_{yx}. \]

31.4.3 Gradient

For \(f(x,y)\),

\[ \nabla f=(f_x,f_y). \]

For \(f(x,y,z)\),

\[ \nabla f=(f_x,f_y,f_z). \]

For \(f(x_1,\dots,x_n)\),

\[ \nabla f=\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1},\dots,\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}\right). \]

The gradient is perpendicular to level curves and level surfaces.

31.5 5. Linear Approximation, Chain Rule, Jacobians, and Hessians

31.5.1 Linearization and tangent planes

For \(z=f(x,y)\), the tangent plane at \((a,b,f(a,b))\) is

\[ z=f(a,b)+f_x(a,b)(x-a)+f_y(a,b)(y-b). \]

The linearization is

\[ L(x,y)=f(a,b)+f_x(a,b)(x-a)+f_y(a,b)(y-b). \]

In vector form, for \(f:\mathbb R^n\to \mathbb R\),

\[ f(a+h)\approx f(a)+\nabla f(a)\cdot h. \]

The differential is

\[ df=f_x\,dx+f_y\,dy \]

for two variables, and

\[ df=\nabla f\cdot dx \]

in vector notation.

For a level surface \(F(x,y,z)=c\), the tangent plane at \(p=(x_0,y_0,z_0)\) is

\[ \nabla F(p)\cdot((x,y,z)-p)=0. \]

Equivalently,

\[ F_x(p)(x-x_0)+F_y(p)(y-y_0)+F_z(p)(z-z_0)=0. \]

31.5.2 Directional derivatives

For a unit vector \(u\),

\[ D_u f(a)=\nabla f(a)\cdot u. \]

The maximum rate of increase is

\[ \|\nabla f(a)\|, \]

and it occurs in the direction

\[ \frac{\nabla f(a)}{\|\nabla f(a)\|} \]

when \(\nabla f(a)\ne 0\).

31.5.3 Chain rule

If \(z=f(x,y)\) and \(x=x(t)\), \(y=y(t)\), then

\[ \frac{dz}{dt}=f_x\frac{dx}{dt}+f_y\frac{dy}{dt}. \]

If \(z=f(x,y)\), \(x=x(s,t)\), and \(y=y(s,t)\), then

\[ \frac{\partial z}{\partial s}=f_x\frac{\partial x}{\partial s}+f_y\frac{\partial y}{\partial s}, \]

\[ \frac{\partial z}{\partial t}=f_x\frac{\partial x}{\partial t}+f_y\frac{\partial y}{\partial t}. \]

For a map \(F:\mathbb R^n\to \mathbb R^m\), the Jacobian matrix is

\[ J_F(x)= \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial F_1}{\partial x_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial F_1}{\partial x_n}\\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots\\ \frac{\partial F_m}{\partial x_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial F_m}{\partial x_n} \end{bmatrix}. \]

For a composition \(H=F\circ G\),

\[ J_H(x)=J_F(G(x))J_G(x). \]

31.5.4 Hessian and Taylor approximation

The Hessian matrix of \(f:\mathbb R^n\to \mathbb R\) is

\[ H_f(x)= \begin{bmatrix} \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1^2} & \cdots & \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1\partial x_n}\\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots\\ \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_n\partial x_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_n^2} \end{bmatrix}. \]

Second-order Taylor approximation:

\[ f(a+h)\approx f(a)+\nabla f(a)\cdot h+\frac12 h^T H_f(a)h. \]

For \(f(x,y)\) near \((a,b)\),

\[ f(x,y)\approx f(a,b)+f_x(a,b)\Delta x+f_y(a,b)\Delta y +\frac12\left[f_{xx}(a,b)(\Delta x)^2+2f_{xy}(a,b)\Delta x\Delta y+f_{yy}(a,b)(\Delta y)^2\right]. \]

31.6 6. Optimization

31.6.1 Critical points and second derivative test

Critical points of \(f(x,y)\) satisfy

\[ f_x=0,\qquad f_y=0. \]

Let

\[ D=f_{xx}f_{yy}-f_{xy}^2. \]

At a critical point:

Condition Classification
\(D>0\) and \(f_{xx}>0\) local minimum
\(D>0\) and \(f_{xx}<0\) local maximum
\(D<0\) saddle point
\(D=0\) inconclusive

For \(f:\mathbb R^n\to\mathbb R\), a critical point satisfies

\[ \nabla f=0. \]

The Hessian test is based on eigenvalues of \(H_f\):

Hessian eigenvalues Local behavior
all positive local minimum
all negative local maximum
mixed signs saddle point
zero eigenvalues present inconclusive without more analysis

31.6.2 Gradient descent and Newton’s method

Gradient descent:

\[ x_{k+1}=x_k-\alpha_k\nabla f(x_k). \]

Newton’s method for optimization:

\[ x_{k+1}=x_k-H_f(x_k)^{-1}\nabla f(x_k). \]

31.6.3 Lagrange multipliers

For one constraint \(g(x,y)=c\), extrema of \(f\) occur where

\[ \nabla f=\lambda \nabla g, \]

with

\[ g(x,y)=c. \]

For several constraints \(g_1=c_1,\dots,g_k=c_k\),

\[ \nabla f=\lambda_1\nabla g_1+\cdots+\lambda_k\nabla g_k, \]

with all constraints imposed.

31.7 7. Double and Triple Integrals

31.7.1 Double integrals

Over a rectangle \(R=[a,b]\times[c,d]\),

\[ \iint_R f(x,y)\,dA=\int_a^b\int_c^d f(x,y)\,dy\,dx =\int_c^d\int_a^b f(x,y)\,dx\,dy. \]

For a Type I region

\[ D=\{(x,y):a\le x\le b,\ g_1(x)\le y\le g_2(x)\}, \]

\[ \iint_D f(x,y)\,dA=\int_a^b\int_{g_1(x)}^{g_2(x)}f(x,y)\,dy\,dx. \]

For a Type II region

\[ D=\{(x,y):c\le y\le d,\ h_1(y)\le x\le h_2(y)\}, \]

\[ \iint_D f(x,y)\,dA=\int_c^d\int_{h_1(y)}^{h_2(y)}f(x,y)\,dx\,dy. \]

Area:

\[ A(D)=\iint_D 1\,dA. \]

Average value:

\[ f_{\text{avg}}=\frac{1}{A(D)}\iint_D f(x,y)\,dA. \]

Mass of a lamina with density \(\rho(x,y)\):

\[ m=\iint_D \rho(x,y)\,dA. \]

Center of mass:

\[ \bar x=\frac{1}{m}\iint_D x\rho(x,y)\,dA, \qquad \bar y=\frac{1}{m}\iint_D y\rho(x,y)\,dA. \]

31.7.2 Polar coordinates and planar change of variables

Polar coordinates:

\[ x=r\cos\theta,\qquad y=r\sin\theta. \]

\[ r^2=x^2+y^2,\qquad \tan\theta=\frac{y}{x}. \]

Area element:

\[ dA=r\,dr\,d\theta. \]

Polar double integral:

\[ \iint_D f(x,y)\,dA=\int_\alpha^\beta\int_{r_1(\theta)}^{r_2(\theta)} f(r\cos\theta,r\sin\theta)r\,dr\,d\theta. \]

For a transformation \((x,y)=T(u,v)\),

\[ \iint_D f(x,y)\,dA=\iint_S f(x(u,v),y(u,v))\left|\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right|\,du\,dv, \]

where

\[ \frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}= \begin{vmatrix} x_u & x_v\\ y_u & y_v \end{vmatrix}. \]

31.7.3 Triple integrals

Volume:

\[ V(E)=\iiint_E 1\,dV. \]

Mass with density \(\rho(x,y,z)\):

\[ m=\iiint_E \rho(x,y,z)\,dV. \]

Average value:

\[ f_{\text{avg}}=\frac{1}{V(E)}\iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV. \]

Center of mass:

\[ \bar x=\frac{1}{m}\iiint_E x\rho\,dV, \qquad \bar y=\frac{1}{m}\iiint_E y\rho\,dV, \qquad \bar z=\frac{1}{m}\iiint_E z\rho\,dV. \]

31.7.4 Cylindrical and spherical coordinates

Cylindrical coordinates:

\[ x=r\cos\theta,\qquad y=r\sin\theta,\qquad z=z. \]

Volume element:

\[ dV=r\,dr\,d\theta\,dz. \]

Spherical coordinates:

\[ x=\rho\sin\phi\cos\theta, \qquad y=\rho\sin\phi\sin\theta, \qquad z=\rho\cos\phi. \]

Volume element:

\[ dV=\rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta. \]

Useful identities:

\[ \rho^2=x^2+y^2+z^2, \qquad r=\rho\sin\phi, \qquad z=\rho\cos\phi. \]

31.8 8. Line Integrals, Conservative Fields, and Green’s Theorem

31.8.1 Scalar line integrals

For a curve \(C\) parameterized by \(r(t)=(x(t),y(t),z(t))\), \(a\le t\le b\),

\[ \int_C f\,ds=\int_a^b f(r(t))\|r'(t)\|\,dt. \]

Mass of a wire with density \(\rho\):

\[ m=\int_C \rho\,ds. \]

31.8.2 Vector line integrals and work

For a vector field \(F\),

\[ \int_C F\cdot dr=\int_a^b F(r(t))\cdot r'(t)\,dt. \]

In the plane, if \(F=(P,Q)\),

\[ \int_C F\cdot dr=\int_C P\,dx+Q\,dy. \]

In space, if \(F=(P,Q,R)\),

\[ \int_C F\cdot dr=\int_C P\,dx+Q\,dy+R\,dz. \]

31.8.3 Conservative fields

A vector field \(F\) is conservative if

\[ F=\nabla f \]

for some potential function \(f\).

Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals:

\[ \int_C \nabla f\cdot dr=f(r(b))-f(r(a)). \]

If \(F\) is conservative, then line integrals are path independent and

\[ \oint_C F\cdot dr=0 \]

for every closed curve \(C\) in the appropriate domain.

For \(F=(P,Q)\) on a simply connected domain, a useful test is

\[ P_y=Q_x. \]

For \(F=(P,Q,R)\) on a simply connected solid region, a useful test is

\[ \nabla\times F=0. \]

31.8.4 Green’s theorem

Circulation form:

\[ \oint_C P\,dx+Q\,dy=\iint_D (Q_x-P_y)\,dA. \]

Flux form:

\[ \oint_C P\,dy-Q\,dx=\iint_D (P_x+Q_y)\,dA. \]

Area formulas:

\[ A(D)=\oint_C x\,dy=-\oint_C y\,dx=\frac12\oint_C x\,dy-y\,dx. \]

31.9 9. Curl, Divergence, Surface Integrals, Stokes’ Theorem, and the Divergence Theorem

31.9.1 Curl and divergence

For \(F=(P,Q,R)\),

\[ \nabla\cdot F=P_x+Q_y+R_z. \]

\[ \nabla\times F= \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf i & \mathbf j & \mathbf k\\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\\ P & Q & R \end{vmatrix}. \]

Equivalently,

\[ \nabla\times F=(R_y-Q_z,\,P_z-R_x,\,Q_x-P_y). \]

The Laplacian of a scalar field \(f\) is

\[ \Delta f=\nabla\cdot\nabla f=f_{xx}+f_{yy}+f_{zz}. \]

Useful identities:

\[ \nabla\times(\nabla f)=0, \]

\[ \nabla\cdot(\nabla\times F)=0. \]

31.9.2 Parameterized surfaces and surface area

For a parameterized surface

\[ r(u,v)=(x(u,v),y(u,v),z(u,v)), \]

a normal vector is

\[ r_u\times r_v. \]

Surface area:

\[ A(S)=\iint_D \|r_u\times r_v\|\,dA. \]

Scalar surface integral:

\[ \iint_S f\,dS=\iint_D f(r(u,v))\|r_u\times r_v\|\,du\,dv. \]

For a graph \(z=g(x,y)\),

\[ dS=\sqrt{1+g_x^2+g_y^2}\,dA. \]

31.9.3 Flux integrals

For an oriented surface \(S\) with unit normal \(n\),

\[ \iint_S F\cdot n\,dS \]

is the flux of \(F\) through \(S\).

Using a parameterization \(r(u,v)\),

\[ \iint_S F\cdot n\,dS=\iint_D F(r(u,v))\cdot(r_u\times r_v)\,du\,dv. \]

For a graph \(z=g(x,y)\) with upward orientation,

\[ n\,dS=(-g_x,-g_y,1)\,dA. \]

31.9.4 Stokes’ theorem

If \(C=\partial S\) is the positively oriented boundary of an oriented surface \(S\), then

\[ \oint_C F\cdot dr=\iint_S (\nabla\times F)\cdot n\,dS. \]

31.9.5 Divergence theorem

If \(S=\partial E\) is a closed surface oriented outward, then

\[ \iint_S F\cdot n\,dS=\iiint_E \nabla\cdot F\,dV. \]

31.10 10. Probability and Statistics Formulas

31.10.1 Joint densities

For a joint density \(f_{X,Y}(x,y)\),

\[ P((X,Y)\in D)=\iint_D f_{X,Y}(x,y)\,dA. \]

Marginal densities:

\[ f_X(x)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X,Y}(x,y)\,dy, \]

\[ f_Y(y)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X,Y}(x,y)\,dx. \]

Conditional density:

\[ f_{Y|X}(y|x)=\frac{f_{X,Y}(x,y)}{f_X(x)}. \]

Independence:

\[ f_{X,Y}(x,y)=f_X(x)f_Y(y). \]

Expectation:

\[ E[g(X,Y)]=\iint g(x,y)f_{X,Y}(x,y)\,dA. \]

Covariance:

\[ \operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=E[(X-E[X])(Y-E[Y])]. \]

31.10.2 Change of variables for densities

If \(Y=T(X)\) and \(T\) is invertible, then

\[ f_Y(y)=f_X(T^{-1}(y))\left|\det J_{T^{-1}}(y)\right|. \]

31.10.3 Multivariate normal distribution

For \(x\in\mathbb R^n\), mean \(\mu\), and positive definite covariance matrix \(\Sigma\),

\[ f(x)=\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{n/2}|\Sigma|^{1/2}} \exp\left[-\frac12(x-\mu)^T\Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu)\right]. \]

Level sets of the multivariate normal are ellipsoids:

\[ (x-\mu)^T\Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu)=c. \]

31.10.4 Likelihood, score, and observed information

For log-likelihood \(\ell(\theta)\),

\[ \text{score}=\nabla \ell(\theta). \]

The maximum likelihood estimator often satisfies

\[ \nabla \ell(\widehat\theta)=0. \]

Observed information:

\[ J(\widehat\theta)=-H_\ell(\widehat\theta). \]

Local quadratic approximation:

\[ \ell(\theta)\approx \ell(\widehat\theta)-\frac12(\theta-\widehat\theta)^T J(\widehat\theta)(\theta-\widehat\theta). \]

Delta method approximation:

\[ \operatorname{Var}(g(\widehat\theta))\approx \nabla g(\theta)^T\operatorname{Cov}(\widehat\theta)\nabla g(\theta). \]

31.11 11. Data Science and Machine Learning Formulas

31.11.1 Least squares

For data matrix \(X\in\mathbb R^{m\times p}\), response \(y\in\mathbb R^m\), and parameter vector \(\beta\in\mathbb R^p\),

\[ L(\beta)=\frac12\|X\beta-y\|^2. \]

Gradient:

\[ \nabla L(\beta)=X^T(X\beta-y). \]

Hessian:

\[ H_L=X^TX. \]

Normal equations:

\[ X^TX\widehat\beta=X^Ty. \]

Ridge regression:

\[ L_\lambda(\beta)=\frac12\|X\beta-y\|^2+\frac{\lambda}{2}\|\beta\|^2. \]

Gradient:

\[ \nabla L_\lambda(\beta)=X^T(X\beta-y)+\lambda\beta. \]

Ridge solution:

\[ \widehat\beta=(X^TX+\lambda I)^{-1}X^Ty. \]

31.11.2 Logistic regression

Sigmoid function:

\[ \sigma(t)=\frac{1}{1+e^{-t}}. \]

Binary logistic model:

\[ p_i=\sigma(x_i^T\beta). \]

Negative log-likelihood:

\[ L(\beta)=-\sum_{i=1}^m\left[y_i\log p_i+(1-y_i)\log(1-p_i)\right]. \]

Gradient:

\[ \nabla L(\beta)=X^T(p-y). \]

Hessian:

\[ H_L=X^T W X, \]

where

\[ W=\operatorname{diag}(p_i(1-p_i)). \]

31.11.3 Neural-network chain rule

For a computational graph with intermediate variables,

\[ \frac{dL}{d\theta}=\sum_j \frac{\partial L}{\partial z_j}\frac{\partial z_j}{\partial \theta}. \]

For composition of maps, gradients are propagated by Jacobian products:

\[ J_{F_k\circ\cdots\circ F_1}=J_{F_k}\cdots J_{F_1}. \]

Backpropagation is reverse-mode repeated application of the chain rule.

31.11.4 Numerical gradient check

For small \(h\) and standard basis vector \(e_i\),

\[ \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(x)\approx \frac{f(x+he_i)-f(x-he_i)}{2h}. \]

31.12 12. Applied Mathematics and PDE Formulas

Gradient:

\[ \nabla u=(u_x,u_y,u_z). \]

Divergence of a vector field \(F=(P,Q,R)\):

\[ \nabla\cdot F=P_x+Q_y+R_z. \]

Curl:

\[ \nabla\times F=(R_y-Q_z,\,P_z-R_x,\,Q_x-P_y). \]

Laplacian:

\[ \Delta u=u_{xx}+u_{yy}+u_{zz}. \]

Heat equation:

\[ u_t=\kappa\Delta u. \]

Advection equation with velocity field \(v\):

\[ u_t+v\cdot\nabla u=0. \]

Conservation law:

\[ u_t+\nabla\cdot F=0. \]

Poisson equation:

\[ -\Delta u=f. \]

Laplace equation:

\[ \Delta u=0. \]

Gradient flow for minimizing an energy \(E(u)\):

\[ \frac{du}{dt}=-\nabla E(u). \]

Continuous diffusion, optimization, and probabilistic smoothing are connected by gradient, divergence, Laplacian, and conservation laws.

# Formula Sheet

This appendix collects the central formulas used throughout the book.  It is designed as a quick reference, not as a substitute for understanding the geometry behind each formula.  The best way to use it is to identify the object first: point, vector, curve, surface, scalar field, vector field, density, loss function, or constraint.

## 1. Euclidean Space and Vector Geometry

### Points, vectors, distance, and norm

For points $a=(a_1,
\dots,a_n)$ and $b=(b_1,\dots,b_n)$ in $\mathbb R^n$,

$$
b-a=(b_1-a_1,\dots,b_n-a_n)
$$

is the displacement vector from $a$ to $b$.

$$
\|v\|=\sqrt{v_1^2+\cdots+v_n^2}
$$

$$
d(a,b)=\|b-a\|=\sqrt{(b_1-a_1)^2+\cdots+(b_n-a_n)^2}
$$

The unit vector in the direction of a nonzero vector $v$ is

$$
\widehat v=\frac{v}{\|v\|}.
$$

A sphere in $\mathbb R^3$ with center $(h,k,\ell)$ and radius $r$ has equation

$$
(x-h)^2+(y-k)^2+(z-\ell)^2=r^2.
$$

More generally, the sphere in $\mathbb R^n$ centered at $a$ with radius $r$ is

$$
\|x-a\|=r.
$$

### Dot product, angles, orthogonality, and projections

For $u,v\in \mathbb R^n$,

$$
u\cdot v=u_1v_1+\cdots+u_nv_n.
$$

$$
u\cdot v=\|u\|\|v\|\cos\theta.
$$

Thus,

$$
\cos\theta=\frac{u\cdot v}{\|u\|\|v\|}.
$$

Vectors $u$ and $v$ are orthogonal if and only if

$$
u\cdot v=0.
$$

The scalar projection of $u$ onto $v$ is

$$
\operatorname{comp}_v(u)=\frac{u\cdot v}{\|v\|}.
$$

The vector projection of $u$ onto $v$ is

$$
\operatorname{proj}_v(u)=\frac{u\cdot v}{v\cdot v}v.
$$

The component of $u$ perpendicular to $v$ is

$$
u-\operatorname{proj}_v(u).
$$

Work by a constant force $F$ along displacement $d$ is

$$
W=F\cdot d.
$$

## 2. Lines, Planes, Hyperplanes, and Cross Products

### Lines and line segments

A line through a point $p$ in direction $v\ne 0$ is

$$
r(t)=p+tv,\qquad -\infty<t<\infty.
$$

The line segment from $a$ to $b$ is

$$
r(t)=(1-t)a+tb=a+t(b-a),\qquad 0\le t\le 1.
$$

The symmetric form of a line in $\mathbb R^3$ is

$$
\frac{x-x_0}{a}=\frac{y-y_0}{b}=\frac{z-z_0}{c}
$$

when the direction vector is $(a,b,c)$ and the nonzero denominators are meaningful.

### Planes and hyperplanes

A plane through $p=(x_0,y_0,z_0)$ with normal vector $n=(A,B,C)$ is

$$
A(x-x_0)+B(y-y_0)+C(z-z_0)=0.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
n\cdot (x-p)=0.
$$

A parametric plane through $p$ spanned by two nonparallel vectors $u$ and $v$ is

$$
r(s,t)=p+su+tv.
$$

A hyperplane in $\mathbb R^n$ has the form

$$
w\cdot x=b.
$$

The distance from a point $q$ to the plane $n\cdot(x-p)=0$ is

$$
\operatorname{dist}(q,\text{plane})=\frac{|n\cdot(q-p)|}{\|n\|}.
$$

The angle $\theta$ between two planes with normal vectors $n_1$ and $n_2$ satisfies

$$
\cos\theta=\frac{|n_1\cdot n_2|}{\|n_1\|\|n_2\|}.
$$

### Cross product and triple product

For $u=(u_1,u_2,u_3)$ and $v=(v_1,v_2,v_3)$,

$$
u\times v=
\begin{vmatrix}
\mathbf i & \mathbf j & \mathbf k\\
u_1 & u_2 & u_3\\
v_1 & v_2 & v_3
\end{vmatrix}.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
u\times v=(u_2v_3-u_3v_2,\,u_3v_1-u_1v_3,\,u_1v_2-u_2v_1).
$$

The magnitude is

$$
\|u\times v\|=\|u\|\|v\|\sin\theta.
$$

Area of parallelogram spanned by $u$ and $v$:

$$
A=\|u\times v\|.
$$

Area of triangle with side vectors $u$ and $v$:

$$
A=\frac12\|u\times v\|.
$$

The scalar triple product is

$$
u\cdot(v\times w)=
\begin{vmatrix}
u_1&u_2&u_3\\
v_1&v_2&v_3\\
w_1&w_2&w_3
\end{vmatrix}.
$$

The volume of the parallelepiped spanned by $u,v,w$ is

$$
V=|u\cdot(v\times w)|.
$$

## 3. Curves and Motion in Space

For a vector-valued curve

$$
r(t)=(x(t),y(t),z(t)),
$$

velocity, speed, and acceleration are

$$
v(t)=r'(t),\qquad \text{speed}=\|r'(t)\|,\qquad a(t)=r''(t).
$$

Arc length from $t=a$ to $t=b$ is

$$
L=\int_a^b \|r'(t)\|\,dt.
$$

For a plane curve $r(t)=(x(t),y(t))$,

$$
L=\int_a^b \sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2+\left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)^2}\,dt.
$$

For a space curve $r(t)=(x(t),y(t),z(t))$,

$$
L=\int_a^b \sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2+\left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)^2+\left(\frac{dz}{dt}\right)^2}\,dt.
$$

The unit tangent vector is

$$
T(t)=\frac{r'(t)}{\|r'(t)\|}.
$$

Curvature is

$$
\kappa(t)=\frac{\|T'(t)\|}{\|r'(t)\|}.
$$

When $r'(t)\times r''(t)\ne 0$,

$$
\kappa(t)=\frac{\|r'(t)\times r''(t)\|}{\|r'(t)\|^3}.
$$

## 4. Functions, Limits, Partial Derivatives, and Gradients

### Scalar fields and level sets

A scalar field is a function

$$
f:\mathbb R^n\to \mathbb R.
$$

For $f(x,y)$, the graph is

$$
z=f(x,y).
$$

Level curves satisfy

$$
f(x,y)=c.
$$

Level surfaces satisfy

$$
f(x,y,z)=c.
$$

### Partial derivatives

For $f(x,y)$,

$$
f_x(a,b)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(a+h,b)-f(a,b)}{h},
$$

$$
f_y(a,b)=\lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(a,b+h)-f(a,b)}{h}.
$$

Second partial derivatives are

$$
f_{xx},\qquad f_{xy},\qquad f_{yx},\qquad f_{yy}.
$$

If $f_{xy}$ and $f_{yx}$ are continuous near a point, then

$$
f_{xy}=f_{yx}.
$$

### Gradient

For $f(x,y)$,

$$
\nabla f=(f_x,f_y).
$$

For $f(x,y,z)$,

$$
\nabla f=(f_x,f_y,f_z).
$$

For $f(x_1,\dots,x_n)$,

$$
\nabla f=\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1},\dots,\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}\right).
$$

The gradient is perpendicular to level curves and level surfaces.

## 5. Linear Approximation, Chain Rule, Jacobians, and Hessians

### Linearization and tangent planes

For $z=f(x,y)$, the tangent plane at $(a,b,f(a,b))$ is

$$
z=f(a,b)+f_x(a,b)(x-a)+f_y(a,b)(y-b).
$$

The linearization is

$$
L(x,y)=f(a,b)+f_x(a,b)(x-a)+f_y(a,b)(y-b).
$$

In vector form, for $f:\mathbb R^n\to \mathbb R$,

$$
f(a+h)\approx f(a)+\nabla f(a)\cdot h.
$$

The differential is

$$
df=f_x\,dx+f_y\,dy
$$

for two variables, and

$$
df=\nabla f\cdot dx
$$

in vector notation.

For a level surface $F(x,y,z)=c$, the tangent plane at $p=(x_0,y_0,z_0)$ is

$$
\nabla F(p)\cdot((x,y,z)-p)=0.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
F_x(p)(x-x_0)+F_y(p)(y-y_0)+F_z(p)(z-z_0)=0.
$$

### Directional derivatives

For a unit vector $u$,

$$
D_u f(a)=\nabla f(a)\cdot u.
$$

The maximum rate of increase is

$$
\|\nabla f(a)\|,
$$

and it occurs in the direction

$$
\frac{\nabla f(a)}{\|\nabla f(a)\|}
$$

when $\nabla f(a)\ne 0$.

### Chain rule

If $z=f(x,y)$ and $x=x(t)$, $y=y(t)$, then

$$
\frac{dz}{dt}=f_x\frac{dx}{dt}+f_y\frac{dy}{dt}.
$$

If $z=f(x,y)$, $x=x(s,t)$, and $y=y(s,t)$, then

$$
\frac{\partial z}{\partial s}=f_x\frac{\partial x}{\partial s}+f_y\frac{\partial y}{\partial s},
$$

$$
\frac{\partial z}{\partial t}=f_x\frac{\partial x}{\partial t}+f_y\frac{\partial y}{\partial t}.
$$

For a map $F:\mathbb R^n\to \mathbb R^m$, the Jacobian matrix is

$$
J_F(x)=
\begin{bmatrix}
\frac{\partial F_1}{\partial x_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial F_1}{\partial x_n}\\
\vdots & \ddots & \vdots\\
\frac{\partial F_m}{\partial x_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial F_m}{\partial x_n}
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

For a composition $H=F\circ G$,

$$
J_H(x)=J_F(G(x))J_G(x).
$$

### Hessian and Taylor approximation

The Hessian matrix of $f:\mathbb R^n\to \mathbb R$ is

$$
H_f(x)=
\begin{bmatrix}
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1^2} & \cdots & \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_1\partial x_n}\\
\vdots & \ddots & \vdots\\
\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_n\partial x_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_n^2}
\end{bmatrix}.
$$

Second-order Taylor approximation:

$$
f(a+h)\approx f(a)+\nabla f(a)\cdot h+\frac12 h^T H_f(a)h.
$$

For $f(x,y)$ near $(a,b)$,

$$
f(x,y)\approx f(a,b)+f_x(a,b)\Delta x+f_y(a,b)\Delta y
+\frac12\left[f_{xx}(a,b)(\Delta x)^2+2f_{xy}(a,b)\Delta x\Delta y+f_{yy}(a,b)(\Delta y)^2\right].
$$

## 6. Optimization

### Critical points and second derivative test

Critical points of $f(x,y)$ satisfy

$$
f_x=0,\qquad f_y=0.
$$

Let

$$
D=f_{xx}f_{yy}-f_{xy}^2.
$$

At a critical point:

| Condition | Classification |
|---|---|
| $D>0$ and $f_{xx}>0$ | local minimum |
| $D>0$ and $f_{xx}<0$ | local maximum |
| $D<0$ | saddle point |
| $D=0$ | inconclusive |

For $f:\mathbb R^n\to\mathbb R$, a critical point satisfies

$$
\nabla f=0.
$$

The Hessian test is based on eigenvalues of $H_f$:

| Hessian eigenvalues | Local behavior |
|---|---|
| all positive | local minimum |
| all negative | local maximum |
| mixed signs | saddle point |
| zero eigenvalues present | inconclusive without more analysis |

### Gradient descent and Newton's method

Gradient descent:

$$
x_{k+1}=x_k-\alpha_k\nabla f(x_k).
$$

Newton's method for optimization:

$$
x_{k+1}=x_k-H_f(x_k)^{-1}\nabla f(x_k).
$$

### Lagrange multipliers

For one constraint $g(x,y)=c$, extrema of $f$ occur where

$$
\nabla f=\lambda \nabla g,
$$

with

$$
g(x,y)=c.
$$

For several constraints $g_1=c_1,\dots,g_k=c_k$,

$$
\nabla f=\lambda_1\nabla g_1+\cdots+\lambda_k\nabla g_k,
$$

with all constraints imposed.

## 7. Double and Triple Integrals

### Double integrals

Over a rectangle $R=[a,b]\times[c,d]$,

$$
\iint_R f(x,y)\,dA=\int_a^b\int_c^d f(x,y)\,dy\,dx
=\int_c^d\int_a^b f(x,y)\,dx\,dy.
$$

For a Type I region

$$
D=\{(x,y):a\le x\le b,\ g_1(x)\le y\le g_2(x)\},
$$

$$
\iint_D f(x,y)\,dA=\int_a^b\int_{g_1(x)}^{g_2(x)}f(x,y)\,dy\,dx.
$$

For a Type II region

$$
D=\{(x,y):c\le y\le d,\ h_1(y)\le x\le h_2(y)\},
$$

$$
\iint_D f(x,y)\,dA=\int_c^d\int_{h_1(y)}^{h_2(y)}f(x,y)\,dx\,dy.
$$

Area:

$$
A(D)=\iint_D 1\,dA.
$$

Average value:

$$
f_{\text{avg}}=\frac{1}{A(D)}\iint_D f(x,y)\,dA.
$$

Mass of a lamina with density $\rho(x,y)$:

$$
m=\iint_D \rho(x,y)\,dA.
$$

Center of mass:

$$
\bar x=\frac{1}{m}\iint_D x\rho(x,y)\,dA,
\qquad
\bar y=\frac{1}{m}\iint_D y\rho(x,y)\,dA.
$$

### Polar coordinates and planar change of variables

Polar coordinates:

$$
x=r\cos\theta,\qquad y=r\sin\theta.
$$

$$
r^2=x^2+y^2,\qquad \tan\theta=\frac{y}{x}.
$$

Area element:

$$
dA=r\,dr\,d\theta.
$$

Polar double integral:

$$
\iint_D f(x,y)\,dA=\int_\alpha^\beta\int_{r_1(\theta)}^{r_2(\theta)} f(r\cos\theta,r\sin\theta)r\,dr\,d\theta.
$$

For a transformation $(x,y)=T(u,v)$,

$$
\iint_D f(x,y)\,dA=\iint_S f(x(u,v),y(u,v))\left|\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right|\,du\,dv,
$$

where

$$
\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}=
\begin{vmatrix}
x_u & x_v\\
y_u & y_v
\end{vmatrix}.
$$

### Triple integrals

Volume:

$$
V(E)=\iiint_E 1\,dV.
$$

Mass with density $\rho(x,y,z)$:

$$
m=\iiint_E \rho(x,y,z)\,dV.
$$

Average value:

$$
f_{\text{avg}}=\frac{1}{V(E)}\iiint_E f(x,y,z)\,dV.
$$

Center of mass:

$$
\bar x=\frac{1}{m}\iiint_E x\rho\,dV,
\qquad
\bar y=\frac{1}{m}\iiint_E y\rho\,dV,
\qquad
\bar z=\frac{1}{m}\iiint_E z\rho\,dV.
$$

### Cylindrical and spherical coordinates

Cylindrical coordinates:

$$
x=r\cos\theta,\qquad y=r\sin\theta,\qquad z=z.
$$

Volume element:

$$
dV=r\,dr\,d\theta\,dz.
$$

Spherical coordinates:

$$
x=\rho\sin\phi\cos\theta,
\qquad
y=\rho\sin\phi\sin\theta,
\qquad
z=\rho\cos\phi.
$$

Volume element:

$$
dV=\rho^2\sin\phi\,d\rho\,d\phi\,d\theta.
$$

Useful identities:

$$
\rho^2=x^2+y^2+z^2,
\qquad
r=\rho\sin\phi,
\qquad
z=\rho\cos\phi.
$$

## 8. Line Integrals, Conservative Fields, and Green's Theorem

### Scalar line integrals

For a curve $C$ parameterized by $r(t)=(x(t),y(t),z(t))$, $a\le t\le b$,

$$
\int_C f\,ds=\int_a^b f(r(t))\|r'(t)\|\,dt.
$$

Mass of a wire with density $\rho$:

$$
m=\int_C \rho\,ds.
$$

### Vector line integrals and work

For a vector field $F$,

$$
\int_C F\cdot dr=\int_a^b F(r(t))\cdot r'(t)\,dt.
$$

In the plane, if $F=(P,Q)$,

$$
\int_C F\cdot dr=\int_C P\,dx+Q\,dy.
$$

In space, if $F=(P,Q,R)$,

$$
\int_C F\cdot dr=\int_C P\,dx+Q\,dy+R\,dz.
$$

### Conservative fields

A vector field $F$ is conservative if

$$
F=\nabla f
$$

for some potential function $f$.

Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals:

$$
\int_C \nabla f\cdot dr=f(r(b))-f(r(a)).
$$

If $F$ is conservative, then line integrals are path independent and

$$
\oint_C F\cdot dr=0
$$

for every closed curve $C$ in the appropriate domain.

For $F=(P,Q)$ on a simply connected domain, a useful test is

$$
P_y=Q_x.
$$

For $F=(P,Q,R)$ on a simply connected solid region, a useful test is

$$
\nabla\times F=0.
$$

### Green's theorem

Circulation form:

$$
\oint_C P\,dx+Q\,dy=\iint_D (Q_x-P_y)\,dA.
$$

Flux form:

$$
\oint_C P\,dy-Q\,dx=\iint_D (P_x+Q_y)\,dA.
$$

Area formulas:

$$
A(D)=\oint_C x\,dy=-\oint_C y\,dx=\frac12\oint_C x\,dy-y\,dx.
$$

## 9. Curl, Divergence, Surface Integrals, Stokes' Theorem, and the Divergence Theorem

### Curl and divergence

For $F=(P,Q,R)$,

$$
\nabla\cdot F=P_x+Q_y+R_z.
$$

$$
\nabla\times F=
\begin{vmatrix}
\mathbf i & \mathbf j & \mathbf k\\
\frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\\
P & Q & R
\end{vmatrix}.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
\nabla\times F=(R_y-Q_z,\,P_z-R_x,\,Q_x-P_y).
$$

The Laplacian of a scalar field $f$ is

$$
\Delta f=\nabla\cdot\nabla f=f_{xx}+f_{yy}+f_{zz}.
$$

Useful identities:

$$
\nabla\times(\nabla f)=0,
$$

$$
\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times F)=0.
$$

### Parameterized surfaces and surface area

For a parameterized surface

$$
r(u,v)=(x(u,v),y(u,v),z(u,v)),
$$

a normal vector is

$$
r_u\times r_v.
$$

Surface area:

$$
A(S)=\iint_D \|r_u\times r_v\|\,dA.
$$

Scalar surface integral:

$$
\iint_S f\,dS=\iint_D f(r(u,v))\|r_u\times r_v\|\,du\,dv.
$$

For a graph $z=g(x,y)$,

$$
dS=\sqrt{1+g_x^2+g_y^2}\,dA.
$$

### Flux integrals

For an oriented surface $S$ with unit normal $n$,

$$
\iint_S F\cdot n\,dS
$$

is the flux of $F$ through $S$.

Using a parameterization $r(u,v)$,

$$
\iint_S F\cdot n\,dS=\iint_D F(r(u,v))\cdot(r_u\times r_v)\,du\,dv.
$$

For a graph $z=g(x,y)$ with upward orientation,

$$
n\,dS=(-g_x,-g_y,1)\,dA.
$$

### Stokes' theorem

If $C=\partial S$ is the positively oriented boundary of an oriented surface $S$, then

$$
\oint_C F\cdot dr=\iint_S (\nabla\times F)\cdot n\,dS.
$$

### Divergence theorem

If $S=\partial E$ is a closed surface oriented outward, then

$$
\iint_S F\cdot n\,dS=\iiint_E \nabla\cdot F\,dV.
$$

## 10. Probability and Statistics Formulas

### Joint densities

For a joint density $f_{X,Y}(x,y)$,

$$
P((X,Y)\in D)=\iint_D f_{X,Y}(x,y)\,dA.
$$

Marginal densities:

$$
f_X(x)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X,Y}(x,y)\,dy,
$$

$$
f_Y(y)=\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X,Y}(x,y)\,dx.
$$

Conditional density:

$$
f_{Y|X}(y|x)=\frac{f_{X,Y}(x,y)}{f_X(x)}.
$$

Independence:

$$
f_{X,Y}(x,y)=f_X(x)f_Y(y).
$$

Expectation:

$$
E[g(X,Y)]=\iint g(x,y)f_{X,Y}(x,y)\,dA.
$$

Covariance:

$$
\operatorname{Cov}(X,Y)=E[(X-E[X])(Y-E[Y])].
$$

### Change of variables for densities

If $Y=T(X)$ and $T$ is invertible, then

$$
f_Y(y)=f_X(T^{-1}(y))\left|\det J_{T^{-1}}(y)\right|.
$$

### Multivariate normal distribution

For $x\in\mathbb R^n$, mean $\mu$, and positive definite covariance matrix $\Sigma$,

$$
f(x)=\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{n/2}|\Sigma|^{1/2}}
\exp\left[-\frac12(x-\mu)^T\Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu)\right].
$$

Level sets of the multivariate normal are ellipsoids:

$$
(x-\mu)^T\Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu)=c.
$$

### Likelihood, score, and observed information

For log-likelihood $\ell(\theta)$,

$$
\text{score}=\nabla \ell(\theta).
$$

The maximum likelihood estimator often satisfies

$$
\nabla \ell(\widehat\theta)=0.
$$

Observed information:

$$
J(\widehat\theta)=-H_\ell(\widehat\theta).
$$

Local quadratic approximation:

$$
\ell(\theta)\approx \ell(\widehat\theta)-\frac12(\theta-\widehat\theta)^T J(\widehat\theta)(\theta-\widehat\theta).
$$

Delta method approximation:

$$
\operatorname{Var}(g(\widehat\theta))\approx \nabla g(\theta)^T\operatorname{Cov}(\widehat\theta)\nabla g(\theta).
$$

## 11. Data Science and Machine Learning Formulas

### Least squares

For data matrix $X\in\mathbb R^{m\times p}$, response $y\in\mathbb R^m$, and parameter vector $\beta\in\mathbb R^p$,

$$
L(\beta)=\frac12\|X\beta-y\|^2.
$$

Gradient:

$$
\nabla L(\beta)=X^T(X\beta-y).
$$

Hessian:

$$
H_L=X^TX.
$$

Normal equations:

$$
X^TX\widehat\beta=X^Ty.
$$

Ridge regression:

$$
L_\lambda(\beta)=\frac12\|X\beta-y\|^2+\frac{\lambda}{2}\|\beta\|^2.
$$

Gradient:

$$
\nabla L_\lambda(\beta)=X^T(X\beta-y)+\lambda\beta.
$$

Ridge solution:

$$
\widehat\beta=(X^TX+\lambda I)^{-1}X^Ty.
$$

### Logistic regression

Sigmoid function:

$$
\sigma(t)=\frac{1}{1+e^{-t}}.
$$

Binary logistic model:

$$
p_i=\sigma(x_i^T\beta).
$$

Negative log-likelihood:

$$
L(\beta)=-\sum_{i=1}^m\left[y_i\log p_i+(1-y_i)\log(1-p_i)\right].
$$

Gradient:

$$
\nabla L(\beta)=X^T(p-y).
$$

Hessian:

$$
H_L=X^T W X,
$$

where

$$
W=\operatorname{diag}(p_i(1-p_i)).
$$

### Neural-network chain rule

For a computational graph with intermediate variables,

$$
\frac{dL}{d\theta}=\sum_j \frac{\partial L}{\partial z_j}\frac{\partial z_j}{\partial \theta}.
$$

For composition of maps, gradients are propagated by Jacobian products:

$$
J_{F_k\circ\cdots\circ F_1}=J_{F_k}\cdots J_{F_1}.
$$

Backpropagation is reverse-mode repeated application of the chain rule.

### Numerical gradient check

For small $h$ and standard basis vector $e_i$,

$$
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(x)\approx \frac{f(x+he_i)-f(x-he_i)}{2h}.
$$

## 12. Applied Mathematics and PDE Formulas

Gradient:

$$
\nabla u=(u_x,u_y,u_z).
$$

Divergence of a vector field $F=(P,Q,R)$:

$$
\nabla\cdot F=P_x+Q_y+R_z.
$$

Curl:

$$
\nabla\times F=(R_y-Q_z,\,P_z-R_x,\,Q_x-P_y).
$$

Laplacian:

$$
\Delta u=u_{xx}+u_{yy}+u_{zz}.
$$

Heat equation:

$$
u_t=\kappa\Delta u.
$$

Advection equation with velocity field $v$:

$$
u_t+v\cdot\nabla u=0.
$$

Conservation law:

$$
u_t+\nabla\cdot F=0.
$$

Poisson equation:

$$
-\Delta u=f.
$$

Laplace equation:

$$
\Delta u=0.
$$

Gradient flow for minimizing an energy $E(u)$:

$$
\frac{du}{dt}=-\nabla E(u).
$$

Continuous diffusion, optimization, and probabilistic smoothing are connected by gradient, divergence, Laplacian, and conservation laws.
